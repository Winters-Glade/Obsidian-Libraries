# Latino History

## Overview

This video traces the history of Latino peoples and cultures in the Americas, with particular focus on the 19th century — a period of revolutionary upheaval, nation-building, and shifting power relations between Latin America, the United States, and Europe. Rudyard Lynch and Austin Padgett examine the legacy of Spanish and Portuguese colonialism, the wars of independence (1810-1825), the 19th-century struggles to build stable republics, the massive demographic and territorial changes triggered by U.S. expansion, and the emergence of Latino identity as a distinct but internally diverse category that would come to play an increasingly important role in the story of the Americas.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### The Colonial Legacy
- **Three centuries of Spanish rule** (1492-1820s): extractive economies (silver, sugar), racial hierarchy (*casta* system), Catholic monopoly on religion and education
- **Casta system**: peninsulares (Spanish-born), criollos (American-born Spaniards), mestizos (mixed indigenous-European), indigenous, African slaves, and many subcategories
- **Economic structure**: latifundia (large estates), mining, tribute labor (repartimiento, encomienda)
- **Political centralization**: viceroyalties of New Spain, Peru, New Granada, Rio de la Plata; governed for Spanish crown
- **Cultural foundations**: language, religion, legal traditions, and social hierarchies that persisted after independence

### The Wars of Independence (1808-1825)
- **Triggered by Napoleon's invasion of Spain** (1808) — colonial administration decapitated
- **Key leaders**: Simón Bolívar (northern South America), José de San Martín (southern cone), Miguel Hidalgo (Mexico)
- **Haitian Revolution** (1791-1804): first successful slave revolt; established second independent republic in Americas
- **Outcome**: independence achieved across Spanish America, but at enormous cost — economies devastated, infrastructure destroyed
- **Fragmentation**: Gran Colombia fractured into Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador; United Provinces of Rio de la Plata became Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay
- **Brazil's unique path**: Portuguese monarchy fled to Brazil (1808); independence declared by Pedro I (1822) — peaceful transition, retained monarchy until 1889

### The 19th Century Nation-Building Struggle
- **Political instability**: caudillismo (strongman rule), constitutions rewritten frequently, civil wars between liberals and conservatives
- **Liberal vs. Conservative divide**: liberals wanted federalism, secularism, free trade, land reform; conservatives wanted centralism, church power, protectionism, large estates
- **Economic dependency**: export economies (guano, nitrates, coffee, sugar, beef) tied to European and U.S. markets — neocolonialism
- **Land and labor**: abolition of slavery (gradual through 19th century); continuation of debt peonage and hacienda system
- **Racial hierarchies**: independence didn't end racial inequality; criollos replaced peninsulares at top

### The U.S.-Mexican War and Its Consequences
- **Texas independence** (1836): Anglo settlers in Mexican Texas rebelled; U.S. annexed (1845)
- **Mexican-American War** (1846-1848): U.S. invasion of Mexico; Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo
- **Territorial losses**: Mexico lost 55% of its territory — California, Arizona, New Mexico, Texas, Nevada, Utah, parts of Colorado and Wyoming
- **Hispanic populations became U.S. minorities**: Mexican citizens in the conquered territories became foreigners in their own land
- **Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo promised protections**: land rights, cultural rights, citizenship — but these were often violated
- **California Gold Rush** (1848): brought Anglo miners, marginalized Californios; violence, land theft, segregation

### Latino Migration and Diaspora
- **Mexican Revolution** (1910-1920): pushed millions northward as refugees
- **Economic migration**: U.S. labor needs in agriculture, railroads, mining, manufacturing
- **Bracero Program** (1942-1964): temporary guest worker program for Mexican laborers
- **Puerto Rico**: became U.S. territory after Spanish-American War (1898); citizenship granted 1917; migration to mainland
- **Cuban diaspora**: post-1959 revolution migration; Cuban American community in Florida
- **Central American migration**: driven by civil wars, U.S. intervention, economic crisis (1980s)
- **Dominican Republic**: U.S. occupation (1916-1924); later migration waves

### Latino Identity and Civil Rights
- **Diverse identities**: Mexican-American, Puerto Rican, Cuban-American, Central American — united by language, colonial heritage, but distinct histories
- **Chicano Movement** (1960s-1970s): civil rights activism, cultural pride, political organizing (Cesar Chavez, Dolores Huerta, United Farm Workers)
- **Language politics**: English-only movements vs. bilingual education; Spanish as marker of identity
- **Demographic transformation**: by 2020, Latinos were the largest minority in the U.S. (62 million, ~19% of population)
- **Political influence**: growing electoral power, representation in government, but persistent underrepresentation

## Important Figures

- **Simón Bolívar** (1783-1830): "El Libertador" — liberated Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia
- **Miguel Hidalgo y Costilla** (1753-1811): priest who sparked Mexican independence
- **José de San Martín** (1778-1850): liberator of Argentina, Chile, Peru
- **Benito Juárez** (1806-1872): indigenous Mexican reformer, president, defeated French intervention
- **Porfirio Díaz** (1830-1915): dictator who modernized Mexico at great social cost
- **Emiliano Zapata** (1879-1919): revolutionary leader, agrarian reform, "Tierra y Libertad"
- **Pancho Villa** (1878-1923): revolutionary general, folk hero
- **José Martí** (1853-1895): Cuban independence leader, poet, intellectual
- **Toussaint Louverture** (1743-1803): leader of Haitian Revolution
- **Cesar Chavez** (1927-1993): labor leader, United Farm Workers, civil rights activist
- **Dolores Huerta** (b. 1930): labor leader, co-founder of United Farm Workers
- **Rita Moreno** (b. 1931): Puerto Rican actress, EGOT winner, cultural icon
- **Roberto Clemente** (1934-1972): Puerto Rican baseball legend, humanitarian
