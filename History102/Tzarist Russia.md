# Tzarist Russia

**Source:** [YouTube - History102](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3ZX4rye0JBg)
**Hosts:** Rudyard Lynch & Austin Padgett
**Duration:** 56 min

## Overview

A focused examination of the Tsarist Russian state — from the rise of the Grand Duchy of Moscow through the Romanov dynasty to the 1917 Revolution. Rudyard and Austin discuss how Russia's position between the steppe and Europe, its Mongol inheritance, and its unique political structure shaped one of history's most enduring authoritarian states.

## Key Topics

- **Mongol Inheritance** — Russia's political structure was deeply shaped by its subjugation to the Golden Horde (Mongol successor state). The princes of Moscow collected tribute for the Mongols and adopted Mongol techniques of autocratic rule.
- **Ivan III (the Great, r. 1462–1505)** — Threw off Mongol rule. Married the Byzantine princess Sophia Palaiologina. Adopted the title "Tsar" (from Caesar). Declared Moscow the "Third Rome."
- **Ivan IV (the Terrible, r. 1547–1584)** — The first crowned Tsar. Expanded Russia massively. Created the oprichnina (state terror apparatus). Alternately reformist and psychotic.
- **Time of Troubles (1598–1613)** — Dynastic crisis, famine, Polish invasion. Ended with the election of Michael Romanov, founding the Romanov Dynasty.
- **Peter the Great (r. 1682–1725)** — Westernized Russia by force. Built St. Petersburg as a "window to the West." Modernized the army, navy, and bureaucracy. Forced the boyars (nobles) to adopt Western customs. Created the Table of Ranks.
- **Catherine the Great (r. 1762–1796)** — Expanded Russia into Crimea and Poland. Enlightened absolutism — corresponded with Voltaire and Diderot. The Pugachev Rebellion exposed the brutality of serfdom.
- **Serfdom** — The foundation of Tsarist Russia. Serfs were effectively slaves, owned by nobles. Unlike the West, Russia's serfdom grew harsher over time instead of being abolished. This created an immense gulf between the Westernized elite and the masses.
- **The Intelligentsia and Revolutionary Movements** — The Decembrist Revolt (1825), the Populists (Narodniks), anarchists, and Marxists. A cycle of repression, radicalization, and assassination.
- **The Romanov Decline** — Nicholas I, Alexander II (emancipated the serfs in 1861, then assassinated), Alexander III (repression), Nicholas II (weak, indecisive, dominated by Rasputin).
- **1905 Revolution** — Defeat in the Russo-Japanese War triggered a revolution. Nicholas II was forced to create a parliament (Duma) but soon undermined it.
- **World War I and the 1917 Revolution** — The war exposed every weakness of the Tsarist state. Mass casualties, food shortages, and the Tsar's disastrous decision to personally command the army led to the February Revolution and the end of 300 years of Romanov rule.
- **Russian Character** — Rudyard argues that Russia's culture was shaped by its endless frontier and Mongol heritage — a combination of autocratic governance, suspicion of the West, and a sense of unique destiny (Third Rome, Holy Russia).

## Summary

Tsarist Russia was a unique political formation — a hybrid of Byzantine, Mongol, and Western influences grafted onto an Eastern Slavic base. Emerging from Mongol domination, the Tsardom of Moscow expanded across Eurasia to become the world's largest state, stretching from Poland to the Pacific.

The central tension of Tsarist history was between Westernization and autocracy. Peter the Great forced Russia to modernize technologically, but the political structure remained autocratic. Serfdom, which the West had largely abolished by the 19th century, persisted in Russia until 1861. This created a society where a Westernized elite ruled over a deeply traditional peasant masses.

The attempt at reform (Alexander II's emancipation) came too late and satisfied no one. The revolutionaries wanted more; the conservatives wanted the old order. The cycle of repression and radicalization intensified until World War I broke the system entirely. The Tsarist state, which had seemed so powerful, collapsed in a matter of days in February 1917.

## Key Takeaways

- Russia's political structure inherited much from the Mongols (Golden Horde)
- The Tsar claimed to be both heir to Byzantium ("Third Rome") and absolute autocrat
- Peter the Great forced Westernization from above
- Serfdom persisted far longer in Russia than in the West (until 1861)
- The intelligentsia was increasingly radical and revolutionary
- The 1861 emancipation of the serfs satisfied no one
- WWI broke the Tsarist state — it collapsed in days in Feb 1917
- The gap between Westernized elite and traditional masses was central to Russia's tragedy
