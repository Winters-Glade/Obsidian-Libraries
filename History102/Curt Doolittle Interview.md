# Curt Doolittle Interview: Philosopher, CEO

> **Source:** [History102](https://www.youtube.com/@History102-qg5oj) — [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E9bgRFbNTls)

## Episode Overview

Curt Doolittle, philosopher and CEO of The Propertarian Institute, joins History102 co-hosts Dave Hamilton and Rudyard Lynch (of "WhatifAltHist" fame) for a deep philosophical conversation spanning evolutionary neurology, sex differences, class structure, falsification as a universal method, the origins of Western civilization, and the nature of truth vs. goodness in social science. The conversation — originally aired on "Common Ground" (later renamed History102) — explores Doolittle's propertarian framework which integrates AI, computational law, economics, and evolutionary psychology into a unified system of governance and ethics. The three men, despite differing temperaments (Doolittle as the aristocratic "king," Lynch as the empathetic "priest," and Hamilton as the pragmatic bridge-builder), find substantial common ground through mutual respect and a shared commitment to truth-seeking and nuanced conversation.

## Guest Biography

Curt Doolittle is a philosopher and the CEO of The Propertarian Institute. He develops and promotes the philosophical system of Propertarianism, a framework combining natural law, property rights, and evolutionary psychology to derive principles of governance, ethics, and social organization. His background spans artificial intelligence (he was one of the first to write an AI for law), computational law, economics, and neurology. He describes his work as sitting at the intersection of cause (neurology), cooperation (economics), and conflict (law) — three empirical sciences that compete with one another to produce surviving truth rather than socially desirable outcomes.

## Key Arguments & Discussion Points

### Falsification as Universal Method

Doolittle argues that falsification — the extension of the scientific method to every aspect of life — is the only reliable way to determine truth. The humanities and social sciences reject falsification in favor of religious narratives (e.g., "if you're oppressed, you're good; if you're oppressor, you're bad"). The behavioral sciences are "trying to discover the good instead of the true." Doolittle insists you must discover what is true first, then decide good and bad from that foundation. Those who don't value truth at all are a fundamental obstacle to rational discourse.

### The Three Sciences: Neurology, Economics, Law

Doolittle's philosophical system rests on three empirical sciences at the intersection of which he works:

- **Neurology** (cause) — how the brain produces behavior
- **Economics** (cooperation) — the science of cooperation
- **Law** (conflict) — the science of conflict

These three sciences are in competition with one another. Truth is what survives this competition — not what is socially desirable or politically convenient. Doolittle's background in AI (he was one of the first to write an AI for law), computational law, and economics led him to this synthesis. "Law is the mirror of economics."

### Constraint-Based Reasoning and Empathy

"Everything makes sense under the circumstances." Doolittle argues that understanding others requires understanding the constraints they operate under — geographic, historical, neurological, and strategic. Examples include: Russia's geography of open plains making unquestioning loyalty to authority seem like a moral virtue (leading from Mongol invasions to Stalinism), or the "degenerate" male strategy of impregnating many women without providing care as a gene-survival strategy. This is constraint-based reasoning: empathy as understanding, not sympathy.

### Sex Differences in Cognition

A central theme of the conversation is the neurological basis of sex differences:

- **Masculine cognition** (systematizing): Predicts outcomes *over time*; predator orientation; specializes in transforming the physical world; processes information quickly through the left hemisphere
- **Feminine cognition** (empathizing): Predicts emotions *in time*; prey orientation; extends the nervous system over dependents; processes prey information across time; more horizontally organized

Women hyper-consume *in time*; men hyper-capitalize *over time*. Status for men = responsibility for capital and others; status for women = consumption. Doolittle emphasizes these are distributions on a continuum, not absolutes — everyone is an admixture.

### Politics as Secular Religion

When religion is removed from society, its remnants appear everywhere — in pop culture, in politics, in ideology. People need to believe in something not of this world, because "everything of this world will eventually let you down." Politics becomes a religion without being religious: positions become absolute moral goods (immigration must always be good, free trade must always be good), and nuanced conversation becomes impossible because disagreement feels like heresy.

### Class, IQ, and Responsibility Over Time

Doolittle defines class not by income but by "the ability to carry responsibility over time." This is driven primarily by IQ (neurological capacity) and conscientiousness (trait persistence). Social mobility is uncommon; populations regress toward the mean of their reproductive class. Races differ not inherently but in the *distribution of classes* within them — where the mean is located. The ability to integrate across groups is possible at the top and upper-middle class but increasingly difficult below that, where people are more dependent on local norms, traditions, and institutions.

### The Origins of Western Civilization

Doolittle provides a sweeping origin story for the West: Europe's geography of forests and lack of navigable rivers (unlike the river civilizations of the Middle East, India, and China) made it impossible to concentrate power and taxation. This forced Europeans into a military-derived society organized by contract, law, meritocracy, and militia — essentially a "pirate economy and polity" on land. The aristocratic roots of Western democracy come from the steppe nomads (bronze, wheel, horse) whose entrepreneurial clan-based structure was codified in Sparta, not Athens. Western high-trust culture is "irrational" from a global perspective — and it's precisely that irrationality that makes Westerners vulnerable to sedition by Abrahamic-Marxist ideologies.

### Civilizational Fatal Flaws

Each major civilization has a characteristic fatal flaw:

- **China**: Inability to judge authority
- **Islam**: Inability to judge religious texts
- **The West**: Too high social trust — incapable of seeing evil within its own society; when facing struggle, the West doubles down on self-criticism and trust, the very process that caused the problem

### Doolittle's Mission: Making Law a Science

Doolittle's ultimate goal is to make law into a science so that "nobody can ever lie to us and deceive us the way that we have been ever again." He identifies the current era as a "feminine crime wave" — industrialized criminal activity using female strategies (gossip, shrill, ostracism — GSR) at industrial scale for the first time in history. His role is to give judges, juries, scientists, and intellectuals the tools to stop deception; others must create the demand for that law.

### The Doolittle-Lynch Temperament Difference

A notable meta-discussion emerges about the difference between Doolittle and Rudyard Lynch:

- **Doolittle** (aristocrat/king): Sees the world as inherently comprehensible through constraints; thinks in large-scale patterns over time; wants to make things decidable
- **Lynch** (priest): Sees the world as inherently irrational and chaotic; thinks from others' perspectives; worries about the people and the misuse of metrics

Despite these different temperaments — and despite political disagreements — they consistently arrive at the same conclusions through different paths, which Doolittle takes as validation of their shared methodology.

## Points of Agreement/Disagreement

- **Agreement**: Both Doolittle and Lynch share a commitment to truth-seeking over ideological conformity; both reject the blank slate/noble savage paradigm; both see the left's social justice framework as scientifically dead; both believe falsification is critically important; both value nuanced, contextual conversation over absolutism
- **Disagreement / Tension**: Doolittle believes the world is inherently comprehensible through empirical constraints; Lynch believes reality is fundamentally irrational and chaotic. Doolittle is more comfortable with genetic and IQ-based explanations; Lynch warns about the historical misuse of such metrics. Doolittle thinks like an "aristocrat" concerned with large-scale systems; Lynch thinks like a "priest" concerned with individual perspectives. Doolittle believes free choice operates within natural constraints; Lynch emphasizes the role of chaos and contingency
- **Hamilton's role**: Acts as bridge/pragmatist, pushing both to make their insights operational and practical
