# David Linton Interview: How College Debt is Killing Society

> **Source:** [History102](https://www.youtube.com/@History102-qg5oj) — [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oH0taVuh-lA)

## Guest Biography

David Linton is an economist and author who has studied the college debt crisis in depth. He wrote a book titled *Crushed* that examines the history, economics, and policy failures of the American higher education financing system. He focuses on tuition inflation, the student loan industrial complex (particularly Sallie Mae), and the long-term effects of indebtedness on young Americans' life outcomes. He advocates for long-term structural reforms rather than short-term debt cancellation.

## Key Arguments & Discussion Points

### The Scale of the Crisis

- The college debt crisis has been **40-50 years in the making**, spanning one to two generations
- The system can be corrected over 10-30 years with proper incentive alignment
- Short-term debt cancellation does not fix the underlying problem — it merely kicks the can down the road
- **1.7 trillion dollars** in outstanding student debt, affecting **42-44 million Americans**

### Tuition Inflation Data

- **1969:** Average cost of one year tuition + room & board at a public school = ~$1,500 = **19% of median household income**
- **2020:** Same costs ≈ ~$30,000 = **42% of median household income** — over double the income share
- Inflation-adjusted: cost has risen **~4×** over 50 years
- **Key comparison:** One year of tuition/room/board today costs what a **full degree** cost in 1970
- Tuition has inflated at roughly **inflation + 3% per year** since 1970

### Return on Investment Collapse

- **1978:** Average degree cost = $10,900; incremental annual earnings after graduation = ~$8,000 (men) / ~$10,000 (women) → **paid back in ~1.5 years**
- **Today:** Average degree cost ≈ $112,000; incremental annual earnings ≈ $20,000 → **paid back in ~6 years**
- A study by a Texas-based think tank found that if you account for the likelihood of dropping out or taking >4 years, **over a third of students have a negative internal rate of return** — they are financially worse off for having gone to college
- The societal pressure to attend college peaked around 2015-2017 (referenced Michelle Obama/Jay Pharaoh rap video, college enrollment peak)

### Causes of Tuition Inflation

1. **Bennett Hypothesis** (William Bennett, 1987): Every time the federal government increases student loan availability by a dollar, colleges raise prices by a dollar
2. **Sallie Mae's perverse incentives** (created in the 1970s): Operated as a public-private intermediary earning a ~3% spread, with the government fully reimbursing defaulted loans — so Sallie Mae's only limit was growing its portfolio, leading to aggressive lobbying for higher loan limits
3. **US News & World Report rankings**: Designed to measure quality via spending-per-student, which incentivizes colleges to raise tuition and spend more (on facilities, administration, etc.) to climb rankings, regardless of educational outcomes
4. **Administrative bloat**: Mid-level management at universities has exploded **5-10×** over the last 70 years — DEI staff, alumni relations, counselors, etc. — with little direct student benefit
5. **Credential inflation / Signaling**: Degrees serve as hiring signals (a risk-mitigation strategy for HR), creating artificial demand. Michael Spence's signaling theory cited
6. **International students paying full sticker price** — they subsidize domestic students but also drive demand
7. **Universities spend everything they take in** (no retained earnings, unlike corporations), so any revenue increase gets spent the following year

### Societal Consequences

- **Social mobility declined**: 60% of students at top 20 universities come from the top 1% income bracket — the loan system entrenched upper/middle classes rather than helping the poor
- USA has the **lowest level of social mobility in American history**
- **Delayed life milestones**: Marriage, homeownership, childbearing pushed back due to debt burden
- **Gender imbalance**: College enrollment is now **60% female / 40% male** — last time a 60/40 ratio existed (in either direction) was **1910** (60% male, before women's suffrage)
- **Discourse collapse**: In the 19th century (0.5% college attendance), presidential debates were at college-level reading; now (33% have degrees), debates are at **5th grade reading level**
- **Historical parallels**: David Graeber's *Debt* — societies that deny bankruptcy to debtors create conditions for revolution (Babylonian/Greek debt jubilees, serfdom)

### The Purpose of College

- Historically swung back and forth: seminaries (1600s-1700s) → vocational (pre-Civil War) → undefined today
- Key question: Is it intellectual discovery, elite networking, or workforce training?
- The implied purpose (given poor economic returns) seems to be "benefit society" rather than generate economic value

## Policy Solutions Proposed by David Linton

### 1. Colleges Buy 10% of Student Debt
- Every college must purchase 10% of the student debt originated at their institution
- If they lack cash, they can borrow from the Treasury
- This aligns incentives: schools that produce graduates who can repay their loans are rewarded; those with high default rates are penalized
- Forces cost rationalization and responsible degree offerings

### 2. Price Controls Tied to Median Household Income
- Cap average tuition at **20% of median household income** (the threshold before student debt went parabolic around 1990)
- Public and private schools both subject to the cap
- Schools above the cap cannot raise prices until median income catches up
- Gradual adjustment over 10-50 years — similar to CAFE standards (fuel economy) which reduced gasoline consumption by ~2/3 over 40 years
- Carve-outs for high-cost areas (25% vs 20%)

### 3. Bankruptcy Reform
- Currently: student loans are nearly impossible to discharge in bankruptcy (extreme "Brunner test" standard)
- Originally (1978 Bankruptcy Reform Act): loans non-dischargeable for only **5 years** after leaving college, or upon showing undue hardship
- Congress extended the window to 7 years, then eliminated it entirely — making the Brunner test (intended as temporary) permanent
- Proposal: Restore the 5-year window, treat student debt like other consumer debt
- Concern: Some students might take loans and immediately declare bankruptcy — but this also forces lenders to be more disciplined

### 4. Gainful Employment Rules
- Extend to all schools the kind of accountability already applied to for-profit colleges
- Measure employment rates, graduation rates, and ability to repay after graduation
- Schools with persistently poor outcomes could lose access to federal student loans

## Points of Agreement/Disagreement

- **Strong agreement** among all participants on the severity and unsustainability of the student debt crisis
- Linton is skeptical of blanket debt cancellation — it doesn't fix the incentive structure and perpetuates the problem
- Tension between short-term relief (politically expedient) vs. long-term structural reform (necessary but difficult)
- Co-host David Hamilton (classical liberal) and Rudyard both push back on aspects of Linton's policy proposals but agree on the diagnosis
- Agreement that the current political environment offers **no serious policy solutions** — one party proposes bad ideas, the other proposes no ideas

## Historical Context

- The GI Bill opened college as a route to social mobility, but over time it became a tool of class entrenchment
- Sallie Mae was created in the 1970s to act as an intermediary making it easier for banks to extend student loans — Linton describes it as "literally a money printing machine"
- The original federal government entry into student loans (Space Race era) aimed to produce more mathematicians and engineers
- The Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978 created the carve-out for student loans; originally only a 5-year window of non-dischargeability
- Linton traces parallels to ancient Babylon, Greece, and Rome — civilizations that denied debt relief experienced revolutions and debt jubilees
- The pharmaceutical industry's self-imposed ban on extravagant doctor gifts cited as a precedent for collective action on price controls
