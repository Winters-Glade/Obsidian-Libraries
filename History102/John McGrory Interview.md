# John McGrory Interview

> **Source:** [History102](https://www.youtube.com/@History102-qg5oj) — [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jl9_ZaL-AYM)
> **Podcast:** Common Ground — Co-hosts: Rudyard Lynch and Dave Hamilton

## Guest Biography

John McGrory is a political commentator, researcher for Newsmax, and a Catholic who engages deeply with questions of Christianity, culture, idolatry, and the role of religious faith in modern life. He brings a distinctive Catholic intellectual perspective to discussions about what makes the Christian tradition distinct among world religions.

## Key Arguments & Discussion Points

### 1. The Impossibility of Atheism
McGrory argues that "atheists may not believe in God, but I don't believe in atheists." He contends that every society is religious in some form — when you remove God, something else takes His place. The United States is not godless, he says, but **idolatrous** — we replace God with money, power, pleasure, science, knowledge, ideology, or whatever else becomes the ultimate concern. This builds on the first commandment: "I am God, you shall have no other God but me" — a commandment broken constantly, not by sacrificing to Thor or Zeus, but by pushing God aside and elevating substitutes.

### 2. The Old Testament Pattern: Lather, Rinse, Repeat
The Old Testament reveals a recurring cycle: times are hard → the Jews go back to God → times are easy → they replace God with other gods → times are hard again. McGrory compares this to a 1,000-year pattern, asking us to consider our own track record over the last millennium. Dave Hamilton adds that even the disciples, who witnessed miracles firsthand, still faltered — a humbling observation that reveals the Bible's comfort: even the most fallible are not abandoned by God.

### 3. "Normie Mindset" and Historical Amnesia
Rudyard Lynch introduces the concept of "normie mindset" — the assumption that one's current frame of reference is the only valid one. People forget how rapidly norms change: working weekends wasn't normal in 2010, the trans issue wasn't front and center, and so on. This human trait of belittling and ignoring what doesn't fit one's rigid worldview makes people unable to process paradigm-shifting events. As McGrory notes, paraphrasing Barbra Streisand, "What's too painful to remember, we simply choose to forget."

### 4. The Parable of the Prodigal Son
McGrory delivers an extended, nuanced reading of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15). Key insights:
- Jesus never describes the younger son's dissolute life because every listener conjures the image themselves.
- The younger son rehearses his apology the whole way home — a universal human experience.
- The older son has done everything right but is miserable in his resentment, living "in his head" wishing he could be like his brother.
- The father's hands in Rembrandt's painting — one masculine, one feminine — represent the dual nature of divine love: discipline and warmth.
- The younger son's journey mirrors addiction, relapse, and the difficulty of starting over.

### 5. Christianity's Balance of Standards and Warmth
Rudyard Lynch argues that Christianity uniquely balances masculine discipline (standards) with feminine warmth. Remove the standards and you get the left — which appears kind and open but becomes tyrannical due to lack of stability. Remove the warmth and you get Islam — a restrictive society that can slow development. Christianity's standards enable its warmth: it abolished slavery, gave women the best treatment of any major religion, and calls people to something better while offering grace.

### 6. "Neither Do I Condemn You; Go and Sin No More"
McGrory identifies this as the essential tension of Christianity. The church that only says "neither do I condemn you" never calls people to anything better; the church that only says "go and sin no more" is unforgiving. Both statements together create the dynamic tension that makes Christianity work. **Neither can be taken alone.**

### 7. Science as a Substitute Religion
McGrory draws a powerful parallel between COVID response and organized religion:
- **Pope Fauci** making infallible statements (sometimes contradictory)
- **Clergy** (public health officials) proclaiming doctrine
- **Prophet Andrew Cuomo** delivering daily updates
- **Religious garb** — wearing multiple masks; vaccination buttons as "true believer" markers
- **Sacraments and rituals** — standing six feet apart
- **Heretics** — those who questioned were shunned and ostracized
- **The angel of death** that "passed over" BLM protesters but "smote" those at church services or Trump rallies — a perverse mirror of the Passover

> "When you throw out religion, something else is going to come in and take its place."

### 8. Christianity and the Birth of Modern Science
Rudyard Lynch argues that science is philosophically dependent upon Christianity. The Greeks and Romans were incredibly intelligent but lacked humility — they thought intellectual argument trumped external evidence. Christianity's unique combination enabled the Scientific Revolution:
1. **Humility** (leads to empiricism and testing)
2. **A rational universe** (discovered as God's creation)
3. **Belief in abstract truth**

The founders of modern science were overwhelmingly Christian monks in the 1300s who invented the scientific method. McGrory adds that Thomas Aquinas argued the world was made according to God's order, and humans, made in God's image, are capable of discerning that order — the philosophical foundation of science. Harvard's original motto was *Veritas pro Christo in Ecclesia* (Truth for Christ in the Church), and the original seal had one book upside down to signify that humans cannot know everything — only God does.

### 9. Christianity's Distinctive Humility
McGrory contrasts face-driven cultures (where admitting error dishonors the tribe) with Christianity's embrace of humility. The Gospel writers recorded Peter's denials even though Peter was the leader of the church — he could have suppressed that story. Even after seeing the risen Christ, the apostles went back into hiding. God deliberately chooses flawed, fallible people: Moses (who couldn't speak), Paul (a former persecutor), Gideon (whose army was reduced to 1%). As McGrory puts it: **The people Jesus chose were extremely fallible** — and that's the point.

### 10. Objective Truth vs. "My Truth"
McGrory rejects the phrase "my truth" vs. "your truth" as logically incoherent: "If my truth and your truth are different, then one of them is not truth." He notes the self-refuting nature of relativism: "There are no truths" or "There are no rules as a rule" are both statements of truth. Rudyard Lynch adds that most people don't believe in the idea of reality anymore, but "if I punch you in the face, you are punched in the face — it doesn't matter what you think."

### 11. Abortion and Logical Inconsistency
The conversation turns to abortion as a case study in motivated reasoning. Key arguments:
- The first question must be: **Is it a human being?** If yes, then certain requirements follow; if no, then abort freely. "Your ought should follow your is."
- Science has advanced since Roe v. Wade — sonograms, in utero surgery — yet the argument shifted from "we don't know if it's a human being" to "it's about choice."
- The inconsistency: the same fetus is protected if the mother wants it but not if she doesn't; a dog receives more legal protection in some cases.
- McGrory cites the **Didache** (first-century Christian teaching document): "We do not abort our children" — this was a distinctive Christian practice from the very beginning.
- John Haidt's research: ~90% of people feel an emotion first, then rationalize; only ~10% think rationally and add emotion afterward.

### 12. Emotions as Emissaries, Not Masters
Lynch argues emotions are "emissaries" — they should be listened to but not put in the driver's seat, because they just get bigger and bigger. McGrory adds that feelings come and go; you cannot control how you feel, only how you react. Acknowledging emotion prevents it from going underground and finding another way out.

### 13. Freedom of Religion vs. Freedom of Worship
McGrory critiques Obama's shift in language from "freedom of religion" to "freedom of worship," arguing it imposes a schizophrenic division on believers: "I see the world through Catholic eyes... I can't not be a Catholic when I walk into that voting booth." This privatization of faith is incompatible with the Founding vision and with any coherent religious worldview.

### 14. The God-Shaped Hole
McGrory closes with Augustine's famous line from the Psalms: *"You've made us for you, O God, and our hearts are restless until they rest in you."* We try to fill that hole with other things — money, fame, possessions — but they never satisfy. The example of Imelda Marcos's 3,000 pairs of shoes illustrates that when you remove God, whatever replaces Him is never enough.

### 15. Celebrity Culture and the Prodigal Son
Rudyard Lynch notes a pattern where celebrities who achieve worldly success (Kanye West, Mark Wahlberg, Justin Bieber) often turn to Christianity — because once you've had everything the world offers and it doesn't plug the hole, you look for what's next. A study of nuns shows they are incredibly happy with their simple, meditative lives, while those who aspire to be Kardashians are deeply unhappy. "If happiness is unmet expectations, if your expectations are ridiculous, you're gonna be ridiculously unhappy."

## Points of Agreement/Disagreement

- **Agreement among all three:** The impossibility of pure atheism; every society has religious substitutes; Christianity is uniquely balanced between standards and warmth; science has become a pseudo-religion in the modern West; objective truth exists and relativism is self-refuting.
- **McGrory's Catholic perspective:** Emphasizes the continuity between Judaism and Christianity, the Didache as evidence of early Christian teaching, and the Augustinian concept of the restless heart.
- **Lynch's historical analysis:** Contributes the "normie mindset" theory, the philosophical dependence of science on Christianity, and cultural observations about celebrity and happiness.
- **Hamilton's personal arc:** Shares his journey from agnostic/anti-religion to a more open posture, and his experience debating abortion as a pro-choice advocate who later reconsidered.

## Thematic Analysis

### Christianity's Uniqueness
The conversation repeatedly returns to what makes Christianity **fundamentally different** from other worldviews: (1) its humility — the willingness to record the failures of its leaders; (2) its tension between grace and moral standards; (3) its teaching that humans are made in the image of God, giving every person inherent dignity; (4) its compatibility with and philosophical foundation for modern science; (5) its insistence on objective truth; (6) the concept of a "God-shaped hole" that nothing else can fill.

### Idolatry Then and Now
McGrory sees the modern condition not as secular but as **idolatrous** — the same impulse that drove Israelites to build temples to Baal now drives people to worship science, politics, identity, or celebrity. The COVID pandemic revealed this with striking clarity: the structure of faith (prophets, priests, sacraments, heretics) remained intact; only the content changed.

### The Cycle of Return
A pattern runs through both Testaments and contemporary life: comfort produces forgetfulness, crisis produces return. McGrory's cruise ship anecdote — 3,000 people attending Mass when the ship was rocking, returning to indifference once the danger passed — illustrates this human tendency perfectly.
