# South-East Asian Kingdoms

**Source:** [YouTube - History102](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6AEn4Gl_ZpM)
**Hosts:** Rudyard Lynch & Austin Padgett
**Duration:** 1h 24min

## Overview

A survey of the major kingdoms and empires of Southeast Asia — a region that served as a meeting point between Chinese and Indian civilizational influences. Rudyard and Austin cover the Funan, Khmer (Angkor), Srivijaya, Pagan, Ayutthaya, and other kingdoms that flourished in the region.

## Key Topics

- **Indianization of Southeast Asia** — Unlike East Asia (China-dominated), Southeast Asia was heavily influenced by Indian culture — Hinduism, Buddhism, Sanskrit writing systems, and concepts of divine kingship (devaraja).
- **Funan (1st–6th century)** — The earliest known Southeast Asian kingdom, located in the Mekong Delta. An Indianized maritime trading state.
- **Khmer Empire (9th–15th century)** — The most powerful Southeast Asian empire, centered at Angkor. Built an enormous hydraulic infrastructure (barays, canals) for rice agriculture. Angkor Wat is the largest religious monument in the world.
- **Srivijaya (7th–13th century)** — A maritime empire based in Sumatra that controlled the Strait of Malacca, the vital choke point of Indian Ocean trade. A major center for Buddhist learning.
- **Pagan Kingdom (9th–13th century)** — Unified Burma under Theravada Buddhism. Built thousands of Buddhist temples in the plains of Bagan.
- **Ayutthaya Kingdom (14th–18th century)** — A major Siamese (Thai) kingdom that controlled much of modern Thailand and Cambodia. Known for trade with China, India, and European powers.
- **Champa** — A collection of Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms in what is now Vietnam. Constantly conflicted with the Chinese-influenced Vietnamese to the north.
- **European Colonialism** — The Portuguese, Dutch, Spanish, French, and British gradually colonized all of Southeast Asia. Only Thailand (Siam) remained independent as a buffer state.
- **Zomia Connections** — The highlands of Southeast Asia (Zomia) served as an anarchist refuge for peoples fleeing lowland kingdoms' slavery and conscription.

## Summary

Southeast Asia's history is defined by its position between the two great civilizational poles of India and China. Unlike East Asia (Korea, Japan, Vietnam), which adopted Chinese writing and political models, Southeast Asia was primarily "Indianized" — adopting Hinduism and Buddhism, Sanskrit-based scripts, and the concept of the god-king.

The region's geography produced two types of states: agrarian kingdoms based on rice agriculture (Khmer, Pagan, Ayutthaya) and maritime empires based on controlling trade routes (Srivijaya, Majapahit). The Khmer Empire was the most powerful, building the magnificent temple complex of Angkor Wat and maintaining a vast irrigation network.

The arrival of European colonial powers in the 16th century reshaped the region. The Portuguese took Malacca, the Dutch established the East Indies (Indonesia), the British took Burma and Malaya, and the French created Indochina (Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia). Only Thailand maintained independence through diplomatic maneuvering.

## Key Takeaways

- Southeast Asia was "Indianized" rather than Sinicized — Hinduism, Buddhism, and Sanskrit scripts
- The Khmer Empire (Angkor) was the region's most powerful state
- Maritime empires (Srivijaya) controlled the Strait of Malacca trade choke point
- The region's history is defined by the tension between Indian and Chinese influence
- European colonialism eventually divided the region (except Thailand)
- The highlands (Zomia) served as refuge from lowland states
