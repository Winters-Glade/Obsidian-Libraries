# Dark Age Europe

## Overview
The European Dark Ages (c. 400–1000 AD) — the period between the fall of Rome and the rise of medieval Europe. Lynch critiques the "Whig history" narrative that skips over this era as a mere gap between classical civilization and the Renaissance.

## What the Dark Ages Actually Were
- A period of genuine civilizational decline: cities shrank, literacy declined in some regions, infrastructure (roads, aqueducts, baths) decayed
- However, the period is far more complex than its reputation suggests
- By 1000 AD, Europe was recovering and poised for the medieval flowering

## Historiographical Critique
- **Whig history** — the narrative of inevitable progress cannot account for periods of decline and stagnation
- **Anti-religious bias** — academia often dismisses religious societies as backward, even when they produced genuine civilization
- The Middle Ages (1000–1500) were NOT a "dark age" — by 1250 Europe had become the most advanced society up to that point (barring China)

## Key Developments
- Collapse of Roman institutions and infrastructure
- Rise of monasticism as a preservative of knowledge
- Formation of new political entities (Frankish Kingdom, Anglo-Saxon kingdoms)
- Gradual Christianization of Northern Europe
