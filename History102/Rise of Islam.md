# Rise of Islam

## Summary
Islam emerged in the early 7th century AD when the Prophet Muhammad, a merchant from the Quraysh tribe of Mecca, began receiving revelations from God (Allah) through the Angel Gabriel, which were later compiled as the Quran. The context was a Middle East devastated by decades of plague (population halved) and brutal wars between the Byzantine (Eastern Roman) and Persian Empires, creating a massive power vacuum. Muhammad succeeded where Jesus did not — he was not only a prophet but also a successful warlord who unified Arabia, conquered Mecca, and established a state. After his death, his successors (the Rashidun Caliphate) exploded out of Arabia, conquering the Sassanid Persian Empire entirely and taking two-thirds of the Byzantine Empire within decades. The Umayyad Caliphate created the largest empire the world had yet seen, stretching from Portugal to India, and the Abbasid Caliphate that followed produced Islam's Golden Age — a period of extraordinary scientific, philosophical, and economic achievement fueled by Greek, Persian, and Indian knowledge synthesized under Muslim rule. Key differences from Christianity: Muhammad never claimed divinity (he was a prophet), God is utterly transcendent and incomprehensible (not humanlike), and Islam provided a complete legal, political, and social system — not just a spiritual path. The Golden Age declined due to political fragmentation, Turkish barbarian slave-soldiers taking over, and the theological victory of Al-Ghazali's traditionalism over rationalist philosophy, after which Islam turned inward toward Quranic literalism and away from scientific inquiry.

## Key Concepts
- **[[Islam]]**: The youngest major world religion, meaning "submission" to God; the latest of the Abrahamic faiths, following Judaism and Christianity
- **[[Quran]]**: The shortest major religious text (~300 pages), dictated orally by the illiterate Prophet Muhammad, believed by Muslims to be the literal word of God
- **[[Caliphate]]**: Islamic political-religious state combining spiritual and temporal authority; the successor system to Muhammad's leadership
- **[[Sunni-Shia Split]]**: Originating from the political dispute over Muhammad's successor (Abu Bakr vs. Ali), evolved into deep theological divisions; Sunnis are ~90% of Muslims
- **[[Rashidun Caliphate]]**: The "Rightly Guided" period of Muhammad's immediate successors (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali) — the golden ideal that all later Islamic movements try to recreate
- **[[Umayyad Caliphate]]**: First hereditary dynasty of Islam; Arab tribal warrior empire centered in Damascus; ruled a majority Christian population; suppressed conversions to maintain tax revenue
- **[[Abbasid Caliphate]]**: Persian-influenced dynasty centered in Baghdad; peak of Islamic Golden Age; moved empire from Greek/Roman orbit toward Persian cultural base
- **[[Al-Ghazali's Traditionalism]]**: The decisive philosophical turn in Islamic history (~1000 AD) that declared scientific study of the outside world useless and that all truth resides in the Quran — the point where Islam's scientific Golden Age ended
- **[[Jizya]]**: Special tax on non-Muslims (Christians, Jews, Zoroastrians) living under Muslim rule
- **[[Hadith]]**: The vast body of sayings and actions attributed to Muhammad and his companions, forming the basis of Islamic law (Sharia)

## Key People
- **[[Muhammad]]**: Prophet of Islam, born ~570 AD in Mecca, orphaned, became a merchant, received revelations at age 40, conquered Arabia, died in 632 AD
- **[[Abu Bakr]]**: Muhammad's father-in-law and close friend; first caliph; the Sunni line of succession
- **[[Ali]]**: Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law; fourth caliph; the Shia line of succession
- **[[Justinian]]**: Byzantine emperor (~527-565) who reconquered parts of the Western Roman Empire, exhausting both Byzantines and Persians before Islam arrived
- **[[Harun al-Rashid]]**: The greatest Abbasid caliph, featured in *One Thousand and One Nights*; his reign marked the peak before decline
- **[[Al-Ghazali]]**: The theologian/philosopher whose attack on rationalism (~1000 AD) turned Islam away from science toward Quranic literalism
- **[[Charles Martel]] ("The Hammer")**: Frankish leader who stopped the Muslim advance at the Battle of Tours (732 AD), halting Islam's expansion into Western Europe
- **[[The Berbers]]**: Indigenous North Africans who fiercely resisted Arab conquest, then converted and became the vanguard of Islamic expansion into Spain

## Key Events
- **Muhammad's first revelation (~610 AD)**: Gabriel appears to Muhammad in a cave near Mecca, beginning the Quranic revelations
- **The Hijra (622 AD)**: Muhammad flees persecution in Mecca to Medina, marking year 1 of the Islamic calendar
- **Conquest of Mecca (630 AD)**: Muhammad returns with an army and takes Mecca peacefully, establishing Islam as the dominant force in Arabia
- **Death of Muhammad (632 AD)**: Succession crisis produces the Rashidun Caliphate and begins the Sunni-Shia split
- **Islamic Conquest of Persia (633-654 AD)**: The Sassanid Empire, exhausted by war with Byzantium, falls completely to Arab armies
- **Battle of Yarmouk (636 AD)**: Decisive Arab victory over Byzantines, leading to the loss of Syria and Palestine
- **Battle of the Talas River (751 AD)**: Arabs defeat Chinese army in Central Asia; Chinese papermakers captured, leading to paper technology spreading to Europe
- **Abbasid Revolution (750 AD)**: Persian-backed Shia rebels overthrow the Umayyads, establishing the Abbasid Caliphate in Baghdad
- **Siege of Constantinople (717-718 AD)**: Byzantines use Greek Fire to destroy Arab fleet, saving Europe from Muslim conquest
- **Battle of Tours (732 AD)**: Charles Martel stops Muslim advance into France
- **Al-Ghazali's *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* (~1100 AD)**: The philosophical turning point that ended Islamic rationalism and scientific inquiry
