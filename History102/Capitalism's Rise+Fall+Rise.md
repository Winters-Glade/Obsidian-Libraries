# Capitalism's Rise+Fall+Rise

## Overview

This video presents a sweeping history of capitalism — from its medieval origins through its explosive growth during the Industrial Revolution, its crises and transformations in the 20th century, and its resurgence in the neoliberal era. Rudyard Lynch and Austin Padgett examine capitalism not just as an economic system but as a cultural and political force that has reshaped every aspect of modern life. They explore the conditions that enabled capitalism to emerge, the critiques it generated (from Marxism to populism), the crises that nearly destroyed it (Great Depression, world wars), and its remarkable adaptability that has allowed it to survive and thrive into the 21st century.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### The Rise of Capitalism (c. 1500-1870)
- **Preconditions**: collapse of feudalism, enclosure movement, discovery of the New World (precious metals, new crops)
- **Protestant Reformation**: Weber thesis — Protestant (especially Calvinist) ethic of thrift, hard work, and rational accumulation
- **Mercantilism**: early modern capitalism — state-directed trade, chartered companies (East India Company 1600)
- **Financial innovations**: banking, bills of exchange, insurance, joint-stock companies, stock markets (Amsterdam 1602)
- **Agricultural Revolution**: productivity gains freed labor for industrial work
- **Industrial Revolution**: factory system, wage labor, mechanization — capitalism's breakthrough moment
- **Classical economics**: Adam Smith (invisible hand, division of labor), David Ricardo (comparative advantage), Thomas Malthus (population trap)
- **Free trade**: Britain's repeal of Corn Laws (1846) exemplified shift from mercantilism to laissez-faire

### The Contradictions and Crises (c. 1870-1945)
- **Marxist critique**: Marx argued capitalism contained internal contradictions (falling rate of profit, exploitation, crisis-prone)
- **Great Depression** (1929-1939): near-fatal crisis — mass unemployment, bank failures, collapse of international trade
- **Business cycles**: periodic boom-bust patterns inherent to capitalism
- **Monopoly capitalism**: concentration of industry into trusts and cartels (Rockefeller, Carnegie)
- **Labor movements**: rise of unions, social democratic parties, demands for regulation and redistribution
- **Two world wars**: capitalism's competitive logic contributed to imperial rivalry and war; war also transformed economies
- **Keynesian revolution**: John Maynard Keynes argued for government intervention to manage demand and stabilize capitalism

### The Fall (c. 1917-1970s)
- **Rise of alternatives**: Russian Revolution (1917) created communist alternative; fascism (Italy, Germany) offered third way
- **New Deal** (1933-1938): U.S. experiment in regulated capitalism; Social Security, SEC, minimum wage
- **Mixed economy**: post-WWII consensus — embedded liberalism, welfare state, nationalized industries in Europe
- **Bretton Woods system** (1944): managed exchange rates, capital controls, international institutions (IMF, World Bank)
- **Golden Age of Capitalism** (1945-1973): high growth, low inequality, strong unions — capitalism's best performance
- **Decolonization**: former colonies pursued state-led development, import substitution

### The Rise Again — Neoliberalism (c. 1980-present)
- **Stagflation** (1970s): high inflation + high unemployment discredited Keynesianism
- **Thatcher and Reagan**: privatization, deregulation, tax cuts, union weakening
- **Financialization**: shift from industrial to financial capitalism; banks, speculation, debt
- **Globalization**: free trade agreements (NAFTA), open capital markets, global supply chains
- **China's capitalist turn**: Deng Xiaoping's reforms (1978) brought 800 million out of poverty, reshaped global economy
- **Fall of USSR** (1991): capitalism's great ideological rival collapsed
- **Washington Consensus**: neoliberal orthodoxy imposed on developing countries via IMF/World Bank
- **Tech capitalism**: Silicon Valley, platform economies, venture capital, data as capital

### The Return of Crises
- **2008 Global Financial Crisis**: worst since 1929 — triggered by subprime mortgages, derivatives, deregulation
- **Rising inequality**: Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2014) documented return of patrimonial capitalism
- **Populist backlash**: Brexit (2016), Trump (2016) — revolts against globalization and financial capitalism
- **COVID-19 pandemic** (2020-2021): massive state intervention, debate over capitalism's future
- **Climate crisis**: capitalism's growth imperative vs. environmental sustainability
- **AI and automation**: new challenges to employment and inequality

## Important Figures

- **Adam Smith** (1723-1790): father of modern economics, invisible hand, free markets
- **David Ricardo** (1772-1823): comparative advantage, iron law of wages
- **Karl Marx** (1818-1883): capitalism's greatest critic, theory of surplus value, historical materialism
- **John Maynard Keynes** (1883-1946): revolutionized macroeconomics, government intervention to manage demand
- **Friedrich Hayek** (1899-1992): Austrian school, free market advocate, critic of central planning
- **Milton Friedman** (1912-2006): monetarist, Chicago school, influence on Thatcher and Reagan
- **Joseph Schumpeter** (1883-1950): creative destruction, entrepreneurship as engine of capitalism
- **John D. Rockefeller** (1839-1937): Standard Oil, monopoly capitalism
- **Andrew Carnegie** (1835-1919): steel magnate, vertical integration, "Gospel of Wealth"
- **Henry Ford** (1863-1947): mass production, assembly line, high wages for workers
- **Margaret Thatcher** (1925-2013): privatization, deregulation, neoliberal revolution
- **Deng Xiaoping** (1904-1997): market reforms in China, capitalist-authoritarian model
- **Thomas Piketty** (b. 1971): documented rising inequality in the 21st century
