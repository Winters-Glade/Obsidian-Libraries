# Protestantism's History

## Summary
An extensive survey of Protestantism from the 17th century to the present, arguing that Protestantism has been vastly underappreciated in its role in building the modern world. The video covers the major Protestant sects — Lutherans, Calvinists, Anglicans, Baptists, Methodists, Quakers, Mormons, and Pentecostals — and traces how their theological differences produced divergent political and economic outcomes. Key themes include Max Weber's famous thesis on the Calvinist work ethic and the rise of capitalism, the Protestant roots of the Industrial Revolution (which occurred first in Protestant countries), the role of evangelical missionaries in the abolition of slavery and global Christian expansion, and the long arc of the "death of God" — where Darwinism critically wounded Protestantism a century after Descartes wounded Catholicism. The discussion also examines the decline of religion in Europe versus its persistence in America, the explosive growth of Pentecostalism in the Global South, and the irony that modernity, which now rejects Christianity, was itself built on Protestant foundations.

## Key Concepts
- **Calvinist Work Ethic (Max Weber)**: The Protestant (particularly Calvinist) emphasis on hard work, thrift, and worldly success as signs of divine election fueled the rise of capitalism and the Industrial Revolution.
- **Protestant Sects and Their Differences**: 
  - **Lutherans**: State churches of Northern Europe (Germany, Scandinavia); emphasized faith alone (sola fide) and scripture alone (sola scriptura).
  - **Calvinists / Reformed**: Predestination, iconoclasm, high intellectual rigor; shaped Switzerland, Netherlands, Scotland, and Puritan England.
  - **Anglicans**: The Church of England, a middle way between Catholicism and Protestantism; expanded through the British Empire.
  - **Baptists**: Believer's baptism by immersion, local church autonomy, separation of church and state; became the largest American Protestant denomination.
  - **Methodists**: Arminian (free will) theology, emphasis on personal holiness and social reform; strong in America and Britain.
  - **Quakers**: Pacifism, inner light, abolitionism; disproportionately influential in social reform.
  - **Mormons (Latter-day Saints)**: Founded by Joseph Smith in upstate New York; believe Christ visited America; practiced polygamy; driven west to Utah; known for high social achievement and libertarian politics.
  - **Pentecostals**: Emerged in 1920s Los Angeles; speaking in tongues, shaking, ecstatic worship; fastest-growing Christian movement, now ~800 million adherents globally.
- **Muscular Christianity**: A 19th-century movement that reconciled Christian virtue with colonial warrior manliness, embodied by Teddy Roosevelt. An attempt to reintegrate older spiritual frames with industrialization.
- **The Map Paradigm (Modern Academic Progressive)**: The current ruling class ideology that has replaced religious identity; it strips historical context and claims to be post-Christian while remaining culturally dependent on Christian moral frameworks.
- **Death of God (Nietzsche)**: Nietzsche's late-19th-century diagnosis that God had died in the minds of the European elite, which the video argues took a century to trickle down to the masses — World War I being the puncturing event.
- **Darwin's Impact on Protestantism**: Evolution critically wounded Protestantism in a way analogous to how the Enlightenment wounded Catholicism. Creationism vs. Darwinism became the framing battleground.
- **Evangelical Missionaries**: Badass risk-takers who brought literacy, ended slavery, suppressed human sacrifice, and converted vast populations in Africa, Latin America, and Asia. The most active branch of Christianity globally.

## Key People
- **Martin Luther**: Augustinian monk who posted the 95 Theses in 1517, beginning the Protestant Reformation. Emphasized justification by faith alone and the authority of scripture.
- **John Calvin**: French theologian who systematized Reformed theology in Geneva; emphasized predestination and the sovereignty of God. His thought shaped the Puritans and the Dutch Reformed.
- **Henry VIII**: English king who broke with Rome in 1534 to establish the Church of England (Anglicanism), primarily over the annulment of his marriage to Catherine of Aragon.
- **Max Weber**: German sociologist who wrote *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905), arguing that Calvinist theology created the conditions for modern capitalism.
- **Charles Darwin**: His theory of evolution by natural selection (1859) became the central intellectual challenge to Protestant Christianity, leading to creationism vs. evolution debates.
- **Joseph Smith**: Founder of Mormonism (1830), claimed to have received gold tablets from an angel; Mormons were violently persecuted and driven west to Utah.
- **Friedrich Nietzsche**: German philosopher who declared "God is dead" in the 1880s — a generation before World War I proved him right in the popular consciousness.
- **Theodore Roosevelt**: Embodiment of muscular Christianity — the attempt to reconcile warrior manliness with Christian faith in the late 19th/early 20th century.
- **Billy Graham / American Evangelicals**: Key figures in 20th-century American Protestant revivalism, credited (alongside the Catholic Church) with preventing communist dominance in the Cold War.

## Key Events
- **Protestant Reformation (1517-1648)**: From Luther's 95 Theses to the Peace of Westphalia. A century and a half of religious upheaval and war that shattered the unity of Western Christendom.
- **Thirty Years' War (1618-1648)**: The most devastating of the religious wars, killing a third of Germany and Central Europe's population. Led to the Westphalian system of sovereign states and the privatization of religion.
- **English Civil War (1642-1651)**: Fought between Anglicans/royalists and Puritans/Parliamentarians; resulted in temporary Puritan rule under Cromwell and permanently limited the English monarchy.
- **Great Awakenings (18th-19th Centuries)**: Waves of revivalism in America that created the distinctive American evangelical landscape — the first under Jonathan Edwards and George Whitefield, the second spawning Methodists and Baptists.
- **Industrial Revolution (1760-1840)**: Occurred first in Protestant countries (Britain, then Germany, then America). The video argues this is causally linked to the Protestant work ethic and the openness of Protestant culture to innovation.
- **Global Missionary Expansion (19th-20th Centuries)**: American and European Protestant missionaries spread Christianity across Africa, Asia, and Latin America, often being the primary bearers of literacy, medicine, and modern education.
- **World War I (1914-1918)**: The event that "punctured" the Christian-liberal-scientific synthesis in Europe, accelerating the death of God in the popular consciousness.
- **Pentecostal Emergence (1920s)**: The Azusa Street Revival in Los Angeles began the Pentecostal movement, which has since grown to ~800 million adherents, predominantly in the Global South.
- **Post-WWII American Religious Peak (1950s-1990s)**: America remained over 80% Christian through the year 2000, with most Americans favoring a Christian theocracy. The collapse of religion in America is a 21st-century phenomenon, occurring a few generations later than in Europe.
