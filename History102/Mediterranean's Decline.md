# Mediterranean's Decline

## Overview

This video examines the relative decline of the Mediterranean region from its historic position as the center of the Western world to a peripheral and economically marginalized region by the late 19th century. Once the heart of the Roman Empire, the birthplace of the Renaissance, and the crossroads of trade between Europe, Africa, and Asia, the Mediterranean was eclipsed by the rise of the Atlantic economies (Britain, France, Netherlands), the shift of global trade routes to the Atlantic and Indian Oceans, and the internal decay of the Ottoman Empire — the dominant Mediterranean power for centuries. The video explores how geography, economics, politics, and technology combined to relegate the Mediterranean to a secondary role in global affairs.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### The Rise of the Atlantic Economy
- **Shift in trade routes**: after 1492, wealth and trade flowed through the Atlantic, not the Mediterranean
- **Atlantic powers**: Britain, France, Netherlands, later the United States — built navies, empires, and economies on Atlantic trade
- **Industrial Revolution**: concentrated in Northern Europe (Britain, Belgium, Germany) — the Mediterranean participated only marginally
- **Colonial empires**: Atlantic powers extracted wealth from the Americas, Africa, Asia; Mediterranean empires (Ottoman, Spanish) declined
- **Capital market shift**: financial centers moved from Italian city-states (Venice, Genoa) to Amsterdam, London, then New York

### The Ottoman Decline
- **"Sick Man of Europe"**: by the 19th century, the Ottoman Empire was in advanced decay
- **Military defeats**: lost wars to Russia (1768-74, 1806-12, 1828-29, 1853-56, 1877-78); lost control of Greece (1832), Serbia, Egypt (effectively independent under Muhammad Ali)
- **Economic decline**: capitulations (unequal trade treaties) gave European powers control over Ottoman markets
- **Technological backwardness**: failed to industrialize; agriculture remained primitive
- **Administrative corruption**: weak central control, provincial warlords, inefficient tax collection
- **Tanzimat reforms** (1839-1876): attempts at modernization too little to reverse decline
- **Debt crisis**: Ottoman Empire defaulted on loans (1875); European financial control (Ottoman Public Debt Administration, 1881)

### The Italian Unification and Its Limits
- **Risorgimento** (1815-1871): Italy unified under Piedmont-Sardinia (Garibaldi, Cavour, Mazzini)
- **Unification created a state but not an industrial power**: Italy remained predominantly agricultural and poor
- **Mezzogiorno problem**: deep economic divide between industrializing North and impoverished South
- **Emigration**: massive outflow of Italians to Americas (4 million between 1880-1914) — sign of economic desperation
- **Failed colonialism**: Italy's late attempt at empire (Libya 1911-12, Ethiopia 1896 defeat at Adwa) showed weakness

### Spain's Long Decline
- **Loss of empire**: most Latin American colonies independent by 1825; Spanish-American War (1898) lost Cuba, Puerto Rico, Philippines
- **Political instability**: unstable monarchy, military coups, Carlist Wars, weak governments
- **Economic backwardness**: failed to industrialize; latifundia (large estates) with feudal labor relations
- **Intellectual isolation**: Spanish science and culture stagnated compared to Northern Europe
- **"Generation of '98"**: intellectuals who diagnosed Spain's decline after loss of empire

### The Eastern Mediterranean
- **Egypt**: under Muhammad Ali (1805-1848), a brief modernization — then British occupation (1882-1952)
- **Greece**: independent 1832, but small, poor, politically unstable, and dependent on great powers
- **Levant (Syria, Lebanon, Palestine)**: backward provinces of Ottoman Empire, subsistence agriculture, religious diversity
- **Suez Canal** (1869): ironically, this great engineering project was built by the French and controlled by the British — the Mediterranean became a highway for Atlantic empires, not a source of local prosperity
- **Balkan nationalisms**: new states (Serbia, Bulgaria, Romania) were small and weak, caught between empires

### Structural Causes of Mediterranean Decline
- **Geography**: lacks coal and iron ore (key industrial resources); limited navigable rivers
- **Climate**: Mediterranean agriculture less productive than Northern European plains; droughts, soil erosion
- **Institutions**: weak property rights, corruption, absence of rule of law in Ottoman and Spanish domains
- **Religion**: debates about whether Catholicism/Islam hindered capitalism's development (Weber thesis)
- **Human capital**: lower literacy rates, weaker universities compared to Northern Europe
- **Missing bourgeoisie**: weak middle class in much of the region; landowning elites and peasant majorities

## Important Figures

- **Muhammad Ali Pasha** (1769-1849): modernized Egypt, built a short-lived regional power
- **Giuseppe Garibaldi** (1807-1882): military leader of Italian unification
- **Count Camillo di Cavour** (1810-1861): architect of Italian unification, prime minister of Piedmont-Sardinia
- **Giuseppe Mazzini** (1805-1872): revolutionary thinker, founder of Young Italy
- **Sultan Mahmud II** (1785-1839): reformer who destroyed Janissaries, attempted modernization
- **Sultan Abdul Hamid II** (1842-1918): last autocratic sultan; pan-Islamism
- **Mehmed Ali Pasha** (not the Egyptian): see above
- **Lord Horatio Nelson** (1758-1805): British admiral, Battle of the Nile (1798)
- **Ferdinand de Lesseps** (1805-1894): built the Suez Canal
- **Emilio Aguinaldo** (1869-1964): led Philippines against Spain and then U.S.
- **Theodore Roosevelt** (1858-1919): led charge up San Juan Hill in Spanish-American War
- **Abd-el-Krim** (1882-1963): Rif leader who fought Spanish colonialism
