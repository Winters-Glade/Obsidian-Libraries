# Sex and Power in History

## Overview

This video examines the intricate relationship between sex, gender, and political power throughout history, with particular focus on the 19th century as a period of dramatic transformation in sexual norms, gender roles, and the politics of the body. Rudyard Lynch and Austin Padgett explore how industrialization, urbanization, imperialism, and the rise of the bourgeoisie reshaped intimate life, creating new ideologies of gender difference, regulating sexuality through state and medical authority, and sparking movements for sexual and gender liberation that would culminate in the 20th century.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### The Victorian Sexual System
- **Separate spheres ideology**: men in public (work, politics); women in private (home, family)
- **"Angel in the House"**: ideal of the submissive, pure, domestic woman (from Coventry Patmore's poem, 1854)
- **Sexual double standard**: premarital chastity expected of women; tolerated in men (e.g., prostitution)
- **Respectability**: central Victorian value linking moral character to sexual restraint
- **Medicalization of sex**: doctors claimed authority over sexual matters; masturbation pathologized; female sexuality denied
- **Contraception debate**: knowledge existed (condoms, diaphragms) but discussion suppressed; Comstock Laws (1873) in U.S.

### Gender and Empire
- **Racialized gender**: colonized peoples often depicted as effeminate (Indians) or hyper-masculine/brutal (Africans)
- **Colonial masculinity**: imperial service as proving ground for manliness; sports, hunting, adventure
- **White women's burden**: European women justified colonization as rescue of "oppressed" native women
- **Interracial sex**: colonial officials often took native mistresses; but fears of miscegenation shaped policies
- **Sati controversy** (India): British abolition of widow-burning used as justification for imperial rule

### Prostitution and Regulation
- **Contagious Diseases Acts** (1860s, Britain): allowed police to detain suspected prostitutes, forcibly examine for STDs
- **"White slave trade" panic**: moral panic about women being trafficked into prostitution — led to age of consent laws
- **Josephine Butler's campaign**: feminist campaign against CD Acts — early women's rights organizing
- **Prostitution in cities**: vast scale — 80,000 prostitutes estimated in mid-Victorian London
- **Regulation vs. abolition**: should states regulate prostitution (licensing, medical checks) or abolish it?

### The Birth of Feminism
- **Mary Wollstonecraft** (1792): *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* — foundational feminist text
- **Seneca Falls Convention** (1848): first women's rights convention in the U.S.; Declaration of Sentiments
- **Married Women's Property Acts** (1870s, 1882): women could own property independent of husbands
- **Education**: women's colleges founded (Oberlin 1837, Mount Holyoke 1837, Girton 1869)
- **Suffrage movement**: National Union of Women's Suffrage Societies (NUWSS, 1897); militant WSPU under Emmeline Pankhurst (1903)
- **Intersection of feminism with abolition**: many early feminists were also abolitionists (e.g., Sojourner Truth, Elizabeth Cady Stanton)

### Sexuality and the Law
- **Criminalization of homosexuality**: Labouchere Amendment (1885, Britain) criminalized "gross indecency" between men
- **Oscar Wilde trial** (1895): most famous gay man of the era destroyed by legal persecution
- **Age of consent laws**: raised to 16 in Britain (1885); debates about protecting youth vs. state overreach
- **Eugenics movement**: Francis Galton (1883); concerns about "degeneration" and racial purity; forced sterilization laws later
- **Birth control movement**: Margaret Sanger began advocating (1910s); Comstock Laws prevented distribution of information

### The Scientific Construction of Sexuality
- **Sexology**: new science of sexuality — Havelock Ellis, Richard von Krafft-Ebing (*Psychopathia Sexualis*, 1886)
- **Freud**: Sigmund Freud's psychoanalysis (from 1890s) — sexuality as central to human psychology
- **Distinction between sex and gender**: began to emerge conceptually
- **Race and sexuality**: Social Darwinism, fears of "race suicide" among white middle classes
- **Pornography**: mass production and distribution became possible with printing technology

## Important Figures

- **Mary Wollstonecraft** (1759-1797): early feminist philosopher
- **Emmeline Pankhurst** (1858-1928): leader of British suffragette movement (WSPU)
- **Susan B. Anthony** (1820-1906): American women's rights leader
- **Elizabeth Cady Stanton** (1815-1902): organized Seneca Falls Convention
- **Sojourner Truth** (1797-1883): abolitionist and women's rights activist, "Ain't I a Woman?"
- **Josephine Butler** (1828-1906): campaigned against Contagious Diseases Acts
- **Oscar Wilde** (1854-1900): playwright, persecuted for homosexuality
- **Sigmund Freud** (1856-1939): psychoanalysis, theories of sexuality
- **Havelock Ellis** (1859-1939): sexologist, challenged Victorian sexual norms
- **Richard von Krafft-Ebing** (1840-1902): catalogued sexual "pathologies"
- **Margaret Sanger** (1879-1966): birth control advocate, Planned Parenthood founder
- **John Stuart Mill** (1806-1873): wrote *The Subjection of Women* (1869), advocated women's suffrage
- **André Gide** (1869-1951): writer who challenged heteronormativity
