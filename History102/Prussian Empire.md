# The Prussian Empire

Source: History 102 podcast with Rudyard Lynch and Austin Padet
Video: [[_MOC_20th_Century_Wars]]

## Summary

Prussia started as a marsh people on the frontiers of Germany and grew to control a territory stretching from North Africa to Norway and from the Atlantic nearly to Kazakhstan. The podcast argues Prussia is a compelling case for how leadership determines history — the Hohenzollern dynasty and later Bismarck unified Germany after 600 years of disunity. The Prussian state existed in tension with Christian morality, producing anti-Christian thinkers like Nietzsche. Bismarck's welfare-state concessions to workers were a pragmatic strategy to stabilize monarchical power against classical liberals and socialists.

## Key Points

- **From marsh to empire** — Prussia began as a frontier people and eventually unified Germany after 600 years of fractured city-states and principalities
- **Contingent unification** — without a series of remarkable leaders (Frederick the Great, Bismarck), Germany likely would never have unified
- **Leadership as decisive factor** — Prussia is a strong test case for absolutism: the Hohenzollern dynasty's leadership made the empire
- **Anti-Christian thought** — Prussian thinkers developed much of the anti-Christian philosophy that influenced Nietzsche and later the Nazis
- **Bismarck's pragmatism** — he gave concessions to socialists and workers to stabilize monarchical power; his thought was used by EU architects
- **Mid-19th century context** — they didn't know socialism would become dominant; when they sent Lenin to Russia, they had no idea he'd build the Soviet Union
