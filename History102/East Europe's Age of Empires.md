# East Europe's Age of Empires

## Overview

Eastern Europe in the 19th century was the battleground of three great multi-ethnic empires — the Austrian (later Austro-Hungarian), the Russian, and the Ottoman — each struggling to maintain control over a mosaic of peoples while facing the rising tide of nationalism. This video examines the distinctive political, social, and economic structures of these empires, the "national awakening" movements among subject peoples (Poles, Czechs, Hungarians, Serbs, Romanians, Ukrainians, and others), and the long-term consequences for a region that would become the powder keg of World War I and continue to experience imperial domination through the 20th century.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### The Three Great Empires

**Austrian Empire / Austro-Hungarian Empire (1804-1918)**
- Multi-ethnic patchwork: Germans, Hungarians, Czechs, Slovaks, Poles, Ruthenians, Romanians, Slovenes, Croats, Serbs, Italians
- Ausgleich (Compromise) of 1867 created Dual Monarchy — Austria and Hungary semi-independent under one emperor
- Franz Joseph I (reigned 1848-1916) — the eternal emperor, symbol of stability and stagnation
- Struggle between centralization and federalism; Magyar domination over Slavs in Hungarian half

**Russian Empire (1721-1917)**
- Autocratic rule under the Tsar; no constitution, no parliament until 1905
- Russification policies: suppression of Polish, Ukrainian, Finnish, Baltic, and Jewish cultures
- Polish uprisings (1830-31, 1863-64) brutally suppressed
- Pale of Settlement: restrictions on Jewish population; waves of pogroms
- Serfdom until 1861 — legalized bondage of millions of peasants

**Ottoman Empire (c. 1300-1922)**
- "Sick Man of Europe" — gradual decline through the 19th century
- Loss of control over Balkan peoples: Serbia (autonomous 1817, independent 1878), Greece (independent 1832), Romania, Bulgaria
- Tanzimat reforms (1839-1876): attempts at modernization, equality before law — often too little, too late
- Eastern Question: Great Power competition over the fate of Ottoman territories

### The Rise of Nationalism
- **Romantic nationalism**: Herder's concept of the *Volk* — each nation has a unique spirit; language as the marker of nationhood
- **1848 Revolutions**: "Spring of Nations" — simultaneous revolts across the Austrian Empire, Germany, Italy, Hungary; ultimately crushed by imperial forces
- **Unification movements**: Germany (1871), Italy (1861) — inspired and threatened subject peoples
- **Pan-Slavism**: idea of Slavic unity under Russian leadership — threatened Austria-Hungary and Ottoman Empire
- **National awakenings**: Czech National Revival, Hungarian nationalism (Kossuth), Illyrian movement (South Slavs), Romanian unification

### Economic and Social Structures
- **Vast inequality**: landowning aristocracy (magnates) vs. landless peasantry; weak middle class
- **Industrialization delayed**: some pockets (Bohemia, Silesia, Russian Poland, Budapest) but largely agrarian
- **Jewish population**: Yiddish-speaking *shtetl* culture; urban migration; Haskalah (Jewish Enlightenment); rise of Zionism in response to persecution
- **Peasant life**: serfdom/emancipation, subsistence agriculture, periodic famines
- **Education and literacy**: varying widely; German-speaking areas more literate; Ottoman and Russian areas far less

### The Balkan Powder Keg
- **Declining Ottoman power** created power vacuums; local nationalisms; Great Power rivalry (Austria vs. Russia)
- **Congress of Berlin (1878)**: great powers redrew Balkan boundaries; created independent states but sowed future conflicts
- **Bosnia annexed by Austria** (1908) — enraged Serbia and Russia
- **Balkan Wars (1912-1913)**: Balkan states fought Ottoman Empire, then each other
- **Assassination of Franz Ferdinand** (1914) in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip (Bosnian Serb nationalist) — immediate cause of WWI

## Important Figures

- **Franz Joseph I** (1830-1916): Emperor of Austria, King of Hungary; ruled for 68 years
- **Tsar Alexander II** (1818-1881): emancipated serfs, reformed military and judiciary; assassinated by revolutionaries
- **Tsar Nicholas I** (1796-1855): reactionary autocrat, suppressed 1848 revolutions in Hungary
- **Tsar Nicholas II** (1868-1918): last Tsar; weak leader during war and revolution; executed by Bolsheviks
- **Lajos Kossuth** (1802-1894): Hungarian nationalist leader of 1848 revolution
- **Mihailo Obrenović** (1823-1868) and **Miloš Obrenović** (1780-1860): leaders of Serbian independence
- **Sultan Mahmud II** (1785-1839): reformist Ottoman sultan, abolished Janissaries
- **Sultan Abdul Hamid II** (1842-1918): last autocratic sultan, era of repression and reform
- **Tomas Garrigue Masaryk** (1850-1937): Czech nationalist, later first president of Czechoslovakia
- **Ion Bratianu** (1821-1891): Romanian political leader, architect of Romanian unification
- **Otto von Bismarck** (1815-1898): Prussian/German chancellor, master of diplomacy, dominated Central Europe
- **Metternich** (1773-1859): Austrian foreign minister, architect of post-Napoleonic conservative order
