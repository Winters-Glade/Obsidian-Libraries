# MOC: Axial Age, Religions & Mysticism

> Batch 2 of History102 — 9 videos exploring the Axial Age, the rise and development of the world's major religions, mysticism, and the modern crisis of faith.

## The Axial Age & Foundational Framework

The [[Axial Age]] (~500 BC) saw parallel philosophical and religious revolutions across unconnected civilizations — [[Greece]], [[India]], [[China]], and the [[Middle East]] — driven by the invention of coinage and the collapse of traditional IOU economies. This period established the conceptual foundation for all later world religions.

- **[[Axial Age]]** — `ip_noUSDHDw` — Buddha, Confucius, Socrates, and the birth of universal morality

## Rise of the Abrahamic Religions

Two of the world's largest religions emerged from the [[Middle East]], both shaped by the Axial Age legacy of universal moral order:

- **[[Rise of Christianity]]** — `gsEGn8TBDDs` — From Jesus to Constantine, how a small Jewish sect conquered the Roman Empire
- **[[Rise of Islam]]** — `TvgDkIqYaWQ` — Muhammad, the Caliphates, and the rapid expansion of Islam across three continents

## Thematic Connections

### Coinage & Social Transformation
- [[Coinage]] is the driver of the [[Axial Age]], dissolving IOU-based economies
- [[Decline of Islam]] explores economic decline and stagnation as causes of civilizational collapse

### Reformation Cycles
- The cycle of mystic breakthrough → bureaucratization → corruption → reformation appears in [[Mysticism in History]] and [[Protestant Reformation]]
- [[Catholic Church]] and [[Protestantism's History]] show the same pattern at institutional scale

### The Death → Rebirth Cycle
- [[Death of God]] argues that civilizational atheism is periodic (Axial China, Greco-Roman world)
- [[Decline of Islam]] documents a parallel decline in the Islamic world
- Both point toward the need for a new religious breakthrough

### Buffered vs. Porous Self
- [[Charles Taylor]]'s framework in [[Death of God]] — the buffered self sealed off from spiritual forces
- [[Mysticism in History]] documents the pre-modern porous self that experienced the divine directly
