# Philippe Fabry Interview: Leftist Revolution in France? Russia Invades Europe? America New Rome

> **Source:** [History102](https://www.youtube.com/@History102-qg5oj) — [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1_DNkjNOnoA)

## Guest Biography

Philippe Fabry is a French political commentator and historian of ideas who writes on European politics, revolutionary movements, and the shifting global order. He has been working on historical comparison for 20+ years and independently developed his theory of civilizational cycles before reading Spengler, Toynbee, or Amélie de Riancourt — a fact he considers an advantage in forming original views. He runs a think tank focused on America's future and is translating his books into English, launching an English-language YouTube channel using AI translation. His work covers the structure of history, national revolutions, great power conflict, and the Rome-America parallelism.

## Key Arguments & Discussion Points

### 1. America as New Rome / Caesarism

- America is around 100 BC in the Roman timeline, entering the period of Caesarism
- The Gracchi brothers (populist reformers in late Republican Rome) are analogous to Donald Trump — both arose in highly unequal societies, promising the old middle classes a place against patrician/aristocratic interests; both were/are opposed by the establishment; the Gracchi were assassinated
- Fabry's intellectual origin for this comparison came from a teacher in Rome who compared Europe-America relations to Greeks-Romans: Romans saw Greeks as able to speak but unable to act, much as Americans view Europeans
- Later discovered Albert (French WWI writer) who compared WWI to the Peloponnesian War, seeing it as a European civil war
- Fustel de Coulanges' "The Ancient City" helped crack open how classical civilization worked
- Key authors in this space: David Hackett Fisher (The Great Wave, Albion Seed — price cycles and British regional cultures in America), Peter Turchin, Amélie de Riancourt (The Coming Caesars), and Oswald Spengler (whom Fabry calls "the worst writer in history — incomprehensible")

### 2. National Revolution Theory

- Every nation state is an empire that has held stable frontiers for centuries, leading to homogenization of elites and a unified national consciousness
- The "national revolution" is the final struggle in building a nation state — triggered by a war where the rising nation expects to become a hegemon but the regime fails
- Two conditions for regime collapse: (1) loss of legitimacy (failure to accomplish its mission), and (2) lack of financial resources. If a regime has money it can survive even without legitimacy; without both it collapses
- European sequence: England (17th c.) → France (18th c.) → Germany (early 20th c.) → USSR/Russia (1991)
- Global examples: Iran (1979 revolution as national revolution), Turkey (19th c.), Tunisia, Morocco, Egypt (since 2011), Japan, China
- India is the exception to every rule — at the stage of France under François I (early modern) but with parliamentary democracy, likely an artifact of British colonization

### 3. France's Coming Revolution

- The French Fifth Republic, established by de Gaulle after WWII, is an "absolute president" system — effectively an elected dictatorship for five years where parliamentary elections have no real importance
- De Gaulle hated parliamentarianism (blamed it for France's 1940 defeat) and reinstated a regime with the same structure as modern Russia — a "parliamentary monarchy"
- France is at a more advanced stage of national development than its regime allows; neighboring countries (Italy, Spain, Germany, UK) all have parliamentary systems while France has a quasi-monarchical presidency
- The regime has lost legitimacy due to a succession of humiliations, particularly COVID-19 — France's national pride centers on its healthcare and social security system, yet the country lacked masks and hospital beds, a national humiliation (especially after French media mocked Italy)
- The regime is fragile; Fabry predicts it will fall with a new national revolution
- The revolution in France would likely trigger revolutions across Europe (Britain, Germany, Russia)

### 4. Russia's Geopolitical Position

- Russia = Macedon to Europe's Greece; Putin compared to Perseus of Macedon (not Mithridates)
- Russia must dominate Europe to acquire the economic mass needed to compete with China and the US
- Putin's vision of "multipolarity": Russia gets Europe, US gets Americas, China gets Asia
- Russia is a "revanchist imperialist" — most analogous to 1930s Japan. Both were small economies (Japan had Italy's national income) making a desperate "last hope" war to become a top-tier world power before entering terminal decline
- Key insight: Russia's demographic and economic trajectory means it can never equal China, which is the real regional threat to Russia. Conquering/dominating Europe is Russia's only hope to remain on the world podium
- Russia has been at war with NATO (in its own perception) since February 2022; performing reasonably well against Ukraine+Western support has emboldened the conviction that now is the time to press further

### 5. The Coming Great Power Conflict

- Fabry predicts the "Great War" (West vs. Russia/China — the "Former Mongol Empire") will be over in ~10 years
- There will be a military confrontation between Russia and NATO
- Wars come in "couples" with a pattern: Seven Years War → Revolutionary/Napoleonic Wars; WWI → WWII; Cold War → Coming War. Each second war is a more intense version of the first
- The coming conflict may not be nuclear but could involve hybrid warfare, economic warfare, and proxy conflicts — potentially "very bloody" without nuclear escalation
- The "previous war syndrome": since February 2022, the West has been fighting the Cold War again, believing economic sanctions and making Ukraine a new Afghanistan for Russia would collapse it — the same mistake Britain made in 1805 (thinking they'd exhaust Napoleon as they exhausted France in the Seven Years War) and France/Britain made in 1940 (thinking the Maginot Line would make a comfortable trench war)
- The Suwalki Gap (the ~100km corridor between Poland and Lithuania, between Russian Kaliningrad and Belarus) is a critical flashpoint
- Wagner Group in Belarus creates a plausible deniability scenario: they could seize the Suwalki Gap, Putin disavows them, Germany hesitates on war, and NATO unity fractures

### 6. Europe-America Conflict in the Long Term

- In the short term, Western unity against China and Russia is necessary
- But in the long term, a conflict between the US and a unified Europe is inevitable — America cannot accept a European empire that would be too dangerous, just as Rome went to war with Greece after Macedon was defeated
- Historical parallel: After Rome defeated Macedon (168 BC, Third Macedonian War), the Achaean League (Greek equivalent of the EU) sought independence from Roman protection — and was crushed
- America would prefer a divided Europe: Latin Europe, Germanic Europe, Slavic Europe around Poland — not a fully united one
- A fully united Europe is a "historical necessity in the very long term" — like Greece becoming the Byzantine Empire — but only achievable after the American Empire collapses

### 7. Geographical Determinism of States

- Certain geographies favor stable frontiers between multiple nation states (ancient Greece, modern Europe)
- Other geographies naturally form single empires (China — flat, isolated from the rest of the world)
- Nation states can only appear when other states of similar dimension exist
- David Cosandé's concept of "isothymia" (or similar): geography determines whether a region develops multiple states or a single empire

### 8. Historical Cycles Across Civilizations

- Different world regions repeat similar cycles tied to their geography:
  - Ancient Greeks / Modern Europeans
  - United States / Rome
  - Arab societies / Ancient Jews
  - Turks / Ancient Assyrians
- A society's history is much more similar to its geographic ancestors than to contemporaries elsewhere

### 9. Prediction Track Record

- Fabry predicted the Russian invasion of Ukraine in January 2019 on his blog ("Towards a Russian Invasion of Ukraine")
- He identified two prerequisites: (1) completion of the Kerch Bridge to Crimea, (2) complete seizure of Belarus
- Belarus was taken over by Russia after the controversial re-election of Lukashenko in August 2020; by end of 2020 Belarus was fully in Russian hands
- First troop buildup around Ukraine followed in spring 2021 — Russia could now attack from three sides (the equivalent of the 1968 Czechoslovakia intervention)
- The plan failed in February 2022, leading to the prolonged war

## Points of Agreement/Disagreement

- Both Fabry and the History102 host share the same broad model of history: America as New Rome entering Caesarism, with the Gracchi as Trump
- Both see Russia's role as analogous to Macedon vs. Greece
- Fabry is pessimistic about both America and Europe in the short term, but sees the US ultimately winning the great power conflict
- The host (Rudyard) and Fabry agree that the Graccus-Trump parallel is strong and that Caesarism is coming to America
- Differing European vs. American perspectives: Fabry offers a continental European view on NATO, Russia, and transatlantic relations that diverges from American assumptions about Western unity

## Detailed Summary

The conversation opens with both participants noting they independently arrived at the same Rome-America comparison. Fabry traces his intellectual journey: sparked by a teacher's comparison of Europe-America to Greece-Rome during the Iraq War debate, then deepened through Albert's WWI writings (comparing it to the Peloponnesian War) and Fustel de Coulanges' "The Ancient City." He deliberately avoided Spengler and Toynbee to form an independent view.

The middle section explores Fabry's "national revolution" theory — the idea that nation states form through a final revolutionary struggle after centuries of imperial consolidation. France had its first national revolution in 1789, but the current Fifth Republic (established by de Gaulle after WWII) is an anachronistic "absolute presidency" that no longer fits France's level of development. Combined with humiliations like COVID-19 preparedness, the regime is losing legitimacy and resources simultaneously — the recipe for collapse.

The latter half covers great power geopolitics. Fabry sees Russia as a "revanchist imperialist" in the mold of 1930s Japan — a declining power making a desperate bid for great-power status by dominating Europe before demographic/economic trends render it irrelevant. He predicts a military confrontation between Russia and NATO, potentially triggered by a Wagner-provoked incident in the Suwalki Gap. The long-term trajectory points to US-EU rivalry after Russia and China are dealt with, following the Rome-Greece pattern. He estimates the current great power conflict will resolve in ~10 years.
