# Native American History

## Overview

Native American history in the 19th century is a story of dispossession, resistance, adaptation, and survival in the face of relentless American westward expansion. From the Louisiana Purchase (1803) through the closing of the frontier (1890), the United States pursued policies of removal, assimilation, and sometimes outright extermination against Indigenous peoples. The video traces the major conflicts, treaties, and policy shifts that shaped Native American experience during this century, while also examining Native resistance movements, cultural preservation, and the creation of the reservation system that continues to define Native life today.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### Removal and Dispossession
- **Indian Removal Act (1830)**: Andrew Jackson's policy to remove all Eastern tribes west of the Mississippi
- **Trail of Tears (1838-1839)**: forced removal of Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, and Seminole — thousands died
- **Land cession treaties**: hundreds of treaties signed, nearly all broken by the U.S. government
- **Doctrine of Discovery**: legal justification for European claims to Indigenous lands
- **Manifest Destiny**: ideological belief that American expansion was divinely ordained

### The Indian Wars (1860s-1890s)
- **Plains Wars**: conflicts with Sioux, Cheyenne, Arapaho, Comanche, Apache over the Great Plains
- **Sand Creek Massacre (1864)**: Colorado militia attacked peaceful Cheyenne village — 150+ killed
- **Fetterman Fight (1866)**: Lakota victory in Red Cloud's War
- **Battle of the Little Bighorn (1876)**: Lakota and Cheyenne under Crazy Horse and Sitting Bull defeat Custer
- **Nez Perce War (1877)**: Chief Joseph's epic retreat toward Canada
- **Wounded Knee Massacre (1890)**: last major conflict — 300+ Lakota killed, symbol of the end of the Indian Wars

### U.S. Indian Policy
- **Civilization Program**: attempt to assimilate Native Americans through Christianity, education, farming
- **Dawes Act (1887)**: broke up tribal lands into individual allotments, devastating communal landholding
- **Boarding schools**: Carlisle Indian Industrial School (founded 1879) — "Kill the Indian, save the man"
- **Reservation system**: confinement of tribes to designated lands, often the least productive territories
- **Indian Appropriations Act (1871)**: ended treaty-making with tribes; they became wards of the state

### Native Resistance and Adaptation
- **Religious revitalization**: Ghost Dance movement (1890) — spiritual response to dispossession
- **Legal resistance**: tribes fought for rights in courts (e.g., Cherokee Nation v. Georgia, Worcester v. Georgia)
- **Economic adaptation**: some tribes adopted ranching, farming, tourism
- **Cultural preservation**: languages, ceremonies, arts maintained despite suppression
- **Warrior societies**: continuity of military tradition as a path to honor

### The Close of the Frontier
- **Census of 1890**: declared the frontier line closed
- **Frederick Jackson Turner's Frontier Thesis** (1893): argued that the frontier shaped American democracy
- By 1900, Native American population had fallen from an estimated 5-10 million pre-Columbus to about 250,000
- Loss of 98-99% of original land base

## Important Figures

- **Andrew Jackson** (1767-1845): president who championed Indian Removal
- **Chief John Ross** (1790-1866): Cherokee leader who fought removal through courts
- **Sequoyah** (c. 1770-1843): Cherokee silversmith who created the Cherokee syllabary
- **Sitting Bull** (1831-1890): Lakota holy man and war chief; defeated Custer; killed at Wounded Knee
- **Crazy Horse** (c. 1842-1877): Lakota war leader; symbol of resistance
- **Chief Joseph** (1840-1904): Nez Perce leader; known for his eloquent surrender speech
- **Geronimo** (1829-1909): Apache leader; last Native leader to formally surrender to U.S.
- **Red Cloud** (1822-1909): Lakota war chief; won Red Cloud's War; later advocated for education
- **Sarah Winnemucca** (c. 1844-1891): Paiute activist, writer, lecturer on Native rights
- **Ely Parker** (1828-1895): Seneca engineer; Grant's military secretary; first Native Commissioner of Indian Affairs
- **Richard Henry Pratt** (1840-1924): founded Carlisle school; "Kill the Indian, save the man"
- **Quanah Parker** (1845-1911): Comanche leader who led resistance then adapted to reservation life
