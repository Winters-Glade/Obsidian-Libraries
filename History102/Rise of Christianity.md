# Rise of Christianity

## Summary
Christianity began as a small Jewish sect centered on an unemployed carpenter's son from Nazareth and grew into the largest religion in the world with about 2 billion followers. The religion emerged in the context of the Roman Empire, a vast globalized state that had conquered the Mediterranean world, displaced local cultures, and created a deeply unequal society where a quarter to a third of the population were slaves with no legal rights. Jesus of Nazareth taught that love is the center of the universe, challenged both Jewish legalism (the Pharisees) and Hellenistic assimilation, and was executed by Roman authorities at the instigation of Jewish religious leaders. Christianity's breakthrough was its universality — unlike all previous religions tied to ethnic blood, it welcomed anyone regardless of background. The religion spread fastest among rootless urban populations in multicultural port cities, among women (who were protected by Christian sexual ethics in a brutally exploitative Roman society), and among the poor and enslaved. Roman persecution backfired, providing free advertising as Christians died fearlessly for their faith. In the 4th century, Emperor Constantine converted after a vision and made Christianity the state religion, transforming it from a persecuted minority into the imperial faith. The church then absorbed elements from competing religions (Zeus imagery for God, Isis iconography for Mary, Mithra's birthday for Christmas) and adapted brilliantly to different cultures, keeping civilization alive through the Dark Ages.

## Key Concepts
- **[[Universalism]]**: Christianity's revolutionary idea that religion is not tied to ethnicity — anyone of any background can convert, unlike Greek, Roman, or tribal religions tied to blood
- **[[Persecution as Advertising]]**: Roman martyrdom made Christianity visible and attractive — watching Christians die happily for their faith made spectators wonder what they had
- **[[Roman Globalization]]**: The Roman Empire created a cosmopolitan world where local folkways were destroyed, creating a population of culturally rootless people receptive to a universal religion
- **[[Christian Adaptability]]**: Christianity's remarkable ability to recontextualize itself for different cultures (Viking warrior God, African tribal father, Inuit "Seal of God" instead of "Lamb of God")
- **[[City of God]]**: St. Augustine's concept distinguishing the earthly empire (Rome) from the divine empire (God's kingdom), providing a framework for Christianity to survive Rome's fall
- **[[The Way]]**: Early Christianity's original name, emphasizing following God's path rather than chaotic self-direction
- **[[Arianism]]**: The heresy that Jesus was not divine; adopted by many Germanic barbarian tribes who conquered Western Europe
- **[[Council of Nicaea]] (325 AD)**: Constantine's council that standardized Christian doctrine, creating orthodoxy while defining heresies

## Key People
- **[[Jesus Christ]] (Joshua the Messiah)**: Jewish carpenter's son from Nazareth who taught love, performed miracles, was crucified, and whose followers believed he rose from the dead
- **[[Pontius Pilate]]**: Roman governor who sentenced Jesus to death, comparable (in historical impact) to someone who turned down the Beatles
- **[[Constantine the Great]]**: Roman emperor who converted after the Battle of Milvian Bridge (seeing a cross in the sky), made Christianity the state religion, convened the Council of Nicaea
- **[[St. Augustine of Hippo]]**: North African Berber theologian, the most important Christian thinker besides Christ himself; wrote *The City of God*
- **[[Julian the Apostate]]**: Emperor who tried to revive paganism by blending it with Christian elements (afterlife, heaven/hell) — failed attempt at a "Hinduism-style" pagan reform
- **[[Peter Brown]]**: Historian who wrote about Roman globalization and the rootless urban demographics that embraced Christianity
- **[[Paul the Apostle]]**: Though not named in the transcript, referred to as the key early missionary who spread Christianity beyond Jews to Gentiles
- **[[Emperor Diocletian]]**: Constantine's predecessor who tried to persecute Christians into extinction, failed, paving the way for Constantine's opposite strategy

## Key Events
- **Jesus' crucifixion (~30 AD)**: Executed by Rome at Pharisees' instigation for challenging religious and political authorities
- **First Jewish Revolt (66-73 AD)**: Massive rebellion against Rome, led to the destruction of the Temple and Jewish diaspora, which spread Christianity as Jewish Christians dispersed
- **Persecution under Nero, Diocletian, etc.**: Romans tried to suppress Christianity for 300 years, but martyrdom attracted converts
- **Battle of Milvian Bridge (312 AD)**: Constantine's vision of the cross led to his victory and subsequent conversion, the turning point for Christianity's legalization
- **Council of Nicaea (325 AD)**: Established orthodox Christian doctrine (Trinity, divinity of Christ), defined heresies
- **Edict of Thessalonica (380 AD)**: Christianity became the official state religion of the Roman Empire
- **Fall of the Western Roman Empire (476 AD)**: The church filled the power and literacy vacuum, preserving civilization through the Dark Ages
- **Conversion of the Franks**: The Franks chose Catholicism over Arianism, which helped them gain legitimacy with the native Roman population
