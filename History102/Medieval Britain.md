# Medieval Britain

## Overview
Medieval England (c. 5th–15th century) was a tiny population (1–4 million) on the northwest edge of Europe that produced one of the greatest cultural flowerings in human history. Lynch argues that the Anglo-Saxons are among the most consequential ethnic groups in history.

## Why Medieval England Matters
- **Language** — English emerged from the fusion of Anglo-Saxon (Germanic) and Norman French
- **Legal system** — common law, trial by jury, habeas corpus all have medieval English origins
- **Political structures** — Parliament, Magna Carta (1215), limits on royal power
- **Cultural identity** — the English people formed their national consciousness during this period

## Key Periods
1. **Anglo-Saxon period** (c. 450–1066) — Germanic settlement, Christianization, Alfred the Great
2. **Norman Conquest** (1066) — William the Conqueror, feudal restructuring
3. **Plantagenet era** (1154–1485) — Magna Carta, Hundred Years' War, Parliament's rise
4. **Late Middle Ages** — Black Death, Peasants' Revolt, decline of feudalism

## Historiographical Note
- Three models of history: (1) demographic/structural trends, (2) great man theory, (3) Marxist/class-based analysis
- Medieval England doesn't fit neatly into any of these models
- The probabilistic approach — treating historical claims as bets with measurable confidence — is proposed as a superior framework
