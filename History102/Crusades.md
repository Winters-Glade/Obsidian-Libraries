# The Crusades

## Overview
The Crusades (c. 1095–1291) were a series of religious wars called by the Latin Church to reclaim the Holy Land from Muslim rule. Lynch frames them as a "white people moment" — a burst of expansive energy from a society emerging from centuries of defensive survival mode.

## Historical Context
- By 1100 AD, Europe had emerged from the worst of the Dark Ages
- After the Frankish Empire fell apart in the 800s, Europe faced invasions from Magyars, Vikings, and Muslims (800s–900s)
- The Crusades represent the moment when Western Europe stopped being a target and became an aggressor

## Spengler's "Faustian Civilization"
- Lynch draws on Oswald Spengler's concept of the "Faustian" spirit — the drive to do the most extreme thing possible
- The Crusades were Europe's first expression of this expansive, world-transforming energy
- This same spirit would later fuel the Age of Discovery, colonialism, and industrial revolution

## Key Crusades
- **First Crusade (1096–1099)** — captured Jerusalem, established Crusader states
- **Third Crusade (1189–1192)** — Richard the Lionheart vs Saladin
- **Fourth Crusade (1204)** — sacked Constantinople (a Byzantine Christian city)
- The Crusader states lasted nearly 200 years

## Legacy & Controversy
- Deepened the East-West schism in Christianity
- Long-term Muslim-Christian antagonism
- Transferred knowledge (Greek philosophy, science) from Islamic world to Europe
- Strengthened Papal authority and Italian merchant republics
