# Mike Ter Maat Interview: Libertarian Presidential Candidate

> **Source:** [History102 (Common Ground)](https://www.youtube.com/@History102-qg5oj) — [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0Q9nMchR7R4)

## Guest Biography

Mike Ter Maat is an economist and former Libertarian Party presidential candidate. He brings a highly diverse professional background: he worked in the banking industry after business school, earned a graduate degree from George Washington University, worked for international agencies and the federal government, served at the White House during the George H.W. Bush administration (1991-92), ran his own strategic consulting business, worked as a free-market advocate in Washington for financial services, taught economics at multiple universities, served as a substitute teacher in public schools, and became a police officer in South Broward County, Florida — a second career that he says turned him into an economist by showing him the real-world results of bad public policy. He became a registered Libertarian around 2010-2011, having come to libertarianism from both the "right-hand side" (recognition that the world works better when people make decisions for themselves) and the "left-hand side" (the government lacks the moral and ethical authority to make decisions for you). His campaign platform is branded the **Gold New Deal**, emphasizing sound money, free-market solutions, limited constitutional government, and decentralized federalism.

## Key Arguments & Discussion Points

1. **Two Paths to Libertarianism** — Ter Maat distinguishes between arriving at libertarianism via the "right-hand side" (free-market economics, efficiency arguments) and the "left-hand side" (moral/ethical argument that government has no authority to make personal decisions for individuals). He identifies with both.

2. **Authoritarianism from Democracy** — Both major parties have abandoned their agendas and now prioritize keeping the other party out of power. This creates fertile ground for authoritarianism, as people are told to fear the "other side" rather than the loss of civil liberties.

3. **Roman Republic Parallels** — Deep discussion comparing the fall of the Roman Republic to contemporary America: destruction of the middle class, bread-and-circuses welfare state, military strongmen buying public support, legislature ceasing to function leading to executive and judicial overreach, and ultimate collapse into military dictatorship.

4. **Wealth as Zero-Sum (Refutation)** — Ter Maat identifies the belief that "wealth is a zero-sum game" as "one of the most pernicious ideas in the history of mankind." He traces this to tribalistic origins and argues it is the rationale for government redistribution and empire-building.

5. **Post-Constitutional America** — Argues that the United States now lives in a "post-constitutional nation" — no longer meaningfully bound by constitutional limits. The Constitution, designed to protect individual liberties and delimit federal power, has been eroded over generations. The average American no longer feels emotionally bound to it.

6. **Decentralization as Core Principle** — Power must be reserved to the most local level possible. Uses the example of school boards vs. state governments (e.g., Florida) deciding curricula — even if you agree with the policy direction, the process of centralization itself is dangerous.

7. **Institutional Failure** — Institutions like the Federal Reserve, FBI, and the public education system have lost good-faith credibility and need fundamental reform, not just new leadership. "They have left us more than we have left them."

8. **COVID as a Warning** — The pandemic revealed how easily government can control information, politicize science, and ratchet up expectations of control. Distinguishes between feelings about vaccines (he is vaccinated) and feelings about vaccine *mandates* (even a perfect vaccine should not be mandated by government — the libertarian answer is no). Warns about future crises being leveraged for even greater control.

9. **Gen Z and Freedom** — Observes a split among young people: the "zombies" who have had no agency in their lives (over-scheduled, bureaucratic schooling) and support government control in every aspect; and an active minority who realize the world is rough and need to stand up for themselves. Andrew Tate's popularity is cited as a signal of libertarian-adjacent frustration that could metastasize into something ugly if libertarianism doesn't provide an alternative.

10. **Education as Number One Policy Problem** — The public school system is the most protected industry yet the one with which Americans are most frustrated. Forcing families into failing public schools is unethical and hurts cultural development, economic competitiveness, and the ability of citizens to engage in democratic debate.

11. **Politics as Religion Substitute** — With the decline of traditional religion, politics has become a replacement — people treat political positions as sacred dogma, lose all nuance, and engage in shaming rather than discussion. This undermines democracy.

12. **Free Speech and the First Amendment** — Recalls the Skokie Nazi march as a teachable moment about protecting unpopular speech. Argues the First Amendment has no real meaning without an unpopular opinion that tests it. Discusses the 303 Creative gay wedding website case as an Alinsky-esque manufactured conflict.

13. **Policy-Forward Campaign Strategy** — Ter Maat advocates for a "policy forward" campaign that differentiates libertarians from both major parties. He urges voters to "stop wasting your vote" on the two-party duopoly and instead vote for their ethics and principles.

14. **Ross Perot Legacy** — Discussion of Ross Perot as the most successful third-party candidate in 100 years — he changed the national discourse on fiscal responsibility even though he didn't win, demonstrating the power of a credible third-party message.

## Points of Agreement/Disagreement

- **Strong alignment** with History102 hosts on skepticism of state power, concern about authoritarianism, constitutional decay, and the need for a third political alternative.
- **Agreement** that politics has become a religion substitute and that cultural values should be kept distinct from public policy.
- **Agreement** that the Roman Republic offers cautionary parallels to modern America.
- **Shared concern** about COVID-era government overreach and the politicization of science.
- **Agreement** on school choice and the structural problems with public education.
- Hosts resonate with Ter Maat's distinction between caring about policy vs. politics, and the need for "radical centrism" and finding common ground.

## Notable Quotes

> "I came to libertarianism from what I would call the left-hand side, which is to say that even if it were not true that the world were made better off and the economy worked more efficiently when people make decisions for themselves, the government still doesn't have the authority morally, ethically speaking, to make decisions for you."

> "I would argue that one of the most pernicious ideas in the history of mankind — and possibly the most pernicious when you take into consideration its broad spectrum effects — is the idea that wealth is a zero-sum game, that we get ahead by taking from somebody else."

> "The First Amendment really has no meaning without an unpopular opinion. If you don't have someone willing to say something really dumb, by the standards of most Americans, you have to have someone willing to say something that's unpopular. And a real threat that the government might do something about that — that combination is what is required to test the First Amendment."

> "I would argue that we live in a post-constitutional nation in some sense. I don't think the average American really feels somehow emotionally bound to the Constitution. And I think this is one of the sources of our problems."

> "I do believe that a policy-forward campaign is what will most effectively differentiate us from Republicans and Democrats. I believe that most Americans are looking for a third choice, or at least a different choice, and I believe that most Americans have a libertarian streak."

> "Stop wasting your vote. You should vote for your ethics, you should vote for your principles."

> "They have left us more than we have left them."

> "If you had a perfect vaccine — perfect information, perfectly trustworthy — for a libertarian, would the government have the right to force you to take it? The answer has got to be, from a libertarian point of view, no."

> "Being a police officer, if you weren't an economist before, it'll turn you into an economist because you see the result of bad public policy — up close and personal."

> "One of the most pernicious ideas — the zero-sum notion — is what gave rise to the notion that governments were rationally motivated to take things from people and be in the business of distributing things to people in order to maintain their power."

> "I think that Americans generally are more aware today than ever before that a lot of our problems come from bad public policy."

## Transcript Summary

The conversation opens with Ter Maat's personal journey from Republican fiscal conservative to police officer to registered Libertarian. The hosts (Dave Hamilton, Roger, and Richard) engage him on the parallels between the fall of the Roman Republic and modern America, the zero-sum fallacy in economic thinking, and the erosion of constitutional governance.

The middle portion covers the Libertarian Party's opportunity in 2024 — frustration with both Trump and Biden as party leaders, the post-constitutional condition of America, and the need for decentralized federalism (using school board curricula decisions in Florida as a case study). The conversation explores how institutions like the Federal Reserve and FBI have "left us more than we have left them."

The second half dives into the COVID response as a warning about government control of science and information, the distinction between vaccines and vaccine mandates, the Gen Z generation split (between "zombies" who lack agency and active youth seeking alternatives), and Andrew Tate as a proxy for libertarian-adjacent frustration. They discuss the Skokie Nazi march as a free speech teachable moment, the 303 Creative Supreme Court case as an Alinsky-esque manufactured conflict, and the broader problem of politics becoming a replacement for religion.

The interview concludes with Ter Maat outlining his "Gold New Deal" platform, his policy-forward campaign strategy, the importance of differentiated messaging to break through the two-party duopoly, and his call for voters to "stop wasting your vote" on parties that don't represent their principles. He provides his campaign websites (miketermaat.com and goldnewdeal.org) and notes all his personal contact information is publicly available on the site.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **Gold New Deal** — Ter Maat's branded platform name for his transformational policy agenda combining sound money, free markets, and constitutional restraint.
- **Two paths to libertarianism** — The "right-hand side" (economic efficiency/free markets) vs. the "left-hand side" (moral authority/individual autonomy).
- **Zombie vs. Active Gen Z** — A generational split between youth who have been robbed of agency by bureaucratic upbringing and those who embrace self-reliance, representing both a crisis and an opportunity for libertarianism.
