# Angela McArdle Interview

> **Source:** [History102](https://www.youtube.com/@History102-qg5oj) — [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uDpGPLFDuP8)

## Episode Overview

Angela McArdle, Chairwoman of the Libertarian National Committee, joins History102 co-hosts Dave Hamilton and Rudyard Lynch for a wide-ranging conversation about the state of the Libertarian Party, its strategic direction, and foreign policy. McArdle discusses the Mises Caucus takeover of the party, the shift from mass candidate recruitment to a "low time preference" grassroots strategy, and the challenges of building a serious political force in a two-party system. The conversation also delves deeply into US foreign policy — Ukraine, Israel-Palestine, and the risk of World War III — where the hosts find both agreement and spirited disagreement. McArdle shares her work building cross-ideological anti-war coalitions and her vision for libertarianism as essential to defending the best values of Western civilization.

## Guest Biography

Angela McArdle is the Chairwoman of the Libertarian National Committee (LNC), the governing body of the Libertarian Party of the United States. In this role, she oversees board governance, sets the party's strategic direction, approves all spending, and manages marketing campaigns and political projects.

McArdle has been active in the Libertarian Party since 2016 and was part of the Mises Caucus takeover that began in the summer of 2017 — a reaction to what she describes as the party's "cultural left drift." Describing herself as an anarcho-capitalist ("no government, I just want to be like the little monopoly man"), she emphasizes intellectual honesty, pragmatism, and long-term strategic thinking over slogans and catchphrases.

Her family background includes immigration from the coast to the interior over American history, with family members who "literally remembered clearing the frontier." She grew up an hour outside Philadelphia, Pennsylvania. She also works in media, writes and produces video content, and has been extensively involved in anti-war coalition organizing, including the "Rage Against the War Machine" rally in DC and the upcoming "Defeat the Deep State" and "Defend the West" rallies.

## Key Arguments & Discussion Points

### Strategic Overhaul of the Libertarian Party

- The LP has been in "perpetual startup mode" for 52 years, run by "good meeting activists who don't have good business sense, don't have good vision" (02:48). Under her leadership, the party has adopted a "low time preference strategy" — investing in infrastructure for 10-20 year success rather than chasing every two-year election cycle.
- The previous strategy of running as many candidates as possible (800+ in 2018) was a failure: it stretched resources thin, couldn't provide enough volunteers, and resulted in losing every elected seat the party held (05:58). McArdle argues for fewer, more serious campaigns that build political capital and credibility.
- She advocates for "asymmetrical warfare" tactics inspired by Saul Alinsky's *Rules for Radicals* — citing the party's filing for conservatorship over Joe Biden and Donald Trump as a successful example of generating national conversation about age and fitness for office (07:08).

### The Mises Caucus Takeover and Party Direction

- The Mises Caucus formed in summer 2017 in reaction to what McArdle describes as "the cultural left drift" of the party (05:15). The takeover wasn't just at the leadership level but aimed at the membership level to ensure consistent board elections and a succession plan.
- The previous party messaging heavily emphasized LGBT rights while ignoring issues like homeschooling, parental sovereignty, and raw milk legislation. McArdle has worked to broaden the party's appeal to Christians without making it a "Christian party" (51:58).
- There is significant internal disagreement — activists vs. professionalizers, pragmatists vs. ideologues — making long-term progress difficult. Board turnover every two years compounds this challenge.

### Foreign Policy and Anti-War Stance

- McArdle advocates for a consistent peaceful foreign policy: open free trade with all countries (even those that don't like the US), ending sanctions and embargoes, and avoiding military intervention. "If goods don't cross borders, troops will" (22:36).
- On Ukraine: she sees the Democrat position as heavily influenced by the Russiagate narrative and covering up corruption involving Hunter Biden and Burisma. She opposes continued funding given America's internal difficulties (38:55).
- On Israel-Palestine: she supports a two-state solution and believes ending all US foreign aid would incentivize Israel to negotiate. She acknowledges the complexity of historical grievances but argues "at some point there's a statute of limitations, and we just got to let it go" (33:50).
- On World War II: McArdle would have opposed US intervention "to the extent that we did" — she believes diplomatic backing of the Allies plus the atomic bomb would have sufficed, and that a full ground invasion in Normandy was unnecessary (23:25-24:56).
- She warns that the US is so internally divided it couldn't sustain another major war, and that politicians historically start wars to get re-elected (39:40-40:02).

### Cross-Ideological Coalition Building

- McArdle is deeply involved in anti-war coalition work that brings together anarcho-capitalists, hardcore leftists, communists, and right-wing activists around narrow common goals: abolish the CIA, free Julian Assange, end forever wars (43:10-43:39).
- She describes the "Rage Against the War Machine" rally (3,000 attendees) and the upcoming "Defeat the Deep State" rally on President's Day weekend, as well as the "Defend the West" project a year out, working with Bret Weinstein and Matt Toon.
- She believes "issue coalitions" that are narrow in scope can overcome deep ideological divides — people from BLM shirts to climate activists can hug and celebrate together after working on a shared cause (44:35).

### The Libertarian Personality and Intellectual Honesty

- Libertarians score highest in IQ and rational thinking of any political group (per Jonathan Haidt's research), and are influenced least by emotions. They have low "disgust" sensitivity and don't hold others to their own standards (45:41, 47:42).
- The challenge: "eagles don't flock together" — libertarians have an independent mindset that makes coordination difficult. McArdle rejects the "herding cats" analogy but acknowledges the "herding eagles" problem (16:10).
- She believes libertarians need to adopt more of the cultural cohesion seen in the Jewish community — taking care of each other, setting high expectations, helping one another achieve them, and sticking together (15:51-16:15).

### Branding and Electoral Viability

- Electoral failure is a major part of the LP's reputation, which is "damaging at the polls" and will take significant time to change (11:24). Many people hold libertarian views but won't vote LP because they don't see it as viable.
- The presidential race serves primarily as a "signaling mechanism" to maintain ballot access, not where to put most resources (11:44).

## Points of Agreement/Disagreement

### Areas of Strong Agreement

- **Anti-war sentiment**: All three agree that US foreign policy is deeply flawed, that the wars in Afghanistan and Iraq were failures, and that the US is overly militaristic. McArdle's anti-war coalition work is praised by the hosts.
- **The value of civil discourse**: The hosts explicitly compliment McArdle (and libertarians generally) for being able to have nuanced conversations without it devolving into attacks. This is contrasted with the broader political culture post-COVID.
- **Distrust of the Deep State / entrenched interests**: Shared skepticism of bureaucratic power, with the hosts noting that most Americans (even across political divides) oppose it.
- **Rationality of libertarians**: Hosts agree with Haidt's framing that libertarians score highest in rational thinking and are less emotionally driven.

### Points of Tension and Disagreement

- **American empire as stabilizer**: The hosts (particularly Rudyard) argue that the American empire has been the primary force preventing global war and that its withdrawal would lead to chaos (Russia in Ukraine, China in Taiwan, Middle East conflict). McArdle is more optimistic about peaceful coexistence through trade and diplomacy, though she concedes the complexity.
- **World War II counterfactual**: Significant disagreement emerges over whether the US should have intervened in WWII. Hosts take a hawkish position — that diplomacy doesn't work and power is the only language the world understands. McArdle believes a more restrained approach (no ground invasion) would have been sufficient.
- **Israel's survival**: The hosts argue that without US support, Israel would be destroyed. McArdle contends that ending foreign aid would force regional players to negotiate and that "it's not so much our problem anymore" (36:25).
- **The moral arc of history**: Rudyard explicitly states "I don't believe in the moral arc of history" and argues that the current period of relative peace is a product of American dominance, not inevitable progress. McArdle points to free trade and capitalism as forces lifting people out of poverty and reducing conflict.
- **Alinsky and *Rules for Radicals***: McArdle acknowledges using Alinsky-inspired tactics (conservatorship filing) while the hosts express discomfort with Alinsky's methods and his dedication of the book to Satan — though Rudyard concedes "I'm happy to file conservatorship" (07:50).
