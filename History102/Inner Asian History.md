# Inner Asian History

**Source:** [YouTube - History102](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eNd-t1lJGfw)
**Hosts:** Rudyard Lynch & Austin Padgett
**Duration:** 2h 9min

## Overview

An exploration of "Inner Asia" — the mountainous desert region south of the Eurasian steppe, encompassing Central Asia (Khorasan/Turkestan), Afghanistan, Xinjiang, Tibet, and Zomia. This region acts as the geographic and civilizational hinge of Eurasia, where China, India, Persia, and Europe meet.

## Key Topics

- **Defining Inner Asia** — The desert/mountain belt that forms the spine of Asia. Distinct from the Eurasian steppe (grasslands to the north). Includes the "-stans" (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan), Afghanistan, Xinjiang, Tibet, and Zomia.
- **Zomia Parallel** — James C. Scott's concept of Zomia (highlands between China and Southeast Asia) used as an anarchist parallel to Inner Asia. Flipping the perspective: these aren't peripheries but the central hub.
- **The Wakhān Corridor** — The tiny strip of Afghanistan connecting to China, the divide between West Eurasian and East Asian populations. Rudyard's friend Lord Miles planned to be the first Westerner to hike it since WWII.
- **Khorasan** — The pre-modern Persian name for Central Asia, once one of the wealthiest, most advanced regions on Earth. The industrial revolution bypassed it entirely.
- **Buddhist Era** — Central Asia was primarily Buddhist before Islam. The Silk Road carried Buddhism, Manichaeism, and Nestorian Christianity through the region.
- **Turkic Replacement** — The gradual replacement of Persian-speaking populations by Turkic nomads from the steppe. Only Tajikistan remains Persian-speaking.
- **Mongol Devastation** — The Mongols destroyed the irrigation systems of Central Asia, turned cities into "piles of skulls," and the region never recovered. Genghis Khan killed 80 million; Tamerlane killed another 15 million.
- **Tamerlane (Timur)** — The last great Central Asian conqueror. Built Samarkand into a magnificent capital while devastating every neighboring region. Book recommendation: "After Tamerlane" argues he was the tipping point that shifted world power to Europe.
- **The Great Game** — 19th-century Anglo-Russian rivalry over Central Asia. Russia eventually conquered the region; British paranoia about Russia invading India drove policy.
- **Modern Central Asia** — Dictatorships of varying brutality, landlocked and isolated from the global oceanic economy. Kazakhstan is the most enlightened; Turkmenistan is the "North Korea of Central Asia."

## Summary

Inner Asia (Central Asia, Afghanistan, Xinjiang, Tibet) was for centuries the center of the Eurasian world system — the crossroads where the great civilizations traded, warred, and exchanged ideas. The region was Buddhist before Islam, Persian-speaking before Turkic, and wealthy beyond measure before the Mongols.

The Mongol invasion (1200s) was a civilizational catastrophe from which Central Asia has never recovered. Genghis Khan and Tamerlane together killed perhaps 95 million people, destroyed the irrigation infrastructure, and turned the region from a center of global civilization into a backwater. After Tamerlane, power shifted permanently to the maritime civilizations of Western Europe.

In the modern period, Russia and China divided Inner Asia between them. The "-stans" remain dictatorships, isolated from global trade, with uncertain futures. The Chinese government's treatment of Uyghurs in Xinjiang is characterized as a genocide underway with Western media complicity.

## Key Takeaways

- Inner Asia = the mountainous/desert region south of the steppe, the hinge of Eurasia
- Was once the wealthiest, most advanced region on Earth (Khorasan civilization)
- Buddhist before Islamic, Persian before Turkic
- The Mongols (Genghis Khan + Tamerlane) killed ~95 million and the region never recovered
- Tamerlane's destruction is argued to be the tipping point for Europe's rise
- Today: "-stans" are isolated dictatorships; Xinjiang is under Chinese colonial control
- The region's potential for future importance remains, particularly if a new ideological movement emerges
