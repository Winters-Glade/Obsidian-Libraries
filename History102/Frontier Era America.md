# Frontier Era America

## Overview

The Frontier Era (roughly 1803-1890) was the period in which the United States expanded across the North American continent, from the Appalachian Mountains to the Pacific Ocean. This westward movement — propelled by ideology, economics, demographic pressure, and government policy — reshaped the landscape, displaced Native peoples, created new states and communities, and forged an American identity centered on rugged individualism, opportunity, and violence. The video explores the drivers of expansion, the diverse peoples who moved west, the economies they built, and the closing of the frontier that Frederick Jackson Turner argued marked the end of America's first great epoch.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### Drivers of Westward Expansion
- **Manifest Destiny** (1845): belief that Americans were divinely ordained to spread across the continent
- **Land hunger**: cheap or free land (Homestead Act 1862) drew millions of settlers
- **Economic opportunity**: gold rushes (California 1848, Colorado 1858, Klondike 1896), timber, cattle ranching
- **Demographic pressure**: population growth in Eastern states pushed settlement westward
- **Government policy**: railroad land grants, military campaigns against Native Americans, territorial governance
- **Transportation revolution**: canals (Erie Canal 1825), railroads (Transcontinental Railroad 1869)

### Peoples of the Frontier
- **White settlers**: homesteaders from Eastern states and European immigrants (Irish, German, Scandinavian)
- **Native Americans**: forcibly displaced, resisting encroachment, confined to reservations
- **African Americans**: exodusters (1879) migrating from the Jim Crow South to Kansas and the Great Plains
- **Hispanic peoples**: Californios, Tejanos, Nuevomexicanos — communities with centuries-old land claims, often dispossessed
- **Chinese immigrants**: built railroads, worked in mining, faced virulent racism (Chinese Exclusion Act 1882)
- **Mormons**: religious persecution drove them to Utah (Great Basin settlement 1847)

### Frontier Economies
- **Fur trade**: mountain men, trading posts — the earliest frontier economy
- **Mining frontiers**: gold and silver rushes — boomtowns, lawlessness, environmental destruction
- **Cattle kingdom**: open-range ranching from Texas to the Dakotas; cattle drives (Chisholm Trail)
- **Homestead farming**: 160-acre grants under the Homestead Act; sod houses, wheat farming, barbed wire
- **Timber industry**: logging in the Pacific Northwest and Great Lakes regions
- **Railroads**: the transcontinental railroad and its subsidiaries shaped settlement patterns; land grants to rail companies

### The Frontier as Myth and Reality
- **Myth**: rugged individualism, self-reliance, the "cowboy" as heroic figure; lawless Wild West
- **Reality**: heavy reliance on federal government (land grants, military protection, subsidies)
- **Violence**: racial violence against Native Americans, African Americans, Chinese; vigilante justice; range wars (Johnson County War)
- **Environmental transformation**: bison near-extinction (from 30 million to fewer than 1,000), deforestation, soil depletion
- **Gender on the Frontier**: women often gained expanded roles but still faced patriarchal constraints; Wyoming first to grant women's suffrage (1869)

### Closing the Frontier
- **Census of 1890**: announced no clear frontier line remained
- **Frederick Jackson Turner's Frontier Thesis** (1893): the frontier shaped American democracy, individualism, and nationalism; its closing raised anxieties about American character
- **"Safety valve" theory**: frontier provided an outlet for social discontent; its closure would intensify class conflict
- **Critique of Turner**: ignored the dispossession of Native Americans, the role of women, ethnic diversity, and the environmental cost

## Important Figures

- **Meriwether Lewis** (1774-1809) and **William Clark** (1770-1838): explorers of the Louisiana Purchase
- **Andrew Jackson** (1767-1845): president who aggressively pursued Indian removal and expansion
- **John C. Frémont** (1813-1890): explorer, military officer, "The Pathfinder"
- **Stephen F. Austin** (1793-1836): "Father of Texas"; brought American settlers to Mexican Texas
- **Brigham Young** (1801-1877): Mormon leader who led migration to Utah
- **John Sutter** (1803-1880): his mill sparked the California Gold Rush
- **Calamity Jane** (1852-1903): frontier woman, scout, storyteller
- **Billy the Kid** (1859-1881): outlaw, symbol of frontier violence
- **Wyatt Earp** (1848-1929): lawman, gunfight at the OK Corral
- **Sitting Bull** (1831-1890): Lakota leader of resistance
- **Chief Joseph** (1840-1904): Nez Perce leader
- **Frederick Jackson Turner** (1861-1932): historian who defined the Frontier Thesis
- **John Wesley Powell** (1834-1902): explorer, scientist, argued for sustainable Western development
- **Leland Stanford** (1824-1893): railroad magnate, Central Pacific, governor of California
