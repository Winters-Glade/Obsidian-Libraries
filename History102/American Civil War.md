# American Civil War

## Overview

The American Civil War (1861-1865) was the bloodiest conflict in American history and the defining crisis of the American republic. Fought between the industrializing Northern states (Union) and the slaveholding Southern states (Confederacy), the war determined whether the United States would remain a unified nation and whether slavery would continue to exist within its borders. The video examines the deep structural causes — economic, social, and political — that made war inevitable, the major campaigns and turning points, and the war's lasting consequences for American identity, race relations, and federal power.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### Causes of the War
- **Slavery as root cause**: the moral, economic, and political conflict over slavery drove all other tensions
- **Economic divergence**: industrializing North (wages, free labor, tariffs) vs. agrarian South (cotton, enslaved labor, free trade)
- **Territorial expansion**: conflict over whether new Western territories would be slave or free (Missouri Compromise 1820, Compromise of 1850, Kansas-Nebraska Act 1854)
- **Political breakdown**: collapse of the Second Party System, rise of the Republican Party (1854)
- **Dred Scott decision** (1857): declared Black people could not be citizens, struck down limits on slavery's expansion
- **John Brown's Raid** (1859): radical abolitionist attack on Harpers Ferry polarized opinion
- **Election of 1860**: Lincoln's victory (without a single Southern electoral vote) triggered secession
- **States' rights**: Southern argument that states had the right to nullify federal law and secede

### The Two Sides
- **Union advantages**: larger population (22M vs. 9M, including 3.5M enslaved), industrial capacity, railroad network, navy, established government
- **Confederate advantages**: defensive war, superior cavalry and military leadership, cotton diplomacy (hoped Britain/France would intervene)
- **Leadership**: Abraham Lincoln vs. Jefferson Davis; Ulysses S. Grant vs. Robert E. Lee

### Major Phases of the War
- **1861-1862**: Early Confederate victories (First Bull Run, Seven Days Battles, Second Bull Run); Union naval blockade established
- **1863**: Turning point — Emancipation Proclamation (Jan 1), Vicksburg (July 4), Gettysburg (July 1-3)
- **1864-1865**: Total war — Sherman's March to the Sea, Grant's Overland Campaign, Appomattox surrender (April 9, 1865)
- **Naval warfare**: blockade, ironclads (*Monitor* vs. *Merrimack*), commerce raiders

### Emancipation and the Meaning of the War
- Lincoln initially framed the war as preserving the Union, not ending slavery
- Emancipation Proclamation (1863) freed slaves only in rebel states — a military and diplomatic calculation
- 13th Amendment (1865) abolished slavery throughout the United States
- 200,000 Black soldiers served in Union army — crucial to Northern victory
- The war transformed from a war for Union into a war for freedom

### Reconstruction and Aftermath
- **Presidential Reconstruction** (Lincoln/Johnson): lenient, rapid reintegration of Southern states
- **Congressional Reconstruction** (Radical Republicans): military occupation, civil rights, 14th and 15th Amendments
- **"Lost Cause" mythology**: post-war Southern narrative romanticizing the Confederacy and minimizing slavery's role
- **Jim Crow**: segregation, disenfranchisement, sharecropping replaced formal slavery
- The war's unresolved issues — racial equality, federal vs. state power — continue to shape American politics

## Important Figures

- **Abraham Lincoln** (1809-1865): 16th president, preserved the Union, emancipated slaves, assassinated
- **Jefferson Davis** (1808-1889): President of the Confederacy
- **Robert E. Lee** (1807-1870): Confederate commanding general, post-war symbol of reconciliation
- **Ulysses S. Grant** (1822-1885): Union commanding general, 18th president
- **Frederick Douglass** (1818-1895): escaped slave, abolitionist leader, orator, writer
- **William Tecumseh Sherman** (1820-1891): Union general, pioneered total war strategy
- **Stonewall Jackson** (1824-1863): Confederate general, brilliant tactician, killed at Chancellorsville
- **Harriet Tubman** (1822-1913): abolitionist, Underground Railroad conductor, Union spy
- **John Brown** (1800-1859): radical abolitionist, martyr to the cause
- **Thaddeus Stevens** (1792-1868): Radical Republican leader in Congress, champion of Black equality
- **Clara Barton** (1821-1912): nurse, founded the American Red Cross
