# Victorian Britain

## Overview

The Victorian era (1837-1901) was the longest reign of any British monarch and the period when Britain reached its zenith as a global power. Named after Queen Victoria, this era saw Britain become the "Workshop of the World," the center of the largest empire in history, and a laboratory for modern politics, culture, and social reform. The video explores how Britain navigated the tensions between industrial capitalism, imperial expansion, social reform, and cultural change, and how the foundations of modern British identity were forged during this period.

## Key Themes and Concepts

### The British Empire at Its Peak
- By 1900, the British Empire covered roughly 25% of the world's land surface and population
- **"The sun never sets on the British Empire"** — a reflection of global reach
- Key imperial holdings: India (the "Jewel in the Crown"), Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, Egypt, Singapore, Hong Kong
- Imperial ideology: "White Man's Burden" (Rudyard Kipling), civilizing mission, racial hierarchy
- Economic exploitation: raw materials extraction, captive markets, opium trade with China

### Politics and Reform
- **Gradual democratization**: Reform Acts of 1832, 1867, and 1884 expanded suffrage
- **Gladstone vs. Disraeli**: the great political rivalry — Liberal vs. Conservative visions
- **Chartism** (1838-1857): working-class political movement demanding democratic rights
- **Irish Home Rule**: the defining constitutional crisis of late Victorian politics
- **Municipal reform**: clean water, sewerage, public health acts transformed cities

### Society and Class Structure
- **Rigid class system**: aristocracy (landed gentry), middle class (bourgeoisie), working class (proletariat)
- **"Separate spheres" ideology**: men in public/work, women in domestic/private
- **Respectability**: central Victorian value — thrift, sobriety, piety, self-improvement
- **Urban poverty**: shocking conditions documented by Henry Mayhew, Charles Booth, Jack London
- **Philanthropy**: charitable works by figures like Lord Shaftesbury, Dr. Barnardo
- **Social Darwinism**: survival-of-the-fittest thinking applied to society and empire

### Culture and Intellectual Life
- **Charles Dickens**: novels exposing social injustice (*Oliver Twist*, *Hard Times*)
- **Charles Darwin**: *On the Origin of Species* (1859) challenged religious orthodoxy
- **John Stuart Mill**: *On Liberty* (1859) — foundations of modern liberalism
- **Oxford Movement**: revival of Catholic tradition in Anglicanism
- **Aesthetic movement**: "art for art's sake" — Oscar Wilde, Pre-Raphaelites
- **Empire as spectacle**: Great Exhibition (1851), colonial exhibitions, world's fairs

### Moral Contradictions
- Britain championed abolition of slavery (1833) while maintaining exploitative colonial labor systems
- Championed free trade while imposing opium on China (Opium Wars, 1839-1842, 1856-1860)
- Celebrated domestic virtue while colonial officials engaged in exploitation abroad
- Era of both reform and repression (e.g., response to Indian Rebellion of 1857)

## Important Figures

- **Queen Victoria** (1819-1901): symbol of an era, restored dignity to monarchy
- **William Gladstone** (1809-1898): Liberal prime minister, reformer, "Grand Old Man"
- **Benjamin Disraeli** (1804-1881): Conservative prime minister, imperialist, social reformer
- **Charles Dickens** (1812-1870): novelist who chronicled Victorian society
- **Charles Darwin** (1809-1882): revolutionized science with theory of evolution
- **John Stuart Mill** (1806-1873): philosopher of liberty, utilitarianism, women's rights
- **Florence Nightingale** (1820-1910): pioneered modern nursing (Crimean War)
- **Lord Shaftesbury** (1801-1885): social reformer, factory acts, child labor
- **Oscar Wilde** (1854-1900): playwright, wit, victim of Victorian moral hypocrisy
- **David Livingstone** (1813-1873): missionary-explorer in Africa
- **Cecil Rhodes** (1853-1902): imperialist, diamond magnate, Rhodesia namesake
