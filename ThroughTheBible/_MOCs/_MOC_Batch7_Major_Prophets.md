## Overview

This comprehensive batch covers the Major Prophets of the Old Testament — [[Isaiah]], [[Jeremiah]], [[Lamentations]], [[Ezekiel]], and [[Daniel]] — spanning the history of Judah from the Assyrian threat through the Babylonian exile and beyond to the end-times visions of Daniel.

These prophetic books represent God's sustained communication with His people during the most turbulent centuries of Israel's history. From Isaiah's vision of the Holy One of Israel in the 8th century BC, through Jeremiah's tearful warnings before the fall of Jerusalem, Ezekiel's dramatic visions among the exiles in Babylon, to Daniel's apocalyptic prophecies of Gentile world empires — the Major Prophets form the theological backbone of Old Testament prophetic literature.

The batch also includes two New Testament insertions — a Palm Sunday message from [[Luke]] and a resurrection defense from [[Acts]] — that connect the prophetic themes to their fulfillment in Christ.

## Key Themes Across This Batch

### The Holiness of God
Isaiah's vision of the Lord high and lifted up (Isaiah 6), the consuming fire (Isaiah 33), and Ezekiel's walls of separation all emphasize God's transcendent holiness. The Majors are haunted by the gap between God's purity and human sin — and God's relentless work to bridge it.

### Covenant Faithfulness
Isaiah calls Israel to 'reason together' with God. Jeremiah indicts them for exchanging the fountain of living water for broken cisterns. Ezekiel traces their history as an abandoned infant rescued and adorned. God's covenant loyalty persists through generations of unfaithfulness.

### The Prophetic Calling
The cost of prophetic ministry is on full display: Isaiah's traumatic commissioning, Jeremiah's rejection and imprisonment, Ezekiel's role as watchman among exiles, Daniel's endurance through four empires. Speaking God's truth is a costly vocation.

### Judgment and Restoration
No collection of Scripture is more honest about judgment — yet none is more hopeful about restoration. The exile is real and devastating, but a remnant returns. The valley of dry bones lives again. A new heart and a new spirit are promised. Daniel's 70 weeks point to an ultimate deliverance.

### Messianic Expectation
The Major Prophets are saturated with Messianic hope: Isaiah's Child born (9:6) and Suffering Servant (53), Jeremiah's Righteous Branch (23:5, 33:15), Ezekiel's Shepherd David (34:23), Daniel's Son of Man (7:13) and cut-off Anointed One (9:26). Together they form a composite portrait of the coming Christ.

### The Sovereignty of God Over History
From Isaiah's 'I am God and there is none else' to Daniel's 'the Most High rules in the kingdom of men,' the Major Prophets declare that history is not random. Assyria, Babylon, Persia, Greece, and Rome are all instruments in God's hands. The kingdoms of this world will become the kingdom of our God.

## 📚 Prophetic Literature — Research Context

### The Nature of Israelite Prophecy

Classical prophecy in Israel (8th–5th centuries BC) was a distinct phenomenon in the Ancient Near East, though it shared features with prophetic figures in surrounding cultures (Mari on the Euphrates, Phoenician ecstatics, Egyptian seers). Israel's prophets were not primarily fortune-tellers but **covenant enforcement mediators** — they brought God's word to bear on the nation's faithfulness to the Sinai covenant. Their message follows a consistent pattern: **indictment** (you have broken the covenant), **judgment** (here are the consequences), and **restoration** (but God will not abandon you).

The Major Prophets — designated 'major' not by importance but by the length of their books — represent the full arc of this ministry during the most pivotal centuries of Israel's history.

### Assyrian & Babylonian Period — Timeline

| Period | Dates | Key Events | Prophetic Activity |
|--------|-------|------------|-------------------|
| **Late Assyrian Period** | 745–627 BC | Tiglath-Pileser III expands empire; Fall of Samaria (722 BC); Sennacherib invades Judah (701 BC) | **Isaiah** (740–700 BC) — ministered through the Assyrian crisis, called Judah to trust God over alliances |
| **Assyrian Decline / Babylonian Rise** | 627–605 BC | Ashurbanipal dies (627 BC); Nineveh falls (612 BC); Battle of Carchemish (605 BC) — Babylon supreme | **Jeremiah** (627–586 BC) — called in Josiah's reforms, warned of Babylon as God's instrument of judgment |
| **Babylonian Supremacy** | 605–539 BC | First deportation (605 BC); Second deportation (597 BC); Jerusalem destroyed (586 BC); Third deportation (582 BC) | **Jeremiah** in Jerusalem; **Ezekiel** among exiles by the Chebar (593–571 BC); **Daniel** in Nebuchadnezzar's court |
| **Exile** | 586–538 BC | Temple in ruins; Judah desolate; Royal line in eclipse | **Ezekiel** — visions of dry bones and restored temple; **Daniel** — apocalyptic visions of four world empires |
| **Persian Period Begins** | 539–331 BC | Cyrus conquers Babylon (539 BC); Edict of Cyrus (538 BC) — return permitted | **Daniel** (d. c. 536 BC) — saw transition to Persian rule and received final vision of resurrection |

### Where Each Prophet Fits

**Isaiah (8th century BC)** — The prophet of Jerusalem's aristocracy. His ministry spanned Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah — a period of Assyrian dominance. Isaiah's message is the most theologically comprehensive of any prophet: he unveils the Holy One of Israel, the remnant doctrine, the Messianic King, the Suffering Servant, and the new heavens and new earth. He is quoted more in the New Testament than any other Old Testament book.

**Jeremiah (7th–6th century BC)** — The prophet of Judah's dying decades. Called as a young man in Josiah's 13th year (627 BC), Jeremiah witnessed Josiah's revival, the horrific reigns of Jehoiakim and Zedekiah, and Jerusalem's final destruction. His message is intensely personal — the 'weeping prophet,' whose heart is torn by the judgment he must announce. No prophet reveals more of his inner life.

**Ezekiel (6th century BC)** — The prophet of the exile. Deported to Babylon in 597 BC, Ezekiel ministered to the exiles by the River Chebar. His visions are the most dramatic in Scripture: the chariot-throne of God, the valley of dry bones, the visionary temple. His book is carefully structured: judgment on Jerusalem (1–24), judgment on the nations (25–32), restoration and new temple (33–48). The glory of God — its departure and return — is his central theme.

**Daniel (6th century BC)** — The prophet-statesman. Deported to Babylon in 605 BC, Daniel served Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, Darius, and Cyrus. His book shifts from court narratives (1–6) to apocalyptic visions (7–12). Daniel's prophecies of the four kingdoms (Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, Rome) and the seventy weeks (9:24–27) provide the chronological framework for biblical eschatology.

### The Exile as Theological Watershed

The Babylonian exile (586–538 BC) is the single most important event in Old Testament history after the Exodus. It shattered Israel's foundational assumptions: the land, the Temple, the monarchy — all gone. Everything had to be rethought. Each Major Prophet addresses this crisis:

- **Isaiah** (chapters 40–55) announces comfort and return before it happens, declaring Cyrus by name two centuries before his birth
- **Jeremiah** tells the exiles to settle in Babylon, build houses, plant gardens, and pray for the city's peace — a revolutionary approach to life under pagan rule
- **Ezekiel** shows God's glory departing the Temple (chapters 8–11) and then returning (chapter 43) — God is not confined to Jerusalem
- **Daniel** demonstrates that even in a foreign court, God rules over the kingdoms of men, raising and deposing kings at His pleasure

### Prophetic Genres & Literary Forms

The Major Prophets employ a remarkable variety of literary forms:

- **Covenant lawsuit (rîb)** — God as plaintiff prosecuting Israel for covenant breach (Isaiah 1, Jeremiah 2, Ezekiel 22)
- **Woe oracle** — Pronouncement of doom beginning with 'woe' (Isaiah 5; Habakkuk 2)
- **Extended allegory** — Jerusalem as abandoned infant (Ezekiel 16), faithless bride (Ezekiel 23), vine (Isaiah 5)
- **Apocalyptic** — Symbolic visions of cosmic conflict and the end of history (Daniel 7–12; Ezekiel 38–39)
- **Messianic prophecy** — Direct predictions of the coming Anointed One
- **Lament / funeral dirge** — Structured grieving over national catastrophe (Lamentations; Ezekiel 19)
- **Symbolic action** — Prophets enacting God's message: Jeremiah's yoke (27–28), Ezekiel's siege-diagram (4:1–3)

### Theological Cross-Currents Within the Major Prophets

The Major Prophets frequently echo and develop each other's themes:

- **New Covenant**: Jeremiah 31:31–34 is the fullest expression, but Isaiah 59:21 and Ezekiel 36:26–27 develop the same promise of internal transformation
- **The Shepherd**: Ezekiel 34 (my servant David) builds on Jeremiah 23:1–6 (the Righteous Branch) and Isaiah 40:11 (He shall feed His flock like a shepherd)
- **The Spirit poured out**: Isaiah 44:3, Ezekiel 39:29, and Joel 2:28 share this promise — fulfilled at Pentecost
- **The remnant**: Isaiah's son Shear-Jashub ('a remnant shall return,' 7:3) is developed by Jeremiah (23:3) and Ezekiel (6:8, 14:22)
- **New heart and spirit**: Ezekiel 11:19 and 36:26 are the classic texts, but Jeremiah 24:7 ('I will give them a heart to know me') anticipates the same promise
- **The Day of the Lord**: Isaiah 2:12, Jeremiah 46:10, Ezekiel 30:3, and Daniel 12 all point to this culminating intervention of God

### New Testament Fulfillment

The Major Prophets provide much of the theological vocabulary for the New Testament:

- Jesus reads Isaiah 61:1–2 in the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4:16–21) and declares it fulfilled in Himself
- John the Baptist is identified as the voice crying in the wilderness (Isaiah 40:3; Matthew 11:10)
- The 'abomination of desolation' from Daniel 9:27 and 11:31 is referenced directly by Jesus (Matthew 24:15)
- The New Covenant of Jeremiah 31 is the theological foundation of Hebrews (Hebrews 8:8–12)
- Ezekiel's temple river (47:1–12) finds its counterpart in Revelation 22:1–2 — the river of life
- Daniel's Son of Man (7:13) is Jesus' own preferred self-designation, appearing over 80 times in the Gospels
