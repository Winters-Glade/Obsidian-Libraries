## Overview

This batch covers twenty teachings spanning the Kingdom Era of Israel's history — from the transition from the Judges (Hannah and Samuel) through the United Monarchy under Saul, David, and Solomon, to the Divided Kingdom and the eventual fall of both Israel (722 BC) and Judah (586 BC). The books of Samuel, Kings, and Chronicles trace God's covenant faithfulness through the rise and fall of human kings, the ministry of prophets like Samuel, Nathan, Elijah, and Elisha, and the tragic consequences of idolatry and disobedience.

One New Testament insertion appears at #45 (1 Peter 1:3), addressing the living hope of the resurrection.

## Key Themes Across This Batch

- **The Davidic Covenant** — God's promise of an eternal throne through David's line, ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ as the Son of David (#43, echoed throughout)
- **A Heart After God** — God's search for faithful hearts (Hannah, David) versus the tragedy of those who turn away (Saul, Jeroboam) (#39–42)
- **The Power of Prayer** — From Hannah's pouring out her soul to Jehoshaphat's desperate cry and Jabez's bold request, prayer is the thread that runs through the entire era
- **Grace and Restoration** — Even after grievous failure (David's sin with Bathsheba), God offers a path of repentance and restoration (#44)
- **Prophetic Ministry** — Samuel, Nathan, Ahijah, Elijah, and Elisha serve as God's messengers, confronting kings and calling the nation back to covenant faithfulness
- **The Danger of Idolatry** — From Jeroboam's golden calves to the worship of Baal under Ahab, idolatry is the root sin that destroys both Israel and Judah
- **The Still Small Voice** — God's gentleness with the weary Elijah contrasts with the dramatic fire of Carmel, revealing that God meets us where we are (#48)
- **The Battle is the Lord's** — David and Goliath, Asa's prayer, and Jehoshaphat's praise all teach that victory comes through faith, not human strength
- **National Decline and Judgment** — The slow erosion of covenant faithfulness leads to the fall of Israel (#51) and Judah (#58), with the sobering warning of 'no remedy'
- **The Living Hope** — The NT insertion (#45) anchors the entire story in the resurrection hope that transcends earthly kingdoms

## 📚 Research Notes & Further Reading

### Archaeology & History
- **The Tel Dan Stele** (c. 870 BC) — An Aramaic inscription referencing the "House of David" (Beth David), providing the first extra-biblical evidence for the Davidic dynasty outside the Bible
- **The Mesha Stele (Moabite Stone)** (c. 840 BC) — Moabite inscription mentioning Omri, king of Israel, and the god Chemosh — cross-referencing 2 Kings 3
- **The Siloam Inscription** (c. 701 BC) — Describes Hezekiah's tunnel, built during Assyrian threat (2 Kings 20:20, 2 Chronicles 32:30)
- **The Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III** (c. 827 BC) — Depicts Jehu of Israel (or his envoy) paying tribute — the only known image of a biblical Israelite king
- **The Lachish Reliefs** (c. 700 BC) — Assyrian reliefs from Sennacherib's palace depicting the siege of Lachish (2 Kings 18-19, 2 Chronicles 32)
- **The Babylonian Chronicles** (c. 600-500 BC) — Cuneiform tablets recording the fall of Jerusalem (597 BC and 586 BC), corroborating 2 Kings 24-25 and 2 Chronicles 36
- **The Cyrus Cylinder** (c. 539 BC) — Proclamation allowing exiled peoples to return home, paralleling Ezra 1:1-4 and 2 Chronicles 36:22-23
