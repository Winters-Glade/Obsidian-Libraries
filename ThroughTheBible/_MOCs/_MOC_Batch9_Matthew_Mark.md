# Matthew & Mark — Map of Content (Batch 9)

> **T2000 Topical Series** — Pastor Chuck Smith  
> Batch 9: Matthew (13 lectures) + Mark (8 lectures)  
> Playlist Index: 145–165

## Overview

This batch covers thirteen topical lectures from the Gospel of [[Matthew]] and eight from the Gospel of [[Mark]], spanning the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus' parables and healings, His journey to the cross, and the crucifixion itself. Together these 21 teachings trace the life and ministry of Christ from His ethical teaching on the mountain to the cry of dereliction from the cross.

Matthew presents Jesus as the promised King and Messiah, the Son of David who fulfills the Old Testament. Mark presents Jesus as the suffering Servant — action-oriented, fast-paced, and focused on the passion narrative. This batch captures both perspectives: the King who teaches with authority and the Servant who suffers in our place.

## Key Themes Across This Batch

- **The Sermon on the Mount** — Three lectures cover Matthew 5-7 (Salt, Prayer, Sure Foundation), establishing the ethics of the Kingdom through the teaching of the King
- **Faith and Trust** — Multiple lectures explore faith as the essential response to Christ: the withered hand healed, the woman's touch, the futility of worry
- **The Person of Christ** — Jesus is presented as greater than Solomon, the Lord of David, and the One whose identity demands a decision
- **The Cost of Discipleship** — Following Jesus requires self-denial, surrender of possessions, and taking up the cross
- **The Danger of Riches** — The rich young ruler and the camel through the eye of the needle warn of spiritual peril in wealth
- **Prayer and Dependence** — The Lord's Prayer, Gethsemane, and the call to watchfulness emphasize dependence on God
- **The Atonement** — The final lectures trace Jesus from Gethsemane to the cross, with the cry of dereliction revealing the cost of our redemption
- **Failure and Restoration** — Peter's bitter defeat shows that failure is not final when it leads to genuine repentance

## Research Context — Gospel Composition

### The Gospel of Matthew
Traditionally attributed to the apostle Matthew (Levi), a former tax collector who left his profession to follow Christ. Early church tradition (Papias, c. AD 110; Irenaeus, c. AD 180) places Matthew's composition in the late first century (c. AD 80–90), though some conservative scholars date it to the 50s–60s. The Gospel's frequent references to the Temple's destruction (Matthew 22:7, 24:1–2) suggest a post-AD 70 date for the final form. Matthew was likely composed in Antioch of Syria, a major early Christian center with a mixed Jewish-Gentile congregation. The Gospel presents Jesus as the new Moses (five discourses mirroring the five books of the Torah), emphasizes His fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy (some 60+ direct quotations), and addresses a Jewish-Christian audience navigating the aftermath of the Temple's destruction and the widening separation from mainstream Judaism.

### The Gospel of Mark
Widely considered the earliest Gospel, Mark was likely composed around AD 60–70, possibly in Rome. Early tradition (Papias, Justin Martyr, Irenaeus) identifies the author as John Mark, the companion of Peter and Paul (Acts 12:12, 1 Peter 5:13). Mark's Gospel is essentially the memoirs of Peter, explaining its vivid eyewitness detail and fast-paced narrative (the Greek _euthys_, 'immediately,' appears 41 times). Mark writes for a predominantly Gentile, Roman audience: he explains Jewish customs (7:3–4), uses Latin loanwords (_legion_, _centurion_, _praetorium_), and emphasizes Jesus as the suffering Son of God. The Gospel reflects the experience of a persecuted church — possibly under Nero's persecution (AD 64–68) — and presents the cross, not earthly power, as the path to glory. Matthew and Luke both used Mark as a primary source (the Synoptic relationship), making Mark foundational to the Gospel tradition.

### 1st Century Judea — Key Context
Judea and Galilee in the first century were provinces of the Roman Empire under a layered governance structure. Galilee was ruled by Herod Antipas (tetrarch, 4 BC–AD 39), a Roman client king who built Sepphoris and Tiberias. Judea was governed directly by Roman prefects (Pontius Pilate, AD 26–36, being the fifth). The Jewish people were divided among several competing religious sects: Pharisees (scribal tradition and oral law), Sadducees (Temple establishment and priestly aristocracy), Essenes (ascetic communities like Qumran), and Zealots (armed resistance). Messianic expectation was widespread, fueled by Roman oppression and prophetic promises of a deliverer. Economic life was dominated by subsistence agriculture — grain, olives, and grapes — with a crushing tax burden from Rome, the Temple establishment, and local rulers. This was the world into which Jesus stepped — a world of political tension, religious fervor, and desperate hope for redemption.
