# 201 On to Rome — Acts 23:11

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#201](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BFx9kkczySo)

## Overview

After Paul's arrest in Jerusalem, a plot to kill him is foiled by his nephew. Transferred to Caesarea for safety, Paul appears before the Sanhedrin, causing division between Pharisees and Sadducees over the resurrection. That night, the Lord stands by Paul and reassures him: 'Be of good cheer, Paul; for as you have testified for Me in Jerusalem, so you must also bear witness at Rome.' This divine promise frames the remainder of Acts.

## 📜 Historical Background

The setting of Acts 23:11 is the Antonia Fortress, the Roman military barracks adjacent to the Temple Mount in Jerusalem where Paul had been rescued from a mob by the Roman commander Claudius Lysias (Acts 21:31-36). Paul had just endured a chaotic appearance before the Sanhedrin, dividing the Pharisees and Sadducees over the resurrection. A plot by more than forty Jewish zealots to assassinate Paul was foiled by his nephew, prompting the commander to transfer Paul under heavy guard to Caesarea Maritima. The Lord's appearance 'the following night' demonstrates God's timing — coming at the darkest moment. The promise 'you must also bear witness at Rome' was God's sovereign declaration that Paul's journey to the imperial capital was already ordained. Rome represented the center of the known world, and Paul's testimony there would establish the gospel in the heart of the empire.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The Lord's reassurance to Paul echoes Jesus' promise to all His disciples: 'I am with you always, even to the end of the age' (Matthew 28:20). Paul's encouragement to 'be of good cheer' (tharsei) is the same word Jesus used in the storm-tossed boat (Matthew 14:27) and in the face of tribulation (John 16:33). God's sovereign plan to bring Paul to Rome — despite Jewish plots, Roman legal delays, and a deadly sea voyage — demonstrates that no human opposition can thwart divine purposes. Paul's letter to the Romans (Romans 1:8-15, 15:22-29) expresses his long-standing desire to visit them, a desire God fulfills through circumstances Paul never anticipated. The angelic encouragement in Acts 27:24 reaffirms this same promise during the storm.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith found deep comfort in this passage, teaching that God's encouragement often comes at the darkest hour — not before it, but in the midst of it. The Lord did not remove Paul's chains but stood beside him in them. Smith emphasized that God's promises are sure, even when circumstances seem to contradict them: Paul was in chains, yet God promised he would testify in Rome. The lesson for believers is that circumstances do not determine God's faithfulness — His word does. Smith also noted the significance of Paul's 'on to Rome' mindset: once God gives a vision, the believer must press toward it regardless of obstacles. The very opposition that seemed to block Paul's path was the vehicle God used to get him there.
