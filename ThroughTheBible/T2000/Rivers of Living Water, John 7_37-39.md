## Summary

John 7:37-39 records Jesus' invitation on the last day of the Feast of Tabernacles: 'If any man thirst, let him come unto Me and drink.' He promised that rivers of living water would flow from the believer's heart — referring to the Holy Spirit. Pastor Chuck examines the Holy Spirit's work in the life of the believer and the picture of spiritual thirst satisfied by Christ alone.

## 📖 Research Context

The Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot) commemorated Israel's wilderness wandering and looked forward to the Messianic age. The water-pouring ceremony, drawn from Isaiah 12:3 ('With joy you will draw water from the wells of salvation'), involved a priest processing from the Pool of Siloam to the Temple while the people recited the Hallel (Psalm 113-118). The water was poured out at the altar as the people sang. On the seventh and greatest day — when the ceremony reached its climax — Jesus stood and cried out His invitation. The phrase 'If any man thirst' echoes Isaiah 55:1 ('Ho, everyone who thirsts, come to the waters'). John's editorial note clarifies that 'the Spirit was not yet given because Jesus was not yet glorified' — a crucial theological link: the gift of the Spirit depends on the completed work of Christ (His death, resurrection, and ascension). The water that 'flows from the believer's belly' (koilias) suggests a spring that does not merely pool but actively flows outward — the Spirit's work is both for the believer and through the believer.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Isaiah 12:3 (drawing water from the wells of salvation), Isaiah 44:3 (I will pour water on the thirsty), Ezekiel 47:1-12 (the river flowing from the Temple — deeper and deeper), Joel 2:28-29 (I will pour out My Spirit on all flesh).

## 📜 Historical Background

The Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot) was one of three major pilgrim feasts. Each day, a priest would draw water from the Pool of Siloam and pour it out at the altar, accompanied by prayers for rain. This ceremony looked back to God providing water in the wilderness and forward to the Messianic age when living waters would flow from Jerusalem (Zechariah 14:8). On the seventh and greatest day of the feast, Jesus stood and made His dramatic announcement. The 'living water' is identified by John as the Holy Spirit, who would be given after Jesus was glorified.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

John 4:10-14 — the woman at the well: the water I give will become a spring of water welling up to eternal life. Acts 2:1-4 — Pentecost, the fulfillment: they were all filled with the Holy Spirit. Ezekiel 47 — waters flowing from the Temple. Joel 2:28-29 — I will pour out My Spirit on all flesh. Revelation 22:1-2 — the river of the water of life, clear as crystal, flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb. Romans 5:5 — the love of God is shed abroad in our hearts by the Holy Spirit.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the thirst Jesus speaks of is universal. Everyone is thirsty — for meaning, love, peace, purpose. We try to satisfy that thirst with many things, but only Jesus gives living water. And the water He gives doesn't just satisfy us — it flows through us to others. The Holy Spirit is not just for our enjoyment but for ministry to a thirsty world. The question is: are we drinking, and are we letting the water flow?
