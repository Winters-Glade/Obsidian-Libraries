## Summary

[[Ezekiel 14_1-6.md]] exposes the problem of hidden [[idolatry]] — elders of Israel coming to inquire of the Lord while cherishing idols in their hearts. Pastor Chuck focuses on God's penetrating question: why would someone seek God while clinging to the very things that separate them from Him? The call is to repent and turn away from idols.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[Ezekiel 14.md]] addresses a specific situation: exiled elders come to Ezekiel seeking a word from the Lord, yet they harbor idols in their hearts. God exposes this contradiction. The chapter reflects the spiritual condition of the exiles — even though they had been physically removed from the idolatrous environment of Jerusalem, idols remained entrenched in their hearts. The Hebrew term for 'idols' (gillulim) is a derogatory term meaning 'dung pellets,' reflecting God's contempt for false worship. This passage is part of Ezekiel's larger indictment of Israel's spiritual [[Prophecy|adultery]] that led to the exile. The call to repentance is a call to [[Restoration]] — turning from [[idolatry]] back to covenant [[faithfulness]].

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[James 1_8]] describes the 'double-minded man' who is unstable in all his ways. Jesus' teaching in [[_Bible/Matthew/Matthew 6_24.md]] — 'No man can serve two masters' — directly addresses the incompatibility of clinging to idols while seeking God. John's first epistle closes with the simple but profound command: 'Little children, keep yourselves from idols' ([[1 John]]). Paul's call in [[2 Corinthians 6_14.md]]-7:1 urges believers to cleanse themselves from all defilement.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often warned that the most dangerous idols are not statues in a shrine but the things we cherish in our hearts — money, relationships, reputation, comfort, or even ministry itself. The elders came to Ezekiel looking spiritual, but God saw their hearts. True repentance means not just changing behavior but uprooting the idols we secretly hold dear.
