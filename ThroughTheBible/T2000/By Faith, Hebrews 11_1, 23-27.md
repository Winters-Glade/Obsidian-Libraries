## Summary

Faith is defined as 'the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen.' The passage focuses on Moses: by faith he was hidden by his parents, by faith he chose to suffer with God's people rather than enjoy the pleasures of sin for a season, and by faith he forsook Egypt, seeing Him who is invisible. Moses' choices demonstrate that faith sees beyond the immediate to the eternal.

## 📜 Historical Background

Hebrews 11 is the great 'Faith Chapter' of the Bible, a carefully constructed hall of fame of those who trusted God against all odds. The chapter is built as a rhetorical chain (catena) of examples, each introduced by the refrain 'By faith' (pistei), forming one of the most powerful literary structures in the New Testament. The definition of faith in verse 1 uses two Greek terms: hypostasis ('substance' or 'assurance') and elegchos ('evidence' or 'conviction'). Hypostasis was used in legal documents for the title deed—faith is the title deed of things hoped for. Elegchos carries the sense of proving or demonstrating—faith brings invisible realities into tangible certainty.

Moses is one of the most developed examples in the chapter, spanning verses 23-28. His life is presented in three faith decisions: (1) his parents' faith in hiding him (v. 23), defying Pharaoh's edict; (2) his own choice to identify with God's people rather than enjoy Egypt's pleasures (vv. 24-26); and (3) his departure from Egypt, 'fearing not the wrath of the king' (v. 27). The phrase 'the reproach of Christ' (v. 26) is striking—Moses suffered reproach that was ultimately Christ's reproach, because identification with God's people has always meant identification with God's Anointed. Jewish tradition (Philo, Josephus, and the Midrash) elaborates on Moses' choice: as Pharaoh's adopted grandson, he had access to Egyptian wealth, wisdom, and power. The 'treasures of Egypt' were legendary, but Moses made a deliberate, rational choice based on eternal values. The phrase 'seeing Him who is invisible' (v. 27) is the essence of faith—not blindness but a different kind of sight.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul writes in Romans 8:18 that 'the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us.' Jesus taught that those who lose their life for His sake will find it (Matthew 16:25). Stephen, the first martyr, saw Christ standing at God's right hand (Acts 7:55)—seeing the invisible as Moses did. The definition of faith in Hebrews 11:1 is the foundation for understanding the entire Christian life.

The Pentateuchal background of Moses' story (Exodus 2-3) provides the narrative framework. The author of Hebrews interprets the Exodus events typologically: the Passover lamb, the Red Sea crossing, the wilderness journey—all prefigure the greater redemption in Christ. The phrase 'the reproach of Christ' connects to the Suffering Servant of Isaiah 53, who was 'despised and rejected of men.' Paul develops the same theme in 2 Corinthians 4:18: 'We look not at the things which are seen, but at the things which are not seen; for the things which are seen are temporal, but the things which are not seen are eternal.' Peter echoes the faith definition in 1 Peter 1:8: 'Whom having not seen, ye love; in whom, though now ye see Him not, yet believing, ye rejoice with joy unspeakable and full of glory.' This note connects to [[_transcripts/Looking Unto Jesus, Hebrews 12_1-3.md|Looking Unto Jesus (Hebrews 12)]] where the 'cloud of witnesses' from Hebrews 11 surrounds us and Jesus Himself becomes the supreme example of faith. The perseverance theme is central: faith is not a one-time decision but a sustained orientation that sees beyond the present to the eternal reward.

## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** Hebrews 11 is the "Faith Chapter" — a carefully constructed rhetorical chain (*catena*) of OT examples, each introduced by the refrain "By faith" (*pistei*). The Greek definition of faith in verse 1 uses two legal terms: *hypostasis* ("substance/assurance") — used in ancient papyri as a "title deed" — and *elegchos* ("evidence/conviction") — a legal demonstration proving a case. Faith, then, is both the title deed of future realities and the proof of invisible ones. Moses is the most developed example: his three faith decisions (parents' defiance of Pharaoh, his own choice to identify with God's people, his departure from Egypt fearing not the king's wrath) illustrate progressive faith. The phrase "the reproach of Christ" (v. 26) — applied to Moses centuries before Christ's incarnation — is theologically profound: identification with God's people has always meant identification with God's Anointed.

**Cross-Batch Connections:** The Exodus narrative of Moses connects to [[_MOC_Batch2_Exodus_Wilderness|Batch 2 — Exodus & Deuteronomy]] (Exodus 2-3: Moses' birth, flight, and call). The hall of faith touches many earlier batches: the patriarchs from [[_MOC_Batch1_Genesis|Batch 1 — Genesis]] (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph), Moses' parents from the Exodus story ([[_MOC_Batch2_Exodus_Wilderness|Batch 2]]), and the conquest of Jericho from [[_MOC_Batch3_Judges_Ruth|Batch 3 — Joshua & Ruth]]. Paul's words in Romans 8:18 (from [[_MOC_Batch12_Romans_Corinthians|Batch 12]]) — "the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory" — echo Moses' choice to suffer reproach for a greater reward. The "cloud of witnesses" in [[_transcripts/Looking Unto Jesus, Hebrews 12_1-3.md|255 — Looking Unto Jesus]] are the very heroes catalogued here, making chapters 11 and 12 a continuous literary unit.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith stressed that faith is not wishful thinking—it is acting on God's Word as if it were true because it IS true. Moses made a deliberate choice based on what he valued. Every believer faces the same choice: the temporary pleasures of the world or the eternal reward of following Christ. Faith sees what others don't see—it sees God. The 'reproach of Christ' was real for Moses, and it's real for believers today, but the reward far outweighs the cost.
