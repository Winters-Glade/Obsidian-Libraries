The angel explains to John the meaning of the ten horns on the beast: they are ten kings who give their power and authority to the beast, and they make war with the Lamb. But the Lamb will overcome them: 'for He is Lord of lords, and King of kings: and they that are with Him are called, and chosen, and faithful.' Against all the political and military power of the world, the Lamb and His followers prevail.

## 📜 Historical Background

Revelation 17 is the vision of Babylon the Great, the apostate world system that opposes God. The chapter draws heavily on the apocalyptic beast tradition of Daniel 7 — the four beasts from the sea, the little horn, and the judicial scene where the Ancient of Days pronounces judgment. The beast with seven heads and ten horns represents the final world empire in its fullest manifestation. The ten horns are ten kings (δεκα βασιλεῖς) who share power with the beast for a short time (μίαν ὥραν — "one hour," apocalyptic shorthand for a brief, predetermined period). The image of the harlot Babylon (πόρνη) seated on many waters draws on Jeremiah 51:7 and the prophetic condemnation of Babylon/Rome as the center of idolatry and persecution. The wilderness setting (ἔρημος) for this vision is significant — apocalyptic visions are frequently given in desolate places (Ezekiel's river Chebar, John's Patmos). The three descriptions of Christ's followers — called (κλητοί), chosen (ἐκλεκτοί), and faithful (πιστοί) — represent the interplay of God's sovereign election and human responsibility. The Greek term κλητοί (called) echoes Paul's "called according to His purpose" (Romans 8:28); ἐκλεκτοί (chosen) recalls Jesus' words "many are called, but few are chosen" (Matthew 22:14); πιστοί (faithful) is the human response that validates divine calling. Their war against the Lamb (μετὰ τοῦ ἀρνίου πολεμήσουσιν) seems formidable, but the outcome is never in doubt because the Lamb is "Lord of lords and King of kings" (Κύριος κυρίων καὶ Βασιλεὺς βασιλέων) — a title traced through Deuteronomy 10:17, Psalm 136:2-3, and Daniel 2:47, asserting Yahweh's supremacy over all earthly rulers. Writing under Domitian (c. AD 95), who demanded divine honors as "dominus et deus noster," this title was a direct repudiation of Caesar's pretensions. The apocalyptic "already but not yet" appears: the Lamb already possesses the title (already), but the final battle and decisive victory await the culmination of history (not yet).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

This title "Lord of lords and King of kings" appears earlier in Revelation 19:16 and is drawn from Deuteronomy 10:17 and Daniel 2:47. Paul writes that Christ is "far above all principality, and power, and might, and dominion" (Ephesians 1:21). The battle is described in Revelation 19:11-21 where the Word of God returns with the armies of heaven to defeat the beast. The paradox is that the Lamb wins — the symbol of sacrifice becomes the symbol of supreme power. In the Olivet Discourse, Jesus describes the gathering of the nations for judgment (Matthew 25:31-46), the sheep and the goats — the same final separation depicted here. Paul's "man of lawlessness" (2 Thessalonians 2:3-4) is the beast-like figure who exalts himself against God, the same rebel power described in Revelation 17. Jesus' parable of the ten virgins (Matthew 25:1-13) warns His followers to be faithful (πιστοί) while waiting for the Bridegroom, directly connecting to the threefold description of the Lamb's followers. Paul's teaching on election in Romans 8:28-30 — "whom He called, them He also justified" — echoes the "called" (κλητοί) of Revelation 17:14. Peter exhorts believers to be "faithful unto death" (1 Peter 1:5-9), showing that faithfulness is the evidence of divine calling. The image of the harlot Babylon contrasts with the Bride of Christ (Revelation 19:7-9; 21:2), the two women representing two world systems — the apostate and the redeemed. From a futurist perspective (Chuck Smith's framework), Revelation 17 describes the final one-world religious and political system that opposes God in the last days, destroyed at Christ's return.

## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** The threefold description of Christ's followers — "called" (*klētoi*), "chosen" (*eklektoi*), and "faithful" (*pistoi*) — represents the interplay of divine sovereignty and human responsibility. *Klētoi* echoes Paul's "called according to His purpose" (Romans 8:28); *eklektoi* recalls Jesus' words "many are called, but few are chosen" (Matthew 22:14); *pistoi* (faithful) is the human response that validates divine calling. The war against the Lamb (*meta tou arniou polemesousin*) seems formidable — ten kings uniting their power — but the outcome is never in doubt because the Lamb is "Lord of lords and King of kings" (*Kyrios kyriōn kai Basileus basileōn*), a title asserting Yahweh's supremacy over all earthly rulers. Writing under Domitian (c. AD 95), who demanded divine honors as *dominus et deus noster*, this title was a direct repudiation of Caesar's pretensions.

**Apocalyptic Literature Context:** Revelation 17 draws on the apocalyptic beast tradition of Daniel 7 — the four beasts from the sea, the little horn, and the judicial scene where the Ancient of Days pronounces judgment. The harlot Babylon (*porne*) seated on many waters draws on Jeremiah 51:7 and the prophetic tradition of Babylon/Rome as the center of idolatry and persecution. The wilderness setting (*erēmos*) for this vision is significant — apocalyptic visions are frequently given in desolate places (Ezekiel's river Chebar, John's Patmos). The "one hour" of the ten kings' power (*mian hōran*) is apocalyptic shorthand for a brief, predetermined period under God's sovereign control.

**Cross-Batch Connections:** The beast tradition of Daniel 7 ([[_MOC_Batch7_Major_Prophets|Batch 7 — Daniel & Minor Prophets]]) is the primary OT source for Revelation's vision of world empires. The title "Lord of lords" originates in Deuteronomy 10:17 ([[_MOC_Batch2_Exodus_Wilderness|Batch 2]]) and Psalm 136:2-3 ([[_MOC_Batch6_Wisdom_Literature|Batch 6]]). Paul's declaration that Christ is "far above all principality, and power, and might" (Ephesians 1:21, from [[_MOC_Batch12_Romans_Corinthians|Batch 12]]) echoes the same supremacy. The distinction between the harlot Babylon and the Bride of Christ (Revelation 19:7-9; 21:2) continues the prophetic contrast between the faithful and apostate communities that runs through the Minor Prophets (Hosea's marriage metaphor, Gomer as unfaithful Israel — [[_MOC_Batch7_Major_Prophets|Batch 7]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that the outcome of history is not in question. The world may seem to be spiraling out of control, but Christ is Lord of lords and King of kings. Those who stand with Him are described in a threefold way: called by the Father, chosen in Christ before the foundation of the world, and faithful in their walk. Faithfulness is the evidence of calling and election. When the world aligns against God, the believer's confidence is not in political power but in the conquering Lamb.
