## Summary

Mark 5:21-34 weaves together two interlocking stories of healing and faith. While Jesus is on His way to heal Jairus' dying daughter, a woman who had suffered from an issue of blood for twelve years reaches out in the crowd and touches the hem of His garment. Immediately she is healed. Jesus stops, feeling that 'power had gone out of Him,' and calls her forward — not to rebuke her but to affirm her faith. Pastor Chuck explores the woman's desperate, persistent faith and Jesus' tender response, teaching that even a touch of faith — imperfect and fearful — is met by the power and grace of Christ.

## 📜 Historical Background

The woman's condition — a constant flow of blood for twelve years — would have made her ceremonially unclean according to Leviticus 15:25-27. This meant she was excluded from worship, social contact, and even physical touch. She had spent all her money on physicians and only grew worse. Her decision to touch Jesus' garment in the crowd was an act of desperate faith — technically violating the purity laws, as her touch would make Him unclean, but demonstrating her belief that His power exceeded her uncleanness. The crowd pressing around Jesus was large, but only this woman's touch drew power from Him — faith made the difference between physical contact and spiritual connection.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Gospel of Mark:** The woman's condition carried devastating social and economic consequences. According to Jewish purity laws (Leviticus 15:25–27), a woman with a discharge of blood was considered unclean, and anyone who touched her was also unclean until evening. This meant twelve years of social isolation — unable to participate in worship at the synagogue or Temple, unable to engage in normal physical contact with family, and likely unable to marry or maintain a household. That she had 'spent all her money on physicians' (Mark 5:26) reflects the medical reality of first-century Galilee: physicians were often ineffective, and treatment could be expensive. Mark's Gospel, written for a persecuted Roman audience (likely under Nero's reign, c. AD 64–68), presents this story as a powerful example of faith that persists despite all obstacles — a message of hope for believers facing their own desperate circumstances. The interlocking structure of this passage (the story of Jairus's daughter frames the woman's healing) is a hallmark of Mark's sophisticated narrative technique, demonstrating that the Gospel, though written in simple Greek, is a carefully crafted literary work.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Matthew 9:18-26 and Luke 8:40-56 record parallel accounts. The writer of Hebrews describes faith as 'the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen' (Hebrews 11:1) and notes that 'without faith it is impossible to please Him' (Hebrews 11:6). Paul writes that the righteous shall live by faith (Romans 1:17). The woman's healing 'immediately' (Mark 5:29) demonstrates the instantaneous nature of divine power — a contrast to her twelve years of suffering. Jesus' call for her to come forward teaches that faith is not just private but meant to be confessed publicly.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often used this story to encourage those who feel unworthy or excluded to reach out to Jesus in faith anyway. The woman had every reason to believe she was disqualified — her condition, her uncleanness, her lack of access to the Teacher. But her desperation drove her past every barrier. Jesus did not rebuke her but affirmed her: 'Daughter, thy faith hath made thee whole.' He transformed her from an unclean outcast into a beloved child. No matter how long we have suffered or how hopeless our situation seems, the touch of faith still releases His power.
