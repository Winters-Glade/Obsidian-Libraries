## Summary

Luke 19:1-10 recounts the story of Zacchaeus, the chief tax collector who climbed a sycamore tree to see Jesus. Jesus called him down and went to his house, causing the crowd to murmur that He was going to be a guest of a sinner. Zacchaeus repented, gave half his goods to the poor, and restored fourfold what he had taken unjustly. Pastor Chuck highlights the purpose of Christ's coming: to seek and save the lost.

## 📖 Research Context

This story is the last conversion narrative in Luke's Gospel before the Passion — and it epitomizes Luke's theme of Jesus seeking the lost. Jericho was a city of priests and Levites (many served at the nearby Temple), making Zacchaeus' presence as a tax collector especially scandalous. As a 'chief tax collector' (architelōnēs), Zacchaeus was at the top of a despised profession — he had purchased the right to collect taxes for Rome and employed others beneath him. His climbing a sycamore tree was beneath his dignity — but desperation to see Jesus overcame social pride. Jesus' statement 'Today salvation has come to this house' uses the Greek sōtēria — salvation in its fullest sense. The declaration 'the Son of Man came to seek and to save that which was lost' echoes Ezekiel 34:16, where God Himself promises to seek His scattered sheep.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Ezekiel 34:11-16 (I will seek My sheep and deliver them), Isaiah 1:18 (though your sins are like scarlet), Psalm 51:10-12 (restore to me the joy of Your salvation).

## 📜 Historical Background

Jericho was a wealthy city, a center of trade and agriculture. As a chief tax collector, Zacchaeus was likely wealthy and deeply despised. Tax collectors could collect more than the required amount and keep the surplus. Zacchaeus was short in stature, so he climbed a sycamore tree — a tree with low, spreading branches. Jesus' willingness to go to the house of a tax collector was scandalous to the religious establishment. Zacchaeus' response — giving half to the poor and restoring fourfold — exceeded the requirements of the Law (which required restitution plus one-fifth).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Ezekiel 34:11-16 — God seeks His scattered sheep. Luke 15:4-7 — the parable of the lost sheep. Matthew 18:11 — the Son of Man has come to save that which was lost. Romans 5:8 — while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us. 1 Timothy 1:15 — Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners. Revelation 3:20 — behold, I stand at the door and knock; if anyone hears My voice and opens the door, I will come in.

Romans 3:10-12 — there is none righteous, no not one; there is none who seeks after God. Romans 10:8-13 — the word is near you, in your mouth and in your heart; whoever calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that salvation always produces transformation. When Jesus comes into a life, things change. Zacchaeus didn't just feel sorry — he made restitution. The proof of genuine repentance is not emotional response but changed behavior. Jesus came not to condemn the world but to save it, and He is still seeking those who are willing to climb out of their comfort zone to see Him.
