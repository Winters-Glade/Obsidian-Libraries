# 246 The Holy Bible — 2 Timothy 3:16-17

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#246](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l75liHSSROw)

## Overview

"All scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness: That the man of God may be perfect, throughly furnished unto all good works." Paul's definitive statement on the nature and purpose of Scripture. The Bible is God-breathed (theopneustos), fully authoritative, and comprehensively useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training in righteousness.

This is the most important single verse on the doctrine of Scripture in the New Testament. "Inspiration of God" translates theopneustos — literally "God-breathed." Scripture is not merely inspired in the sense that Shakespeare or Mozart were inspired; it is breathed out by God Himself, carrying His authority. Paul outlines four purposes of Scripture: (1) **Doctrine** — teaching what is true; (2) **Reproof** — showing where we have strayed; (3) **Correction** — restoring us to the right path; (4) **Instruction in righteousness** — training us to live rightly. The goal of this comprehensive work is that "the man of God may be perfect" (artios — complete, competent, fully equipped) and "thoroughly furnished" (exērtismenos — fully outfitted, like a ship fully rigged for voyage) for every good work.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Scripture | All Scripture — the Old Testament Scriptures that Timothy knew from childhood, and by extension the apostolic writings — carries divine authority |
| Inspiration of the Bible | Theopneustos — God-breathed — is the unique quality of Scripture, distinguishing it from all other writings |
| Doctrine | Scripture's first purpose is to teach truth — it is the source and standard of all Christian doctrine |
| Teaching | Scripture provides reproof (elenchos — conviction), correction (epanorthōsis — restoration to uprightness), and instruction (paideia — training, discipline) |
| Spiritual Growth | The ultimate purpose of Scripture is not information but transformation — equipping the believer for every good work |

## Background & Context

Paul writes from prison, near death, to his beloved spiritual son. Chapter 3 describes the "perilous times" of the last days — people who are lovers of self, lovers of money, boastful, proud, blasphemous, unholy, having a form of godliness but denying its power. Against this dark backdrop, Paul sets the light of Scripture. He reminds Timothy of his heritage: from childhood he has known the Holy Scriptures (2 Timothy 3:15), which are able to make him "wise unto salvation through faith which is in Christ Jesus." The Scriptures Timothy learned from his grandmother Lois and mother Eunice (2 Timothy 1:5) were the Hebrew Scriptures (the Old Testament). But by the time Paul wrote, the apostolic writings were already being recognized as Scripture alongside the Old Testament (2 Peter 3:15-16). This verse has been the foundational text for the doctrine of biblical inspiration throughout church history.
