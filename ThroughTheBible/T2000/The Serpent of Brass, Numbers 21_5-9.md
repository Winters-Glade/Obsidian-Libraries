## Summary

Numbers 21:5-9 recounts the sin of Israel, the judgment of fiery serpents, and God's provision of healing through the bronze serpent lifted on a pole. Pastor Chuck explores this remarkable type of salvation: look and live — the simplest, most universal gospel in the Old Testament.

## 📜 Historical Background

Numbers 21 records Israel's journey around Edom. The people grew impatient and spoke against God and Moses, asking why they had been brought out of Egypt to die in the wilderness. As judgment, the Lord sent 'fiery serpents' among them — the Hebrew 'seraphim' suggests burning or venomous serpents. Many died. When the people repented, God instructed Moses to make a bronze serpent (nachash nechoshet) and set it on a pole. Whoever was bitten could look at the bronze serpent and live. This simple act of faith — looking — became the means of healing. The bronze serpent was preserved for centuries until Hezekiah destroyed it when it became an object of idolatry (2 Kings 18:4).

**Hebrew Word Studies:**
- *Saraph* (שָׂרָף) — "fiery" or "burning" (Numbers 21:6, 8). The same word is used for the seraphim in Isaiah 6:2, the angelic beings whose name means "burning ones." The serpent bites produced a burning inflammation, a physical correlate of the burning wrath of God against sin. The serpents were instruments of divine judgment — each bite was a microcosm of the fire of God.
- *Nachash* (נָחָשׁ) — "serpent" — the common Hebrew word for serpent, first appearing in Genesis 3:1. The serpent was the instrument of humanity's original fall; now a serpent becomes the instrument of healing — a profound paradoxical reversal.
- *Nechoshet* (נְחֹשֶׁת) — "bronze" or "brass." In the Hebrew text, the phrase *nachash nechoshet* (נְחַשׁ נְחֹשֶׁת) is a striking wordplay — the words for "serpent" and "bronze" sound nearly identical. Bronze was a metal associated with judgment in the tabernacle: the bronze altar (where sin was judged), the bronze laver (where impurity was cleansed). The serpent of bronze was a serpent-shaped judgment, bearing the form of the curse.
- *Nehushtan* (נְחֻשְׁתָּן) — the name given to the bronze serpent by the time of Hezekiah (2 Kings 18:4). The name combines *nachash* (serpent) and *nechoshet* (bronze) — and possibly a dismissive suffix. By Hezekiah's day, the once-holy symbol had become an idol, and the king ground it to powder — an act of thorough reformation.
- *Nes* (נֵס) — "pole" or "standard" (Numbers 21:8-9). The same word is used for a banner or ensign lifted high for all to see. In Isaiah 11:10, the Root of Jesse stands as a *nes* for the peoples. The lifted serpent was a visible, public proclamation of salvation.

**ANE Context:** Serpent imagery was common in the ancient Near East. The Egyptian uraeus (the cobra on Pharaoh's crown) represented divine authority and protection. The Canaanite goddess Asherah was sometimes associated with serpents. In Mesopotamia, the serpent was a symbol of healing and life — the *caduceus* of the god Ningishzida. What makes the biblical account unique is that Israel's healing comes not through a magical serpent but through an act of obedient faith in God's word. The bronze serpent had no intrinsic power; it was the means by which God chose to channel His healing grace. The same pole that displayed the judgment became the instrument of deliverance — a direct foreshadowing of the cross.

**Later History:** The bronze serpent was preserved and kept in Jerusalem until the reign of King Hezekiah (c. 715-686 BC). By then, the Israelites had begun burning incense to it — a practice of superstitious veneration that turned a divinely ordained symbol into an idol. Hezekiah's reform (2 Kings 18:4) included breaking it in pieces, calling it *Nehushtan* — a term of contempt meaning "a mere piece of bronze." This demonstrates the perennial danger of venerating the means of grace rather than the God of grace.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus explicitly identifies the bronze serpent as a type of His own crucifixion in the most well-known verse in Scripture: 'And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have eternal life' (John 3:14-15). This is Jesus's own interpretation of the typology — making it the firmest foundation for any typological reading of the Old Testament.

The parallel is profoundly layered:

1. **The Lifted Up:** As the bronze serpent was lifted on a pole (*nes*), so Christ was lifted up on the cross (and exalted in His ascension — John 12:32: 'And I, if I be lifted up from the earth, will draw all men unto Me').

2. **The Form of Judgment:** The bronze serpent was made in the form (*temunah*) of the judgment-causing serpents. Christ was 'made in the likeness of sinful flesh' (Romans 8:3), and 'He hath made Him to be sin for us, who knew no sin' (2 Corinthians 5:21). The sinless One took the form of the curse — becoming a serpent, as it were — to destroy the work of the serpent.

3. **The Look of Faith:** The condition was simple — look and live. The verb used in the LXX (John 3:15) is *pisteuo* — "believe." The act of looking was an act of faith. It required the bitten Israelite to turn from staring at their wound and fix their eyes on God's provision. Salvation is not by works but by faith — by looking away from self to the Savior.

4. **Universality:** Every bitten Israelite who looked lived — regardless of age, tribe, or severity of the bite. So too, 'whosoever believeth' in Christ receives eternal life. The gospel is as simple and universal as the bronze serpent.

Paul captures the paradox: 'Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us' (Galatians 3:13). The serpent was the symbol of the curse (Genesis 3:14-15); Christ, by hanging on the tree (which itself was a cursed death — Deuteronomy 21:23), became the curse to break the curse.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often marveled at the simplicity of the gospel in this Old Testament type. The Israelites didn't need to perform rituals, make sacrifices, or earn healing. They simply looked. Salvation is not by works; it is by looking to Jesus, the author and finisher of our faith (Hebrews 12:2). The bronze serpent also teaches that the very thing that brought judgment became the vehicle of healing — the cross, the instrument of death, became the source of eternal life.

This passage is a powerful evangelistic tool. The gospel can be summarized in three words: **look and live.** The sinner, like the bitten Israelite, is perishing under the judgment of sin. The remedy is not self-improvement, religious effort, or moral reformation. It is the simple act of fixing one's eyes on the crucified and risen Savior. Faith is the eye of the soul that turns from the wound to the Healer.

For believers, the principle extends beyond conversion. When we are stung by the consequences of our own failures, when the 'fiery serpents' of life's afflictions bite, the answer remains the same — look to Christ. Not to our own efforts to fix the situation, but to the One who was lifted up for us. The gospel is not just the door we enter but the air we breathe.

The lesson of Nehushtan (the bronze serpent's later fate) is a pastoral caution: even God-given means of grace can become idols. The cross itself must never become a talisman or a good-luck charm. We do not venerate the instrument but the One who hung upon it. The object of our faith is not a symbol but a Person.
