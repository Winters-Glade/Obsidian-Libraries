## Summary

In Matthew 6:25-33, Jesus commands His followers not to worry about their basic needs — food, drink, and clothing. He points to the birds of the air as an object lesson: they do not sow, reap, or gather into barns, yet God feeds them. Pastor Chuck explores anxiety as a fundamental trust issue, showing how worry is incompatible with faith in a heavenly Father who knows and provides for His children's needs. The passage culminates in the great priority: 'Seek first the kingdom of God and His righteousness, and all these things shall be added unto you.'

## 📜 Historical Background

The Sermon on the Mount was delivered to a largely agrarian audience who understood the daily realities of food scarcity, manual labor, and economic uncertainty. First-century Palestine had no social safety net — crop failure or illness could mean genuine destitution. Jesus' call not to worry was radical because it addressed real concerns, not trivial ones. The lilies of the field (Matthew 6:28-29) refer to the wild anemones that blanketed the Galilean hillsides each spring, more beautifully arrayed than Solomon in all his glory — a stark contrast to the human tendency to obsess over appearances.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** Under Roman rule, Galilean peasants faced a crushing triple burden: Roman taxes (tribute to Caesar), Temple tithes and offerings, and Herod Antipas's own taxes to fund his building projects in Sepphoris and Tiberias. Most families lived at subsistence level — a single bad harvest could push them into debt and loss of their land. Jesus' audience knew real hunger and anxiety; His call to 'seek first the kingdom' was not a dismissal of genuine need but an invitation to a deeper trust than their circumstances seemed to warrant. The wild lilies (anemones) that bloomed each spring were a visible testament to God's provision — free, abundant, and more glorious than even Solomon's royal robes. Matthew's Gospel, addressed to a Jewish-Christian community likely in Syria (possibly Antioch) around AD 80–90, frames Jesus' teaching as the authoritative interpretation of Torah, presenting Him as the Messiah who fulfills not only prophecy but the deepest hopes of Israel for restoration and provision.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul echoes this theme in Philippians 4:6-7: 'Be careful for nothing; but in every thing by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known unto God. And the peace of God, which passeth all understanding, shall keep your hearts and minds through Christ Jesus.' Peter also picks up the theme: 'Casting all your care upon Him; for He careth for you' (1 Peter 5:7). Hebrews 13:5-6 warns against the love of money and reminds us that God Himself has said, 'I will never leave thee, nor forsake thee.'

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often taught that worry is not just a psychological problem but a spiritual one — it reveals what we truly trust. The command to 'seek first the kingdom' is the antidote: when we make God's rule and righteousness our priority, our lesser concerns find their proper perspective. Worry is wasteful prayer — it spends energy on problems without entrusting them to the only One who can solve them.
