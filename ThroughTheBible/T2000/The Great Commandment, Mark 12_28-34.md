## Summary

Mark 12:28-34 records a scribe's sincere question: 'Which is the first commandment of all?' Jesus answers by quoting the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-5) — 'Hear, O Israel; The Lord our God is one Lord: And thou shalt love the Lord thy God with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy mind, and with all thy strength' — and adds the second: 'Thou shalt love thy neighbor as thyself' (Leviticus 19:18). The scribe responds with wisdom, and Jesus tells him, 'Thou art not far from the kingdom of God.' Pastor Chuck explores how all of God's law hangs on these two commands and how love for God and love for neighbor are inseparable.

## 📜 Historical Background

This exchange occurs in Jerusalem during Passion Week, following a series of challenges from various religious factions. The Pharisees had tried to trap Jesus with a question about taxes (Mark 12:13-17), the Sadducees with a question about the resurrection (Mark 12:18-27). This scribe, however, seems genuinely sincere — unlike the others, he commends Jesus' answer. The Shema (from the Hebrew word for 'hear') was and remains the central prayer of Jewish liturgy, recited daily by observant Jews. By combining Deuteronomy 6:5 with Leviticus 19:18, Jesus distilled the entire Law into two principles: wholehearted love for God and practical love for neighbor. The scribe's agreement that love is 'more than all whole burnt offerings and sacrifices' shows that he understood the prophetic emphasis on heart religion over ritual (1 Samuel 15:22, Hosea 6:6, Micah 6:6-8).

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Gospel of Mark:** The Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4–9) was the most important prayer in Jewish life, recited twice daily and written on scrolls placed in _mezuzot_ (doorpost boxes) and _tefillin_ (phylacteries). By the first century, the scribes had catalogued 613 commandments in the Torah (365 negative, 248 positive), and debates about which was 'greatest' were common among rabbis. Jesus' answer — identifying love for God and love for neighbor as the sum of the Law — was not entirely original (similar summaries appear in rabbinic tradition, e.g., Hillel's 'What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbor'), but His integration of the two as inseparable was distinctive. Mark places this exchange at the climax of Jesus' public ministry in Jerusalem, immediately before His teaching about the widow's offering (Mark 12:41–44) and the Olivet Discourse (Mark 13). The placement is deliberate: the summary of the Law comes just before the passion narrative, demonstrating that Jesus not only taught love but would demonstrate it fully through His sacrificial death. Mark's Gospel, written for a Roman audience unfamiliar with Jewish law, presents this exchange as accessible — the heart of religion is love, not complex regulations.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Matthew 22:34-40 and Luke 10:25-28 record parallel versions. Luke's account introduces the parable of the Good Samaritan (Luke 10:29-37) as a response to the question 'Who is my neighbor?' — expanding the command to include even enemies. Paul writes in Romans 13:8-10 that 'love worketh no ill to his neighbor: therefore love is the fulfilling of the law.' James 2:8 calls the command to love your neighbor as yourself 'the royal law.' John's first epistle develops the inseparable link between love for God and love for others: 'If a man say, I love God, and hateth his brother, he is a liar' (1 John 4:20).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often pointed out that Jesus did not abolish the Law but summarized it. The whole duty of man is captured in these two commands. Loving God with all our being is the vertical dimension of faith; loving our neighbor as ourselves is the horizontal. You cannot have one without the other. The scribe was 'not far from the kingdom' because he understood this truth — but understanding is not the same as entering. The kingdom requires not just knowing the right answer but living it out through love empowered by the Spirit.
