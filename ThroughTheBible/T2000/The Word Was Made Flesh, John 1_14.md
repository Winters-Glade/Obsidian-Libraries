## Summary

'And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we beheld His glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father, full of grace and truth.' Chuck Smith expounds the single most profound verse in Scripture — the Incarnation. God took on human flesh. Not appearing as, but truly becoming human while remaining fully God. The word 'tabernacled' (dwelt) evokes the Old Testament Tabernacle — God's presence now dwells among His people in person.

## Key Themes

- **The Incarnation** — God became flesh; the Creator entered His creation
- **Grace and Truth** — The two defining attributes of the New Covenant revealed in Christ
- **Shekinah Glory** — The glory of God now seen in the face of Jesus Christ
- **Tabernacle** — God dwelling (skēnoō) among His people as He did in the wilderness
- **Deity of Christ** — The Word was God and became flesh without ceasing to be God

## 📜 Historical Background

John 1:14 is the theological and literary climax of the Prologue (John 1:1-18). It echoes Genesis 1:1 ('In the beginning') and draws on the Tabernacle imagery from Exodus 40:34-38, where the glory of the Lord (the Shekinah) filled the Tabernacle as a cloud by day and fire by night. Now, instead of a tent made of skins and curtains, God dwells in human flesh.

The Greek word *skēnoō* ('tabernacled' or 'pitched His tent') would have resonated powerfully with first-century Jewish readers who knew the wilderness Tabernacle and later the Temple as the dwelling place of God's presence. When Solomon dedicated the Temple, the Shekinah glory filled the house (1 Kings 8:10-11). But the prophet Ezekiel saw the glory depart from the Temple because of Israel's sin (Ezekiel 10:18-19; 11:22-23). Now, in John 1:14, the glory has returned — not to a building of stone, but in the person of Jesus Christ.

The term 'the Word' (Greek: *Logos*) carried deep meaning for both Jewish and Greek audiences. For Jewish readers, it evoked the creative Word of God in Genesis 1 and the personified Wisdom of Proverbs 8. For Greek readers, the *Logos* was the rational principle ordering the universe. John transforms both concepts: the Logos is not an impersonal force or abstract principle but a divine Person who became flesh.

This verse stands as the definitive New Testament statement on the Incarnation, countering both Jewish denial that God could take human form and Greek philosophical dualism (Gnosticism) that considered matter inherently evil and therefore denied Christ's true humanity. Early church councils (Nicaea, AD 325; Chalcedon, AD 451) would later formalize the doctrine: Jesus Christ is fully God and fully man, two natures united in one Person.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The Incarnation is the foundation of all New Testament theology. Paul writes that Christ 'emptied Himself, taking the form of a bond-servant, being made in the likeness of men' (Philippians 2:6-8). The author of Hebrews declares that the Son is 'the radiance of His glory and the exact representation of His nature' (Hebrews 1:3). In Colossians, Paul affirms that 'in Him dwells all the fullness of the Godhead bodily' (Colossians 2:9). The Incarnation makes possible the atonement — the God-man is the only suitable mediator between God and man (1 Timothy 2:5).

The same 'Word' (John 1:1) who was with God and was God is the agent of creation (John 1:3; Colossians 1:16). He is the 'light of the world' (John 8:12) and the 'true Light which gives light to every man' (John 1:9). The grace and truth that come through Jesus Christ (John 1:17) stand in continuity with — and fulfillment of — the Law given through Moses.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that the Incarnation is not merely a doctrine to be believed but a reality to be experienced. God is no longer distant and unapproachable — He has made Himself known in Jesus Christ. The same Word that spoke creation into existence became a baby in Bethlehem, lived among us, and invites us to behold His glory. The Incarnation means that God understands our humanity from the inside. He is not a distant deity but a personal Savior who 'tabernacles' with His people. As the Tabernacle was the meeting place between God and man in the wilderness, Jesus Christ is now the meeting place between God and man. He is the 'one mediator between God and men' (1 Timothy 2:5). The Incarnation also assures us that God is not only powerful but personal — He who 'pitched His tent' among us invites us to dwell with Him forever.
