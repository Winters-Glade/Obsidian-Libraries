## Summary

The law is described as a shadow, not the true image of good things. The repeated sacrifices of the old covenant could never make the worshiper perfect—if they could, they would have ceased being offered. But Christ, after offering one single sacrifice for sins forever, sat down at God's right hand. By one offering He has perfected forever those who are sanctified.

## 📜 Historical Background

The Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur, Leviticus 16) was the most sacred day in the Jewish calendar, the only day the high priest could enter the Holy of Holies. The ritual involved the high priest sprinkling the blood of a bull for his own sins and the blood of a goat for the sins of the people. The repetition 'year by year' (v. 1) proved the system's inadequacy—if the sacrifices had truly cleansed, they would have ceased. The Greek word ephapax ('once for all,' v. 10) means 'once and for all time'—a single, unrepeatable, infinitely sufficient act. The law is described as a 'shadow' (skia) of good things to come, not the 'very image' (eikon) itself. This language echoes Plato's cave allegory, where shadows are mere copies of the true reality—but the author uses it to describe the relationship between the Levitical system and the heavenly reality it foreshadowed.

The tabernacle structure itself was designed as a copy and shadow of the heavenly sanctuary (Exodus 25:40, quoted in Hebrews 8:5). The outer court represented the realm of earthly worship; the Holy Place, the priestly ministry; the Holy of Holies, the direct presence of God. The veil separating the Holy Place from the Holy of Holies symbolized the barrier between sinful humanity and a holy God—a barrier that only the high priest could cross, and only once a year. When Christ died, that veil was torn from top to bottom (Matthew 27:51), signifying that the way into God's presence was now open to all through His flesh (Hebrews 10:20). The contrast between the standing priests (their work never finished) and the seated Christ (His work complete) is a powerful visual argument from the Septuagint text of Psalm 110:1.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Psalm 40:6-8 is quoted: 'Sacrifice and offering Thou wouldest not, but a body hast Thou prepared Me.' Jesus came to do God's will—to offer His body as the perfect sacrifice. John the Baptist declared, 'Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world' (John 1:29). Paul writes that we are 'justified freely by His grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus' (Romans 3:24). The tearing of the temple veil at Christ's death (Matthew 27:51) signified that the way into God's presence was now open.

The Pentateuchal typology is foundational: the entire Levitical system was designed by God as a shadow pointing to Christ. The daily sacrifices (tamid), the annual Day of Atonement, the red heifer (Numbers 19), the Paschal lamb (Exodus 12)—all prefigured the one true sacrifice. The author's quotation of Psalm 40:6-8 is significant because it places the words of Christ into the text: 'Then said I, Lo, I come... to do Thy will, O God.' The New Testament elsewhere develops the 'once for all' theme: 1 Peter 3:18 declares 'Christ also hath once suffered for sins, the just for the unjust'; Romans 6:10 states 'the death that He died, He died unto sin once'; and Jude 3 speaks of 'the faith which was once delivered unto the saints.' The perfection theme (teleioo—'to perfect,' v. 14) echoes throughout Hebrews (2:10, 5:9, 7:28, 11:40) as the goal of Christ's work: He perfects forever those who are sanctified. ## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** The contrast between the "shadow" (*skia*) of the law and the "very image" (*eikon*) of Christ's sacrifice is central to understanding NT faith. The Greek *ephapax* ("once for all," v. 10) means "once and for all time" — a single, unrepeatable, infinitely sufficient act. The author's argument is structural: the Levitical priests *stand* daily because their work is never finished, but Christ *sat down* because His work is complete. This posture shift (standing vs. seated) is the visual sermon of Hebrews. The "perfection" language (*teleioo*, v. 14) means to bring to completion, to reach the intended goal — Christ has perfected forever those who are being sanctified. The present passive participle "those who are sanctified" (*hagiazomenous*) indicates ongoing sanctification built on a completed justification — the foundation for perseverance.

**Cross-Batch Connections:** The tabernacle typology connects to [[_MOC_Batch2_Exodus_Wilderness|Batch 2 — Exodus & Deuteronomy]] (the construction of the tabernacle, Exodus 25-40; the Day of Atonement, Leviticus 16). The quotation of Psalm 40:6-8 connects to [[_MOC_Batch6_Wisdom_Literature|Batch 6 — Psalms & Wisdom Literature]] (the psalm of consecration). The tearing of the temple veil (Matthew 27:51) connects to [[_MOC_Batch9_Matthew_Mark|Batch 9 — Matthew & Mark]]. Peter develops the same "once for all" theme in [[_transcripts/Redemption, 1 Peter 1_18-20.md|258 — Redemption (1 Peter 1:18-20)]], where Christ is the spotless lamb foreordained before the foundation of the world. The same Greek *teleioo* (perfection) appears in Hebrews 2:10, 5:9, 7:28, and 11:40, forming a chain of references that culminates here in chapter 10.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often contrasted the repetitive nature of the old sacrifices with the finality of Christ's work. The Levitical priests stood daily because their work was never done; Christ sat down because His work was finished. There is nothing we can add to what Christ has done. Trying to earn salvation through religious activity is like trying to paint over a shadow—it misses the reality. The question isn't 'What must I do?' but 'What has He done?'
