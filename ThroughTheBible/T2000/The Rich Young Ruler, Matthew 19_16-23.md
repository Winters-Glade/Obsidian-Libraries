## Summary

Matthew 19:16-23 tells the story of the rich young ruler who comes to Jesus asking what good thing he must do to inherit eternal life. Jesus first redirects him to the goodness of God alone, then tests him on the commandments. When the young man claims to have kept them all, Jesus delivers the heart-searching challenge: 'Go and sell that thou hast, and give to the poor... and come and follow Me.' The young man goes away sorrowful because he had great possessions. Pastor Chuck explores the exposure of this man's true god — his wealth — and the principle that whatever we love more than Jesus is the thing that must be surrendered.

## 📜 Historical Background

The rich young ruler is described in Matthew as 'young' (Matthew 19:20, 22), in Luke as a 'ruler' (Luke 18:18), and in all three Synoptics as 'very rich.' As a ruler, he likely held a position in the local synagogue or community leadership. His question reveals the common assumption of his day that eternal life was earned by doing good works. Jesus' response challenges this assumption at its roots. The young man's claim to have kept all the commandments from his youth may have been sincere but revealed his shallow understanding of the Law's depth. Jesus' command to sell everything was not a universal requirement for salvation but a specific diagnosis of this man's particular idol.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** First-century Judea was characterized by extreme economic stratification. The Roman occupation created a wealthy elite — tax farmers, large landowners, and those connected to the Herodian court — while the vast majority lived in subsistence poverty. The rich young ruler's wealth would have been conspicuous in a society where many were struggling under the combined weight of Roman tribute, Temple taxes, and Herod's building projects (the Temple renovation alone employed thousands and was funded by heavy taxation, Josephus, Antiquities 15.11.2). The common Jewish belief that wealth was a sign of God's blessing (Deuteronomy 28:1–14) made Jesus' teaching about the danger of riches all the more shocking — it overturned a foundational assumption. Matthew's Gospel, written to a community that included both Jewish and Gentile believers, consistently warns against the spiritual dangers of wealth (Matthew 6:19–24, 13:22) and presents generous discipleship as the hallmark of kingdom living.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul writes in 1 Timothy 6:9-10 that 'the love of money is the root of all evil' and warns those who desire to be rich of falling into temptation. Jesus' statement that 'it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of God' (Matthew 19:24) underscores the spiritual danger of wealth. Yet He immediately adds, 'With God all things are possible' (v. 26). The account of Zacchaeus (Luke 19:1-10) provides a positive example of a wealthy man who responded to Jesus with joyful generosity. Hebrews 13:5 commands contentment: 'be content with such things as ye have.'

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often pointed out that the rich young ruler's problem was not his wealth but his unwillingness to let it go. Jesus did not demand that all His followers sell everything, but He did demand that nothing be held back from Him. The question for every believer is not 'How much should I give?' but 'Is there anything I love more than Jesus?' The young ruler came seeking eternal life but went away sorrowful because he discovered that he loved his possessions more than the One who offered him eternal life.
