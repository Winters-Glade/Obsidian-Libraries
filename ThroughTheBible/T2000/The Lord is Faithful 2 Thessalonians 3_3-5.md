# 241 The Lord is Faithful — 2 Thessalonians 3:3-5

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#241](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kxQRE0mTTVM)

## Overview

"But the Lord is faithful, who shall stablish you, and keep you from evil. And we have confidence in the Lord touching you, that ye both do and will do the things which we command you. And the Lord direct your hearts into the love of God, and into the patient waiting for Christ." Paul emphasizes the faithfulness of God as the foundation for the believer's stability, protection from evil, and patient endurance as they await Christ's return.

Second Thessalonians addresses a crisis in the Thessalonian [[church]]. Some believers, convinced that "the day of the Lord is already here" (2 Thessalonians 2:2), had stopped working and were living in disruptive idleness. Paul writes to correct this error, reaffirm the signs preceding Christ's return, and call believers back to orderly, productive living. Yet beneath the correction lies profound pastoral comfort: "The Lord is faithful." The believer's stability does not depend on their own faithfulness but on God's. He establishes (stērizō — makes firm), keeps (phylassō — guards), and directs (kateuthynō — guides) their hearts. The goal of this divine work is twofold: "the love of God" and "patient waiting for Christ" (literally, the patience of Christ — the endurance that Christ both exemplifies and supplies).

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Faithfulness of God | God's character is the believer's security — He is faithful even when we are faithless, and His faithfulness ensures our perseverance |
| Perseverance | The believer's endurance is not self-generated but flows from God's establishing work — He makes firm what we cannot hold |
| Prayer | Paul's prayers consistently prioritize spiritual realities — that God would direct hearts into love and patience |
| Protection from Evil | God keeps (phylassō) believers from evil — not necessarily from temptation's presence but from its power and ultimate harm |
| Patient Waiting | The Christian life is lived between Christ's first and second coming — patient endurance is the posture of faithful waiting |

## Background & Context

Paul's second letter to the Thessalonians was written shortly after the first, likely still from Corinth (c. AD 50-51). A misunderstanding of Paul's teaching about the "day of the Lord" had led some believers to quit their jobs and wait passively for Christ's return. Paul clarifies that certain events must precede that day — notably the "man of sin" (the Antichrist) being revealed and a great "falling away" (apostasia) occurring first. The "restrainer" who holds back lawlessness will be removed, and then the lawless one will be revealed, whom the Lord will destroy with the brightness of His coming (2 Thessalonians 2:3-8). This teaching remains one of the most debated prophetic passages in Paul's writings. The letter ends with the firm instruction: "If any would not work, neither should he eat" (2 Thessalonians 3:10) — a practical rebuke to those who used eschatology as an excuse for idleness.
