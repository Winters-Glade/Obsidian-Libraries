## Summary

Luke 16:1-13 contains the parable of the unjust steward — one of Jesus' most challenging teachings. A shrewd manager, facing termination, secures his future by forgiving debts owed to his master. Surprisingly, the master commends the steward's shrewdness. Pastor Chuck explores the lesson: believers should use worldly resources with heavenly wisdom, being faithful stewards of what God has entrusted to them.

## 📖 Research Context

This parable follows immediately after the three parables of grace in Luke 15 and directly addresses the disciples (Luke 16:1), though the Pharisees are listening (Luke 16:14). The parable's difficulty has spawned numerous interpretations. The steward's actions are likely not outright fraud but the reduction of his own commission — a common practice in which stewards added their profit margin to the debt. By forgiving his commission, he curried favor with the debtors while still satisfying his master. Jesus' point is not to commend dishonesty but to contrast worldly shrewdness with spiritual dullness. The 'unrighteous mammon' (mamōna tēs adikias) means 'wealth of unrighteousness' — not that money is inherently evil but that it is often used unjustly. The principle is stark: how we use material resources reveals our true loyalty.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Proverbs 3:9-10 (honor the Lord with your wealth), Malachi 3:8-12 (tithes and offerings), Deuteronomy 8:17-18 (remember the Lord your God who gives you power to get wealth).

## 📜 Historical Background

In first-century Palestine, stewards (or estate managers) had significant authority over their master's affairs. They could negotiate debts, collect payments, and manage resources. The steward in this parable was accused of wasting his master's goods — a serious offense. His decision to reduce the debts owed to his master was likely removing his own commission rather than cheating the master, which explains the master's commendation. The point is not dishonesty but shrewdness in preparing for the future.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Matthew 6:24 — you cannot serve God and mammon. 1 Corinthians 4:2 — it is required in stewards that one be found faithful. 1 Peter 4:10 — as good stewards of the manifold grace of God. 2 Corinthians 9:6-8 — God loves a cheerful giver. 1 Timothy 6:17-19 — charge the rich to be generous and lay up treasure for eternity. Matthew 25:14-30 — the parable of the talents.

2 Corinthians 8:1-5 — the Macedonians gave themselves first to the Lord, demonstrating the true spirit of sacrificial stewardship. 2 Corinthians 9:6-11 — he who sows bountifully will also reap bountifully; God supplies seed to the sower and multiplies your harvest of righteousness.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that Jesus uses a negative example to teach a positive lesson. If the unjust steward used worldly wisdom to secure his temporal future, how much more should believers use spiritual wisdom to secure their eternal future? We are stewards, not owners, of everything God has given us. The test of faithfulness is how we use what we have been entrusted with. The person who is faithful in little is faithful in much.
