# 233 The Walk of the Believer — Ephesians 4:1

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#233](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ImiWCDqjacY)

## Overview

"I therefore, the prisoner of the Lord, beseech you that ye walk worthy of the vocation wherewith ye are called." Paul transitions from doctrine (chapters 1-3) to duty (chapters 4-6), urging believers to live a life consistent with their heavenly calling. The worthy walk is marked by humility, gentleness, patience, forbearance in love, and the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace.

This "therefore" is one of the most important transitional words in the New Testament. It bridges the indicative (what God has done — chapters 1-3) with the imperative (how believers should live — chapters 4-6). Paul's pattern is always: doctrine before duty, grace before growth, position before practice. "Walk worthy" (peripateō axiós) means to live a life that corresponds to the value of one's calling. Since believers have been blessed with every spiritual blessing, chosen before the foundation of the world, redeemed by Christ's blood, sealed by the Holy Spirit, made alive together with Christ, saved by grace, and brought near to God — they are to walk in a manner that reflects this incredible calling. The virtues Paul lists (humility, gentleness, patience, forbearance) are not natural virtues but the character of Christ formed in the believer.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Christian Walk | The believer's daily conduct must match their heavenly position — doctrine always leads to duty, grace always produces godliness |
| Unity | The seven "ones" (one body, one Spirit, one hope, one Lord, one faith, one baptism, one God) form the foundation of [[church]] unity — not uniformity but harmony in diversity |
| Humility | Lowliness of mind (tapeinophrosynē) — the recognition of our complete dependence on God, the virtue Christ supremely demonstrated |
| Calling | The believer's vocation is not a career but a holy identity — called out of darkness into God's marvelous light |
| Peace | The bond of peace is the Holy Spirit's work, holding the body together through mutual forbearance and love |

## Background & Context

The second half of Ephesians (chapters 4-6) is a sustained call to live out the theological realities of the first half. Paul writes as "the prisoner of the Lord" — likely from Roman custody (c. AD 60-62). The emphasis on unity reflects the Jew-Gentile tensions in the early [[church]]. Paul had just explained that Christ "has broken down the middle wall of partition" (Ephesians 2:14) between Jew and Gentile, creating one new man. Now he calls believers to maintain this unity in practice. The seven "ones" of verses 4-6 form an early creedal statement, anchoring unity in the triune God. Paul goes on to describe the diversity of gifts (apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors, teachers) given by the ascended Christ to build up the body into maturity.
