# 244 True Riches — 1 Timothy 6:5-6

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#244](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=28msW5hv62o)

## Overview

"Supposing that gain is godliness: from such withdraw thyself. But godliness with contentment is great gain." Paul contrasts false teachers who view religion as a means of material profit with the true gain of godliness joined with contentment. He warns against the love of money, which is the root of all kinds of evil, and exhorts believers to pursue righteousness, godliness, faith, love, patience, and meekness.

First Timothy 6 contains Paul's most extended teaching on wealth and contentment. The false teachers in Ephesus were treating ministry as a career path to prosperity — "supposing that gain is godliness" (nomizontōn porismon einai tēn eusebeian). Paul reframes the entire equation: godliness with contentment (autarkeia — self-sufficiency, sufficiency in Christ) is the true gain. The famous statement "the love of money is the root of all evil" (riza pantōn tōn kakōn hē philargyria) is often misquoted as "money is the root of all evil" — but Paul condemns the love of money, not money itself. Riches are uncertain and temporary; the living God gives us "richly all things to enjoy." The wealthy are charged not to abandon their wealth but to use it generously, laying up treasure for the coming age.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Contentment | True satisfaction is found not in accumulating possessions but in Christ-sufficiency — the ability to be at peace regardless of material circumstances |
| Wealth | Money is not evil in itself, but the love of money is a root of many evils — the pursuit of wealth often leads to spiritual ruin |
| Godliness | Genuine piety is not a means to material prosperity but is itself the great gain — the character of Christ formed in the believer |
| False Teaching | False teachers are often motivated by greed — they distort the gospel for financial advantage and must be recognized and avoided |
| Eternal Perspective | We brought nothing into this world and can carry nothing out — this reality should liberate believers from materialism and fear |

## Background & Context

First Timothy 6 is the climax of Paul's instructions to [[Timothy]]. The letter has warned against false teachers (chapter 1), described proper worship (chapter 2), listed leadership qualifications (chapter 3), warned about apostasy (chapter 4), and given instructions for [[church]] relationships (chapter 5). Now Paul addresses the root motive of false teaching — greed. The "love of money" was driving some to corrupt the faith. Paul's antidote is contentment — the recognition that life's true riches are not material but spiritual. The passage ends with the "good confession" Timothy made before many witnesses (1 Timothy 6:12-13) — a call to persevere in faithfulness regardless of cost. The letter closes with the affirmation that God "dwells in unapproachable light" (1 Timothy 6:16) — a doxology that sets material wealth in its proper, insignificant place.
