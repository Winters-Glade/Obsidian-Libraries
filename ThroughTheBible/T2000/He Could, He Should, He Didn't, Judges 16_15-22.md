## Summary

Chuck Smith examines the tragic downfall of Samson — the man of supernatural strength brought low not by the Philistines but by his own moral weakness. Consecrated as a Nazirite from birth, empowered by the Spirit, Samson had every advantage. Yet Delilah's persistent nagging wore down his resistance until 'he told her all his heart.' The tragedy climaxes in the most terrifying verse in Samson's story: he woke from sleep thinking 'I will go out as before and shake myself free' — but he 'did not know that the Lord had departed from him.' Spiritual strength is not automatic; it depends on ongoing relationship with God.

## Key Themes

- **Presumption** — Taking God's grace and power for granted; assuming yesterday's blessing guarantees today's
- **Compromise** — Samson's slow, incremental slide into spiritual disaster
- **The Nazirite Vow** — Consecration to God requires discipline in every area of life
- **Spiritual Decline** — The process is gradual before the fall is catastrophic
- **Redemption** — Despite failure, God demonstrates grace even in judgment
- **Delilah** — The seduction of persistent temptation wearing down resolve
- **The Spirit of the Lord** — Samson's source of power; departure when grieved
- **Sight vs. Faith** — Samson walked by sight, not by faith — and lost his physical sight as judgment
- **Philistine Religion** — The temple of Dagon as the backdrop of Samson's final act

## 📜 Historical Background

Samson is the last of the major judges before Samuel (Judges 13-16), judging Israel for 20 years during the Philistine oppression (ca. 1100-1080 BC). He is unique among the judges: he never leads an army, never gathers the tribes for battle, and never liberates a region — his exploits are personal vendettas against the Philistines, who dominated Israel with their monopoly on iron technology (1 Samuel 13:19-22).

**The Nazirite Vow (Numbers 6:1-8):** Samson was a Nazirite from birth — the only person in Scripture consecrated as a Nazirite before birth (along with John the Baptist, Luke 1:15; and by implication, Jeremiah 1:5). The vow required three things, all of which Samson violated:
1. **No wine or strong drink** — Samson's feast and celebration likely involved drinking (Judges 14:10)
2. **No cutting of hair** — His uncut hair was the external sign of his consecration
3. **No contact with the dead** — Samson killed a lion with his bare hands (14:6), later took honey from its carcass (14:8-9), and killed thousands of Philistines (15:15; 16:30) — all involving corpse contact

**Samson's Gradual Compromise:** The transcript reveals a pattern of incremental spiritual decline:
1. He went down to Timnath and saw a Philistine woman — marrying outside Israel (14:1-3)
2. He touched the lion's carcass — violating his Nazirite vow (14:8-9)
3. He celebrated with a feast and made a wager — fraternizing with the enemy (14:10)
4. He went to a prostitute in Gaza — engaging in sexual immorality (16:1-3)
5. He loved Delilah in the Valley of Sorek — putting his relationship with a Philistine woman above his covenant with God (16:4)
6. He played with his consecration — teasing Delilah with false answers (16:5-14)
7. He told her all his heart — revealing the secret of his strength (16:17)

**Archaeological Evidence:** The Philistine temple of Dagon, where Samson died, had a roof supported by two central pillars — a design confirmed by archaeological discoveries at Tell Qasile (near modern Tel Aviv), where a Philistine temple with two pillar bases was excavated. The temple was approximately 50 feet wide, with the pillars spaced about 20-25 feet apart, supporting a roof that could hold thousands of people on the upper level.

**The Name Dagon:** Dagon was the chief Philistine deity, a grain/fish god whose name is related to the Hebrew *dagan* ('grain'). The temple of Dagon appears elsewhere in Scripture — the Ark of the Covenant was placed in Dagon's temple in Ashdod, where the idol fell before the Ark (1 Samuel 5:1-5). This earlier incident at Dagon's temple foreshadows Dagon's ultimate defeat. In Samson's final act, he destroys the temple of Dagon, killing more Philistines in his death than in his life — a dramatic demonstration that the God of Israel is more powerful than the Philistine deity.

**The Poetic Justice of Samson's Eyes:** Samson 'walked by sight' rather than faith. His sin began with seeing: 'He saw a woman' in Timnath (14:1), and again 'he saw a harlot' in Gaza (16:1). The Philistines punished him by gouging out his eyes — a fitting judgment for the man who was led by his eyes to his ruin. 'The eye is the lamp of the body' (Matthew 6:22), and Samson's lamp had led him into darkness.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Despite Samson's failures, he is listed among the heroes of faith in Hebrews 11:32-34 — not for his moral perfection but because his final act was one of faith, destroying more of God's enemies in death than in life. Paul warns against the same presumption: 'Let him who thinks he stands take heed lest he fall' (1 Corinthians 10:12). Paul also speaks of disciplining his body 'lest after preaching to others I myself should be disqualified' (1 Corinthians 9:27). Samson's story illustrates Jesus' teaching: 'Apart from Me you can do nothing' (John 15:5). The gradual nature of Samson's compromise also echoes the warning in Proverbs: 'There is a way which seems right to a man, but its end is the way of death' (Proverbs 14:12). But the note of hope — 'his hair began to grow again' (Judges 16:22) — points to the New Testament truth that God can redeem even the worst failures, as seen in the restoring of Peter after his denial (John 21:15-19).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that Samson's tragedy is the danger of spiritual presumption — the belief that past experiences of God's power guarantee future blessing regardless of present obedience. Samson said 'I will go out as before' but did not realize the Lord had departed. The saddest phrase in the Bible is 'he did not know.' Samson lost his strength not in a single moment of sin but through a long process of compromise that dulled his spiritual sensitivity. The final judgment — his eyes gouged out, grinding at the prison mill — fittingly describes the condition of a believer who has lost spiritual vision and is enslaved to the consequences of their choices. Yet the note of hope is that his hair began to grow again, and in his final act, Samson destroyed more in death than in life — pointing to the truth that God can still redeem our failures at the point of genuine repentance. Smith often emphasized that Samson had everything going for him — divine calling, supernatural empowerment, godly parents — but he lacked self-discipline. Spiritual gifts without character lead to ruin.
