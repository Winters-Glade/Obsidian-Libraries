# 207 Keys to Faith — Romans 5:1-9

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#207](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g_bG7ipCtrI)

## Overview

Having been justified by faith, believers now have peace with God through the Lord Jesus Christ. Paul unfolds the benefits of justification: access into grace, rejoicing in hope of the glory of God, and the love of God poured out in our hearts by the [[Holy Spirit]]. Suffering itself becomes a means of developing character and hope. This passage opens the great section of Romans dealing with the assurance that flows from justification — peace with God, access to grace, rejoicing in glory, and love poured out by the Spirit. The chain from tribulation to patience to experience to hope demonstrates how God redeems even suffering for our good.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Peace with God | Not merely a subjective feeling but an objective reconciliation — the war is over, and believers stand in a state of grace through Christ |
| Justification | The legal declaration that the believer is righteous before God, resulting in free access to His grace and the hope of sharing His glory |
| Hope | A confident expectation of future glory that does not disappoint because it is anchored in God's love already demonstrated at the cross |
|| Love of God | Shed abroad in our hearts by the [[_Concepts/Doctrines/Holy Spirit]] — the experiential dimension of God's love that assures us of our standing in Christ |
| Suffering and Character | Tribulation produces patience, patience produces experience, and experience produces hope — the transformative purpose of trials |

## Pauline Context

Romans 5:1-11 is the bridge between Paul's doctrinal argument (justification by faith, Romans 1-4) and its practical outworking ([[Sanctification]] and assurance, Romans 5-8). The phrase "peace with God" uses the objective eirēnēn echomen (we have peace), not a subjective feeling but a settled state of reconciliation. Paul's logic moves from past (justification by faith) to present (access into grace) to future (hope of glory), showing the comprehensive scope of salvation. The love of God "poured out" (ekkechytai) through the Holy Spirit connects to Paul's teaching on the Spirit's work in Romans 8 and anticipates the outpouring of the Spirit in Ephesians and Titus. The connection between suffering and hope is unique to Paul's theology — affliction is not meaningless but produces eternal glory (2 Corinthians 4:17).
