# 248 Charge It to My Account — Philemon 1:16-17

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#248](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B_okqEbQJmg)

## Overview

Paul appeals to Philemon on behalf of Onesimus, a runaway slave who had become a believer. "Not now as a servant, but above a servant, a brother beloved... If thou count me therefore a partner, receive him as myself. If he hath wronged thee, or oweth thee ought, put that on mine account." This brief letter is a beautiful picture of the gospel — Christ standing in for the guilty, taking their debt upon Himself, and appealing to the Father on their behalf.

Philemon is Paul's shortest and most personal letter — a private appeal to a wealthy Colossian believer whose slave, Onesimus, had run away, possibly stealing money or property (Philemon 1:18). Onesimus encountered Paul in prison (likely Rome) and was converted. Paul now sends him back to Philemon with this letter — not as a fugitive to be punished but as "a brother beloved." The letter is a masterpiece of pastoral diplomacy and gospel illustration. Paul's offer — "put that on my account" (touto emoi elloga) — is the language of accounting and substitution. He will pay what Onesimus owes. This is exactly what Christ does for sinners: He takes our debt and pays it in full. The entire letter is a living parable of substitutionary atonement, reconciliation, and the revolutionary social implications of the gospel.

## Background & Context

Philemon is one of the four "Prison Epistles" (along with Ephesians, Philippians, Colossians), written during Paul's first Roman imprisonment (c. AD 60-62). The letter was likely delivered to Colossae by Tychicus and Onesimus himself, alongside the letter to the Colossians (Colossians 4:7-9). The social context is critical: in the Roman Empire, a runaway slave (fugitivus) could be severely punished, even crucified. Paul's letter is a bold request — not only for mercy but for full acceptance of Onesimus as a brother. Paul subtly hints that he would like to keep Onesimus with him but respects Philemon's rights. The letter never explicitly condemns slavery as an institution, but its gospel logic — slave becomes brother, master receives him "as myself" — plants the seeds that would eventually undermine the entire system. The letter also reminds us that the early [[church]] included people of very different social positions united in Christ.
