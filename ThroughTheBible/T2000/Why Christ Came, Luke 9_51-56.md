## Summary

Luke 9:51-56 marks a pivotal moment: Jesus 'steadfastly set His face to go to Jerusalem,' knowing what awaited Him. When Samaritan villagers rejected Him, James and John wanted to call down fire from heaven. Jesus rebuked them, revealing that His mission was not destruction but salvation. Pastor Chuck explores the determination of Christ and the lesson in responding to rejection with grace rather than retaliation.

## 📖 Research Context

Luke 9:51 is the most pivotal verse in the Gospel of Luke — the moment Jesus 'set His face' toward Jerusalem, marking the beginning of the 'Travel Narrative' (Luke 9:51–19:44) that occupies nearly half the Gospel. The Greek phrase to prosōpon estērisen literally means 'He fixed His face' — an idiom of resolute determination, often used of prophets setting their faces against opposition. Isaiah 50:7 declares: 'I have set My face like a flint, and I know that I shall not be ashamed.' Jesus' journey to Jerusalem is not a pilgrimage but a death march — He knows what awaits Him: betrayal, rejection, crucifixion. The Samaritan village's rejection foreshadows the greater rejection He will face in Jerusalem. James and John's desire to call down fire from heaven — the same 'sons of thunder' (Mark 3:17) — reveals how deeply the old covenant mindset of retribution resisted the new covenant of grace.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Isaiah 50:4-9 (the Servant sets His face like flint), Jeremiah 1:18 (set your face against them), Ezekiel 3:8-9 (I have made your face strong against their faces), 2 Kings 1:9-15 (Elijah calls fire — the very precedent James and John cite).

## 📜 Historical Background

Luke 9:51 is a crucial transition verse in the Gospel — Jesus turns from His Galilean ministry and heads toward Jerusalem and the cross. The Samaritans and Jews had centuries of hostility. Samaritan rejection of Jewish pilgrims heading to Jerusalem was common. James and John's request for fire from heaven echoes Elijah's actions against the servants of Ahaziah (2 Kings 1). Jesus' rebuke marks a shift from the old covenant of judgment to the new covenant of grace.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

John 3:17 — God sent not His Son into the world to condemn the world but that the world through Him might be saved. Luke 13:33 underscores Jesus' prophetic awareness: 'it cannot be that a prophet should perish outside of Jerusalem' — His journey is a death march He fully understands. Philippians 2:5-8 captures the same determination: Christ emptied Himself, taking the form of a servant, becoming obedient to the point of death. Hebrews 12:1-2 calls us to run with endurance, 'looking unto Jesus, who for the joy that was set before Him endured the cross, despising the shame.' Romans 12:14 — bless those who persecute you. 1 Peter 2:23 — when reviled, Christ did not revile in return. Matthew 5:44 — love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith observed that the disciples were still thinking in Old Testament terms of judgment, but Jesus came to introduce a new way — the way of grace. When we face rejection for our faith, our natural instinct is to want vindication or retaliation. But Jesus calls us to respond as He did: to keep walking toward the cross, trusting the Father's timing and leaving judgment in His hands. The hardest thing is to love those who reject us.
