## Summary

[[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 19.md]] records the dramatic destruction of [[Sodom and Gomorrah]], cities consumed by fire and brimstone from heaven for their grievous sin. [[Lot]], Abraham's nephew, had chosen to dwell in the fertile plain of the Jordan near Sodom, gradually becoming entangled in the city's culture and compromised in his witness. When the angels arrive to deliver Lot and his family before judgment falls, the urgency of their departure is underscored by the command: "Look not behind thee." But Lot's wife looks back, longing for what she is leaving behind, and becomes a pillar of salt. Jesus Himself holds up Lot's wife as a warning: "Remember Lot's wife" ([[Luke 17_32]]) — a caution against looking back with longing at the world while trying to escape its judgment.

## Key Takeaways

1. Lot's wife is not remembered for what she did but for what she looked at — her heart was still in Sodom.
2. Jesus' warning to "remember Lot's wife" is a call to wholehearted discipleship, not looking back at the world.
3. Lot's gradual compromise (choosing the plain, pitching his tent toward Sodom, dwelling in the city) is a cautionary tale of incremental worldliness.
4. Deliverance from judgment requires decisive action — "look not behind thee."
5. God's judgment on Sodom is held up throughout Scripture as an example of His certain judgment on unrepentant sin.
6. Lot was saved despite his compromise — he is called "just" and "righteous" in 2 Peter, saved as by fire.

## Further Context

### Historical & Cultural Background
The location of Sodom and Gomorrah is debated, with the leading candidate being Tall el-Hammam (northeast of the Dead Sea), which shows evidence of a catastrophic Middle Bronze Age destruction event involving high-temperature fires. The Dead Sea region is rich in bitumen, sulphur, and salt deposits — natural elements consistent with the biblical description of "brimstone and fire." Lot's wife becoming a "pillar of salt" may reference the salt formations common near the Dead Sea's southwestern shore (Jebel Usdum, "Mount of Sodom"). The sin of Sodom in ancient Near Eastern context included not only sexual immorality but a profound violation of hospitality codes — a grave offence in desert culture where sheltering strangers was sacred.

### Old Testament Connections
- **Ezekiel 16:49-50** — God identifies Sodom's sins as "pride, fulness of bread, and abundance of idleness... neither did she strengthen the hand of the poor and needy" — revealing that Sodom's judgment was rooted in social injustice as well as sexual sin.
- **Isaiah 1:9-10** — Isaiah calls the rulers of Jerusalem "rulers of Sodom," using the destroyed city as a warning that covenant unfaithfulness brings judgment.
- **Isaiah 3:9** — "They declare their sin as Sodom, they hide it not" — the shamelessness of sin that invites judgment.
- **Deuteronomy 29:23** — The land of Sodom becomes a byword for total judgment — "brimstone, and salt, and burning."
- **Jeremiah 23:14** — The prophets of Jerusalem are compared to Sodom, showing that God's standard of judgment applies to His own people.
- **Zephaniah 2:9** — Moab and Ammon will become "as Sodom and Gomorrah" — the judgment of the cities becomes a template for all who oppose God.

### New Testament Connections
- **Luke 17:28-33** — Jesus explicitly uses the days of Lot as parallel to His coming: "Likewise also as it was in the days of Lot... the same day that Lot went out of Sodom it rained fire and brimstone." The command "Remember Lot's wife" is Jesus' call to wholehearted discipleship without looking back.
- **2 Peter 2:6-9** — Peter holds up Sodom and Gomorrah as "an ensample unto those that after should live ungodly," while emphasizing that God rescued "just Lot" — the pattern of judgment and deliverance.
- **Jude 7** — "Even as Sodom and Gomorrah... are set forth for an example, suffering the vengeance of eternal fire" — a warning to the ungodly.
- **Romans 9:29** — Paul quotes Isaiah: "Except the Lord of Sabaoth had left us a seed, we had been as Sodoma" — the mercy of the remnant.
- **Revelation 11:8** — Jerusalem is spiritually called "Sodom and Egypt" — the city that kills the prophets inherits the Sodom legacy.

### Extra-Biblical Sources
- Josephus (*War of the Jews* 4.8.4) describes the region of Sodom as still showing "the remains of that fire" and "the shadows of the five cities" visible in the water, reporting that the ashes of Sodom's fruits appeared edible but dissolved to smoke when touched.
- Tacitus (*Histories* 5.7) mentions the plain of Sodom as a region of ruined cities, blackened and barren.
- Archaeological excavations at Tall el-Hammam have revealed a destruction layer with pottery melted to glass by temperatures exceeding 2,000°C, consistent with an airburst or cosmic impact event, though this interpretation remains debated.
