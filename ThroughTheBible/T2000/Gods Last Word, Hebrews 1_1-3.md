## Summary

Hebrews opens with a grand declaration: God, who spoke in many ways through the prophets, has now spoken definitively through His Son. Jesus Christ is the radiance of God's glory, the exact representation of His being, sustaining all things by His powerful word. After providing purification for sins, He sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high—a position of finished work and absolute authority.

## 📜 Historical Background

The book of Hebrews was written to Jewish believers who were tempted to return to Judaism under persecution. The authorship of Hebrews remains one of the great mysteries of the New Testament canon. While traditionally attributed to Paul, the elegant Greek style, the absence of Paul's standard salutation, and the method of Old Testament quotation (from the Greek Septuagint rather than the Hebrew) suggest other candidates: Apollos, Luke, Barnabas, or even Priscilla. What is undisputed is the book's sophisticated rhetorical structure. The author employs the argument from lesser to greater (qal vahomer), a classic rabbinical reasoning method: if the old covenant mediated by angels was binding, how much more the new covenant proclaimed by the Son? The Greek word polytropos ('in many ways,' v. 1) captures the diverse modes of divine revelation—visions, dreams, prophetic oracles, the Urim and Thummim—all now superseded by the Son.

Chapter 1 establishes Christ's superiority to the angels, a critical point because angels were highly revered in Second Temple Judaism. Jewish tradition held that the Law was mediated through angels (Acts 7:53, Galatians 3:19, Jubilees 1:29), and angelic hierarchies were extensively developed in intertestamental literature (1 Enoch, the Dead Sea Scrolls). Some Jewish Christians may have been drawn back to this angel-centered spirituality. The author counters by demolishing any notion of angelic superiority: Christ is the Son, not a servant; He sits on the throne, not stands as a messenger; He is worshiped by angels, not worshiping with them. The seven Old Testament quotations in chapter 1 are arranged as a carefully constructed catena (chain) of proof texts, each one elevating Christ above every created being. The verb pherein ('sustaining all things,' v. 3) is a present active participle—Christ continually upholds the universe by His powerful word, not as a clockmaker who winds and walks away but as the constant sustainer of all existence.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The author directly quotes Psalm 2:7, 2 Samuel 7:14, and Psalm 104:4 to establish Christ's divine sonship and superiority. The Christology of Hebrews 1:1-3 resonates deeply with the Johannine Prologue (John 1:1-14): both emphasize Christ as the divine Logos/Word, the agent of creation, the radiance of God's glory, and the one who reveals the otherwise invisible God. Paul's hymn in Colossians 1:15-20 mirrors this same structure—Christ as the image of God, the firstborn over creation, the sustainer of all things, and the reconciler through His blood. The phrase 'sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high' (v. 3) directly echoes Psalm 110:1, the most-quoted Old Testament verse in the New Testament, which Jesus Himself used to confound the Pharisees (Matthew 22:41-45).

The typological connections to the Pentateuch are equally profound. Just as God spoke creation into being through His Word in Genesis 1, so 'the worlds were framed by the word of God' through the Son (Hebrews 11:3). The 'radiance of His glory' (apaugasma) echoes the Shekinah glory that filled the tabernacle (Exodus 40:34-35) and the wilderness cloud, now fully manifested in the person of Christ. This note connects to [[_transcripts/Saved to the Uttermost, Hebrews 7_25.md|Saved to the Uttermost (Hebrews 7)]] where Christ's eternal priesthood is developed, and [[_transcripts/Once for All, Hebrews 10_1-14.md|Once for All (Hebrews 10)]] where His once-for-all sacrifice is contrasted with the repeated animal offerings.

## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** Hebrews 1 establishes the *Christological foundation* for every subsequent call to endurance in the epistle. The five warning passages (2:1-4; 3:7-4:13; 5:11-6:12; 10:19-39; 12:14-29) all rest on the premise established here: if the Son of God is this supreme — greater than prophets, angels, and Moses — then how much more accountable are we to hold fast our confidence and persevere in faith? This "lesser to greater" (*qal vahomer*) argument is the rhetorical engine of the entire book. The Greek *pherein* ("sustaining all things," v. 3) is a present active participle: Christ *continually* upholds the universe. This ongoing sustenance is the ground of perseverance — the same power that holds the galaxies together holds the believer secure.

**Cross-Batch Connections:** The Christology of Hebrews 1:1-3 connects backward to [[_MOC_Batch9_Matthew_Mark|Batch 9 — Matthew & Mark]] (the Transfiguration, where Peter, James, and John saw Christ's glory — Matthew 17:1-8, Mark 9:2-8) and forward to [[_MOC_06_Isaiah_Major_Prophets|Batch 6 — Major Prophets]] (Isaiah's vision of God's glory in the temple, Isaiah 6:1-5, and the Messianic prophecies of Isaiah 9:6-7, 11:1-5). The phrase "sat down at the right hand" directly fulfills Psalm 110:1, the most-quoted OT verse in the NT, connecting to [[_transcripts/Saved to the Uttermost, Hebrews 7_25.md|252 — Saved to the Uttermost]] where Christ's Melchizedekian priesthood is developed from the same psalm.

**Apocalyptic Context:** Hebrews' opening establishes the "already but not yet" tension that characterizes NT eschatology: Christ has already been exalted to the right hand of God (already), but His enemies are not yet made His footstool (not yet). This tension is the framework for perseverance — we live between the decisive victory and the final consummation, sustained by the certainty of what has been accomplished.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized that while people search for truth through many channels—philosophy, science, religion—God has given us His final and complete word in Jesus Christ. We don't need to look further or wait for another revelation. Christ is the complete picture. If we want to know what God is like, we look at Jesus. The sitting posture at God's right hand signifies that the work of salvation is finished—nothing more needs to be added.
