# 193 The Volume of the Book — Acts 8:29-35

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#193](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GYptLlg3dnw)

## Overview

Philip is led by the Spirit to approach the Ethiopian eunuch who is reading Isaiah 53 — the Suffering Servant passage. This encounter represents the gospel's expansion to a new ethnic group (a Gentile from Ethiopia, possibly a convert to Judaism or a God-fearer). The eunuch's question — 'Of whom does the prophet speak?' — becomes Philip's entry point to preach Jesus from the Scriptures.

## 📜 Historical Background

The Ethiopian eunuch was a high-ranking official in the court of Candace, the queen of Ethiopia (the ancient Kingdom of Kush, modern-day Sudan). As a eunuch, he could not fully convert to Judaism (Deuteronomy 23:1), but he was a God-fearer who had made the long pilgrimage to Jerusalem to worship. He was returning via the Gaza road (an ancient route through the Negev desert) when Philip encountered him. The gospel's expansion to an Ethiopian — a Gentile from Africa — represents a significant step beyond Samaria (Acts 8:5-25). The passage the eunuch was reading (Isaiah 53:7-8) was from the Suffering Servant song, one of the most significant messianic prophecies in the Hebrew Scriptures. Philip's approach — starting from the Scriptures and showing Jesus — became the model for Christian evangelism and apologetics.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The eunuch reading Isaiah 53 and asking 'Of whom does the prophet speak?' finds its answer in the good news of Jesus Christ, the ultimate fulfillment of the Suffering Servant. Jesus Himself quoted from the Servant songs and applied them to His mission (Luke 22:37, Mark 10:45). The eunuch's baptism 'in a certain water' (Acts 8:36-39) echoes the pattern of believer's baptism throughout Acts — immediate upon profession of faith. The Spirit's sudden snatching away of Philip parallels Elijah's translation (2 Kings 2), demonstrating that the same Spirit who empowered the prophets now empowers the evangelists. Psalm 68:31 had prophesied that 'Ethiopia would stretch out her hands to God,' fulfilled in this encounter.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often highlighted Philip's sensitivity to the Holy Spirit's leading — first told to 'arise and go' with no explanation (Acts 8:26), then told to 'join this chariot' (Acts 8:29). The divine appointment model of evangelism — knowing Scripture and following the Spirit's timing — was central to Smith's teaching. The eunuch's question is the essential question every seeker asks: 'Of whom does the prophet speak?' Smith emphasized that all Scripture ultimately points to Jesus, and the best evangelism is opening the Bible and showing people Christ. The eunuch's joy after baptism (Acts 8:39) is the natural result of finding the One whom the entire volume of the Book is about.
