## Summary

Chuck Smith explores the beautiful concept of the Goel — the Kinsman-Redeemer. When Ruth approaches Boaz on the threshing floor and says 'I am Ruth, your maidservant. Take your maidservant under your wing, for you are a close relative' (literally 'a redeemer'), she is invoking one of the most significant institutions in Israelite law. The Goel had the right and responsibility to redeem: land sold due to poverty, relatives sold into slavery, the name of a deceased relative through levirate marriage, and blood through the avenger of blood. The book of Ruth presents the Goel as a beautiful picture of Christ, our ultimate Kinsman-Redeemer who became flesh to redeem us.

## 📜 Historical Background

The book of Ruth is set 'in the days when the judges ruled' (Ruth 1:1, ca. 1100 BC). Against the dark backdrop of the Judges period — when 'every man did that which was right in his own eyes' (Judges 21:25) — Ruth shines as a story of loyalty, kindness, and redemption.

**The Significance of Names:** The Hebrew names in the story carry profound meaning, as noted in the C2000 transcript:
- **Elimelech** — 'My God is King'
- **Naomi** — 'Pleasantness' (she later asks to be called Mara, 'Bitter')
- **Mahlon** — 'Sickly'
- **Chilion** — 'Pining' (these names suggest the sons were born frail)
- **Ruth** — 'Friendship' or 'Companion'
- **Boaz** — 'In Him is Strength'
- **Obed** — 'Servant' (the child who served Naomi in her old age)
- **Orpah** — 'Stiff-necked' or 'Gazelle' (she turned back to Moab)

**The Goel Institution in Mosaic Law:** The Kinsman-Redeemer (*Goel*, from Hebrew *ga'al* — 'to redeem, to act as a kinsman') had four primary responsibilities:
1. **Redeem land** sold due to poverty (Leviticus 25:25-28) — preserving ancestral inheritance within the tribe
2. **Redeem relatives** sold into slavery (Leviticus 25:47-49) — buying back a kinsman from bondage
3. **Continue a brother's line** through levirate marriage (Deuteronomy 25:5-10) — providing an heir for a deceased brother
4. **Avenge blood** (Numbers 35:12; Deuteronomy 19:6-12) — the avenger of blood (goel haddam)

In Ruth's case, two of these functions merged: Boaz would redeem the land that Elimelech had sold (the property right) and marry Ruth to preserve Mahlon's name (the levirate duty). The nearer kinsman was willing to redeem the land but withdrew when he learned it required marrying Ruth — likely because a child born to Ruth would inherit the land and carry Mahlon's name rather than his.

**The Threshing Floor — Cultural Context:** The threshing floor was not merely a place of agricultural labor but also a place of social gathering and legal transactions at harvest time. Ruth's approach to Boaz at night, while culturally sensitive, was a bold act of faith. She was risking her reputation by following Naomi's instructions to the letter. The 'spreading of the wing' (kanaph) was a recognized marriage proposal formula in ancient Israel — sheltering a woman under one's garment signified marriage protection. The same Hebrew word is used when God says He bore Israel 'on eagles' wings' (Exodus 19:4) and in the protective imagery of Psalm 91:4 ('He shall cover you with His feathers').

**The Genealogical Significance:** Ruth, a Moabitess, is remarkably included in the genealogy of Jesus Christ (Matthew 1:5) despite the Torah's statement that 'a Moabite shall not enter the assembly of the Lord' (Deuteronomy 23:3-6). The exception proves the rule: God's grace transcends ethnic boundaries. Ruth's inclusion alongside Tamar (another unconventional inclusion), Rahab the Canaanite, and Bathsheba (the wife of Uriah the Hittite) in Matthew's genealogy demonstrates that God's redemptive plan incorporates Gentiles and sinners into the lineage of the Messiah. Ruth's son Obed became the grandfather of King David, and ultimately, through David's line, the ancestor of Jesus Christ.

**Contrast with the Judges Period:** The book of Ruth provides the counterpoint to the moral chaos of Judges. Where Judges shows Israel's unfaithfulness, Ruth shows covenant loyalty (chesed). Where Judges shows men doing 'what was right in their own eyes,' Ruth shows men acting with legal integrity at the city gate. Where Judges ends with civil war and anarchy, Ruth ends with a marriage that leads to the royal line of David.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The Goel is one of the clearest Old Testament types of Christ. Jesus is our Kinsman-Redeemer who became flesh and blood to redeem us (Hebrews 2:14-17). Like Boaz, He is both willing and able to redeem — unlike the nearer kinsman who could not (or would not). The price of our redemption was not silver or gold but His own precious blood (1 Peter 1:18-19). Paul writes that 'in Christ we have redemption through His blood, the forgiveness of sins' (Ephesians 1:7). Job's declaration — 'I know that my Redeemer lives' (Job 19:25) — finds its ultimate fulfillment in the risen Christ. Isaiah proclaims God as 'your Redeemer, the Holy One of Israel' (Isaiah 43:14; 54:5). Naomi's journey from emptiness (Ruth 1:21) to fullness (Ruth 4:14-17) mirrors the believer's journey from spiritual emptiness to redemption in Christ. The New Testament explicitly names Ruth in Matthew's genealogy (Matthew 1:5), affirming her place in redemptive history.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that the Goel reveals essential truths about God's character. First, God is our near Kinsman — He did not remain distant but drew near to us in Jesus Christ. Second, our Redeemer must be both willing and able. No one else could pay the price because no one else was sinless. Third, redemption cost the Redeemer something — Boaz had to pay a price, and Christ paid with His life. The story of Ruth demonstrates that God is at work even in ordinary, difficult circumstances, weaving a tapestry of redemption that extends from a Moabite widow to King David to the Messiah. Every believer can say with Job: 'I know that my Redeemer lives.' Smith also drew attention to the parallel between the Incarnation (John 1:14 — 'the Word became flesh') and the Goel: to be our Redeemer, Christ had to become one of us. He is our Goel who 'tabernacled' among us to buy us back from the slavery of sin.
