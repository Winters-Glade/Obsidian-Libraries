The holy city, New Jerusalem, descends from God out of heaven, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband. A great voice from heaven declares: 'Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men, and He will dwell with them, and they shall be His people, and God Himself shall be with them, and be their God. And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain: for the former things are passed away.'

## 📜 Historical Background

Revelation 21 is the glorious culmination of the entire biblical narrative, presented as an apocalyptic new creation vision drawing on Isaiah 65:17 ("For, behold, I create new heavens and a new earth") and Isaiah 66:22 ("the new heavens and the new earth"). The Greek phrase καινὴ γῆ and καινὸς οὐρανός means "new" in the sense of "renewed, transformed" (καινός), not "brand new in time" (νέος) — the old creation is not annihilated but redeemed and glorified. The New Jerusalem descending "from God out of heaven" (ἐκ τοῦ οὐρανοῦ ἀπὸ τοῦ Θεοῦ) emphasizes that salvation is entirely God's gift. The city is prepared "as a bride adorned for her husband" — marriage imagery drawn from Hosea 2:19-20, Ezekiel 16:8-14, and the entire prophetic tradition of Yahweh as Israel's husband. The tabernacle (σκηνή) language in "the tabernacle of God is with men" (ἡ σκηνὴ τοῦ Θεοῦ μετὰ τῶν ἀνθρώπων) echoes the wilderness tabernacle (Exodus 25:8-9) and the incarnation: "the Word became flesh and tabernacled (ἐσκήνωσεν) among us" (John 1:14). The new creation reverses every aspect of the Genesis curse (Genesis 3:14-19): no more death, sorrow, crying, or pain. The "Alpha and Omega" (τὸ Ἄλφα καὶ τὸ Ὦ) — the first and last letters of the Greek alphabet — is an apocalyptic inclusio framing Revelation 1:8 and 21:6, declaring God's absolute sovereignty over all history. Writing during Domitian's persecution (c. AD 95), when Christians faced death for their faith, this vision of a new creation where God personally dwells with His people was the ultimate hope — not escape from the world but its complete redemption. The apocalyptic "already but not yet" reaches its final resolution: the already of God's presence with His people (through the Spirit, in the church) becomes the not yet of unmediated, eternal dwelling. The interpretive frameworks converge here: preterists see the new creation beginning with Christ's resurrection; historicists see it unfolding through church history; futurists (including Chuck Smith) see it as the literal new heavens and new earth established after Christ's millennial reign; idealists see it as the spiritual reality of God's presence breaking into the present age.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

This is the fulfillment of God's promise throughout Scripture. Isaiah prophesied: "For, behold, I create new heavens and a new earth" (Isaiah 65:17). Jesus promised: "I go to prepare a place for you... I will come again, and receive you unto Myself" (John 14:2-3). Peter wrote: "Nevertheless we, according to His promise, look for new heavens and a new earth, wherein dwelleth righteousness" (2 Peter 3:13). The garden of Eden (Genesis 2) finds its ultimate fulfillment in the garden city of Revelation 21-22. The curse of Genesis 3 is fully reversed. In the Olivet Discourse, Jesus promised that the faithful would "inherit the earth" (Matthew 5:5), which finds its ultimate fulfillment in the new earth. Paul writes that "the creation itself also shall be delivered from the bondage of corruption into the glorious liberty of the children of God" (Romans 8:19-22), directly anticipating the new creation. The "tabernacle of God is with men" echoes the incarnation — "the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us" (John 1:14) — suggesting that the incarnation is the first step toward God's permanent dwelling with humanity. The promise "I will be his God, and he shall be My son" (Revelation 21:7) fulfills the Davidic covenant (2 Samuel 7:14) and echoes Paul's declaration that believers are "heirs of God, and joint-heirs with Christ" (Romans 8:17). The "water of life" (ὕδωρ ζωῆς) given freely (δωρεάν) echoes Jesus' invitation to the Samaritan woman (John 4:10-14) and the great invitation on the last day of the feast (John 7:37-39). From a futurist/premillennial perspective (Chuck Smith's framework), this new heaven and new earth follow Christ's millennial reign (Revelation 20), representing the eternal state after Satan's final rebellion is crushed and the Great White Throne judgment is complete.

## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** Revelation 21:1-8 is the *telos* of the entire biblical narrative — the new creation where every promise of God is fulfilled. The Greek *kainē* (new) in "new heaven and new earth" means "renewed, transformed" — the old creation is not annihilated but redeemed. The New Jerusalem descending "from God out of heaven" emphasizes that salvation is entirely God's gift. The tabernacle language — "the tabernacle of God is with men" (*hē skēnē tou Theou meta tōn anthrōpōn*) — echoes the wilderness tabernacle (Exodus 25:8-9) and the incarnation: "the Word became flesh and tabernacled among us" (John 1:14). The reversal of the Genesis curse (Genesis 3:14-19) is complete: no more death, sorrow, crying, or pain. The *inclusio* of "Alpha and Omega" (Revelation 1:8, 21:6) declares God's absolute sovereignty over all history — the same God who spoke creation into being (Alpha) brings it to its glorious consummation (Omega).

**Apocalyptic Literature Context:** The new creation vision draws on Isaiah 65:17 ("new heavens and a new earth") and 66:22, establishing continuity with OT prophetic eschatology. The New Jerusalem as a bride adorned for her husband uses marriage imagery drawn from Hosea 2:19-20, Ezekiel 16:8-14, and the entire prophetic tradition of Yahweh as Israel's husband. The "water of life" (*hydōr zōēs*) given freely (*dōrean*, "as a gift free of charge") echoes Jesus' invitation to the Samaritan woman (John 4:10-14) and the great invitation on the last day of the feast (John 7:37-39). This is apocalyptic literature's ultimate promise: not escape from the world but its complete redemption. The interpretive frameworks converge here — all schools of eschatology agree that this chapter describes the final, eternal state of the redeemed.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often described heaven as the ultimate homecoming. The God who walked with Adam in the garden in the cool of the day will again dwell with His people—this time forever. Every tear, every death, every sorrow, every pain—all the effects of sin—are gone forever. The new creation is not just a restoration of the old but something even more glorious. The promises to the overcomer: they will inherit all things, be God's children, and dwell in His presence forever. This hope is the anchor of the Christian faith.
