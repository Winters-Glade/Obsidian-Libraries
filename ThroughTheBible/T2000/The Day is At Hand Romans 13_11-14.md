# 212 The Day is At Hand — Romans 13:11-14

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#212](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e7QXckrSNOw)

## Overview

An urgent call to wake from spiritual slumber. The night is far spent and the day of salvation is at hand. Believers are to cast off the works of darkness, put on the armor of light, and make no provision for the flesh. The return of Christ gives moral urgency to holy living. This passage transitions from Paul's ethical instructions in Romans 12-13 to an eschatological motivation for righteousness — because salvation is nearer now than when we first believed, every day demands greater vigilance and purity.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Watchfulness | The believer's constant vigilance, knowing that Christ's return is imminent and each day brings us closer to that moment |
| Holiness | The urgent call to cast off the deeds of darkness and walk in the light, motivated by the approaching day of salvation |
| Eschatology | The nearness of salvation transforms present morality — eschatology is not speculative but practical, driving holy living |
|| [[Sanctification]] | To "put on Christ" is to clothe oneself with His character and make no provision for gratifying the flesh |
| Walking in the Light | The contrast between darkness and light as ethical categories — believers belong to the day and should live accordingly |

## Pauline Context

Romans 13:11-14 connects Paul's ethical teaching (Romans 12-13) to his eschatological framework. For Paul, the "already but not yet" tension of the Christian life means that present ethics are always shaped by future hope. The metaphor of waking from sleep appears throughout Paul's letters (Ephesians 5:14, 1 Thessalonians 5:6) and always carries moral urgency. The "armor of light" anticipates the full armor of God in Ephesians 6:10-18 and the breastplate of faith and love in 1 Thessalonians 5:8. Paul's command to "make no provision for the flesh" (pronoid to the flesh) is the negative counterpart to his positive command to "put on Christ" — the Christian life involves both active virtue and active avoidance of temptation.
