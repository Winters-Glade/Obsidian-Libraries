## Summary

Hosea 12:10 records God's testimony: "I have also spoken by the prophets, and have multiplied visions, and used similitudes, by the ministry of the prophets." Chuck Smith highlights God's persistent, patient witness to His people through multiple channels — prophets, visions, and parables (similitudes). Despite Israel's ongoing unfaithfulness, God never left Himself without a witness. Smith emphasizes that God goes to great lengths to communicate with His people, using every means available to call them back. The tragedy is not a silent God but a deaf people.

## 📜 Historical Background

Hosea 12 draws a parallel between the patriarch Jacob and the nation Israel. Jacob, whose name means "supplanter," was a deceiver who struggled with God and man yet prevailed (v. 3-4). Israel in Hosea's day had followed Jacob's fleshly pattern of deceit and reliance on human cunning — making treaties with Assyria and Egypt, using dishonest scales, and trusting in wealth. But unlike Jacob, they had not wrestled with God and prevailed.

The verse highlights the richness of God's revelatory methods. "I have spoken by the prophets" refers to the direct verbal proclamation of God's word. "Multiplied visions" refers to the prophetic visions given to seers like Isaiah, Amos, and Hosea himself. "Similitudes" (parables, symbolic acts) includes Hosea's own marriage to Gomer — a living parable of God's relationship with Israel. God had left no stone unturned in reaching His people.

The prophetic activity that Hosea describes — "I have spoken by the prophets, and multiplied visions, and used similitudes" — is illuminated by the broader ancient Near Eastern prophetic tradition. At Mari (modern Tell Hariri on the Euphrates), over 50 prophetic texts from the 18th century BC have been discovered, documenting prophets who delivered oracles to the king concerning political and military affairs. These Mari prophets, like the Hebrew prophets, delivered messages in the name of their god (often Dagan or Adad), using the formula "Thus says the god." The existence of this broader prophetic phenomenon confirms that Israel's prophets operated within a recognizable ancient Near Eastern institution, but with a distinctive theological content: Yahweh's calls for covenant faithfulness, social justice, and exclusive loyalty were unique to Israel's prophetic tradition.

The "visions" (chazon) that God multiplied are attested in the archaeological record through a fascinating discovery: the Lachish Ostraca, a collection of 21 inscribed pottery fragments from the early 6th century BC found at Tel Lachish. These letters, written on the eve of the Babylonian destruction, mention prophets and prophetic activity (e.g., Ostracon III: "Who is your servant, a dog, that my lord should remember the letter which I sent? ... And my lord said, 'Do you not know that they are a wall? They are watching'"). While later than Hosea, these ostraca confirm the ongoing vitality of prophetic communication in ancient Israel. The prophets were not marginal figures but central to the nation's political and spiritual life — precisely because God had "multiplied visions" to reach His people.

The "similitudes" (mashal, parables) that God used — including Hosea's own marriage to Gomer — belong to a well-attested genre of symbolic prophetic acts in the ancient Near East. The Neo-Assyrian records describe a prophet of Ishtar who acted out the fall of Elam by trampling a piece of earth in a symbolic act. Similarly, the Babylonian "prophetic" texts known as the Marduk Prophecy and the Shulgi Prophecy use symbolic language to describe divine action in history. Hosea's marriage to a prostitute, Jeremiah's yoke, and Ezekiel's siege of a brick all belong to this genre of enacted prophecy — a form of communication designed to bypass intellectual resistance and confront the audience with an unforgettable visual sermon. The abundance of these methods, as Hosea 12:10 emphasizes, demonstrates God's relentless pursuit of His wayward people.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 1_1-2.md]] provides the New Testament counterpart: "God, who at various times and in various ways spoke in time past to the fathers by the prophets, has in these last days spoken to us by His Son." The progression of revelation culminates in Jesus Christ, the ultimate Word. Jesus Himself frequently taught in parables (similitudes), continuing the prophetic tradition. In [[John 14_9]], He tells Philip, "He who has seen Me has seen the Father." God's patient speaking finds its fullest expression in the incarnate Word. This [[Prophecy]] of God's persistent witness reaches its goal in the [[Messiah]], who is the final Word. The [[Judgment]] Hosea announced against Israel was not God's last word — [[Restoration]] and a [[Remnant]] were always in view.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith draws a practical challenge from this passage: if God has spoken so clearly and persistently, why are we not listening? He encourages believers to recognize the many ways God still speaks — through Scripture, through preaching, through the still small voice of the Holy Spirit, through circumstances, and through the counsel of other believers. The problem is rarely a lack of communication from God; it is a lack of attentive listening. Smith urges his congregation to cultivate a posture of readiness to hear, to keep a journal of what God is saying, and to respond promptly when God speaks.

## 📖 Key Hebrew Terms

- **Navi** (נביא) — 'prophet, spokesman' — The standard Hebrew word for prophet. It suggests one who is called and authorized to speak on behalf of another. When Moses was made 'god' to Pharaoh and Aaron his 'prophet' (Exodus 7:1), the prophet is one who speaks for God.
- **Chazon** (חזון) — 'vision, revelation' — 'I have multiplied visions.' A chazon is a prophetic vision or oracle. The plural here emphasizes the abundance of divine revelation — not a single glimpse but a cascade of visions.
- **Mashal** (משל) — 'similitude, parable, proverb' — The word translated as 'similitudes.' It means a comparison, a figurative saying, or a parabolic teaching. This is the same root used for Solomon's proverbs (Mishlei) and for the parables of the prophets. God used every genre of communication to reach His people.
- **Dibber** (דבר) — 'spoke' (from davar, word) — The verb emphasizes the personal nature of God's communication. God does not merely send information; He speaks — a relational act. The same verb is used in the Ten Commandments: 'God spoke all these words.'
