## Summary

After being sold into slavery, Joseph finds himself in the house of Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh. Despite his circumstances — far from home, a slave in a foreign land — the Lord is with Joseph, and everything he does prospers. Potiphar recognizes this and puts Joseph in charge of his entire household. Then comes the test: Potiphar's wife repeatedly attempts to seduce Joseph. His response reveals the depth of his character. He refuses not merely on the grounds of loyalty to Potiphar but on the highest possible grounds: "How then can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God?" When she finally seizes him by his garment, he flees — leaving his coat in her hand. Falsely accused, Joseph is cast into prison. Yet even there, the Lord is with him, and the same pattern of faithful stewardship and divine blessing continues.

## Key Takeaways

1. The presence of God is not dependent on our circumstances — He is with us in the pit, the palace, and the prison.
2. Joseph's refusal of Potiphar's wife was rooted in his relationship with God, not just loyalty to man.
3. The question "How can I sin against God?" is the ultimate safeguard against temptation.
4. Sometimes doing the right thing leads to temporary suffering — Joseph went from the house to the prison for his integrity.
5. Fleeing is a legitimate and biblical response to sexual temptation (1 Corinthians 6:18, 2 Timothy 2:22).
6. God's faithfulness is not dependent on our circumstances — He was with Joseph in the prison just as He was in the palace.

## Further Context

### Historical & Cultural Background
Potiphar's title "captain of the guard" (*sar hatabbachim*, Gen 39:1) likely identifies him as the captain of Pharaoh's bodyguard or the chief executioner — a position of significant authority. Egyptian household stewards were often entrusted with full management of their master's affairs, as archaeological evidence from the Middle Kingdom period confirms. The attempted seduction by Potiphar's wife reflects the well-documented autonomy of Egyptian women of the upper class, who enjoyed greater legal and social freedoms than women in many other ancient societies. Joseph's response — leaving his garment behind when he fled — was a culturally significant act: in Egyptian art, grabbing a man's garment was a gesture of sexual intent, and leaving it behind signified complete rejection.

### Old Testament Connections
- **Proverbs 5:1-23** — The extended warning against the "strange woman" whose lips drip honey but whose end is bitter as wormwood — a wisdom reflection on exactly the kind of temptation Joseph faced.
- **Proverbs 6:20-35** — "Whoso committeth adultery with a woman lacketh understanding: he that doeth it destroyeth his own soul" — the wisdom literature's perspective on Joseph's choice.
- **Proverbs 7:4-27** — The seduction of the young man by the adulteress — almost a commentary on Joseph's temptation, with the crucial difference that Joseph fled rather than followed.
- **2 Samuel 11:1-5** — David's fall with Bathsheba is the negative contrast to Joseph's triumph: David saw, lingered, and fell; Joseph saw, fled, and stood.
- **Job 31:1** — "I made a covenant with mine eyes; why then should I think upon a maid?" — Job's preventative discipline, like Joseph's immediate flight.
- **Psalm 119:11** — "Thy word have I hid in mine heart, that I might not sin against thee" — the hidden Word as defence against temptation.

### New Testament Connections
- **1 Corinthians 6:18-20** — "Flee fornication... he that committeth fornication sinneth against his own body" — Paul's command to flee mirrors Joseph's response.
- **1 Corinthians 10:13** — "There hath no temptation taken you but such as is common to man: but God is faithful, who will not suffer you to be tempted above that ye are able" — Joseph's example shows that God always provides a way of escape.
- **2 Timothy 2:22** — "Flee also youthful lusts: but follow righteousness, faith, charity, peace" — the pattern of fleeing and pursuing.
- **Hebrews 13:4** — "Marriage is honourable in all, and the bed undefiled: but whoremongers and adulterers God will judge" — the New Testament reaffirms the sanctity Joseph protected.
- **1 Peter 5:8-9** — "Be sober, be vigilant; because your adversary the devil, as a roaring lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour: whom resist stedfast in the faith" — resisting temptation requires active vigilance.
- **James 4:7** — "Submit yourselves therefore to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you" — Joseph's submission to God and resistance to temptation.

### Extra-Biblical Sources
- The Egyptian *Instruction of Ptahhotep* (c. 2400 BC) contains ethical teachings that value honesty, integrity, and self-control — showing that Joseph's moral framework was recognizable within Egyptian wisdom traditions.
- Jewish tradition (Genesis Rabbah 87) portrays Potiphar's wife as relentlessly pursuing Joseph, changing her clothes several times a day to attract him, and employing astrologers to predict her union with Joseph — making his resistance all the more remarkable.
- The Coptic Christian tradition honours Joseph as "the chaste" (*el-Aff*) and the model of purity, with numerous churches named after him.
