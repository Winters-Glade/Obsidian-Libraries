## Summary

Psalm 118 celebrates the stone that the builders rejected becoming the chief cornerstone. 'This is the day which the Lord hath made; we will rejoice and be glad in it' is a declaration of joy rooted in God's deliverance. This is a key [[Prophecy|Messianic prophecy]] pointing to the [[Messiah]] who would be rejected by Israel's leaders yet become the foundation of salvation.

## 📜 Historical Background

Psalm 118 is the climax of the Hallel psalms (113-118) sung at Passover. The 'stone which the builders rejected' was a proverb for something seemingly worthless that becomes invaluable. It likely referred to Israel herself — despised by the nations but chosen by God.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus quotes Psalm 118:22-23 in Matthew 21:42, applying the rejected stone to Himself. Peter declares Jesus 'the stone which was set at nought of you builders, which is become the head of the corner' (Acts 4:11).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that each day is a gift from God, and we are called to rejoice in it regardless of circumstances. The stone rejected by men has become the cornerstone — what the world rejects, God uses as the foundation.

## 📚 Hebrew Poetry & Worship Context

Psalm 118 is the climax of the **Hallel** (Psalms 113-118) — recited at Passover and the pilgrimage festivals. The "stone which the builders rejected" (verse 22) uses *rosh pinnah* (רֹאשׁ פִּנָּה) — "head of the corner," the capstone determining a building's alignment. Jesus and His disciples sang this very psalm at the Last Supper (Matthew 26:30). The phrase "This is the day" was part of the Hallel procession.

**Worship Significance:** From Passover temple liturgy to the triumphal entry (Matthew 21:9), this psalm has been the song of redemption for both Israel and the Church. It reminds worshippers that each day's joy is rooted in God's act of salvation.

## 📖 Research Context

### The Hallel — Passover Liturgy

Psalm 118 is part of the **Egyptian Hallel** (Psalms 113-118), a collection recited at the major Jewish festivals — especially **Passover**. The Hallel was sung during the Passover meal, and Psalm 118 was the climax. Verses 22-24 were sung at the conclusion of the Passover ceremony.

It was almost certainly this hymn that Jesus and His disciples sang after the Last Supper (Matthew 26:30; Mark 14:26). The "hymn" (*hymnos*) was the Hallel — meaning Jesus sang the very words about the rejected stone on the night of His betrayal.

### The Rejected Stone — Messianic Prophecy

The Hebrew **rosh pinnah** (רֹאשׁ פִּנָּה) — "head of the corner" or "chief cornerstone" — is one of the most quoted Old Testament passages in the New Testament:
- Jesus quotes it (Matthew 21:42)
- Peter quotes it (Acts 4:11)
- Peter applies it to the Church as living stones (1 Peter 2:5-7)
- Paul uses it for the foundation of Christ (Ephesians 2:20)

The cornerstone determined the alignment of the entire structure. The "builders" (religious leaders) rejected the very stone that was essential — simultaneously a prophecy of Christ's rejection and exaltation.

### "This Is the Day"

Verse 24 — "This is the day which the LORD hath made" — has been understood liturgically (the feast day) and messianically (the day of resurrection). The early Church appropriated this verse for Sunday — the Lord's Day.
