Pastor Chuck explores how the Law was not merely a set of rules but a revelation of God's holiness designed to restrain sin and drive people to grace.

## 📜 Historical Background

Exodus 20 records the giving of the Ten Commandments at Mount Sinai — the most significant legal and covenantal event in the Old Testament. The narrative backdrop is theophanic: "thunders and lightnings, and a thick cloud upon the mount, and the voice of the trumpet exceeding loud" (Exodus 19:16). The mountain quaked, smoke billowed as from a furnace, and the people trembled. Moses explains in Exodus 20:20 that God's purpose was not to destroy but **לְנַסּוֹת** (*lenassot* — "to prove, test, assay"). This verb is used elsewhere of testing metal to reveal its purity (Zechariah 13:9; Proverbs 17:3). The giving of the Law was thus a divine assay of the human heart — exposing what lay within.

The Hebrew phrase **יִרְאַת אֱלֹהִים** (*yirat Elohim* — "the fear of God") in this context combines awe, reverence, and healthy dread. In the ANE, the concept of "fear of the god" (*puluhtu* in Akkadian) was a virtue ascribed to the ideal king — one who governed with reverent awareness of the deity's authority. Israel, however, was called to this fear *as a nation*, democratizing what other cultures reserved for royalty. This "fear" was not paralyzing terror but a reverent awareness that produces obedience — "that you sin not."

The Ten Commandments (עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדְּבָרִים, *Aseret ha'Devarim* — "the Ten Words") follow the structural pattern of ANE suzerain-vassal treaties from the Late Bronze Age (c. 1400–1200 BC). Like the Hittite vassal treaties of Suppiluliuma I, they begin with a preamble identifying the Great King ("I am the LORD your God"), followed by a historical prologue ("who brought you out of the land of Egypt"), then stipulations, and finally blessings and curses (Exodus 23:20-33). This form demonstrates that the Law was given within a *covenant relationship*, not as abstract rules. Obedience was the vassal's loyal response to the Suzerain's gracious deliverance.

The archaeological discovery of the **Nuzi Tablets** (c. 1450 BC, from Yorghan Tepe near Kirkuk) and the **Mari Letters** (c. 1800 BC) provide rich context for the legal and social customs reflected in the Mosaic Law. The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1754 BC), while sharing some casuistic formulations with Exodus 21-23, differs fundamentally: Hammurabi's code reflects a king's wisdom; the Mosaic Law reflects divine revelation anchored in redemption. The Law does not begin with "I am the LORD, I give you laws to order society" but "I am the LORD *your God who brought you out of Egypt*" — grace precedes obligation.

The "Book of the Covenant" (Exodus 20:22–23:33) that follows the Decalogue provides case-law applications (*mishpatim*) of the Ten Words. The Hebrew **מִשְׁפָּטִים** (*mishpatim* — "judgments") refers to binding legal decisions that establish social justice, reflecting a God who cares about the widow, orphan, and sojourner (Exodus 22:21-24) — a revolutionary concept in an ancient world where law typically protected the powerful.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul develops the purpose of the Law extensively in Romans and Galatians. The Law, he explains, was a **παιδαγωγός** (*paidagogos* — "schoolmaster, tutor, guardian") to bring us to Christ (Galatians 3:24). In Greco-Roman households, the *paidagogos* was a trusted slave who disciplined and supervised the child until adulthood — not the teacher himself, but the one who prepared the child for the teacher. The Law's role was to restrain sin, expose sin, and drive us to the grace found in Christ.

"By the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified in His sight: for by the law is the knowledge of sin" (Romans 3:20). The Law diagnoses the disease but cannot cure it — that is the work of grace. Jeremiah's prophecy of a New Covenant (Jeremiah 31:31-34) anticipated a time when the Law would be written on hearts, not stone tablets — fulfilled in the gift of the Holy Spirit (2 Corinthians 3:3-6).

Jesus Himself summed up the Law not as a means of salvation but as the standard of love: love for God and love for neighbor (Matthew 22:37-40). He did not come to destroy the Law but to fulfill it (Matthew 5:17). His Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7) repeatedly intensifies the Law's demands — moving from external compliance to internal transformation — proving that only a radical grace can meet such a standard.

The writer of Hebrews contrasts Sinai with Mount Zion — the Law given with trembling, versus grace accessed with boldness (Hebrews 12:18-24). Yet both reveal the same God: the fire at Sinai and the fire of the Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2:3) both manifest divine presence. The difference is that the Law was written on stone, but the Spirit writes on living hearts (2 Corinthians 3:3).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often taught that a healthy fear of God — reverential awe, not terror — is the beginning of wisdom (Proverbs 9:10). The Law's purpose was to set boundaries that protect life and community. When we understand that God's commands are not arbitrary restrictions but loving guidelines for human flourishing, we begin to see the Law differently. "The fear of the Lord is clean, enduring forever" (Psalm 19:9).

Grace does not nullify the Law but fulfills it, enabling us to live in a way that honors God from the heart rather than mere external compliance. The believer who has been justified by faith is freed to obey the Law's true intent — not to earn salvation, but as the delighted response of a redeemed heart. As Augustine famously wrote, "Love God and do as you please" — for the one who truly loves God will delight to do His will.

The dual purpose of the Law — to restrain sin *and* to reveal our need for grace — remains pastorally relevant. Some Christians drift into legalism, measuring spirituality by rule-keeping; others drift into license, ignoring the Law's moral instruction entirely. The balanced view is that the Law, while powerless to save, remains a valuable guide for righteous living (2 Timothy 3:16-17). The redeemed heart says with David, "O how love I Thy law! it is my meditation all the day" (Psalm 119:97).
