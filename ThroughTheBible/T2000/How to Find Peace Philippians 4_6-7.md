# 236 How to Find Peace — Philippians 4:6-7

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#236](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ybCniAh9yDA)

## Overview

"Be careful for nothing; but in every thing by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known unto God. And the peace of God, which passeth all understanding, shall keep your hearts and minds through Christ Jesus." Paul presents the antidote to anxiety: prayer with thanksgiving. The result is not merely a feeling but the peace of God itself — a supernatural guard over the heart and mind.

This passage is one of the most beloved and practical in all of Paul's letters. The command "be careful for nothing" (mēden merimnate) means "stop worrying about anything" — a present imperative calling for a decisive break with anxiety. The alternative is prayer in all its forms: "prayer" (proseuchē) — general worship and petition; "supplication" (deēsis) — specific requests for needs; and "thanksgiving" (eucharistia) — gratitude for what God has already done. The Greek verb for "keep" (phrourēsei) is a military term — it means to guard as a sentinel keeps watch. God's peace stands sentry over the believer's heart and mind, protecting them from anxiety's invasion. Remarkably, Paul wrote this from prison, facing possible execution — lived theology of the highest order.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Peace | The peace of God is not the absence of trouble but the presence of God's tranquility in the midst of trouble — a peace that surpasses human understanding |
| Prayer | Prayer is the comprehensive remedy for anxiety — it includes worship (prayer), specific requests (supplication), and gratitude (thanksgiving) |
| Anxiety | Worry is treated as a sin to be renounced, not a personality trait to be managed — it reflects a failure to trust God's care |
| Thanksgiving | Gratitude is the essential accompaniment to petition — remembering past faithfulness combats fear about future needs |
| Trust in God | The peace that guards the heart comes not from circumstances being resolved but from trust in the God who controls all circumstances |

## Background & Context

Philippians 4 is Paul's final exhortation to a church he deeply loved. The letter is filled with joy (chairein and related words appear 16 times in 4 chapters), yet Paul was writing from chains. This paradox — joy in suffering — is the letter's central theme. The prescription for anxiety in 4:6-7 is followed by the command to think on whatever is true, noble, just, pure, lovely, and praiseworthy (4:8) — a holistic approach to mental and spiritual health that modern psychology increasingly validates. Paul's phrase "the peace of God, which passes all understanding" suggests a peace that transcends intellectual explanation — it works even when circumstances don't make sense. He then adds "through Christ Jesus" — the peace is mediated through union with the risen Lord.
