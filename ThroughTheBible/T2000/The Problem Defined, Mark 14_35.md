## Summary

Mark 14:35 takes us into the Garden of Gethsemane on the night of Jesus' betrayal. He falls on the ground and prays that if it were possible, the hour might pass from Him. Pastor Chuck explores the profound mystery of Christ's agony in the garden — not merely the anticipation of physical suffering but the weight of bearing the sin of the world. Jesus' prayer, 'Abba, Father, all things are possible unto Thee; take away this cup from Me: nevertheless not what I will, but what Thou wilt,' models perfect submission to the Father's will. The 'problem defined' is the cup of divine wrath that only the Son could drink.

## 📜 Historical Background

Gethsemane (meaning 'oil press') was a garden at the foot of the Mount of Olives, across the Kidron Valley from Jerusalem. It was a place Jesus and His disciples often visited. The agony Jesus experienced was so intense that Luke describes His sweat becoming 'as it were great drops of blood falling down to the ground' (Luke 22:44) — a rare medical phenomenon called hematohidrosis that can occur under extreme stress. The 'cup' Jesus prays to have removed is not merely the suffering of crucifixion but the full weight of God's wrath against sin — the cup of God's fury described in the Old Testament (Isaiah 51:17, Jeremiah 25:15, Ezekiel 23:31-34). The cup that Jesus drank was the cup of judgment that we deserve.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Gospel of Mark:** Gethsemane was located on the lower slopes of the Mount of Olives, a hillside covered with olive groves that had been cultivated for centuries. The name itself (from the Hebrew _gat shemanim_, 'oil press') indicates that olives were pressed there for oil — a fitting location for a moment of crushing pressure. Jesus' prayer in Gethsemane is recorded with striking intimacy in Mark's Gospel: He addresses God as 'Abba, Father' (Mark 14:36) — an Aramaic term of intimate family address that appears in only one other New Testament passage (Romans 8:15, Galatians 4:6). The use of 'Abba' in Mark is particularly significant because this Gospel, addressed to a Roman-Gentile audience, preserves the original Aramaic word rather than translating it, suggesting the term was treasured in early Christian memory. The 'cup' metaphor drew on a rich Old Testament tradition where wine/the cup represented God's judgment (Psalm 75:8, Isaiah 51:17, Jeremiah 25:15, Revelation 14:10). Jesus' submission — 'nevertheless, not what I will, but what Thou wilt' — models the perfect obedience that Mark emphasizes throughout his Gospel: the Son of God who came not to be served but to serve and to give His life a ransom for many (Mark 10:45).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Hebrews 5:7-8 describes Jesus' prayers with 'strong crying and tears' and notes that He 'learned obedience by the things which He suffered.' Luke 22:39-46 provides the most detailed account of Gethsemane, including the angel strengthening Him. John's Gospel records Jesus' prayer of intercession (John 17) rather than the agony in Gethsemane. Paul writes in 2 Corinthians 5:21 that God 'made Him to be sin for us, who knew no sin.' Philippians 2:8 describes Jesus' humility and obedience 'even unto death.' Revelation 14:10 speaks of those who 'drink of the wine of the wrath of God' — but Jesus drank that cup so we wouldn't have to.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often pointed to Gethsemane as the place where Jesus' humanity and divinity met in perfect tension. His humanity shrank from the cup; His divinity submitted to the Father's will. The key phrase is 'nevertheless': not my will, but Thine. This is the pattern for every believer facing suffering or impossible circumstances. The problem defined in Gethsemane is that our sin required a price only the Son of God could pay — and He paid it willingly, not because it was easy, but because He loved us.
