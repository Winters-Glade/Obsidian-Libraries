# 192 This Life — Acts 5:12-20

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#192](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_RWzg42BmxQ)

## Overview

The early church is growing rapidly despite opposition. After the judgment of Ananias and Sapphira (Acts 5:1-11), the apostles continue performing many signs and wonders. This leads to jealousy from the religious leaders, resulting in the apostles' arrest. Their miraculous release by an angel and subsequent return to teaching in the temple demonstrates the unstoppable nature of the gospel.

## 📜 Historical Background

The events of Acts 5 follow immediately after the dramatic judgment of Ananias and Sapphira (Acts 5:1-11), a sobering episode that produced great fear in the early church. Despite this internal purging, the apostles continued to perform signs and wonders publicly in Solomon's Porch — the eastern colonnade of the Temple Mount, a covered area where Jesus Himself had taught (John 10:23). The religious leaders' jealousy reflected the high stakes of first-century Judaism: the Sadducees controlled the Temple establishment, and the apostles' message of resurrection directly contradicted their theology. The angelic release from prison echoes Old Testament deliverances (Daniel 6, 2 Kings 6). The apostles' response — returning immediately to the temple to teach — demonstrates the irresistible momentum of the early church, described by Gamaliel in Acts 5:38-39 as a movement that, if from God, cannot be overthrown.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The pattern of apostolic imprisonment, supernatural deliverance, and bold proclamation recurs throughout Acts (Acts 12:1-19, Acts 16:16-34). The angelic ministry here reflects the teaching of Hebrews 1:14 that angels are 'ministering spirits sent to serve those who will inherit salvation.' This episode also parallels Paul's later experience in Philippi (Acts 16), where an earthquake opens prison doors. The apostles' refusal to stop preaching despite the Sanhedrin's orders echoes Jesus' promise that His followers would be brought before rulers and councils but should not worry about what to say (Matthew 10:18-20).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith viewed this passage as a powerful illustration of the unstoppable nature of God's work. When the religious leaders tried to suppress the gospel, it only spread further. The angelic deliverance reminds believers that God sees every unjust imprisonment and has resources beyond human imagination. Smith often said that the church grows best under pressure — persecution purifies motives and forces dependence on God. The apostles' immediate return to the temple after their release challenges believers to maintain boldness even after miraculous deliverance, not retreating into safety but pressing into the very place of opposition.
