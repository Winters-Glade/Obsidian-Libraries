## Summary

Daniel 12 contains the concluding prophecy of Daniel's book, including a specific time frame for the end times and the promise of resurrection. 'How long till the end?' is the question that echoes through this chapter. Pastor Chuck explores the answer: a time of unprecedented trouble, the deliverance of God's people, the resurrection of the just and unjust, and the promise that those who turn many to righteousness will shine as the stars forever.

## 📜 Historical Background

Daniel 12 is the final chapter of Daniel's prophecies, concluding the extended vision that began in chapter 10. The chapter's reference to 'a time, times, and an half' (3.5 years) and '1290 days' and '1335 days' has generated extensive interpretive discussion. These time indicators connect to the 'abomination of desolation' mentioned in [[Daniel 9_27]] and [[Daniel 11_31.md]] and referenced by Jesus in [[Matthew 24_15.md]]. The promise of resurrection in verse 2 is one of the clearest Old Testament statements of a bodily resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked. The chapter closes with Daniel assured of his own rest and inheritance at the end of days. The [[Prophecy]] here echoes the visions of [[Isaiah]], [[Jeremiah]], and [[Ezekiel]], who also spoke of a coming [[Judgment]] and [[Restoration]] of a [[Remnant]].

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[Daniel 12_2]] is a foundational Old Testament text for the doctrine of the resurrection. Jesus affirms this directly in [[John 5_28-29.md]]: 'the hour is coming, in the which all that are in the graves shall hear his voice, and shall come forth; they that have done good, unto the resurrection of life; and they that have done evil, unto the resurrection of damnation.' The time of trouble ([[Daniel 12_1]]) is referenced by Jesus as the 'great tribulation' ([[Matthew 24_21.md]]). The promise that 'the wise shall shine like the brightness of the firmament' prefigures the glorified body described in [[1 Corinthians 15_41-42.md]].

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the book of Daniel ends with a purpose — not just to satisfy curiosity about the future but to motivate faithful living in the present. 'They that be wise shall shine as the brightness of the firmament; and they that turn many to righteousness as the stars forever and ever.' The greatest wisdom is not knowing the exact timetable but winning souls and living righteously. Daniel himself is told to 'go thy way till the end' — a call to faithful service until the Lord returns. This echoes the [[servant|Servant of the Lord]] theme found in [[Isaiah]] and points to the [[Messiah]] who will reign at the end of days. Even the pagan kings like [[Nebuchadnezzar]] and [[Cyrus]] played a role in God's prophetic timetable.
