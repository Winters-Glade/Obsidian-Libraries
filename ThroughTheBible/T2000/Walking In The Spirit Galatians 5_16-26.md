# 230 Walking In The Spirit — Galatians 5:16-26

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#230](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dnOJ3hVEcbQ)

## Overview

Paul contrasts the works of the flesh with the fruit of the Spirit. "Walk in the Spirit, and ye shall not fulfill the lust of the flesh." The flesh and the Spirit are in direct opposition. Those who belong to Christ have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires. If we live in the Spirit, let us also walk in the Spirit — a life marked by love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control.

This passage is the practical climax of Galatians. After establishing [[justification]] by faith alone (chapters 1-4), Paul now addresses the inevitable question: "Shall we sin because we are not under law?" His answer is the Spirit-filled life. Freedom from the law is not freedom to sin but freedom to serve God by the power of the indwelling Spirit. The contrast between "works of the flesh" (erga tēs sarkos — plural, suggesting individual deeds) and "fruit of the Spirit" (karpos tou pneumatos — singular, suggesting a unified character) is intentional. The flesh produces many kinds of sin; the Spirit produces one integrated Christlike character.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Holy Spirit | The Spirit is not an abstract force but the personal agent of [[Sanctification]] who indwells and empowers every believer |
| Sanctification | Holiness is not achieved by law-keeping but produced by Spirit-walking — the Spirit forms Christ's character in us |
| Fruit of the Spirit | Nine-fold character of Christ (love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control) — the natural result of Spirit-filled living |
| Works of the Flesh | Paul's catalogue of fifteen sins (sexual, religious, relational, and destructive) that exclude from the Kingdom |
| Walking in the Spirit | A moment-by-moment dependence on the Spirit's leading — the daily discipline of yielding to His direction |

## Background & Context

Paul's Galatian opponents taught that freedom from the law would lead to moral chaos. Paul turns this argument on its head: the law never produced righteousness, but the Spirit does. The "flesh" (sarx) in Paul's usage is not merely the physical body but the whole fallen human nature in rebellion against God. The "crucifixion of the flesh" in verse 24 echoes the "crucified with Christ" theme from Galatians 2:20 (Note 228). The nine-fold fruit of the Spirit is often compared to the 613 commands of the Old Testament law — the Spirit produces in the believer what the law could only demand. This passage also echoes Christ's teaching in Matthew 7:15-20 about knowing a tree by its fruit.
