## Summary

Zechariah 12:10 is one of the most striking Messianic prophecies in the Old Testament: 'And I will pour upon the house of David, and upon the inhabitants of Jerusalem, the spirit of grace and of supplications: and they shall look upon Me whom they have pierced, and they shall mourn for Him, as one mourneth for his only son, and shall be in bitterness for Him, as one that is in bitterness for his firstborn.' Pastor Chuck Smith expounds this passage as a prophecy of both the first and second comings of Christ — the piercing on the cross and the future outpouring of grace when Israel looks upon the One they pierced and repents. It is a vision of national repentance, spiritual cleansing, and the ultimate restoration of Israel in the end times.

## 📜 Historical Background

Zechariah 12 is part of the 'Burden of the Word of the Lord' section (chapters 12-14) that deals with the end-times siege of Jerusalem and the deliverance of the Lord. This chapter describes a future day when all nations gather against Jerusalem, but God supernaturally intervenes to save His city. In the midst of this military deliverance, an even greater spiritual deliverance occurs: God pours out the Spirit of grace and supplication, and the people look upon the One they pierced. The phrase 'Me whom they have pierced' is remarkable — God identifies Himself with the pierced One, affirming the deity of the Messiah. The mourning described is both individual (as for an only son) and corporate (the whole land mourning), pointing to the deepest possible repentance. The chapter transitions directly into Zechariah 13:1: 'In that day there shall be a fountain opened to the house of David and to the inhabitants of Jerusalem for sin and for uncleanness.'

**The Piercing Motif in Archaeological and Textual Context.** The Hebrew verb *dakar* (דקר) used in Zechariah 12:10 means 'to pierce, thrust through' — a violent penetrating wound. This is the same verb used in 2 Samuel 1:15-16 for the Amalekite's killing of Saul and in Zechariah 13:3 for the piercing of a false prophet. The motif of a pierced messianic figure is unique to Zechariah in the Hebrew Scriptures. Archaeological evidence from the Second Temple period confirms the practice of crucifixion and spear-thrusting as Roman execution methods. The burial of a crucified man named Yehohanan, discovered in 1968 at Giv'at ha-Mivtar in Jerusalem, shows an ossuary containing a heel bone pierced by a 4.5-inch iron nail, providing tangible archaeological evidence of crucifixion in first-century Judaea. While this is post-Zechariah, it confirms the brutal reality of the piercing Zechariah prophesied would befall the Messiah.

**Hadad-Rimmon and the Mourning Motif (Zechariah 12:11).** The mourning in Zechariah 12:10-14 is compared in verse 11 to 'the mourning at Hadad-Rimmon in the plain of Megiddo.' Hadad-Rimmon is likely a location near Megiddo (possibly modern Rummaneh), associated with the mourning for King Josiah, who was killed there in battle against Pharaoh Necho in 609 BC (2 Kings 23:29-30; 2 Chronicles 35:20-25). The mourning for Josiah set a cultural pattern for deepest grief in Israel's history — the prophet Jeremiah composed laments for Josiah that became a tradition. The site of Megiddo (Tel Megiddo) has been extensively excavated, revealing twenty strata of occupation and confirming its strategic importance as a fortress city controlling the Via Maris. The comparison of the mourning for the pierced Messiah to the mourning for Josiah suggests the scale of national grief that will accompany Israel's repentance — the grief of a nation mourning its greatest king, multiplied infinitely by the recognition that they rejected and pierced their divine King.

**The Siloam Pool and Ritual Cleansing.** Zechariah 12:10 leads directly into Zechariah 13:1: 'a fountain shall be opened... for sin and for uncleanness.' The Pool of Siloam (Shiloah), excavated in Jerusalem and uncovered archaeologically in 2004, was a major water reservoir from the Second Temple period, fed by Hezekiah's tunnel (2 Kings 20:20). The Siloam Inscription, discovered in 1880 in Hezekiah's Tunnel, records the dramatic meeting of the two tunneling crews and dates the tunnel to the 8th century BC. In Jesus' ministry, the Pool of Siloam was the site where He healed a man born blind, sending him to wash in its waters (John 9:7). The juxtaposition in Zechariah 12-13 of 'piercing' (the wounding of the Messiah) and 'a fountain opened for cleansing' (the provision of purification) prefigures the Christian understanding that from the pierced side of Christ flow both the Spirit (the spirit of grace) and the cleansing water of salvation (John 19:34; 1 John 5:6-8). The Dead Sea Scrolls (4QXII) preserve Zechariah 12-13 in the Minor Prophets scroll, showing that this connected passage was read as a unified prophecy of end-times cleansing.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Zechariah 12:10 is quoted in the New Testament in John 19:37, where John sees its fulfillment in the piercing of Jesus' side on the cross: 'They shall look on Him whom they pierced.' The piercing happened historically at Calvary, but the 'looking' — the recognition and repentance — is still future for national Israel. The apostle Paul describes this same future repentance in Romans 11:25-27, when 'all Israel shall be saved' and the Deliverer turns away ungodliness from Jacob. The 'spirit of grace and supplication' is the work of the Holy Spirit, whom Jesus promised would 'convict the world of sin, righteousness, and judgment' (John 16:8). Revelation 1:7 echoes the prophecy: 'Behold, He cometh with clouds; and every eye shall see Him, and they also which pierced Him: and all kindreds of the earth shall wail because of Him.' The piercing of Christ is both a past historical event and a future revelation of glory and repentance.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the cross is not just a historical event but a personal encounter. Every person must one day 'look upon Him whom they have pierced' — either in repentance unto salvation or in judgment. Smith highlighted that the mourning described is not the despair of the lost but the brokenness of those who realize the depth of their sin and the cost of their redemption. For believers, this passage reminds us that our salvation was purchased at an infinite cost — the piercing of God's own Son. The 'spirit of grace and supplication' is available now; the same Spirit that will one day bring Israel to national repentance is at work today drawing individuals to the cross. Smith also pointed to the hope in this passage: the same nation that rejected and pierced their Messiah will one day receive Him with open arms of repentance. If God can bring that kind of restoration to a nation that crucified His Son, there is hope for anyone.

## 📖 Key Hebrew Terms

- **Dakar** (דקר) — 'to pierce, thrust through' — 'They shall look upon Me whom they have pierced.' The verb is strong — it implies a violent, penetrating wound, not a scratch. The same word is used for the Amalekite's killing of Saul (2 Samuel 1:15-16). The piercing is at once literal (the crucifixion) and symbolic (Israel's rejection of their Messiah).
- **Safak** (שפך) — 'to pour out' — 'I will pour out the spirit of grace and supplication.' The verb suggests an abundant, unstoppable outpouring — like a torrent of rain. This is the same verb used for the outpouring of the Spirit in Joel 2:28-29 and Acts 2:17-18.
- **Chen** (חן) — 'grace, favor' — 'The spirit of grace (chen).' The first element of the outpouring is grace — unmerited favor. Before the mourning, before the repentance, God pours out grace. This is the order of salvation: grace precedes repentance.
- **Tachanun** (תחנונים) — 'supplications, pleas for mercy' — 'The spirit of... supplications (tachanunim).' The plural suggests an abundance of prayers — a flood of repentance released by the prior flood of grace. The outpouring of the Spirit produces the outpouring of prayer.
- **Bekhor** (בכור) — 'firstborn' — 'As one that is in bitterness for his firstborn.' The mourning for the pierced One is compared to the deepest possible grief — the loss of an only child, a firstborn son. This connects to God's own experience: He gave His only begotten Son.
- **Yachid** (יחיד) — 'only, only one' — 'As one mourns for his only son.' The word emphasizes uniqueness and irreplaceability. The mourning is not ordinary grief but the inconsolable sorrow of losing one's only child.
