# 222 Why God Allows Suffering — 2 Corinthians 1:3-7

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#222](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yv5wzThGJv8)

## Overview

Paul begins his second letter to the Corinthians by blessing the God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our tribulation. Suffering is not meaningless — it equips us to comfort others and deepens our fellowship with Christ's sufferings. The comfort of God overflows in our affliction. This opening benediction sets the tone for Paul's most personal letter, where he defends his apostolic ministry through the lens of suffering and divine comfort. The "God of all comfort" is Paul's answer to the mystery of suffering — not that He removes it, but that He meets us in it with His sustaining presence.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Suffering | Paul does not minimize suffering but transforms its meaning — afflictions are the context in which divine comfort is experienced and shared |
| Comfort | The Greek paraklēsis (comfort, encouragement) is a major theme — God comforts us not to make us comfortable but to make us comforters |
| Affliction | Paul's apostolic ministry was marked by abundant suffering (thlipsis), yet these afflictions deepened his dependence on the God of all comfort |
| God of All Comfort | The Father of mercies is the source of every genuine comfort — He is sufficient for every degree of affliction |
| Sympathy | Suffering equips believers to comfort others with the same comfort they have received — the comforted become comforters |

## Pauline Context

2 Corinthians is Paul's most autobiographical letter, and the opening blessing (1:3-7) establishes the theological framework for everything that follows. The triune structure — Father of mercies, God of all comfort, Christ's sufferings — sets the stage for Paul's "weakness" theology. The word paraklēsis appears ten times in these five verses, making it the most concentrated usage in the New Testament. Paul's theology of suffering is the opposite of the prosperity gospel: he does not view suffering as a sign of God's displeasure but as the arena where God's comfort is most powerfully experienced. This anticipates Paul's great declaration in 2 Corinthians 12:9 — "My grace is sufficient for you." The same theme appears in Romans 5:3-5, where tribulation produces patience, character, and hope.
