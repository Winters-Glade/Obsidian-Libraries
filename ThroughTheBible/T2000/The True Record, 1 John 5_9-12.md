## Summary

John presents the divine testimony concerning His Son. 'If we receive the witness of men, the witness of God is greater.' God has testified that He has given us eternal life, and this life is in His Son. 'He that hath the Son hath life; and he that hath not the Son of God hath not life.' The entire purpose of John's writing is that believers may know they have eternal life.

## 📜 Historical Background

First John 5 forms the climactic conclusion of John's letter, written to give beleaguered believers absolute certainty of their salvation. The Johannine community in Ephesus (c. AD 85–95) faced not only external persecution but internal division caused by proto-Gnostic teachers — notably those following Cerinthus, who taught that the man Jesus and the divine Christ were separate beings, the Christ descending at baptism and departing before the crucifixion. John's threefold witness of 'the Spirit, the water, and the blood' (1 John 5:8) directly refutes this heresy, affirming that Jesus the Christ was one person — anointed by the Spirit at His baptism ('the water') and shedding His blood on the cross ('the blood'), with the Spirit continuing to bear witness in believers. Key Greek terms include marturia (μαρτυρία, witness/testimony), a legal term evoking the requirement of two or three witnesses in Deuteronomy 19:15; zōē aiōnios (ζωὴ αἰώνιος, eternal life), denoting not merely endless duration but the quality of divine life possessed in the present age; and the verb echō (ἔχω, to have) in the present tense, emphasizing present possession of eternal life through the Son. The 'witness of God' (1 John 5:9) is contrasted with human testimony — if we accept human testimony in a court of law, how much more should we accept God's sworn testimony concerning His Son? The passage stands as the definitive New Testament text on the assurance of salvation: assurance is not grounded in subjective feelings but in the objective testimony of God Himself, received through faith.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus Himself declared: 'Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that believeth on Me hath everlasting life' (John 6:47), using the same present-tense 'has' construction that John employs in 1 John 5:12. The same truth is stated in John's Gospel: 'For God so loved the world, that He gave His only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have everlasting life' (John 3:16) and 'He that heareth My word, and believeth on Him that sent Me, hath everlasting life' (John 5:24). The entire Gospel of John was written 'that ye might believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God; and that believing ye might have life through His name' (John 20:31) — the same purpose statement that 1 John 5:13 echoes. Paul confirms: 'The gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord' (Romans 6:23) and that God 'desires all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth' (1 Timothy 2:3-4). The witness of the Spirit described in 1 John 5:8 is paralleled in Romans 8:16 — 'The Spirit itself beareth witness with our spirit, that we are the children of God.' Peter declares the exclusivity of this life in Christ: 'Neither is there salvation in any other: for there is none other name under heaven given among men, whereby we must be saved' (Acts 4:12). The inheritance of eternal life finds its consummation in Revelation: 'He that overcometh shall inherit all things; and I will be his God, and he shall be My son' (Revelation 21:6-7). The Johannine triad of witness — Spirit, water, and blood — establishes that the entire Godhead (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) testifies that eternal life is exclusively in the Son, received by faith, and enjoyed now and forever.

## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** First John 5:9-12 is the definitive NT text on the assurance of salvation. The "witness of God" (*martyria tou Theou*, v. 9) contrasts with human testimony in a legal framework drawn from Deuteronomy 19:15 (two or three witnesses). The threefold witness — "the Spirit, the water, and the blood" (v. 8) — directly refutes the proto-Gnostic heresy of Cerinthus, who taught that the man Jesus and the divine Christ were separate beings. John affirms that Jesus the Christ was one person: anointed by the Spirit at baptism ("the water") and shedding His blood on the cross ("the blood"), with the Spirit continuing to bear witness in believers. The present tense "has" (*echō*, v. 12) in "he that hath the Son hath life" indicates present possession — eternal life is not a future hope but a present reality. The purpose statement in v. 13 — "that ye may *know* that ye have eternal life" — uses the Greek *eidō* (to see, to perceive, to know with certainty). This is not subjective feeling but objective knowledge based on God's testimony.

**Cross-Batch Connections:** The entire Gospel of John was written "that ye might believe that Jesus is the Christ... and that believing ye might have life" (John 20:31, from [[_MOC_Batch10_Luke_John|Batch 10]]) — the same purpose as 1 John 5:13. Jesus' own words in John 5:24 — "he that heareth My word, and believeth... hath everlasting life" — use the same present-tense assurance. Paul's declaration that "the Spirit itself beareth witness with our spirit" (Romans 8:16, from [[_MOC_Batch12_Romans_Corinthians|Batch 12]]) parallels the Spirit's witness in 1 John. The exclusivity of salvation in Christ (Acts 4:12, from [[_MOC_Batch11_Acts|Batch 11]]) echoes "he that hath not the Son of God hath not life." The consummation of eternal life is the new creation in [[_transcripts/The New Age, Revelation 21_1-8.md|271 — The New Age (Revelation 21:6-7)]].

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith loved that John writes so that we 'may KNOW'—not guess, hope, or wonder. Assurance is the birthright of every believer. Eternal life is not something we earn but Someone we possess. If we have the Son, we have life. The question is not 'Have I done enough?' but 'Do I have the Son?' Faith is the hand that receives God's gift. The record, the testimony, the witness—all of it stands on God's authority, not our feelings.
