# 223 In His Image — 2 Corinthians 3:18

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#223](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0Z159djMA5k)

## Overview

With unveiled face, believers behold the glory of the Lord and are transformed into the same image from glory to glory by the Spirit of the Lord. The New Covenant brings a freedom and intimacy that surpasses the glory of Moses' ministry, and the ultimate goal is Christlikeness. This is one of the most profound statements in Paul's letters about the process of [[Sanctification]] — transformation happens not by human effort but by beholding the glory of Christ through the Spirit. The progression "from glory to glory" indicates that sanctification is a gradual but ever-increasing transformation.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Transformation | The believer is progressively conformed to the image of Christ, not through external law-keeping but through the internal work of the Spirit |
| Glory | The New Covenant surpasses the Old in glory — the glory that shone on Moses' face is eclipsed by the glory of Christ reflected in believers |
| Holy Spirit | The Spirit of the Lord is the agent of transformation — He liberates, illuminates, and conforms believers to Christ's image |
| Image of God | The restoration of the divine image in humanity through Christ — believers are being remade into the likeness of their Creator |
| Sanctification | The process of becoming like Christ, which is both a present reality and an ongoing progression from one degree of glory to another |

## Pauline Context

2 Corinthians 3:18 is the climax of Paul's comparison between the Old and New Covenants (2 Corinthians 3). Paul uses the story of Moses' veiled face from Exodus 34 to argue that the Mosaic Covenant, for all its glory, was a ministry of condemnation that fades away. In contrast, the New Covenant, written on hearts by the Spirit, brings permanent and surpassing glory. The transformation "from glory to glory" (apo doxēs eis doxan) suggests both the already-and-not-yet nature of sanctification. This passage anticipates Paul's teaching in Romans 12:2 about being transformed by the renewing of the mind, and in Colossians 3:9-10 about putting on the new man who is being renewed in knowledge according to the image of His Creator.
