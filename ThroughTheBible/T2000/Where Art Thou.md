## Summary

[[Genesis 30_1-43.md]] marks the tragic turning point in human history: the [[Fall of Man|Fall]]. After Adam and Eve disobey God's command by eating from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, their eyes are opened, they become aware of their nakedness, and they hide from God among the trees of the garden. God's first words to fallen humanity are not a sentence of condemnation but a question: *"Where art thou?"* This question is not asked for God's information — He knows where Adam is. It is asked for Adam's sake, to draw him out of hiding and into the light of [[accountability]] and [[Grace]]. God then pronounces [[Judgment]] on the serpent, the woman, and the man, but in the midst of judgment, He provides a covering of animal skins — the first [[sacrifice]], pointing forward to the ultimate covering for [[Sin]] in [[Jesus Christ|Christ]].

## Key Takeaways

1. God's first response to human sin is not judgment but pursuit — He comes seeking His lost creatures.
2. The instinct to hide from God when we sin is universal, but hiding is never the solution.
3. God's question "Where art thou?" is an invitation to self-examination and honest confession.
4. God's provision of animal skins (sacrifice) to cover human shame foreshadows the ultimate sacrifice of Christ.
5. The promise of Genesis 3:15 — the seed of the woman who will crush the serpent's head — is the first glimmer of the gospel in Scripture.

## Further Context

### Historical & Cultural Background
The question "Where art thou?" (Gen 3:9) reflects the ancient Near Eastern concept of divine search — a motif found in Mesopotamian literature where gods search for disobedient humans. The Hebrew word used here (*ayekah*) carries a tone of lament and longing, not merely a question of location. Adam and Eve's sewing of fig leaves (v. 7) reflects the shame culture of the ancient world, where nakedness was associated with vulnerability and loss of honour.

### Old Testament Connections
- **Genesis 4:9** — God's question to Cain ("Where is Abel thy brother?") mirrors His question to Adam, continuing the pattern of divine interrogation meant to draw out confession.
- **Isaiah 65:1** — God says "I was sought of them that asked not for me" — a reversal, showing God's persistent pursuit of His people.
- **Psalm 139:7-12** — David's meditation on the impossibility of hiding from God's presence, answering the question "Where art thou?" by affirming that God is everywhere.
- **Hosea 11:1-4** — God's tender recollection of calling Israel out of Egypt, using the metaphor of a parent teaching a child to walk — the same heart behind the question in the garden.

### New Testament Connections
- **Luke 15:1-32** — The parables of the lost sheep, lost coin, and lost son form Jesus' extended commentary on the meaning of "Where art thou?" — God as the seeking Father.
- **Romans 5:12-21** — Paul contrasts Adam and Christ: where Adam's disobedience brought death and hiding, Christ's obedience brings justification and open access to the Father.
- **Romans 3:10-18** — Paul's citation that "there is none that seeketh after God" underscores that God must seek us first — the doctrine of prevenient grace.
- **1 John 1:5-10** — The call to walk in the light, confess sin, and receive cleansing — the New Testament fulfilment of the invitation God extended to Adam.
- **Ephesians 2:1-10** — The picture of humanity dead in trespasses and sins, made alive by grace — from hiding to being seated in heavenly places in Christ.

### Extra-Biblical Sources
- The ancient Mesopotamian *Epic of Gilgamesh* contains a flood narrative but notably lacks the concept of a morally perfect God who pursues sinful humanity — a distinctive of the Hebrew account.
- Jewish tradition (Genesis Rabbah 19:9) notes that God's question was asked gently, like a king questioning his son, to open the door for repentance.
- Calvin wrote that God's question "shows that God, even when He sees men drowning in destruction, does not cease from His goodness."
