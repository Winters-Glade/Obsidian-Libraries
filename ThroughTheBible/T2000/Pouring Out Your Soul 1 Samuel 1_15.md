## Summary

Hannah pours out her soul to the Lord in desperate [[Prayer]] at the [[Tabernacle]] in [[Shiloh]]. Barren and deeply distressed by Peninnah's provocation, she prays with such intensity that Eli mistakes her for drunk. This passage illustrates the nature of authentic, vulnerable [[Prayer]] — pouring out one's heart before God, not with eloquent words but with raw honesty. Hannah's prayer is answered, and she dedicates her son [[Samuel]] back to the Lord, modeling a life of thanksgiving and faithful commitment.

## 📜 Historical Background

The story of Hannah takes place at the transition between the period of the [[Judges (Biblical)|Judges]] and the monarchy — a time of spiritual and political instability. The [[Tabernacle]] was at [[Shiloh]], where Eli served as high priest, but his sons Hophni and Phinehas were corrupt ([[_Bible/1 Samuel/1 Samuel 2_12-17.md]]), setting the stage for Israel's eventual cry for a king. [[Samuel]], the child of Hannah's desperate [[Prayer]], becomes the pivotal transitional figure: the last judge, the first prophet in the prophetic succession, and the king-maker who anoints both [[Saul]] and [[David]].

In ancient Israel, [[barrenness]] was considered a reproach, and Hannah's desperate prayer reflects the intense personal and cultural pain of childlessness. The pouring out of her soul (Hebrew: shaphak et naphshah) is a phrase used elsewhere of deep emotional [[lament]] before God ([[Psalm 42_4]], [[Psalm 62_8]]). Her vow to dedicate her son to the Lord's service as a [[Nazirite]] would place Samuel in a tradition of special [[consecration]]. Elkanah's annual pilgrimage to Shiloh reflects the religious calendar of the time, likely tied to the Feast of Tabernacles or Passover, when all Israelite males were required to appear before the Lord.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus taught about [[persistent-prayer|persistent prayer]] in Luke 11:5-13 (the parable of the friend at midnight) and Luke 18:1-8 (the parable of the persistent widow). Paul instructs believers to '[[pray-without-ceasing|pray without ceasing]]' (1 Thessalonians 5:17) and to present requests to God with thanksgiving (Philippians 4:6-7). Hannah's pouring out of her soul finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ, who poured out His soul unto death ([[Isaiah 53_12.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the most powerful prayers are not polished but poured out. Hannah teaches us that God welcomes our raw emotion and deep distress — we don't need to clean up our prayers before offering them. The key is not eloquence but honesty before God. Additionally, Hannah's willingness to give back to God what she treasured most challenges us to hold our blessings with open hands. What has God given you that you need to dedicate back to Him through [[consecration]]?

## 📖 Research Flavor

**Archaeology of Shiloh:** Excavations at Khirbet Seilun (identified as ancient Shiloh) have uncovered the site where the Tabernacle rested for over 300 years. The destruction layer dating to ~1050 BC matches the Philistine capture of the Ark of the Covenant (1 Samuel 4). Storage jars, cultic objects, and burned debris tell the story of the site's destruction — a judgment that echoed Eli's corrupt priesthood.

**The Nazirite Vow:** Hannah's vow to make Samuel a Nazirite (Numbers 6:1-21) meant he would abstain from wine, avoid contact with the dead, and never cut his hair. This was a voluntary consecration in an era when the priesthood was hereditary but prophetic calling was charismatically given. Samson (Judges 13) is the other major Nazirite of this period, though his story contrasts sharply with Samuel's faithfulness.

**Names with Meaning:** Samuel's name is a wordplay in Hebrew. Hannah declares "because I have asked him of the Lord" — *sha'al* (asked) + *El* (God) = Sha'ul (Saul) or Shemu'el (Samuel). The name carries the dual meaning of "God hears" and "God is exalted," capturing both Hannah's answered prayer and Samuel's role as God's prophetic voice.
