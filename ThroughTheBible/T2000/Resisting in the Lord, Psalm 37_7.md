## Summary

Psalm 37:7 calls us to 'Rest in the Lord, and wait patiently for Him' — a powerful antidote to the anxiety that comes from seeing the wicked prosper. Pastor Chuck explores the discipline of resting in God's sovereignty rather than fretting over apparent injustice.

## 📜 Historical Background

Psalm 37 is an acrostic wisdom psalm. David wrote it in his old age, offering the hard-won wisdom of a lifetime. The command to 'rest in the Lord' (literally 'be silent to the Lord') speaks of a quiet, settled [[trust]] that does not demand immediate explanations.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul echoes the theme of resting in God's timing in [[Romans 12_19.md]]: 'Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord.' Jesus taught the same principle of [[trust]] over worry in [[_Bible/Matthew/Matthew 6_25-34.md]].

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that fretting is a sin of distrust. When we see evil prospering, our natural response is anxiety. But God commands us to 'rest' — to be still, to [[trust]]. Waiting patiently is not passive resignation but active trust in a sovereign God.

## 📚 Hebrew Poetry & Wisdom Context

Psalm 37 is an **acrostic psalm** — each stanza begins with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet, aiding memorization and suggesting completeness. The psalm belongs to the **wisdom genre** within the Psalter, addressing the classic wisdom theme of the prosperity of the wicked. The Hebrew verb "rest" (*damam*, דָּמַם) means "to be silent, still, or motionless" — active cessation of striving. The alphabet acrostic form is also used in [[_Bible/Psalm/Psalm 119.md]] and [[Proverbs 31_10-31.md]].

## 📖 Research Context

### Acrostic Structure of Psalm 37

Psalm 37 is an **alphabetic acrostic** — each two-verse segment begins with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet (Aleph through Tav). This structure serves as a mnemonic device for oral transmission, and the use of the full alphabet suggests comprehensive coverage of the subject.

### A Wisdom Psalm on Theodicy

Psalm 37 is classified as a **Wisdom Psalm** alongside Psalms 1, 49, 73, 112, 119, 127, and 128. Its theme is the classic wisdom problem: why do the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer? The psalm's answer is distinctive: **wait on the Lord**. The Hebrew *damam* (דָּמַם, "be still, rest, wait") in verse 7 suggests the calm of one utterly dependent on God.

### Key Hebrew Words

- **"Fret not"** — *charah* (חָרָה) literally means "to burn, be kindled" — the same word used of God's anger. The psalmist warns against letting our anger burn against the wicked
- **"The meek"** (*anavim* עֲנָוִים, v. 11) — Those who humbly submit to God; Jesus quotes this in the Beatitudes ([[Matthew 5_5]])
- **"Inherit the earth"** (*yarash aretz*) — A recurring theme (vv. 9, 11, 22, 29, 34); the land promise reappropriated for the faithful
