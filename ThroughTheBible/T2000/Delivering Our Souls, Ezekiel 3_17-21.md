## Summary

Ezekiel [[_Bible/Ezekiel/Ezekiel 3_17-21.md]] describes the prophet's role as a watchman for the house of Israel. The watchman is responsible to warn the wicked from their way and to warn the righteous against turning from righteousness. Pastor Chuck expounds on the solemn responsibility of those who are entrusted with God's message — they must deliver it faithfully, regardless of how it is received.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[Ezekiel]] ministered to the exiles in [[Babylon]] after the first deportation of 597 BC. Chapter 3 records the conclusion of Ezekiel's inaugural vision and the explicit commission to serve as a [[Prophecy|watchman]] (tsaphah) for Israel. The watchman metaphor was drawn from the practice of stationing guards on city walls to warn of approaching danger. In the Ancient Near East, a watchman who failed to sound the alarm was held accountable for the lives lost. God applies this same standard to Ezekiel: to know God's word and warn God's people, whether they listen or not. This commission carries both [[Judgment]] and [[Restoration]] implications — warning the wicked calls for repentance, and the faithful [[servant]] must deliver the message regardless of outcome.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The watchman role finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ, the [[John 10_11|Good Shepherd who lays down His life for the sheep]]. Paul echoes this sense of stewardship in [[Acts 20_26-27.md]], declaring himself 'innocent of the blood of all men' because he has declared the whole counsel of God. [[Hebrews 13_17.md]] speaks of leaders who 'watch for your souls, as they that must give account.'

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith frequently emphasized that [[faithfulness]] in ministry is measured not by results but by obedience. The watchman's job is to sound the alarm — whether anyone heeds it is between them and God. This liberates the believer from the burden of outcomes while simultaneously calling them to faithful delivery of God's message.
