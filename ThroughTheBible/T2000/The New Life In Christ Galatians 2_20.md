# 228 The New Life In Christ — Galatians 2:20

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#228](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dqXxZnfERA0)

## Overview

"I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me: and the life which I now live in the flesh I live by the faith of the Son of God, who loved me, and gave himself for me." This verse is the heart of Paul's gospel — the old self crucified with Christ, and a new life lived by faith in the Son of God. Paul contrasts the bondage of the law with the freedom of life in Christ.

This teaching opens Paul's great polemic against the Judaizers — those who insisted that Gentile believers must keep the law of Moses to be saved. Paul's response is the definitive statement on [[justification]] by faith alone. The believer's identification with Christ in His death means the law has no further claim. The life that follows is not law-keeping but Christ-living — the indwelling Son living out His life through the believer. This is the heart of the Galatian gospel: freedom from the law is not license but union with Christ.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Crucified with Christ | The believer's old self died with Christ on the cross; the law's penalty has been fully paid |
| New Life in Christ | [[Resurrection]] life lived from the risen Christ within — not self-improvement but divine exchange |
| Faith | Saving faith is not a work but a trusting dependence on the Son of God who loved and gave Himself |
| Justification | Declared righteous not by keeping the law but by faith in Christ's finished work |
| Grace | God's unmerited favor — salvation from start to finish is a gift, not a wage |

## Background & Context

Paul wrote Galatians to counter a crisis: Jewish Christian teachers (Judaizers) had infiltrated the Galatian [[church]]es, teaching that Gentile believers must be circumcised and keep the Mosaic law to be truly saved. This directly undermined the gospel Paul had preached. In Galatians 2, Paul recounts confronting even Peter (Cephas) at Antioch for withdrawing from Gentile believers out of fear of "the circumcision party." Verse 20 is Paul's theological summary — the cross ends the law's dominion, and faith alone unites the believer to Christ. This letter became the cornerstone of the Protestant Reformation, with Martin Luther calling Galatians his "Katie von Bora" (his dear wife).
