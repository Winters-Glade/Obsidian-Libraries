## Summary

John 21:15 records Jesus' restoration of Peter after his denial. By the Sea of Galilee, Jesus asked Peter three times: 'Simon, son of Jonas, lovest thou Me?' — one question for each denial. Pastor Chuck explores the quest for love — not Peter's love for Jesus but Jesus' love for Peter. The question is not about past failure but present devotion. Each time Peter affirmed his love, Jesus commissioned him: 'Feed My sheep.' Restoration leads to service.

## 📖 Research Context

John 21 is the epilogue to John's Gospel, likely added to complete the narrative of Peter's restoration before John's original ending (John 20:30-31). The scene echoes the first calling of the disciples (Luke 5) — a night of fruitless fishing, a miraculous catch at Jesus' command, and a charcoal fire. The detail of the charcoal fire (anthrakian) in John 21:9 is significant — the only other use of this word in the Gospels is John 18:18, where Peter warmed himself by a charcoal fire in the high priest's courtyard as he denied Jesus. The same kind of fire now becomes the setting for restoration. Jesus' threefold question mirrors Peter's threefold denial. The Greek wordplay between agapaō (divine, unconditional love — used by Jesus in His first two questions) and phileō (brotherly, affectionate love — used by Peter in his responses, and by Jesus in the final question) has been extensively debated. The most natural reading is that Jesus invites Peter to the highest level of love; Peter humbly responds with the love he can honestly offer; and Jesus accepts that. The commission 'Feed My sheep' (three variations: Feed lambs / Shepherd sheep / Feed sheep) charges Peter with pastoral care of the growing church.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Psalm 51:1-17 (David's restoration after sin — a model of repentant love), Hosea 2:14-20 (God allures Israel into the wilderness to speak comfort — restoration after unfaithfulness), Hosea 14:4 (I will heal their backsliding; I will love them freely).

## 📜 Historical Background

After His resurrection, Jesus appeared to seven disciples who had returned to fishing on the Sea of Galilee. They had caught nothing all night until Jesus — unrecognized — told them to cast the net on the right side. The miraculous catch (153 fish) echoed their first calling (Luke 5). On the shore, Jesus had prepared a charcoal fire — the same kind of fire Peter had warmed himself by when he denied Christ. The threefold question mirrors Peter's threefold denial. Jesus uses the Greek word agapaō (divine love) in the first two questions, and Peter responds with phileō (brotherly love) — Jesus meets Peter where he is. The final question uses phileō, accepting Peter's honest level.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Matthew 26:69-75 — Peter's denial that led to bitter weeping. Luke 22:31-32 — I have prayed for you that your faith fail not; when you are converted, strengthen your brethren. Acts 2 — Peter's preaching at Pentecost, the fulfillment of his commissioning. Acts 20:28 — overseers to shepherd the church of God, which He purchased with His own blood. 1 Peter 5:2-4 — Peter himself later wrote: feed the flock of God. Galatians 2:9 — Peter recognized as a pillar of the church. Romans 8:1 — no condemnation for those in Christ Jesus. 1 John 4:18-19 — perfect love casts out fear; we love because He first loved us.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the most important question Jesus asks is not 'Are you sorry?' or 'Will you try harder?' but 'Do you love Me?' Love is the foundation for everything else. Peter's three affirmations couldn't erase his three denials, but they demonstrated that his love was real. Jesus didn't ask Peter about his theology or his plans — He asked about his heart. And then He gave Peter something to do. True restoration always leads to service. We are restored not just for our sake but for the sake of those we are called to feed.
