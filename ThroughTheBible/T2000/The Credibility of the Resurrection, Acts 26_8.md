## 📜 Historical Background

Paul's defense before [[Agrippa II]] (AD 59-60) is one of the great apologetic speeches of the New Testament. Agrippa was the last king of the Herodian dynasty, a Jewish ruler who understood the Scriptures and the Jewish hope of [[Resurrection]]. Paul masterfully appeals to Agrippa's knowledge of the prophets. [[Festus]], the Roman governor, interrupts to declare Paul mad, but Paul insists on the rationality of the resurrection. The scene powerfully presents Christianity's central claim before both Jewish and Roman authorities.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The [[Resurrection]] is the cornerstone of Christian [[Faith]]. Paul writes in [[1 Corinthians 15_14.md]] that 'if Christ be not risen, then is our preaching vain, and your [[Faith]] is also vain.' The resurrection validates Jesus' divine identity ([[Romans 1_4]]), secures our justification ([[Romans 4_25]]), and guarantees our own future resurrection ([[1 Corinthians 15_20-23.md]]). Every apostolic sermon in Acts centers on the resurrection as a verifiable historical event.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the resurrection is not a matter of blind [[Faith]] but of credible testimony. Over 500 [[witness|witnesses]] saw the risen Christ. The apostles went to their deaths proclaiming it — something people don't do for a lie. Paul's question to Agrippa remains relevant: if God is God, why would resurrection be incredible? The resurrection changes everything — it is the ultimate validation that Jesus is who He claimed to be.
