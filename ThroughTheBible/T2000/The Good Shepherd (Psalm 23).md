## Summary

[[Psalm 23|Psalm 23]] is the most beloved of all psalms — a tender portrait of the Lord as our [[shepherd]] who leads, provides, protects, and restores. [[David]]'s personal testimony ('The Lord is my Shepherd') becomes the foundation for [[Faith|trust]] through every valley of life.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[Psalm 23|Psalm 23]] is attributed to [[David]], who had been a [[shepherd]] himself before becoming king. The imagery would have been deeply personal and culturally familiar. The psalm moves through the various aspects of a shepherd's care: green pastures ([[provision]]), still waters ([[peace]]), paths of righteousness ([[guidance]]), the valley of death ([[protection]]), and the prepared table ([[blessing]]).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus declares Himself 'the Good [[Shepherd]]' in [[John 10_11-14.md]], who 'giveth His life for the sheep.' He is the fulfillment of all that [[Psalm 23|Psalm 23]] anticipates. [[Peter the Apostle|Peter]] calls Jesus the 'Chief Shepherd' ([[1 Peter 5_4|1 Peter 5:4]]), and the writer of Hebrews calls Him the 'Great Shepherd of the sheep' ([[Hebrews 13_20-21.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

[[Chuck Smith]] frequently taught [[Psalm 23|Psalm 23]] as a source of [[comfort]] for every stage of life. 'The Lord is my Shepherd' — this personal relationship is the foundation. 'Though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death' — we walk through, not stay in. 'Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life.'

## 📚 Hebrew Poetry & Worship Context

[[Psalm 23|Psalm 23]] is an exquisite example of Hebrew **extended metaphor** — the entire psalm sustains the [[shepherd]] imagery across multiple dimensions: [[provision]] (green pastures, still waters), [[guidance]] (paths of righteousness), [[protection]] (valley of shadow, rod and staff), and [[blessing]] (prepared table, anointed head). The Hebrew word for "shepherd" (*ra'ah*, רָעָה) also means "to pasture" or "to tend." The psalm uses **synonymous parallelism** extensively: "He maketh me to lie down in green pastures / He leadeth me beside the still waters" — both lines echo the same idea of peaceful [[provision]]. David, himself a former shepherd, draws on lived experience to paint God's care.

**Worship Significance:** This is the most beloved of all psalms, used for centuries in both public [[Worship]] and private devotion. Its personal pronoun — "The Lord is *my* Shepherd" — makes corporate worship intensely individual.


## 📖 Research Context

### The Shepherd Metaphor in the Ancient Near East

The [[shepherd]] metaphor was deeply embedded in ANE culture. Egyptian pharaohs and Mesopotamian kings called themselves "shepherds of the people." David — the psalmist — had been a literal shepherd before becoming king ([[1 Samuel 16_11-13.md]]). The Hebrew word *ra'ah* (רָעָה) means both "to shepherd" and "to feed" — a shepherd provides, leads, protects, and nurtures.

The divine name used in [[Psalm 23|Psalm 23]] is **YHWH** (יְהוָה) — the covenant name revealed at the burning bush ([[Exodus 3_14|Exodus 3:14]]). "The LORD is my Shepherd" is a radical claim: the God of the Exodus relates to the individual believer as a shepherd to his sheep.

### The Poetic Movement

The psalm moves through three stages of the shepherd's care:
1. **[[Rest]] and [[provision]]** (vv. 1-2) — "Green pastures" (*deshe*) and "still waters" (*menuḥah*) — images of abundance and [[peace]]
2. **Protection through danger** (v. 4) — The "valley of the shadow of death" (*tsalmavet* צַלְמָוֶת) — a deep ravine where predators lurked
3. **Blessing and pursuit** (vv. 5-6) — The imagery shifts from shepherd to host — a table prepared "in the presence of mine enemies"

The shift from "He" (vv. 1-4) to "Thou" (vv. 5-6) marks an intensification of intimacy — a movement from speaking *about* God to speaking *to* Him.
