## Summary

[[Ezekiel 16_1-14.md]] is a powerful allegory of God's [[Grace]]. Jerusalem is portrayed as an abandoned infant, unwanted and left to die, whom God rescues, cleanses, raises, and adorns with royal beauty. Pastor Chuck emphasizes that Israel's splendor was entirely a gift of God's grace — she was nothing before He chose her, and her beauty was His beauty upon her.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[_Bible/Ezekiel/Ezekiel 16.md]] is one of the most extended and vivid [[Prophecy|allegories]] in the Bible, tracing the entire history of God's relationship with [[Jerusalem]] from its pagan origins (Amorite and Hittite ancestry, v. 3) through its election, exaltation, and eventual [[faithfulness|unfaithfulness]]. The metaphor of the abandoned infant rescued and adopted by a passing traveler would have resonated powerfully with Ezekiel's audience in exile, who needed to understand both the depths of God's [[Grace]] and the severity of their rebellion. The chapter spans Jerusalem's entire history from the patriarchs to the exile, showing God's sovereign choice to [[Restoration|restore]] a [[Remnant]].

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The allegory of the abandoned infant perfectly illustrates the doctrine of [[Grace]] — salvation is entirely God's initiative, not our deserving. Paul develops this theme extensively in [[Ephesians 2_1-10.md]], describing believers as 'dead in trespasses and sins' whom God made alive. The beauty given to the bride prefigures the Church as Christ's bride, 'without spot or wrinkle' ([[_Bible/Ephesians/Ephesians 5_25-27.md]]), whose righteousness is not her own but is imputed by Christ.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

This passage demolishes all human pride. Israel contributed nothing to her own rescue — she was helpless, unwanted, and covered in blood. Chuck Smith would use this to illustrate that our standing before God is not based on any inherent goodness or attractiveness, but on His sovereign choice and gracious action. The appropriate response is humble gratitude, not arrogance.
