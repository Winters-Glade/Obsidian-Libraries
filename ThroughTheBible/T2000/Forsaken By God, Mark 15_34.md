## Summary

Mark 15:34 records the most agonizing cry ever uttered: 'And at the ninth hour Jesus cried with a loud voice, saying, Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani? which is, being interpreted, My God, My God, why hast Thou forsaken Me?' Pastor Chuck explores the profound mystery of this moment — the Son's sense of abandonment by the Father as He bore the sin of the world. This is not a cry of doubt but the fulfillment of Psalm 22, the quotation of which demonstrates that even in His deepest suffering, Jesus was in complete control, fulfilling Scripture to the very end. The cry reveals the cost of atonement: the Father turning His face away as the Son became sin for us.

## 📜 Historical Background

The crucifixion took place at Golgotha ('the place of a skull'), outside Jerusalem's walls, at the third hour (9 AM). Darkness covered the land from the sixth to the ninth hour (noon to 3 PM). Jesus' cry 'My God, My God, why hast Thou forsaken Me?' is a direct quotation of Psalm 22:1, a Davidic psalm that begins in despair but ends in triumph. Psalm 22 contains striking details that parallel the crucifixion: 'they pierced my hands and my feet' (v. 16), 'they part my garments among them, and cast lots upon my vesture' (v. 18). The bystanders' misunderstanding — thinking He called for Elijah — reflects a garbled hearing of the Aramaic 'Eloi.' The cry was in Aramaic, the common language of the people, ensuring all present could hear His anguish.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** Crucifixion was a Roman punishment reserved for slaves, rebels, and the worst criminals — Roman citizens were exempt by law. It was designed to be as slow, public, and humiliating as possible, serving as a deterrent to rebellion throughout the empire. In Judea, the Romans crucified thousands during the first century: after the death of Herod the Great in 4 BC, the Roman general Varus crucified 2,000 Jews (Josephus, Antiquities 17.10.10); during the Jewish War (AD 66–70), the Romans crucified so many that 'there was not enough room for the crosses and not enough crosses for the bodies' (Josephus, Wars 5.11.1). Golgotha was located outside the city walls near a road, ensuring maximum visibility. The darkness from noon to 3 PM was not an eclipse (Passover was at full moon when eclipses are impossible) but a supernatural sign — possibly the _khamsin_, a dust storm, though Mark presents it as a divine portent. Jesus' quotation of Psalm 22 in Aramaic ('Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani') — preserved in the original language by Mark — reveals that even in His darkest moment, Jesus was reciting Scripture, demonstrating that His death was the fulfillment of God's redemptive plan. Mark's Gospel, written to a persecuted Roman church that may have witnessed Christians being crucified themselves under Nero, presents Jesus' crucifixion not as a defeat but as the revelation of the Son of God — crowned with thorns, yet the true King.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Matthew 27:46 records the parallel cry. Paul explains the theological meaning in 2 Corinthians 5:21: 'For He hath made Him to be sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in Him.' Galatians 3:13 states that Christ 'hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us.' The sense of abandonment reflects the reality of His substitution: as the sin-bearer, He experienced the separation from God that sin causes — the ultimate consequence of the curse. Hebrews 2:9 describes that He 'tasted death for every man.' Isaiah 53:4-6 prophesied centuries earlier that 'the Lord hath laid on Him the iniquity of us all.'

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized that the cry 'Why hast Thou forsaken Me?' is the deepest expression of Christ's love and the fullest revelation of the cost of our salvation. Jesus was never more united to the Father in purpose than in that moment — yet He experienced the subjective sense of abandonment so that we would never have to. Because He was forsaken, we can cry 'Abba, Father' (Romans 8:15). The darkness that covered the land was the shadow of God's judgment that Christ absorbed alone. This is the heart of the Gospel: the innocent died for the guilty, the righteous for the unrighteous, that He might bring us to God (1 Peter 3:18).
