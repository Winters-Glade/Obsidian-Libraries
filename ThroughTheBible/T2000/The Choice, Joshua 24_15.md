## Summary

Chuck Smith examines Joshua's farewell address to Israel — one of the most powerful calls to decision in all of Scripture. Gathering all the tribes at Shechem, Joshua presents the ultimate choice: serve the Lord, or serve the gods of the Amorites or the gods beyond the River. 'Choose for yourselves this day whom you will serve' is the language of covenant renewal. Joshua's personal declaration is uncompromising: 'But as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord.' The passage confronts every generation with the same choice — there is no neutral ground.

## 📜 Historical Background

Joshua 24 records the covenant renewal near the end of Joshua's life (ca. 1390-1370 BC). Joshua, described as 'stricken in age' (Joshua 23:1), was around 110 years old at this point. Unlike Moses, whose strength did not wane even at 120, Joshua's advanced age brought physical feebleness, making this farewell address his final act of leadership.

**The Significance of Shechem:** Shechem held profound meaning in Israel's patriarchal history:
- Abraham first received God's promise of the land there and built an altar (Genesis 12:6-7)
- Jacob settled there, purchased land, and built an altar (Genesis 33:18-20)
- Jacob buried the foreign gods of his household under an oak at Shechem (Genesis 35:2-4)
- Joseph's bones were buried there after the Exodus (Joshua 24:32)
- It was the site where the Law was read and blessings and curses were pronounced (Joshua 8:30-35)
- Shechem later became a city of refuge, located in the heart of the land (Joshua 20:7)
- Tragically, it later became the center of Abimelech's wicked reign (Judges 9)

**Suzerain-Vassal Treaty Structure:** Joshua 24 follows the ancient Near Eastern suzerain-vassal treaty format, common in Hittite and Assyrian diplomatic documents of the second millennium BC:
1. **Preamble** — 'Thus says the Lord God of Israel' (Joshua 24:2)
2. **Historical Prologue** — Recounts God's acts from Abraham through the conquest (Joshua 24:2-13)
3. **Stipulations** — The call to fear and serve God, put away foreign gods (Joshua 24:14, 23)
4. **Witnesses** — The stone set up under the oak (Joshua 24:26-27)
5. **Blessings and Curses** — Implied in Joshua's warning about God's jealousy (Joshua 24:19-20)

**Three Options, Three Geographies:** Joshua presents three choices:
1. **The gods 'beyond the River' (Euphrates)** — The Mesopotamian deities of Abraham's ancestral homeland, including the moon god Sin of Ur
2. **The gods of the Amorites** — The Canaanite deities (Baal, Ashtoreth, Molech) of the land they now possessed
3. **The Lord (Yahweh)** — The God who delivered them from Egypt and gave them the land

This mirrors Elijah's later challenge on Mount Carmel: 'How long will you falter between two opinions? If the Lord is God, follow Him; but if Baal, follow him' (1 Kings 18:21).

**The Witness Stone:** Joshua set up a large stone under an oak near the sanctuary of the Lord as a witness. 'This stone shall be a witness to us, for it has heard all the words of the Lord which He spoke to us' (Joshua 24:27). In the ancient Near East, stones and other natural objects were commonly invoked as witnesses in treaty ceremonies — a practice that underscores the solemnity of the covenant.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus echoes Joshua's call for undivided loyalty: 'No man can serve two masters' (Matthew 6:24). Elijah's challenge on Mount Carmel mirrors Joshua's challenge (1 Kings 18:21). The New Covenant, promised in Jeremiah 31:31-34 and inaugurated by Christ, internalizes this choice, writing God's law on the heart rather than merely summoning external obedience. Paul's appeal to 'present your bodies a living sacrifice' (Romans 12:1) represents the same call to total surrender that Joshua made. Revelation's call to the church in Laodicea — to be hot or cold, not lukewarm (Revelation 3:15-16) — echoes the same principle: neutrality is not an option in matters of faith. The decision to follow Christ is the 'choice' of the New Covenant, and like Joshua's generation, every person must decide whom they will serve.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that Joshua 24:15 is the definitive text on spiritual leadership in the home. Joshua did not merely make a personal decision — he declared it for his entire household. This implies a father's responsibility to create a home environment where serving God is the clear expectation. The verse also confronts the myth of spiritual neutrality. Every person serves something. The only question is whether what we serve is worthy. Joshua's declaration 'as for me and my house' challenges Christian parents to take an uncompromising stand for God in their families, regardless of the prevailing culture. Smith observed that Joshua's generation and the elders who outlived him served the Lord, but the next generation 'did not know the Lord nor the work which He had done for Israel' (Judges 2:10). The failure to pass on spiritual heritage to the next generation is the tragic sequel to Joshua 24:15. Each generation must make its own choice — 'God has no grandchildren,' as Smith often said.
