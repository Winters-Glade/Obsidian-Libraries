# 216 Limitations of Liberty — 1 Corinthians 6:12

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#216](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bGGv_QuMFFE)

## Overview

'All things are lawful unto me, but all things are not expedient.' Paul introduces the principle that Christian liberty must be governed by what is helpful and what brings us under proper control. Freedom in Christ is not license but the opportunity to serve God without bondage to any created thing. This passage is Paul's key statement on the limits of Christian freedom — a principle he applies throughout 1 Corinthians to meat sacrificed to idols, sexual ethics, worship, and [[Spiritual Gifts]]. Liberty is governed by three tests: Is it expedient (helpful)? Does it bring me under power (create bondage)? Does it edify others (build up)?

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Christian Liberty | Freedom in Christ is real, but not all actions are beneficial — liberty must be exercised with wisdom and love |
| Freedom | Christ has set us free from sin, the law, and the fear of death; this freedom is for righteousness, not license |
| Self-Control | The paradox of Christian liberty: true freedom is the power to say no to what would enslave us |
| Edification | Liberty is limited by love — what builds up the body of Christ takes priority over individual rights |
| Glorify God | The ultimate purpose of liberty is to glorify God in body and spirit — believers are not their own |

## Pauline Context

1 Corinthians 6:12 introduces a principle Paul repeats almost verbatim in 1 Corinthians 10:23 — "All things are lawful, but not all things edify." This principle governs Paul's entire approach to Christian liberty. The context in 1 Corinthians 6 is specifically sexual immorality, as Paul rebukes the Corinthians for their tolerance of incest and their misapplication of freedom. Paul's response is twofold: liberty is limited by what is expedient and by what brings us under power. The phrase "brought under the power of any" (exousiasthēsomai) shows Paul's pastoral concern — even lawful things can become enslaving. This connects to Paul's teaching in Romans 6, where believers are freed from sin to become slaves of righteousness.
