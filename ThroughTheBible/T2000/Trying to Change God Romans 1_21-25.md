# 205 Trying to Change God — Romans 1:21-25

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#205](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4ai93VKQD4c)

## Overview

Paul's indictment of humanity's rebellion begins with the refusal to honor God as God — the root of all sin. Rather than glorifying the Creator, people exchanged the truth of God for a lie, worshiping created things instead. This passage describes the downward spiral of sin that begins with deliberate suppression of truth and leads to moral degradation. Paul argues that all humanity is without excuse because creation itself reveals God's eternal power and divine nature (Romans 1:20). The suppression of truth is not intellectual but moral — people know God but refuse to honor Him.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Idolatry | The fundamental sin of exchanging the glory of the incorruptible God for images of corruptible creation — a reversal of the first commandment that distorts humanity's purpose |
| Sin as Suppression | Sin begins not with outward acts but with the deliberate suppression of known truth; humanity's problem is fundamentally moral, not intellectual |
| Judgment | God's judgment takes the form of "giving over" — a threefold abandonment to the consequences of sin's own choices (Romans 1:24, 26, 28) |
| Human Depravity | When people refuse to retain God in their knowledge, moral chaos follows — the downward spiral from idolatry to every form of unrighteousness |
| General Revelation | Creation itself makes God known to all people, leaving them without excuse; Paul grounds universal accountability in the witness of nature |

## Pauline Context

This passage opens the theological argument of Romans that spans chapters 1–3, establishing universal human guilt as the stage for [[justification]] by faith. Paul's diagnosis is comprehensive: all humanity — both Gentile (Romans 1) and Jew (Romans 2) — stands condemned before God. The phrase "changed the glory of the incorruptible God" echoes Psalm 106:20 and Jeremiah 2:11, connecting Paul's argument to Israel's own history of idolatry. This sets up Paul's great theme: since all have sinned and fallen short of the glory of God (Romans 3:23), [[justification]] must be by grace through faith apart from works of the law. The same pattern of exchanging truth for a lie appears in Paul's warning in 2 Thessalonians 2:10-12 about those who refuse to love the truth.
