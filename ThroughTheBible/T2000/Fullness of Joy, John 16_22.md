## Summary

John 16:22 contains Jesus' promise of lasting joy: 'And ye now therefore have sorrow: but I will see you again, and your heart shall rejoice, and your joy no man taketh from you.' In the context of the Upper Room Discourse, Jesus prepared His disciples for His departure with the assurance that their grief would turn to unassailable joy. Pastor Chuck explores the nature of Christian joy — not dependent on circumstances but rooted in Christ's abiding presence.

## 📖 Research Context

This promise of joy comes in the final section of the Upper Room Discourse, just before Jesus' High Priestly Prayer (John 17). The analogy of a woman in childbirth is drawn from the Old Testament prophets who compared Israel's suffering to labor pains (Isaiah 26:17, 66:7-14; Micah 4:9-10; Hosea 13:13). The disciples' coming sorrow — the three days between crucifixion and resurrection — would be the most intense labor pains in history. But the joy of the resurrection would be so overwhelming that the pain would be forgotten — not denied but eclipsed. The phrase 'your joy no man taketh from you' uses the Greek verb aireō ('take away, remove, carry off') — no external force, no circumstance, no enemy can remove this joy. This joy is rooted not in favorable circumstances but in Christ's abiding presence and finished work. The same joy appears in John 15:11 ('that your joy might be full') and is the joy Jesus Himself possesses — the joy set before Him that enabled Him to endure the cross (Hebrews 12:2).

Key Old Testament cross-references: Psalm 30:5 (weeping may endure for a night, but joy comes in the morning), Psalm 126:5-6 (sowing in tears, reaping in joy), Isaiah 61:3 (the oil of joy for mourning), Isaiah 66:7-14 (the birth analogy of Zion's joy).

## 📜 Historical Background

Jesus had just told the disciples they would be scattered and He would leave them alone. Using the analogy of a woman in childbirth, Jesus compares the disciples' coming sorrow to labor pains — intense but temporary, followed by joy that eclipses the memory of pain. The phrase 'no man taketh from you' emphasizes the security of this joy. The Greek word used for joy (chara) is related to grace (charis). This joy is not mere happiness dependent on favorable circumstances but a deep, abiding gladness rooted in the presence of Christ. The world can give temporary happiness but cannot give or take away this joy.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Nehemiah 8:10 — the joy of the Lord is your strength. John 15:11 — these things have I spoken that your joy might be full. Romans 14:17 — the kingdom of God is righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit. Galatians 5:22 — the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace. Philippians 4:4 — rejoice in the Lord always. 1 Peter 4:13 — rejoice in the fellowship of His sufferings. 1 Peter 1:8 — joy unspeakable and full of glory. Hebrews 12:2 — who for the joy set before Him endured the cross.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the world's joy is fragile — it depends on circumstances, possessions, and relationships that can change or be taken away. The joy Jesus gives is different: it survives sorrow, persecution, and loss. This joy comes from knowing that Christ has seen us, that He is coming again, and that nothing can separate us from His love. The sorrow of this world is like childbirth — intense but temporary, and the joy that follows makes it all worthwhile.
