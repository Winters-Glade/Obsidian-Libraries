# 209 Why Some Lives Are Empty — Romans 8:18-21

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#209](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dM_8k2T1-I8)

## Overview

Paul contrasts the temporary sufferings of this present time with the glory that shall be revealed in us. Creation itself groans for redemption, and believers groan inwardly awaiting the redemption of their bodies. The emptiness of lives focused on this world contrasts with the hope of coming glory. Paul's theology of suffering is not pessimistic — it is radically hopeful, viewing present afflictions as light and momentary compared to the eternal weight of glory. The groaning of creation, the groaning of believers, and the groaning of the Spirit (Romans 8:26-27) form a threefold witness that the entire created order awaits redemption.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Suffering | Present afflictions are real but temporary, producing in believers a longing for the glory to come — they are not meaningless but purposeful |
| Hope | The confident expectation of future glory that transforms how believers endure present trials, giving meaning to suffering |
| Creation | The entire created order was subjected to futility through human sin and eagerly awaits liberation from decay at the revelation of God's children |
| Glory | The coming revelation of God's children in glory is the goal of creation — not escape from the physical world but its redemption |
|| Redemption of the Body | The final stage of salvation — the [[_Concepts/Doctrines/Resurrection]] body — when mortality is swallowed up by life |

## Pauline Context

Romans 8:18-27 is the theological high point of Paul's teaching on suffering and hope. The concept of creation's subjection to futility (mataiotēs) echoes Genesis 3 and the fall. Paul's unique contribution is viewing creation not as an independent entity but as intimately connected to humanity's destiny — creation was subjected through Adam and will be liberated through Christ. This passage anticipates Paul's teaching in 2 Corinthians 4-5, where the outward man perishes while the inward man is renewed day by day, and the earthly tent gives way to the eternal house in heaven. The "groaning" motif connects to Paul's teaching on the Spirit's intercession (Romans 8:26-27), showing that even our inability to pray is taken up by the Spirit who groans with and for us.
