## Summary

[[Genesis 6_6-8.md]] describes the moral and spiritual deterioration of humanity in the days leading up to the Flood. The mysterious union between "the sons of God" and "the daughters of men" has been interpreted variously as the line of Seth intermarrying with the line of Cain, angelic beings taking human wives, or a reference to fallen heavenly beings. Regardless of the interpretation, the result is clear: the earth is filled with violence and corruption. God declares that His Spirit will not always strive with man and sets a 120-year limit — a period of grace before judgment. [[Noah]], a righteous man, finds grace in the eyes of the Lord and becomes the instrument of preservation for the human race.

## Key Takeaways

1. God's patience is not infinite — there comes a point where judgment is inevitable.
2. Righteousness is possible even in the midst of a thoroughly corrupt generation.
3. Grace is the foundation of salvation — Noah found grace before he was commanded to build the ark.
4. The 120 years demonstrate God's mercy, giving time for repentance.
5. The days of Noah serve as Jesus' primary analogy for the conditions preceding His return.

## Further Context

### Historical & Cultural Background
The phrase "sons of God" (*bene ha'elohim*) in Genesis 6:2 is used elsewhere in the Old Testament for angelic beings (Job 1:6, 2:1, 38:7). The "daughters of men" are distinguished from these beings, creating a mysterious union. The 120-year period is debated: it may refer to a grace period before the Flood, a reduction of the human lifespan, or the time remaining until judgment. The ancient Near Eastern context includes the Sumerian King List, which also records extraordinarily long lifespans before a great flood. Noah's name (*Noach*, "rest" or "comfort") reflects his father Lamech's hope that he would bring relief from the curse.

### Old Testament Connections
- **Ezekiel 14:14, 20** — Noah is mentioned alongside Daniel and Job as a paradigm of righteousness — showing his status as an enduring example of faithfulness.
- **Isaiah 54:9** — God swears that the waters of Noah will never again cover the earth, linking the Noahic covenant to the enduring faithfulness of God to Israel.
- **Psalm 29:10** — "The Lord sitteth upon the flood" — God's sovereign rule over the waters of judgment, both in Noah's day and in eschatological judgment.
- **Habakkuk 2:4** — "The just shall live by his faith" — the same principle of righteousness by faith that saved Noah from the Flood.

### New Testament Connections
- **1 Peter 3:18-22** — Peter draws a direct typological link between the Flood and baptism: eight souls were saved through water, which now corresponds to baptism saving us through the resurrection of Jesus Christ.
- **2 Peter 2:5** — Noah is called "a preacher of righteousness" — his 120 years of ark-building served as a continuous proclamation of coming judgment, making his life a sermon.
- **2 Peter 3:3-7** — Peter parallels the judgment of the Flood with the coming judgment by fire, arguing that scoffers deliberately ignore the Flood as a pattern of God's judgment.
- **Hebrews 11:7** — "By faith Noah, being warned of God of things not seen as yet, moved with fear, prepared an ark to the saving of his house" — Noah as an exemplar of faith acting on God's word.
- **Matthew 24:36-39** — Jesus explicitly identifies the days of Noah as a type of His own coming, emphasizing the suddenness of judgment.

### Extra-Biblical Sources
- The *Epic of Gilgamesh* (Tablet XI) contains the Babylonian flood account with striking parallels (a boat built to specific dimensions, birds sent out to find land, a sacrifice after the flood) but significant theological differences (the Babylonian gods are capricious and frightened by the flood; the Hebrew God is sovereign and just).
- The *Atrahasis Epic* is an older Akkadian flood account that describes overpopulation as the reason for the flood, contrasting sharply with the biblical account's focus on moral corruption.
- Josephus (*Antiquities* 1.3) records Jewish tradition that Noah warned his generation for 120 years, planting trees to provide wood for the ark as a visual object lesson of coming judgment.
