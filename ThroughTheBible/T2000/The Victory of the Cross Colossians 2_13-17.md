# 238 The Victory of the Cross — Colossians 2:13-17

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#238](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HwK3DYDFPEU)

## Overview

God has made us alive together with Christ, having forgiven all trespasses — wiping out the handwriting of ordinances that was against us, nailing it to the cross. Having disarmed principalities and powers, He made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them through the cross. Paul celebrates the complete victory of the cross — forgiveness of sins and the defeat of spiritual powers. Believers should not be judged by legalistic rules about food, drink, or holy days.

This is Paul's most dramatic depiction of the cross as cosmic victory. Three metaphors converge: (1) **Forgiveness** — the "handwriting of ordinances" (cheirographon) was a certificate of debt, like a signed IOU. God canceled it by nailing it to the cross. (2) **Disarmament** — Christ stripped the principalities and powers of their weapons, like a victor disarming his captives. (3) **Triumph** — Christ led them in a triumphal procession (thriambeusas), the Roman custom where a conquering general paraded his defeated enemies through the streets. The cross, which appeared to be the ultimate defeat, was actually the ultimate victory — the moment when sin's debt was paid, the law's demands were satisfied, and Satan's power was broken.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| The Cross | The cross is not merely a martyr's death but a sovereign act of cosmic victory — forgiveness, legal cancellation, and triumph over evil |
| Forgiveness | Every sin — past, present, and future — was fully forgiven through Christ's death; the certificate of debt has been permanently canceled |
| Spiritual Victory | The principalities and powers that held humanity in bondage were disarmed and defeated — their authority broken by Christ's work |
| Legalism | Human religious rules (dietary laws, festivals, Sabbaths) are shadows pointing to Christ — the substance belongs to Him, not to the shadows |
| Freedom in Christ | Because the debt is paid and the powers are defeated, believers are free from both legalistic obligation and demonic intimidation |

## Background & Context

The Colossian heresy included "worship of angels" and ascetic practices (Colossians 2:18-23) — a form of spiritual bondage that Paul dismantles by showing Christ's complete superiority. In Jewish tradition, the law was given through angels (Galatians 3:19, Acts 7:53), so some Colossian believers may have thought they needed angelic mediation. Paul counters: Christ created the angels, disarmed the hostile powers, and triumphed over them. The "handwriting of ordinances" (cheirographon) is a unique term that likely refers to the Mosaic law — a written code that condemned us. Christ's nailing it to the cross is a bold image of the law's satisfaction. Paul's logic mirrors Galatians 3:13 — "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law, becoming a curse for us." The "shadows" (skia) of verse 17 anticipate the argument of Hebrews 8:5 and 10:1 — the Old Testament ceremonies pointed forward to the reality that is Christ.
