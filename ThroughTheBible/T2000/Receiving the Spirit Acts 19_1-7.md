# 199 Receiving the Spirit — Acts 19:1-7

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#199](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RqlDJnyaHtE)

## Overview

In Ephesus, Paul encounters about twelve disciples who had received John's baptism but had not heard of the Holy Spirit. This transitional passage highlights the difference between John's preparatory baptism (repentance) and Christian baptism (identification with Christ and receiving the Spirit). When Paul baptizes them in Jesus' name and lays hands on them, the Spirit comes upon them.

## 📜 Historical Background

Ephesus, the capital of the Roman province of Asia (modern-day western Turkey), was one of the largest and most important cities of the Mediterranean world. Its crowning glory was the Temple of Artemis (Diana), one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World — four times larger than the Parthenon, drawing pilgrims and merchants from across the empire. The city was a center of magic, occult practices, and the worship of Artemis (Acts 19:11-20 records the burning of magic scrolls worth 50,000 silver coins). The twelve disciples Paul encountered were likely disciples of John the Baptist who had heard only John's message of repentance, but had not yet heard of Jesus' death, resurrection, or the outpouring of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost. This transitional passage shows the bridge between John's preparatory ministry and the fullness of Christian experience in the post-Pentecost era.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

This passage demonstrates the clear distinction between John's baptism (a baptism of repentance preparing for the Messiah) and Christian baptism (identification with Christ's death and resurrection, accompanied by receiving the Holy Spirit). John the Baptist himself had predicted this distinction: 'He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and with fire' (Matthew 3:11). The pattern here mirrors Samaria (Acts 8:14-17), where the Spirit was received through apostolic laying on of hands after water baptism. The Ephesian disciples received the same Spirit — evidenced by tongues and prophecy — that fell on the apostles at Pentecost, confirming that the full Christian experience was now available beyond Samaria to the Gentile world.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that the experience of the Ephesian disciples illustrates an important principle: it is possible to be religiously sincere yet still lack the fullness of the Holy Spirit. These men had John's baptism — they were repentant and prepared — but they needed more. Smith emphasized that the Holy Spirit is not a secondary blessing for super-saints but the birthright of every believer. The apostolic pattern of baptism followed by laying on of hands and receiving the Spirit reminds us that Christian initiation is meant to be a full encounter with the triune God. Smith encouraged believers to move beyond partial spiritual experiences and receive all that God has promised through the Spirit.
