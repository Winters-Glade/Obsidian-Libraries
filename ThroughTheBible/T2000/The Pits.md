## Summary

Joseph, the beloved son of Jacob, is sent by his father to check on his brothers who are pasturing the flocks near Shechem. They see him coming from afar and conspire to kill him, but Reuben intervenes with a plan to cast him into a pit instead. They strip him of his coat of many colours — the symbol of their father's favour that fuelled their jealousy — and throw him into an empty pit with no water. Then, callously, they sit down to eat while their brother's cries echo from the pit. The pit represents the lowest point in Joseph's life: rejected by his own family, stripped of his identity, abandoned to die. Yet this pit becomes the beginning of a journey that leads to the throne of Egypt. The pit is not the end of Joseph's story — it is the crucible that prepares him for his destiny.

## Key Takeaways

1. The pits of life — times of rejection, betrayal, and abandonment — are often God's preparation for greater purposes.
2. Joseph's brothers sat down to eat while he was in the pit — the callousness of those who cause our suffering does not diminish God's presence with us.
3. Joseph was stripped of his coat (his identity) in the pit, but God was preparing a new identity and a greater destiny.
4. The pit did not have the final word — God was already at work through the Ishmaelites, Potiphar, and eventually Pharaoh.
5. Joseph's experience prefigures Christ — the beloved Son rejected by His own, sold for silver, delivered to the Gentiles, and ultimately exalted.

## Further Context

### Historical & Cultural Background
The pit into which Joseph was thrown was likely a dry cistern (*bor* in Hebrew) — a bottle-shaped underground chamber used for collecting rainwater, common in the hill country of Canaan. An empty cistern with no water (Gen 37:24) was a death trap, as the sides were too steep to climb without assistance. The location of Dothan (modern Tell Dothan), about 20 km north of Samaria, was on a major trade route used by Midianite caravans travelling from Gilead to Egypt. The price of twenty shekels of silver for Joseph (Gen 37:28) was the standard price for a male slave in the ancient Near East (cf. Lev 27:5), the same price Judas received for betraying Jesus (Matthew 26:15 — thirty pieces of silver, adjusted for currency differences and inflation).

### Old Testament Connections
- **Psalm 105:17-22** — The psalmist reflects on Joseph: "He sent a man before them, even Joseph, who was sold for a servant... until the time that his word came: the word of the Lord tried him" — the pit and prison as God's appointed preparation.
- **Psalm 40:1-3** — David's cry from the "miry clay" and "horrible pit" — the pit as a metaphor for distress from which God rescues.
- **Jeremiah 38:6-13** — Jeremiah is cast into a miry dungeon (a cistern) by his enemies — the familiar fate of God's servants.
- **Zechariah 9:11** — "I have sent forth thy prisoners out of the pit wherein is no water" — the same language used of Joseph's pit, applied to God's deliverance.
- **Deuteronomy 24:7** — The law condemns "any man that stealeth any of his brethren of the children of Israel" — Joseph's brothers violated this law in their treatment of him.

### New Testament Connections
- **Matthew 21:33-44** — The parable of the vineyard: the son is seized, cast out of the vineyard, and killed — Joseph's rejection by his brothers prefigures Christ's rejection by His own.
- **Acts 7:9-10** — Stephen's speech: "The patriarchs, moved with envy, sold Joseph into Egypt: but God was with him" — the connection between Joseph's betrayal and God's presence.
- **1 Peter 2:4-5** — "Coming to him as unto a living stone, disallowed indeed of men, but chosen of God, and precious" — the rejected one becomes precious.
- **Matthew 26:14-16** — Judas sells Jesus for thirty pieces of silver — the direct typological fulfilment of Joseph being sold for twenty pieces.
- **Philippians 2:5-11** — Christ emptied Himself, taking the form of a servant — the pattern of descent before exaltation that Joseph's life exemplifies.
- **Romans 8:28** — "All things work together for good" — the motto of Joseph's entire life, from the pit to the palace.

### Extra-Biblical Sources
- The story of Joseph is notably absent from Egyptian records, likely because the Hyksos period (during which Joseph likely rose to power) was later expunged from official Egyptian history by native Egyptian dynasties.
- The ancient Egyptian *Tale of Two Brothers* (13th century BC) shares some motifs with the Joseph narrative (a false accusation by a married woman), though the differences highlight the biblical account's distinctive moral and theological framework.
- Josephus (*Antiquities* 2.3-4) provides an expanded account of Joseph's life, including details of his beauty and wisdom that attracted Potiphar's wife.
