## Summary

Exodus 23:27-30 reveals God's strategy for Israel's conquest of Canaan: 'I will send My fear before thee... I will deliver the inhabitants into thine hand... I will drive them out... by little and little.' Pastor Chuck explores the principle of gradual victory — God's covenant warfare that prevented the land from becoming desolate and overrun by wild beasts.

## 📜 Historical Background

Exodus 23 concludes what scholars call the "Book of the Covenant" (Exodus 20:22–23:33) — the oldest legal corpus in the Bible, governing Israel's social, religious, and national life. Verses 27-30 contain God's promise to drive out the Canaanite nations before Israel through a gradual process. The Hebrew phrase **מְעַט מְעַט** (*me'at me'at* — "little by little," literally "a little, a little") uses the distributive repetition common in Semitic expression to emphasize the incremental nature of the conquest.

God gives two practical reasons for this gradual approach. First, ecological: if the land were completely depopulated at once, "the beasts of the field would multiply against you" (Exodus 23:29). The Levant in the Late Bronze Age (c. 1550–1200 BC) was still a region with significant wilderness areas where predators — lions, bears, leopards, and wolves — posed a genuine threat to agricultural communities. The Hebrew word **חַיָּה** (*chayah* — "wild beast") carries the sense of predatory danger; the Palestinian landscape, with its uncultivated hill country and forested regions (cf. Joshua 17:14-18), required active human habitation to keep it safe and productive.

Second, agricultural: the land needed faithful stewards to maintain the vineyards, olive groves, and cisterns that the Canaanites had built (Deuteronomy 6:10-11). A sudden evacuation would have allowed the land to revert to wilderness, undoing centuries of agricultural infrastructure. God's care for the land itself — the land He had promised to Abraham — reflects a holistic concern for creation that permeates the Mosaic covenant (cf. Leviticus 25, the Sabbatical Year).

The nations listed — Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Perizzites, Hivites, and Jebusites (Exodus 23:28) — represent the indigenous peoples of the southern Levant. The **Amorites** (Amurru in Akkadian texts) were a Semitic people prominent in Mari and Babylonian records, while the **Hittites** (Hatti) of Canaan should be distinguished from the great Hittite Empire of Anatolia — these were likely Neo-Hittite groups that had migrated south. The **Perizzites** and **Jebusites** are known almost exclusively from biblical sources, though Jebus is confirmed as the pre-Israelite name for Jerusalem (cf. the Amarna Letters, EA 287-290, where Jerusalem appears as *Urusalim* under a king named Abdi-Heba).

The "hornet" (הַצִּרְעָה, *hatzir'ah*) mentioned in Exodus 23:28 — "I will send hornets before you" — is famously debated. Literalists note that hornets can be extraordinarily aggressive and have been used tactically in warfare (reports from the 19th century describe African tribes using hornet nests against colonial forces). Alternatively, the term may be a metaphor for divine panic sent ahead of Israel — the Egyptian phrase "sending the hornet" appears in the execration texts and was used of Pharaoh's vanguard. Joshua 24:12 confirms: "I sent the hornet before you, which drove them out before you" — a divine terror that prepared the way.

The conquest itself is explicitly framed in covenant terms: "You shall not bow down to their gods, nor serve them, nor do after their works" (Exodus 23:24). The driving out of Canaan was not ethnic conquest for its own sake but the removal of a corrupt culture that had filled up the measure of its iniquity (Genesis 15:16) — a judgment God had declared to Abraham centuries earlier.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The "little by little" principle applies to the Christian life in multiple dimensions. Sanctification is a gradual, progressive process — "from glory to glory" (2 Corinthians 3:18). Peter urges believers to "grow in grace and in the knowledge of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ" (2 Peter 3:18). The Greek verb **αὐξάνω** (*auxanō* — "to increase, grow") is used of both plant growth and spiritual maturation, implying organic, gradual development rather than instantaneous perfection.

The conquest of Canaan serves as a type of the believer's victory over sin. We enter God's kingdom by grace through faith (justification, which is instantaneous), but we *possess our inheritance* through step-by-step obedience and dependence on God's power (sanctification, which is progressive). Paul describes this tension in Philippians 3:12-14 — "Not as though I had already attained, either were already perfect... I press toward the mark for the prize of the high calling of God in Christ Jesus."

Jesus taught that the kingdom of God grows like a mustard seed (Matthew 13:31-32) — the smallest of seeds becomes the largest of garden plants. He also likened growth to leaven working through dough (Matthew 13:33). Both parables emphasize small beginnings leading to full harvest through gradual, sometimes invisible, processes. The farmer who sows seed "grows it — he knows not how" (Mark 4:27) — but the growth is certain.

Paul's military imagery in Ephesians 6:10-18 echoes the conquest motif: "Put on the whole armor of God, that you may be able to stand against the wiles of the devil." The conquest of Canaan — "little by little" — becomes the pattern for spiritual warfare. We do not overcome sin, Satan, and the flesh in a single battle but through sustained, faithful engagement with the enemy, one stronghold at a time.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized the wisdom of God's "little by little" approach. Many believers become discouraged when they don't see instant victory over long-standing sins or circumstances. They expect a sudden, dramatic deliverance and, when it doesn't come, assume God has abandoned them. But God's method is progressive. He works in us "both to will and to do of His good pleasure" (Philippians 2:13) over time, in stages, as we are able to bear it.

There are practical reasons for the gradual approach in our lives as well. Instant victory over a deeply entrenched sin might lead to pride — "I overcame by my own strength." Gradual victory teaches humility, dependence, and perseverance. It also prevents spiritual "desolation" — if God removed every struggle at once, we might find ourselves spiritually unprepared for the responsibilities of mature faith.

The key is to trust the process and take the next step of obedience, knowing that "He who has begun a good work in you will complete it until the day of Jesus Christ" (Philippians 1:6). The farmer does not pull up the seed to check its growth; he waters it, trusts the soil, and waits for the harvest. So too the believer: we are called to faithful, patient obedience in the little battles, trusting that God is bringing about the full victory in His time.

Moreover, this text frees us from the tyranny of immediate results. Not every prayer is answered instantly; not every habit is broken overnight; not every relationship is restored in a week. "Little by little" is not a sign of failure but a mark of God's wise design. Take courage: the Canaanites may still be in the land, but God has promised they will be driven out.
