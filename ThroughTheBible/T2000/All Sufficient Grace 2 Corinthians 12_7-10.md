# 227 All Sufficient Grace — 2 Corinthians 12:7-10

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#227](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zPDIbNNZfH8)

## Overview

Paul shares his thorn in the flesh — a messenger of Satan sent to buffet him — and the Lord's answer: 'My grace is sufficient for you, for My strength is made perfect in weakness.' Paul learns to boast in his weaknesses because when he is weak, then he is strong through Christ's power resting upon him. This is the climax of Paul's "weakness theology" in 2 Corinthians. After cataloging his sufferings and weaknesses, Paul receives a revelation that transforms his entire perspective: the sufficiency of grace and the perfection of divine power in human weakness.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Grace | Not a vague favor but the active, sustaining power of God that is fully sufficient for every trial — "My grace is sufficient" |
| Weakness | Paul's perspective on weakness is radically transformed — weakness is not an obstacle to ministry but the arena where Christ's power is revealed |
| Strength | True strength is not self-sufficiency but Christ-sufficiency — when Paul is weak in himself, he is strong in the Lord |
| Thorn in the Flesh | Paul's undisclosed affliction, permitted by God to prevent pride, used by Satan to harass, and transformed by grace into a means of grace |
| Sufficiency of Christ | Christ's power is not added to human strength but perfected in human weakness — the less we can do, the more He can do through us |

## Pauline Context

2 Corinthians 12:7-10 is the culminating passage of Paul's "Fool's Speech" (2 Corinthians 11-12), where he reluctantly boasts in his weaknesses to defend his apostolic credentials against false apostles. The "thorn in the flesh" (skolops tē sarki) has been the subject of endless speculation — Paul never identifies it, and the text deliberately leaves it ambiguous so that every believer can apply the principle to their own affliction. Paul's threefold prayer for removal and the Lord's answer became the pattern for his theology: he learned that God's power is most clearly displayed when human resources are exhausted. This passage is the practical outworking of the theology Paul developed in Romans 5:3-5 — tribulation produces patience, character, and hope — and the experiential foundation for his teaching in Philippians 4:11-13 about contentment in all circumstances.
