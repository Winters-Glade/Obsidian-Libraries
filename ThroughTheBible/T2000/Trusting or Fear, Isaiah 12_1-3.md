'Behold, God is my salvation; I will trust, and not be afraid.' Pastor Chuck examines the contrast between trusting in God and living in fear.

## 📜 Historical Background

Chapter 12 serves as the doxological conclusion to the first major section of Isaiah (chapters 1-12). It follows the [[Messiah|Messianic]] prophecy of the Branch from Jesse's root (chapter 11) and pictures the restored [[Remnant]] singing praise. The name 'Isaiah' means 'The Lord is salvation,' and the Hebrew word for salvation (yeshu'ah) appears prominently here — the same root as the name of [[Jesus]] (Yeshua).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The phrase 'God is my salvation' points directly to [[Jesus Christ]], whose name means 'The Lord saves' ([[Matthew 1_21.md]]). Paul quotes from this section of Isaiah in [[Romans 15_8-12.md]], applying the prophecy of the root of Jesse to Christ's ministry to both Jews and Gentiles. The call to 'trust and not be afraid' echoes throughout the New Testament, including Jesus' own words in [[John 14_1]] and Paul's declaration in [[Romans 8_31]].

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith noted that fear and trust are mutually exclusive — you cannot genuinely do both at the same time. The song of Isaiah 12 reminds us that salvation is the foundation for fearless living. When we truly believe God is our salvation, fear loses its grip. Thanksgiving is the antidote to anxiety.
