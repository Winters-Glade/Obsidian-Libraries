## Summary

John 19:28-30 records Jesus' final cry from the cross: 'It is finished.' The Greek word is tetelestai — a triumphant declaration of completion. Jesus knew all things were now accomplished. Pastor Chuck explores the meaning of Christ's finished work: the debt of sin fully paid, the Old Testament prophecies fulfilled, the sacrifice complete. 'It is finished' is not a cry of defeat but a shout of victory.

## 📖 Research Context

John's account of the crucifixion is theologically layered with fulfillment language. Jesus' cry 'I thirst' (John 19:28) was not merely a physical expression but a deliberate act 'that the Scripture might be fulfilled' — specifically Psalm 69:21 ('They gave Me also gall for My food, and for My thirst they gave Me vinegar to drink'). The hyssop branch used to offer the sour wine recalls the Passover in Egypt (Exodus 12:22 — hyssop applied the lamb's blood to the doorposts). The word tetelestai ('It is finished') is in the perfect tense in Greek — signifying a completed action with ongoing results. This word was used in the ancient world as a business term meaning 'paid in full' — receipts from the first century survive with tetelestai written across them. Jesus' death was not a tragedy but a triumph. The veil of the Temple was torn (Luke 23:45; Matthew 27:51), the earth shook, tombs opened — creation responded to its Creator's sacrifice. John's final note — 'He bowed His head and gave up His spirit' — uses the Greek paredōken to pneuma, 'committed/delivered' His spirit, emphasizing the voluntary nature of His death.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Psalm 22:1-31 (the Crucifixion Psalm — every detail foretold: pierced hands and feet, gambling for garments, surrounded by evildoers), Psalm 69:21 (vinegar for thirst), Isaiah 53:10-12 (the Suffering Servant pours out His soul unto death), Leviticus 16 (Day of Atonement — the scapegoat bearing sin into the wilderness), Zechariah 12:10 (they shall look upon Me whom they have pierced).

## 📜 Historical Background

Crucifixion was designed to be slow, agonizing, and humiliating. Victims typically lingered for hours or days. Jesus was crucified at the third hour (9 AM) and died at the ninth hour (3 PM) — only six hours, an unusually short time. The phrase 'It is finished' (tetelestai) was a common Greek business term meaning 'paid in full' — used on receipts when a debt was satisfied. Jesus' final declaration was not a gasp of exhaustion but a shout of triumph. He died not because the Romans killed Him but because He willingly gave up His spirit when the work was complete.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Hebrews 10:10-14 — by one offering He has perfected forever those who are sanctified. Colossians 2:13-14 — canceling the record of debt that stood against us, nailing it to the cross. 2 Corinthians 5:21 — He made Him who knew no sin to be sin for us. Romans 3:23-26 — propitiation by His blood. 1 Peter 2:24 — He bore our sins in His own body on the tree. Hebrews 9:12 — entered the Most Holy Place once for all by His own blood, having obtained eternal redemption.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that 'It is finished' is the most liberating announcement in history. If Christ finished the work of salvation, there is nothing left for us to add. We cannot earn it, complete it, or improve it. We can only receive it. The temptation of religion is to add our own efforts to what Christ has done. But the cross stands as the finished work of God. When we try to add our works to grace, we actually diminish grace. Our part is not to finish but to believe — to receive what He has already completed.
