## Summary

On the afternoon of the first Easter, two disciples walk from Jerusalem to Emmaus, crushed by the crucifixion of the One they hoped would redeem Israel. Jesus Himself draws near and walks with them, but their eyes are 'holden' — they do not recognize Him. He opens the Scriptures, explaining how the Messiah had to suffer and enter His glory. When they reach Emmaus and He breaks bread with them, their eyes are opened. He vanishes, and they say to one another: 'Did not our heart burn within us, while He talked with us by the way, and while He opened to us the Scriptures?' This is the burning heart experience that comes when the risen Christ opens both the Word and our understanding.

## 📜 Historical Background

This event takes place on the same day as the resurrection — the first Easter Sunday. The two disciples are leaving Jerusalem in despair, despite the reports of the empty tomb from the women. Under Jewish law, a person could walk up to 2,000 cubits (about 3,000 feet) on the Sabbath; the seven-mile walk to Emmaus was well within permitted travel. The phrase 'their eyes were holden' indicates a divine restraint on their recognition, not a failure of natural sight. This account is unique to Luke's Gospel, emphasizing Jesus' fulfillment of the Law and the Prophets.

The location of Emmaus has been a subject of archaeological and textual debate since the early church. Luke describes it as 'threescore furlongs' (60 stadia, about 7 miles) from Jerusalem. The traditional identification is Nicopolis (modern Imwas), about 30 km (19 miles) from Jerusalem — which requires Luke's figure in some manuscripts to be read as 160 stadia rather than 60. A more compelling candidate is Motza (Arabic: Qaluniya, Hebrew: Motza), which sits exactly 60 stadia (about 7 miles) from Jerusalem on the ancient road to the coastal plain. Excavations at Motza have uncovered a 1st-century Jewish settlement with ritual baths (mikvaot) and tombs, consistent with a village of the period. The identification remains debated, but Motza fits the distance given in the earliest and best Greek manuscripts.

First-century roads in Judea were primarily Roman-built highways and older ancient routes. The road from Jerusalem to Emmaus likely followed the path through the Valley of the Rephaim, a route known from both biblical (Joshua 15:8, 2 Samuel 23:13-14) and later Roman sources. Archaeological surveys have identified segments of paving, milestones, and way stations along the major routes from Jerusalem to the coast. Travel was typically on foot, as the disciples are described, though wealthier travelers might ride donkeys or mules. The seven-mile walk would have taken about two to three hours, placing the conversation and Scripture exposition in the afternoon hours heading into evening.

The practice of 'breaking bread' (klasis tou artou) that led to the disciples' recognition of Jesus became a central term for early Christian worship. The earliest extra-biblical references to Christian gathering use this phrase as a technical term for the Eucharist (Didache 14:1, Ignatius' Letter to the Ephesians 20:2). The Via Mosaics in Jerusalem — excavated in the Jewish Quarter and the Church of the Holy Sepulchre — reveal 4th-6th century mosaic floors with New Testament scenes, including depictions of the Emmaus encounter. The resurrection is also noted in Roman sources: the historian Thallus (c. 52 AD, preserved in Julius Africanus) discusses the darkness at the crucifixion, Tacitus (Annals 15.44) mentions Christ's execution under Pontius Pilate and the subsequent spread of the resurrection belief, and Josephus (Antiquities 18.63-64) testifies to Jesus' identity as a wise teacher executed by Pilate whose followers continued to proclaim Him.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The Emmaus road encounter is a model for Christian experience. Beginning 'at Moses and all the prophets,' Jesus shows that the entire Old Testament points to Him (Luke 24:27, 44). Paul echoes this in 1 Corinthians 15:3-4: 'Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures... and rose again the third day according to the Scriptures.' The burning heart experience parallels the work of the Holy Spirit, who 'opens our eyes' (Ephesians 1:18) and causes our hearts to burn with love for Christ. This encounter also prefigures the Lord's Supper, where Jesus is known in the breaking of bread (Acts 2:42, 1 Corinthians 11:23-26).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that the burning heart is the test of true Bible study. We can read the Scriptures academically, learning facts and doctrines, without our hearts being stirred. But when the risen Christ walks with us through the Word — when we see Him on every page — our hearts burn within us. This is the goal of every sermon, every Bible study, every quiet time: not just information, but transformation through encounter with the living Christ. When our hopes are dashed and we are walking away from Jerusalem, He is already walking beside us, waiting to open our eyes.

## 📖 Key Greek Terms

- **Kaiomene** (καιομένη) — 'burning' — 'Was not our heart burning?' The present participle suggests an ongoing, sustained fire — not a flash but a steady flame. The same root appears in Luke 12:35 ('let your lamps be burning') and 24:32 (the only NT use of this specific construction for an internal burning).
- **Kardia** (καρδία) — 'heart' — The seat of emotion, will, and understanding in Hebrew anthropology. The 'burning heart' signifies the whole person being affected — intellect (understanding the Scriptures), emotion (joy and wonder), and will (turning back to Jerusalem).
- **Dianoigo** (διανοίγω) — 'to open fully' — 'He opened the Scriptures to us.' The verb suggests more than just explaining — it implies unlocking the meaning that was previously hidden. The same verb is used in v. 31 for the opening of their eyes and in v. 45 for the opening of the disciples' understanding.
- **Homileo** (ὁμιλέω) — 'to talk, converse' — 'While He talked with us.' This verb suggests intimate, extended conversation — not a lecture but a dialogue. It is the root of 'homily' — a familiar, pastoral discourse.
- **Epiginosko** (ἐπιγινώσκω) — 'to recognize, know fully' — 'They recognized Him' (v. 31). The compound preposition epi intensifies the verb: it was a full, complete recognition that came through the breaking of bread, connecting the risen Christ to the Eucharist.
