## Summary

Ezekiel 42:20 describes the measurement of the temple's outer wall — 'five hundred reeds' separating the holy from the profane. Pastor Chuck explores the significance of the wall in Ezekiel's visionary temple: God draws a clear line between the sacred and the common. The wall is not to keep people out but to protect the holiness of God's dwelling place.

## 📜 Historical Background

Ezekiel 40-48 comprises the prophet's extended vision of a future [[Temple]], often called the 'Temple Vision.' In chapter 42, the measurements of the outer court and its wall are given in precise detail. The wall's purpose is stated explicitly: 'to make a separation between the sanctuary and the profane place.' This reflects the central concern of Ezekiel's theology — the [[holiness]] of God's name and the need for His people to be distinct from the nations around them. The temple in Ezekiel's vision has been interpreted variously as a literal future temple (premillennial view), a symbolic picture of God's presence with His people (amillennial view), or a picture of the Church as God's temple. The concept of [[Judgment]] on the profane and [[Restoration]] of true worship runs throughout this vision.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

In the New Testament, the concept of separation changes from a physical wall to a spiritual reality. The 'wall of partition' between Jew and Gentile is broken down in Christ ([[Ephesians 2_14.md]]). Yet the principle of holiness remains: believers are themselves 'the temple of God' ([[1 Corinthians 3_16-17.md]]) and are called to 'come out from among them, and be ye separate' ([[2 Corinthians 6_17.md]]). The wall in Ezekiel symbolizes the unbridgeable gap between a holy God and sinful humanity — a gap that only Christ can bridge.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often taught that while the physical wall of separation has been removed in Christ, the principle of holiness remains essential. Believers are called to maintain a distinction between what is sacred and what is profane in their own lives. We are to be in the world but not of it — living distinctly as people indwelt by the Holy Spirit.
