## Summary

[[Jeremiah 43_7.md]] records the tragic decision of Judah's [[Remnant]]: despite solemnly promising to obey God's word, they flee back to Egypt — the very place of their former bondage — dragging Jeremiah with them. Pastor Chuck explores the spiritual tragedy of returning to the bondage from which God has delivered us.

## 📜 Historical Background

After the fall of Jerusalem and the assassination of Gedaliah, the governor appointed by Babylon, the [[Remnant]] of Judah was afraid of Babylonian reprisal. They asked Jeremiah to seek God's guidance, solemnly promising to obey whatever God said. Ten days later, Jeremiah delivered God's answer: stay in the land and trust God's protection — do not go to Egypt. But the leaders accused Jeremiah of lying and led the entire remnant — including Jeremiah himself — to Egypt. This was the ultimate irony: the people God had delivered from Egyptian bondage centuries earlier now voluntarily returned to it.

The historical context is crucial here. After Jerusalem's destruction in 586 BC, the Babylonians had appointed Gedaliah as governor at Mizpah. He encouraged the remnant to serve Babylon and prosper ([[2 Kings 25_24.md]]). But Gedaliah was assassinated by Ishmael son of Nethaniah, a member of the royal family, who may have been backed by Ammon ([[Jeremiah 40_13.md]]-[[Jeremiah 41_15.md]]). Johanan and the other military leaders pursued and recovered the captives but then faced a dilemma: they feared Babylon would retaliate against the entire land. Despite Jeremiah's promise of safety if they stayed, they chose Egypt. Tahpanhes, their destination, was a frontier fortress in the eastern Nile Delta guarding the Way of Horus, the main invasion route. There, Jeremiah buried large stones in the brick pavement as a sign that Nebuchadnezzar would spread his royal canopy over that very spot ([[Jeremiah 43_8-13.md]]) — a prophecy fulfilled when Babylon invaded Egypt c. 568-567 BC.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[Galatians 5_1.md]] says: 'Stand fast therefore in the liberty wherewith Christ hath made us free, and be not entangled again with the yoke of bondage.' Just as Judah returned to Egypt, Christians can be tempted to return to the bondage of sin, legalism, or the world after tasting freedom in Christ. The parable of the prodigal son ([[_Bible/Luke/Luke 15_11-32.md]]) illustrates the foolishness of leaving the Father's house for a distant country. [[Hebrews 3_12.md]] warns against an evil heart of unbelief in departing from the living God.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith highlighted the danger of asking God for direction with our minds already made up. The [[Remnant]] said they wanted God's will, but they had already decided to flee to Egypt. When God's answer contradicted their plan, they rejected it. How often do we pray for guidance but resist the answer when it doesn't match our preferences? True obedience means surrendering our plans to God, not asking Him to bless our predetermined course. Going 'back to Egypt' — returning to whatever God has delivered us from — is always a step backward in our [[servant|spiritual journey]].
