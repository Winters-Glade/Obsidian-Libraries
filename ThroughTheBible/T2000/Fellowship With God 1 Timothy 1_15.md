# 242 Fellowship With God — 1 Timothy 1:15

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#242](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I9uvJjPJvMw)

## Overview

"This is a faithful saying, and worthy of all acceptation, that Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners; of whom I am chief." Paul calls himself the chief of sinners, yet the recipient of abundant grace. The faithful saying — that Christ came to save sinners — is the foundation of the gospel. Paul's own life is a demonstration of Christ's perfect patience and mercy, an example to all who would believe.

First Timothy is a pastoral letter — Paul writing to his spiritual son [[Timothy]], whom he left in Ephesus to oversee the [[church]] and combat false teaching. The "faithful saying" (pistos ho logos) formula appears five times in the Pastoral Epistles, marking key creedal statements that the early [[church]] treasured. Here the saying is that Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners — the gospel in its simplest, most universal form. Paul's stunning addition — "of whom I am chief" (hon prōtos eimi egō) — is not false humility but honest self-assessment. Paul, the former persecutor of the [[church]], sees himself as the prime exhibit of grace. If Christ can save him, Christ can save anyone. This is Paul's pastoral theology: grace is not scarce but abundant, not for the worthy but for sinners.

## Background & Context

The Pastoral Epistles (1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus) are Paul's final letters, written later than the other Pauline epistles (c. AD 62-67). They address [[church]] organization, sound doctrine, and the qualification of [[church]] leaders. Paul is now near the end of his life, writing to his closest associates who carry on his work. First Timothy combats false teachers who were promoting "myths and endless genealogies" (1 Timothy 1:4) — likely early forms of Gnostic speculation. Paul's own testimony (1 Timothy 1:12-17) is placed strategically at the letter's opening to establish his apostolic authority and the grace-centered nature of the gospel. The phrase "faithful saying" suggests these words were memorized and recited in early Christian worship — creedal summaries of the faith.
