## Summary

At the very center of the book of Lamentations — a collection of five funeral dirges over the destruction of [[Jerusalem]] — shines one of the most hopeful passages in all of Scripture: 'This I recall to my mind, therefore have I hope. It is of the Lord's mercies that we are not consumed, because His compassions fail not. They are new every morning: great is Thy faithfulness.' Pastor Chuck explores how [[Jeremiah]] found hope in God's unfailing love in [[Lamentations 3_21-26.md]], pointing to the [[Remnant]] preserved through [[Judgment]].

## 📜 Historical Background

The book of Lamentations is traditionally attributed to [[Jeremiah]], written in the aftermath of [[Jerusalem]]'s destruction by [[Babylon]] in 586 BC. The city was burned, the Temple destroyed, and the people led into exile. Lamentations is built as a series of acrostic poems — each verse beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet — giving structure to raw grief. Chapter 3, the center of the book, contains 66 verses (triple acrostic). Verses 21-26 mark the theological pivot: in the midst of describing unimaginable suffering, the writer 'calls to mind' something that transforms his lament into [[Prophecy|hope]] — the character of God. This pivot from [[Judgment]] to [[Restoration]] is the heart of the [[Remnant]]'s experience of exile.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

'Great is Thy faithfulness' finds its New Testament echo in [[2 Timothy 2_13.md]]: 'If we believe not, yet He abideth faithful: He cannot deny Himself.' The mercies that are 'new every morning' are fulfilled in the resurrection of Christ — the ultimate demonstration that God's love is stronger than death. [[James 1_17]] describes God as the Father of lights with whom there is no variation or shadow due to change. [[Hebrews 13_8.md]] declares Jesus Christ the same yesterday, today, and forever. In [[Revelation 2_14.md]], God Himself will wipe away every tear — the final fulfillment of the hope that sustains us in lament.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often pointed to Lamentations 3 as the pattern for dealing with grief and suffering. The writer does not deny the pain — he describes it in excruciating detail. But then he deliberately turns his mind to what he knows to be true about God: His mercies never fail. Hope is not found in denying our circumstances but in remembering God's character. The discipline of 'calling to mind' God's faithfulness is the key to surviving the darkest nights. Every morning brings fresh mercy, and God's faithfulness is great enough to hold us through every trial.
