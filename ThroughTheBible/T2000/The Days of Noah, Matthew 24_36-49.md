## Summary

Matthew 24:36-49 is part of Jesus' Olivet Discourse, His major teaching on the end times. Jesus declares that no one knows the day or hour of His return — not even the angels or the Son Himself, but only the Father. He compares the days before His coming to the days of Noah: people were eating, drinking, marrying, and carrying on with normal life until the flood came and took them away. Pastor Chuck explores the call to watchfulness and readiness, warning against the deception of assuming everything will continue as it always has. The return of Christ will be sudden, unexpected, and decisive — and the only safe posture is faithful vigilance.

## 📜 Historical Background

The Olivet Discourse (Matthew 24-25) was delivered on the Mount of Olives overlooking Jerusalem, likely with the Temple visible in the distance. Jesus had just predicted the Temple's destruction (Matthew 24:1-2), prompting the disciples to ask about the sign of His coming and the end of the age. The comparison to Noah's day (Genesis 6-7) is striking: the pre-flood world was characterized by normalcy — eating, drinking, marrying — while judgment was imminent. People were not necessarily doing evil things; they were simply living as if God's judgment would never come. The flood 'took them away' — not into salvation but into judgment.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** Jesus' prophecy of the Temple's destruction was fulfilled in AD 70 when the Roman general Titus (later Emperor) besieged Jerusalem and burned the Temple — an event that would have been within living memory for Matthew's original readers if the Gospel was composed around AD 80–90. The Mount of Olives held deep eschatological significance in Jewish tradition: Zechariah 14:4 prophesied that the Lord would stand on the Mount of Olives on the day of judgment. From this vantage point, Jesus and His disciples could see the magnificent Temple complex — the center of Jewish worship, the symbol of God's presence, and the pride of the nation. Within forty years, it would be reduced to rubble, and the daily sacrifices would cease forever. The 'days of Noah' imagery — normal life interrupted by sudden judgment — spoke directly to a generation that had witnessed Jerusalem's destruction and would face continued persecution. Matthew's Gospel, written after this cataclysm, presents Jesus' warnings as a call to vigilance for the ultimate consummation still to come.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Luke 17:26-30 adds the comparison to Lot's day. Paul writes in 1 Thessalonians 5:2-3 that the day of the Lord comes 'as a thief in the night' and warns that when people say 'peace and safety,' sudden destruction comes. Peter develops the Noah typology further in 2 Peter 3:3-13, warning of scoffers who will say that everything continues as it has from the beginning. Revelation 16:15 echoes Christ's warning: 'Behold, I come as a thief. Blessed is he that watcheth, and keepeth his garments.'

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith was a strong proponent of the imminent return of Christ. He often pointed out that the 'days of Noah' describe a world so absorbed in normal routines that it had no thought of God's coming judgment. The same pattern, he taught, would characterize the last days. The antidote is not date-setting (which Jesus explicitly forbids) but faithful, watchful living — doing the Master's work until He returns. Readiness is not knowing the time but being faithful in every time.
