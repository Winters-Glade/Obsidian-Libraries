## Summary

Ecclesiastes 6:10-12 reflects on the limitations of human knowledge and the brevity of life. 'Who knows what is good for man in this life?' the Preacher asks, pointing us beyond 'under the sun' living to the [[fear of the Lord|fear of God]].

## 📜 Historical Background

Ecclesiastes is traditionally attributed to Solomon, writing as 'the Preacher' (Qoheleth). The phrase 'under the sun' describes life viewed from a purely earthly perspective. The rhetorical questions highlight our inability to grasp the full picture.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul echoes: 'For the preaching of the cross is to them that perish foolishness' ([[1 Corinthians 1_18.md]]). Jesus offers abundant life ([[John 10_10]]). The New Testament answers 'Who knows?' with Christ — in Him are hidden all treasures of wisdom ([[_Bible/Colossians/Colossians 2_3.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Ecclesiastes is the record of a man who had everything the world can offer and found it empty without God. It drives us to the only source of meaning: a relationship with God through Jesus Christ.

## 📚 Hebrew Roots & Wisdom Context

The book's Hebrew name is **Qoheleth** (קֹהֶלֶת), from *qahal* (קָהַל, "to assemble") — "the Preacher" who gathers the congregation. The key Hebrew word is **hebel** (הֶבֶל), "[[Vanity]]" — meaning vapor, breath, or mist. It conveys not meaninglessness but **transience** and **incomprehensibility**. "Under the sun" (*tachat hashemesh*, תַּחַת הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ) defines the book's perspective: life viewed horizontally, without divine revelation.

## 📖 Research Context

### Qoheleth — The Preacher's Vocabulary

The Hebrew name for the book is **Qoheleth** (קֹהֶלֶת), from *qahal* (קָהַל, "to assemble"). The Greek rendered it *Ekklesiastes*. Qoheleth uses the word **hevel** (הֶבֶל) — "[[Vanity]]" — 37 times. *Hevel* literally means "vapor, breath" — something transient, insubstantial, elusive. "[[Vanity]] of vanities" (*hevel havalim*) is a Hebrew superlative: "utterly meaningless."

The key interpretive question: Qoheleth does not say life has no meaning, but that life **under the sun** (without God) is ultimately futile.

### "Under the Sun" — The Epistemological Limit

The phrase **under the sun** (*tachat hashemesh*) appears 29 times in Ecclesiastes and nowhere else in the Old Testament. It defines Qoheleth's field of inquiry: life viewed from a purely horizontal, empirical perspective — reason without revelation. Qoheleth's experiments (hedonism, great works, wealth, [[wisdom]]) each yield the same verdict: *hevel*. Only when he looks **above the sun** does he find meaning — "[[fear of the Lord|Fear God]] and keep His commandments" (12:13).

### Skeptical Wisdom

Ecclesiastes represents **critical wisdom** — a sub-genre questioning traditional wisdom's certainties. Qoheleth observes that the righteous perish and the wicked prosper ([[Ecclesiastes 7_15.md]]), the race is not to the swift ([[Ecclesiastes 9_11.md]]), death comes to all ([[Ecclesiastes 9_2-3.md]]). This skepticism drives the reader to [[Faith|trust in God]] even when we cannot understand His ways.
