## Summary

Mark 3:1-3 records Jesus healing a man with a withered hand on the Sabbath. The Pharisees watch closely, looking for grounds to accuse Him. Jesus asks a penetrating question: 'Is it lawful to do good on the Sabbath days, or to do evil? to save life, or to kill?' Pastor Chuck explores the contrast between the Pharisees' rigid legalism — which would leave a man suffering rather than violate their traditions — and Jesus' compassionate lordship over the Sabbath. The healing demonstrates that faith is active, not passive, and that God's heart is always for restoration, even when it challenges human religious systems.

## 📜 Historical Background

Mark 3 opens with Jesus entering a synagogue on the Sabbath where a man with a withered hand is present. The Pharisees watch 'whether He would heal him on the Sabbath day, that they might accuse Him.' By this time, the religious leaders' opposition to Jesus had hardened. The Sabbath was one of the most sacred institutions in Judaism, surrounded by 39 categories of prohibited work. Healing was considered work, and by their interpretation, emergency-only — since the man's condition was chronic, they saw no justification for 'working' on the Sabbath. Jesus 'looked round about on them with anger, being grieved for the hardness of their hearts' (Mark 3:5) — one of the few places in Scripture where Jesus' anger is explicitly mentioned.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Gospel of Mark:** The Gospel of Mark is widely regarded as the earliest of the four Gospels, likely composed around AD 60–70 (possibly just before or after the destruction of the Jerusalem Temple in AD 70). Early church tradition, recorded by Papias (c. AD 110), identifies the author as John Mark, a companion of Peter and Paul — the same Mark who appears in Acts 12:12, 12:25, 15:37–39, and 1 Peter 5:13. Mark's Gospel is thought to reflect Peter's preaching in Rome, which would explain its vivid, eyewitness detail and its fast-paced narrative style (the Greek word _euthys_, 'immediately,' appears 41 times). Mark writes for a predominantly Gentile audience — he explains Jewish customs (Mark 7:3–4, 'for the Pharisees... wash their hands often') and uses Latin loanwords (like _centurion_, _legion_, _praetorium_), suggesting his readers were Roman. The Sabbath controversy in Mark 3 reflects the intensifying conflict between Jesus and the religious establishment — a conflict Mark presents with dramatic urgency, moving swiftly from one confrontation to the next.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus' earlier teaching in Mark 2:27 establishes the principle: 'The Sabbath was made for man, and not man for the Sabbath.' Paul develops this in Colossians 2:16-17, warning against allowing anyone to judge us regarding Sabbath days, 'which are a shadow of things to come; but the body is of Christ.' Hebrews 4:9-11 speaks of a 'rest' that remains for the people of God — a Sabbath rest fulfilled in Christ. John 9 records a similar healing of a man born blind on the Sabbath, which also provoked Pharisee opposition. The theme throughout is that Christ's redemptive work transcends and fulfills the ceremonial law.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often taught that the Pharisees' problem was not zeal for God's law but a heart that had lost compassion while maintaining religious correctness. The withered hand represents every area of our lives that is shriveled and powerless apart from Christ's touch. Jesus still asks: 'Is it lawful to do good on the Sabbath?' — challenging every religious system that elevates rules over people. The Sabbath was God's gift of rest, not a tool of restriction, and Christ is Lord of it.
