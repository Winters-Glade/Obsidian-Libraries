# 247 Before and After — Titus 3:3-8

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#247](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=giaZuFEB6uw)

## Overview

Paul paints a vivid contrast between our former life and our new life in Christ. "For we ourselves also were sometimes foolish, disobedient, deceived, serving divers lusts and pleasures... But after that the kindness and love of God our Saviour toward man appeared, not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to his mercy he saved us, by the washing of regeneration, and renewing of the [[Holy Spirit|Holy Ghost]]." Salvation is entirely God's work — mercy, not merit.

This is one of the most beautiful summaries of the gospel in Paul's letters. The "before" picture is devastating: foolish, disobedient, deceived, enslaved to lusts and pleasures, living in malice and envy, hateful and hating one another. It is a universal portrait of humanity without grace — not just the Cretan culture where Titus ministered but every human heart. The "after" picture is entirely God's doing: "the kindness and love of God our Saviour appeared." Three great divine actions are described: (1) Mercy — God saved us not because of our works but because of His compassion; (2) Washing of regeneration (palingenesia) — the new birth, the spiritual cleansing that makes us new; (3) Renewing of the [[Holy Spirit]] — the ongoing transformation by the indwelling Spirit, "poured out on us abundantly through Jesus Christ our Saviour."

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Grace | Salvation is "not by works of righteousness which we have done" — this is Paul's most explicit statement that good works are the result, not the cause, of salvation |
| Salvation | Salvation is past (we were saved), present (we are being saved), and future (we will be saved) — here Paul emphasizes the past completed act |
| Regeneration | The "washing of regeneration" (palingenesia) is the new birth — the Spirit's work of making us entirely new creatures in Christ |
| Holy Spirit | The [[Holy Spirit]] is "poured out abundantly" — the language of Pentecost (Acts 2) applied to every believer, not just the apostles |
| Before and After Christ | The radical discontinuity between the old life and the new — salvation is not improvement but transformation, not reformation but regeneration |

## Background & Context

Titus was Paul's trusted delegate on the island of Crete, tasked with "setting in order the things that are lacking" and appointing elders in every city (Titus 1:5). Cretan culture had a reputation for dishonesty, laziness, and immorality (Titus 1:12 — "Cretans are always liars, evil beasts, slow bellies"). Paul's letter to Titus emphasizes "good works" — the phrase appears six times in three chapters. This is not a contradiction of salvation by grace; rather, Paul argues that grace creates a people "zealous for good works" (Titus 2:14). Chapter 3 establishes the doctrinal foundation: we were saved by mercy, not merit, so that those who have believed in God might be "careful to maintain good works." The letter closes with the reminder that these things are "good and profitable unto men" (Titus 3:8).
