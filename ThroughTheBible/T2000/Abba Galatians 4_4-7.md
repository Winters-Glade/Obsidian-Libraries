# 229 Abba — Galatians 4:4-7

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#229](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HZ8ohR0MoQY)

## Overview

In the fullness of time, God sent forth His Son, born of a woman, born under the law, to redeem those under the law that we might receive the adoption of sons. Because we are sons, God has sent forth the Spirit of His Son into our hearts, crying, "Abba, Father." Paul explores the remarkable truth that believers are no longer slaves but sons and heirs of God through Christ.

This teaching follows directly from Paul's salvation-history argument in Galatians 3-4. The law served as a "tutor" (paidagogos) to lead us to Christ, but now that faith has come, we are no longer under a tutor. The language of "Abba, Father" is profoundly intimate — "Abba" is the Aramaic term a child would use for their own father. Paul deliberately preserves this Aramaic word (also found in Romans 8:15), suggesting it was a cherished phrase in early Christian prayer. The full scope of redemption is on display: the Son's mission, the Spirit's witness, and the Father's embrace — the Trinity united in the believer's adoption.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Adoption | Believers are legally and relationally made sons and daughters of God through Christ, not by natural birth but by supernatural grace |
| Sonship | The status of son carries full rights of inheritance — no longer a servant but an heir of God |
| Redemption | Christ's incarnation and submission to the law purchased freedom for those enslaved under the law's demands |
| Holy Spirit | The Spirit of the Son is sent into believers' hearts as the inward witness and seal of their adoption |
| Freedom from the Law | The law's tutelage ends when sonship begins — the believer relates to God as Father, not as Master |

## Background & Context

Paul's argument in Galatians 4 hinges on the contrast between slavery and sonship. He uses the allegory of Hagar and Sarah (the slave woman vs. the free woman) to show that those who rely on the law are children of slavery, while those who rely on promise are children of freedom. Verses 4-7 are the theological center — the "fullness of time" marks God's perfect moment in redemptive history. The phrase "born under the law" echoes Christ's identification with humanity at every point, yet without sin. The adoption (huiothesia) Paul describes is a distinctly Roman legal concept — the complete transfer of a person from one family to another, with full inheritance rights. This would resonate powerfully with Paul's Greco-Roman audience.
