## Summary

Jabez is remembered for his [[Prayer]] — a brief but powerful cry that broke through the平淡 genealogical lists of 1 Chronicles: 'Oh, that You would bless me indeed, and enlarge my territory, that Your hand would be with me, and that You would keep me from evil.' God granted his request. This teaching examines the boldness of Jabez's [[Prayer]], the four elements of his request ([[blessing]], territory, presence, protection), and the principle that God delights in answering prayers that align with His purposes. Chuck Smith encourages believers to pray expectantly and specifically rather than settling for spiritual mediocrity.

## 📜 Historical Background

The genealogies of 1 Chronicles (chapters 1-9) trace Israel's history from Adam to the post-exilic community. In the midst of lists of names — often obscure — Jabez suddenly stands out with a brief biographical note. His name means 'he causes pain' (his mother bore him in pain), yet he prays for blessing, enlargement, God's presence, and protection. The Chronicler, writing to the post-exilic community (c. 450-400 BC), uses Jabez as a model of faith: even someone with an inauspicious beginning can, through prayer, experience God's blessing.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus taught that we should ask, seek, and knock (Matthew 7:7-8) and that the Father delights in giving good gifts to His children. James 4:2 declares, 'You do not have because you do not ask.' The apostle Paul prayed for enlargement — not of territory but of influence for the gospel (Romans 15:23-24). The prayer of Jabez echoes in Ephesians 3:20 where God is described as able 'to do exceedingly abundantly above all that we ask or think.'

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Jabez reminds us that our beginnings don't determine our endings. His name meant 'pain,' yet he refused to be defined by his birth circumstances. Chuck Smith taught that God invites us to pray boldly — not from entitlement but from faith in a generous God. The four elements of Jabez's prayer are still relevant: ask for blessing, ask for enlarged influence (not just for selfish gain but for God's purposes), ask for God's presence, and ask for protection from evil. God is still honoring those who pray such prayers.

## 📖 Research Flavor

### Post-Exilic Perspective of Chronicles
The Chronicler compiled these genealogies for a community returning from Babylonian exile (c. 450–400 BC). The genealogy in 1 Chronicles 4 traces Judah's line, and the insertion of Jabez's story — a flash of biographical detail amid dry lists — is intentional. For a dispirited post-exilic generation struggling to rebuild, Jabez's example declared: your past does not limit your future; God hears and answers prayer even when circumstances seem small.

### Name Theology in Ancient Israel
In Hebrew, Jabez (יַעְבֵּץ, *Ya'betz*) sounds like 'he causes pain' (עֶצֶב, *etzev*), connected to the word for pain or stiffness. His mother named him 'because I bore him in pain.' This wordplay is characteristic of Hebrew naming — names expressed circumstances, hopes, or divine encounters. Jabez's prayer to be kept from evil so that he would not cause pain represents a transformation of his very identity, a theme echoed in the renaming of Abram, Jacob, and others.

### Prayer and Seeking God — A Chronicler's Theme
The Chronicler consistently highlights those who 'seek the Lord' (1 Chr 16:11; 22:19; 28:9). Jabez is introduced with 'Jabez called on the God of Israel' — this seeking is the mark of faith that distinguishes him. In Chronicles, seeking God leads to blessing; abandoning God leads to judgment. Jabez stands as a microcosm of the Chronicler's theology.

### Archaeological Context
Bethlehem, noted as Jabez's association, was a small but significant Judahite village. Excavations at the site of ancient Bethlehem (modern Beit Sahur area) have uncovered Iron Age II remains (c. 1000–586 BC), consistent with the period of the united monarchy. The mention of 'the families of the scribes who lived at Jabez' (1 Chr 2:55) suggests Jabez may have been a known location or clan associated with learning and literacy.
