## Summary

Exodus 6:6-7 contains seven 'I will' statements from God to Israel: deliverance from bondage, redemption with an outstretched arm, adoption as His people, and the revelation that 'I am the Lord your God.' Pastor Chuck explores the certainty of God's promises and the progressive revelation of His covenant faithfulness.

## 📜 Historical Background

Exodus 6 follows Moses' first confrontation with Pharaoh, which resulted in increased oppression — the straw quota was intensified (Exodus 5:6-9), and the people's foremen were beaten. Discouraged, Moses questions why God allowed things to get worse (Exodus 5:22-23). God's response — a series of seven "I will" statements — is the most concentrated declaration of divine resolve in the Pentateuch. The passage forms the theological heart of the exodus narrative, grounding the impending deliverance in Yahweh's covenant identity.

The seven declarations in Hebrew are:

1. **וְהוֹצֵאתִי** (*vehotzeiti*) — "I will bring you out" from under the burdens
2. **וְהִצַּלְתִּי** (*vehitzalti*) — "I will rid you out of their bondage"
3. **וְגָאַלְתִּי** (*vega'alti*) — "I will redeem you with an outstretched arm"
4. **וְלָקַחְתִּי** (*velakachti*) — "I will take you to Me for a people"
5. **וְהָיִיתִי** (*vehayiti*) — "I will be your God"
6. **וְהֵבֵאתִי** (*vehaveiti*) — "I will bring you in unto the land"
7. **וְנָתַתִּי** (*venatati*) — "I will give it to you for a heritage"

The verb **גָּאַל** (*ga'al* — "to redeem") carries the specific legal sense of a kinsman-redeemer (*go'el*) who pays a debt or avenges blood. In ANE context, this term described a close relative who restores the family's fortunes — here applied to Yahweh as Israel's divine Kinsman-Redeemer. The "outstretched arm" (זְרוֹעַ נְטוּיָה, *zero'a netuyah*) is an anthropomorphic metaphor rooted in ANE royal iconography, where the extended arm of a king signified both power and deliverance (cf. the Merneptah Stele's depiction of Pharaoh's military might). Israel's God uses the same imagery to promise liberation, not conquest.

The passage shifts from the covenant with Abraham (Genesis 15), Isaac, and Jacob (Exodus 6:3-4) to its fulfillment at the national level. The divine name **YHWH** (יְהוָה) is emphasized — God had appeared to the patriarchs as **El Shaddai** (אֵל שַׁדַּי), but now His covenant name would be known in its redemptive fullness. This is further underscored by the repeated formula "I am the LORD" (אֲנִי יְהוָה, *ani YHWH*), which appears five times in Exodus 6:2-8, framing the entire oracle as a self-revelation of the divine character.

Culturally, the sevenfold structure mirrors the seven-day week of creation, suggesting that this redemption is a new creation act. Just as God spoke the world into existence through seven creative acts, He decrees Israel's redemption through seven sovereign declarations. This literary symmetry would not have been lost on Israel, who were to see the exodus as nothing less than a re-creation of their national identity.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The seven "I will" statements of Exodus 6 prefigure the promises of God in Christ. Paul declares that "all the promises of God in Him are yea, and in Him Amen, unto the glory of God by us" (2 Corinthians 1:20). Jesus Himself uses the divine "I will" — "I will come again and receive you unto Myself" (John 14:3); "I will give you rest" (Matthew 11:28). In the Gospel of John, Jesus appropriates the divine "I AM" language repeatedly (John 6:35; 8:12; 10:9; 11:25; 14:6), connecting His person directly to the covenant God of Exodus.

The pattern of redemption — God taking a people for Himself — is fulfilled in the church, purchased with His own blood (Acts 20:28). Peter echoes the covenant language explicitly: "You are a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a peculiar people" (1 Peter 2:9), drawing directly from Exodus 19:5-6. The kinsman-redeemer motif finds its ultimate expression in Christ, who "gave Himself for us, that He might redeem us from all iniquity, and purify unto Himself a peculiar people, zealous of good works" (Titus 2:14).

In Revelation, the redeemed sing a new song: "You were slain, and have redeemed us to God by Your blood out of every tribe and tongue and people and nation" (Revelation 5:9), fulfilling the exodus pattern on a cosmic scale. The seven "I wills" of Exodus 6 thus echo into eternity.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often noted that when we are most discouraged, God reminds us of what He will do rather than what we must do. The seven "I wills" of Exodus 6:6-7 shift focus from Moses' inadequacy to God's adequacy. This is a crucial pastoral principle: our confidence rests not on our ability to fulfill God's commands but on God's ability to fulfill His promises.

In our own lives, when circumstances grow harder rather than easier — when the pressure intensifies after we step out in obedience — these promises anchor us. God will complete what He started (cf. Philippians 1:6). The sequence of the "I wills" is also encouraging: God brings out before He brings in; He delivers before He dwells with us. There is an order to redemption that requires trust at every stage.

Believers facing prolonged trials can take courage from the fact that God's promises are not conditional on our performance. Each "I will" rests on God's character, not Israel's worthiness. Similarly, our salvation is "not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to His mercy" (Titus 3:5). When doubts assail, we can count the seven "I wills" — each one a stone of remembrance that God keeps His covenant forever.
