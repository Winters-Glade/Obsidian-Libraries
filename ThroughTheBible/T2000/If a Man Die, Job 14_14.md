## Summary

[[Job 14_14|Job 14:14]] poses the central question of human existence: 'If a man die, shall he live again?' In the midst of his [[suffering]], Job wrestles with the [[Hope]] of [[Resurrection]] and life beyond the grave, demonstrating an astonishing [[Faith]] even in the depths of [[despair]].

## 📜 Historical Background

Job's discourse in chapter [[Job 14|14]] is part of his second response to [[Eliphaz]]. Job reflects on the shortness and trouble of human life, comparing it to a flower that fades and a shadow that flees. Yet in verse [[Job 14_14|14]], he asks the penetrating question about life after [[death]]. Job expresses a remarkable hope: that God would hide him in [[Sheol]] until His wrath passes, and then remember him. This is one of the earliest and clearest expressions of [[Resurrection]] hope in the Old Testament.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Job's question finds its definitive answer in the [[Resurrection]] of [[Jesus Christ]]. [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]] declares in [[1 Corinthians 15_20-22.md]], 'But now is Christ risen from the dead, and become the firstfruits of them that slept. For since by man came death, by man came also the resurrection of the dead.' Jesus Himself declared, 'I am the resurrection, and the life: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live' ([[John 11_25|John 11:25]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

[[Chuck Smith]] often used this passage to comfort those facing [[death]]. The question 'If a man die, shall he live again?' is answered with a resounding 'Yes!' through the resurrection of [[Jesus Christ]]. For the believer, death is not the end but a transition into the presence of the Lord.

## 📚 Hebrew Roots & Wisdom Context

Job's question — "If a man die, shall he live again?" ([[Job 14_14|Job 14:14]]) — is framed within the Hebrew concept of *[[Sheol]]* (שְׁאוֹל), the shadowy realm of the dead in Old Testament thought. The word "hope" that Job uses (*tiqvah*, תִּקְוָה) appears earlier in [[Job 14_7|Job 14:7]], where a tree cut down may sprout again — Job longs for a similar hope for humanity. The book of Job stands as the Old Testament's most profound wrestling with the question of life after death, pushing beyond the common ancient Near Eastern view of the grave as the final end.

## 📖 Research Context

### The Question of Life After Death in the Old Testament

[[Job 14_14|Job 14:14]] — "If a man die, shall he live again?" — is perhaps the most direct question about [[Resurrection]] in the Old Testament [[wisdom]] literature. Job contrasts the hope of a felled tree (which can sprout again, vv. 7-9) with the finality of human [[death]]. Yet even in this darkest moment, Job expresses [[Hope]]: "All the days of my appointed time will I wait, till my change come" (v. 14).

### Sheol in Hebrew Thought

The Hebrew concept of **[[Sheol]]** (שְׁאוֹל) — the underworld or realm of the dead — underlies Job's question. In Old Testament theology, Sheol is a place of silence and shadow ([[Psalm 88_10-12.md]]), from which no one returns ([[Job 7_9-10|Job 7:9-10]]), yet not beyond God's reach ([[Psalm 139_8|Psalm 139:8]]; [[Amos 9_2|Amos 9:2]]).

The Old Testament does not contain a fully developed doctrine of resurrection — that comes in the intertestamental period and New Testament — but it plants seeds of expectation: [[_Bible/Job/Job 19_25-27.md|Job 19:25-27]] ("in my flesh shall I see God"), [[Psalm 16_9-11.md]] ("Thou wilt not leave my soul in Sheol"), [[Psalm 80_3, 7, 19|Psalm 49:15]] ("God will redeem my soul from the power of Sheol"), [[Daniel 12_2|Daniel 12:2]] ("many that sleep in the dust shall awake").

### New Testament Fulfillment

The Septuagint translated *Sheol* as **Hades**. When Jesus says "I have the keys of hell (Hades) and of death" ([[Revelation 1_18.md]]), He claims authority over the very realm that haunted Job. The resurrection of Christ transforms Sheol from hopeless waiting into a defeated enemy ([[1 Corinthians 15_55.md]]). Job's question receives its definitive answer: "I am the resurrection and the life" ([[John 11_25|John 11:25]]).
