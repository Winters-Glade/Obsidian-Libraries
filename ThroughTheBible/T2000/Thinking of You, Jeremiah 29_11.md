## Summary

[[Jeremiah 29_11.md]] is one of the most beloved verses in Scripture: 'For I know the thoughts that I think toward you, saith the Lord, thoughts of peace, and not of evil, to give you an expected end.' Pastor Chuck explores this promise in its original context — a letter to exiles in Babylon — revealing the heart of God for His people even in the midst of discipline.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[Jeremiah 29_11.md]] is part of a letter the prophet sent to the Jewish exiles already living in Babylon after the first deportation (597 BC). False prophets in Babylon were telling the exiles that their captivity would be short. Jeremiah's letter told them the opposite: settle down, build houses, plant gardens, pray for the peace of Babylon, because seventy years of exile lay ahead. The famous promise of 'plans to prosper you' was given to people who needed to hear that God had not abandoned them — even though their situation would not change quickly. This context transforms the verse from a generic feel-good promise into a call for patient endurance under God's sovereign timetable.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[Notes Romans Romans 9-10 – Summary & Themes]] echoes this truth: 'All things work together for good to them that love God, to them who are the called according to His purpose.' The 'expected end' (or 'future and hope') Jeremiah spoke of finds its ultimate fulfillment in the hope of the gospel — a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ ([[1 Peter 1_3-4.md]]). Paul writes in [[_Bible/Philippians/Philippians 1_6.md]] that He who began a good work in us will complete it until the day of Christ Jesus. God's plans for His people are always ultimately redemptive.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often pointed out that this verse was not written to people in comfortable circumstances but to exiles in captivity. Its power lies precisely in that context. When we face difficult seasons that feel like exile, we can know that God still has plans for us. He is not against us; He is for us. But His timing may not be our timing. The call is to trust Him in the waiting, build our lives where He has placed us, and believe that He is working — and that [[Restoration]] is coming — even when we cannot see it.
