# 202 The Resurrection of the Dead — Acts 24:14-15

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#202](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=F6cLe8L55IQ)

## Overview

Paul's defense before Governor Felix in Caesarea. The Jewish leaders bring Tertullus, a lawyer, to accuse Paul of being a troublemaker and ringleader of the Nazarene sect. Paul's response includes his confession: 'I worship the God of my fathers, believing all things which are written in the Law and in the Prophets... that there will be a resurrection of the dead, both of the just and the unjust.' Felix, who knew the facts about Christianity, kept Paul imprisoned for two years.

## 📜 Historical Background

Paul's defense before Governor Felix took place in Caesarea Maritima, the magnificent coastal city built by Herod the Great as the Roman administrative capital of Judea. Felix (Antonius Felix) was a former slave who had risen to power through his brother Pallas, a favorite of Emperor Claudius. The Roman historian Tacitus described Felix as one who 'practiced every kind of cruelty and lust, wielding the power of a king with the mind of a slave.' His wife Drusilla was a Jewish princess. Felix's knowledge of 'the Way' (Acts 24:22) likely came from her. The Jewish leaders hired Tertullus, a professional Roman orator, to present their case. Felix's procrastination — keeping Paul imprisoned for two years to curry favor with the Jews (Acts 24:27) — illustrates Roman provincial politics at their worst.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul's confession of the resurrection — 'that there will be a resurrection of the dead, both of the just and the unjust' — is the great hope and central doctrine of the Christian faith. This resurrection of both the righteous and unrighteous is taught in Daniel 12:2 ('many who sleep in the dust shall awake, some to everlasting life, some to shame') and confirmed by Jesus in John 5:28-29. Paul's defense of the resurrection in 1 Corinthians 15 provides the fullest theological treatment: if Christ is risen, believers will also rise, and death has been defeated. The phrase 'the Way' (hodos) to describe the Christian movement reflects the early church's self-understanding as the true path of God (Isaiah 35:8, Acts 9:2). Paul's clear conscience (Acts 24:16) echoes 2 Corinthians 1:12.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith noted the irony of Felix's response: he was afraid — not of Paul, but of the truth Paul spoke. Felix sent for Paul often and heard him 'concerning faith in Christ' (Acts 24:24), yet he delayed his decision. Smith warned that hearing the gospel repeatedly without responding hardens the heart. Felix's procrastination is a timeless warning: 'Go your way for now; when I have a convenient time I will call for you' (Acts 24:25). Many people intend to respond to the gospel but never find a 'convenient time.' The resurrection of both the just and the unjust means that everyone will face God — either in the hope of resurrection to life or the reality of resurrection to judgment.
