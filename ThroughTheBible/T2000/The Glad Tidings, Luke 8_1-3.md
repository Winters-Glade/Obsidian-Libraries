## Summary

Luke 8:1-3 introduces the women who ministered to Jesus from their resources — Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Susanna, and many others. These women had been healed of evil spirits and infirmities and responded by supporting Jesus' ministry. Pastor Chuck highlights the often-overlooked role of women in the Gospel story and the glad tidings that transform lives across all social boundaries.

## 📖 Research Context

Luke — the historian among the evangelists — includes details the other Gospels omit. This passage is unique to Luke and provides a remarkable glimpse into the economic structure of Jesus' itinerant ministry. These women funded the apostolic mission. Mary Magdalene (from Magdala, a fishing village on the Sea of Galilee's western shore) had been delivered from seven demons — a detail suggesting profound spiritual bondage. Joanna was the wife of Chuza, Herod Antipas' steward (epitropos — estate manager), placing her in the Herodian court. Susanna appears only here in Scripture — unnamed elsewhere but recorded by Luke's careful history. The phrase 'proclaiming and bringing the glad tidings of the kingdom of God' (Luke 8:1) uses euangelizomenos — the verb from which 'evangelism' derives. These women were not merely supporters but co-proclaimers of the good news.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Psalm 68:11 (the Lord gives the word; great is the company of women who proclaim it), Proverbs 31:20 (she extends her hand to the poor), Joel 2:28-29 (the Spirit poured out on maidservants).

## 📜 Historical Background

This brief passage is remarkable for its mention of the women who financially supported Jesus' ministry. In first-century Jewish culture, women had limited social and economic rights. That Luke — a meticulous historian — notes these women by name demonstrates their importance. Mary Magdalene had seven demons cast out of her. Joanna was the wife of Chuza, Herod's steward — meaning she came from the elite Herodian court. The presence of women from diverse backgrounds testifies to the Gospel's power to unite people across social barriers.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Galatians 3:28 declares that in Christ there is neither male nor female. Romans 16 lists several women who served prominently in the early church, including Phoebe (a deacon of the church in Cenchrea), Priscilla (a teacher and co-worker), and Junia (esteemed among the apostles). Acts 16:14-15 records Lydia, a businesswoman from Thyatira, whose household was baptized and who insisted on supporting Paul's ministry — a direct parallel to the women of Luke 8. Acts 18:2-3 shows Priscilla and Aquila as tentmakers who partnered with Paul and later instructed Apollos. Philippians 4:3 mentions women who labored with Paul in the Gospel. The women at the tomb were the first witnesses of the resurrection (Luke 24:1-10). The pattern of grateful, generous support for the Gospel ministry is commended throughout the New Testament.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that the glad tidings produce a glad response. These women didn't serve out of duty but out of gratitude for what Jesus had done for them. When we truly experience God's healing and deliverance, our resources naturally flow into His work. The diversity of the women — from those delivered from demonic bondage to a steward's wife — shows that the Gospel transforms and unites people across all social and economic boundaries.
