## Summary

Leviticus 26 is a powerful chapter of blessings for obedience and warnings for disobedience. Pastor Chuck explores God's promise that if we walk in His statutes, He will walk among us — and the sobering alternative of what happens when we reject His ways. The chapter concludes with the hope of restoration through confession.

## 📜 Historical Background

Leviticus 26 stands as the grand covenantal conclusion to the legal section of the book (often called the "Holiness Code," Leviticus 17–26). Structurally, it mirrors the treaty format common in ancient Near Eastern suzerain-vassal covenants of the second millennium BC — particularly the Hittite and Assyrian treaty patterns. These treaties typically contained: (1) a preamble identifying the suzerain (the great king); (2) a historical prologue recounting the king's benevolent deeds; (3) stipulations; (4) blessings for obedience; (5) curses for disobedience; (6) a call for witnesses; and (7) provision for deposition and periodic reading. Leviticus 26 follows this pattern closely, with Yahweh identifying Himself as the Sovereign who brought Israel out of Egypt (v. 13), followed by the blessings (vv. 3-13) and the escalating curses (vv. 14-39), concluding with a restoration passage (vv. 40-45) that is unique to Israel's covenant — no ancient Near Eastern treaty offered grace after covenant violation.

The seven cycles of judgment are structured in escalating severity, each introduced by the phrase "And if ye will not yet for all this hearken unto Me" (or its equivalent):

1. **Terror, disease, and defeat** (vv. 14-17) — breaking the pride of power.
2. **Drought and barrenness** (vv. 18-20) — breaking the pride of productivity.
3. **Wild beasts and depopulation** (vv. 21-22) — breaking the pride of security.
4. **Pestilence and siege** (vv. 23-26) — breaking the pride of self-sufficiency.
5. **Famine and desolation** (vv. 27-35) — breaking the pride of life itself.
6. **Dispersion among the nations** (vv. 36-39) — the final breaking of national identity.

With each level, the stated purpose is "to break the pride of your power" (v. 19) and to discipline Israel so that they might "hearken" and return. The Hebrew word *musar* (discipline/chastening) underlies this entire section — it is the same term used in Proverbs for the loving correction of a father toward his son (Proverbs 3:11-12; cf. Hebrews 12:5-6). God's judgments are never capricious; they are measured, purposeful, and pedagogical.

The blessings section (vv. 3-13) is equally remarkable. The centerpiece is not material prosperity per se but the presence of God: "I will set My tabernacle among you... and I will walk among you, and will be your God, and ye shall be My people" (vv. 11-12). This is the ultimate covenant blessing — the restoration of Edenic fellowship. The Hebrew verb *hithalek* ("I will walk") is the same used in Genesis 3:8 of God "walking" in the Garden. The covenant was always aimed at one thing: God dwelling with His people in unhindered relationship.

Notably, the curses section begins not with a violation of ritual law but with idolatry and Sabbath-breaking (vv. 1-2) — the vertical relationship between God and His people. The entire covenant framework is grounded in exclusive loyalty to Yahweh. The blessings and curses are not a system of works-righteousness but the stipulations of a relationship, analogous to marriage vows.

The chapter concludes (vv. 40-45) with a stunning turn: even when Israel is scattered among the nations, if they confess their iniquity and the iniquity of their fathers, God *will remember* His covenant with Jacob, Isaac, and Abraham. The Hebrew word *zakhar* (remember) here carries covenantal force — it means to act faithfully on a prior commitment. This is not a memory exercise but a promise of restoration. The chapter ends not with the terror of judgment but with the hope of the covenant — "for their sakes I will remember the covenant of their ancestors."

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The blessings and curses of Leviticus 26 find their New Testament counterpart in the Beatitudes and the warnings of Jesus. Paul explains that Christ "hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us" (Galatians 3:13) — that is, Jesus absorbed the covenant curses in His own body on the cross so that the blessings of Abraham might come to the Gentiles. The principle remains: "whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap" (Galatians 6:7). But under the New Covenant, the blessings are primarily spiritual rather than material — "blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who hath blessed us with all spiritual blessings in heavenly places in Christ" (Ephesians 1:3).

Several specific New Testament connections stand out:

1. **The pattern of discipline.** Hebrews 12:5-11 directly echoes the pedagogical purpose of Leviticus 26: "whom the Lord loveth He chasteneth." Discipline is the mark of sonship, not wrath. The escalating judgments of Leviticus 26 are the Father's loving pursuit of His wayward children.

2. **"I will walk among you."** This promise finds its ultimate fulfillment in the incarnation: "And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt [literally 'tabernacled'] among us" (John 1:14). In Christ, God walks among His people in the most intimate way possible. And in the new creation, the promise reaches its consummation: "Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men, and He will dwell with them" (Revelation 21:3).

3. **Confession and restoration.** Leviticus 26:40-42 — "If they shall confess their iniquity... then will I remember My covenant" — finds its New Covenant counterpart in 1 John 1:9: "If we confess our sins, He is faithful and just to forgive us our sins, and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness." The same pattern of confession leading to restoration persists from Sinai to the Upper Room.

4. **The curse absorbed.** The climactic curse of the law — exile and death — fell fully upon Christ at the cross, where He cried out, "My God, My God, why hast Thou forsaken Me?" (Matthew 27:46). That cry of dereliction was the ultimate curse of Leviticus 26 experienced so that we might never know it.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized that God's discipline is never punitive in the sense of vengeance — it is redemptive. The escalating judgments in Leviticus 26 are designed to wake people up and bring them back. God says, "I will walk among you" — that's the goal: relationship. When we stray, God uses circumstances to draw us back. The door of repentance is always open.

Several pastoral applications emerge:

1. **The purpose of hardship.** Not every difficulty is divine discipline, but believers should examine their lives when facing prolonged trial. Is God trying to get my attention? Is there unconfessed sin? Is there pride He is seeking to break? The seven cycles of Leviticus 26 teach us that God is patient but persistent — He will keep increasing the pressure until we turn to Him.

2. **Grace beyond the breaking point.** The most remarkable feature of Leviticus 26 is that it does not end with the curses. It ends with hope. Even when Israel has broken every stipulation, God remembers His covenant. This is the gospel in the Old Testament — grace that is stronger than sin. No matter how far we have strayed, the way back is always open through confession.

3. **Walking with God as the goal.** The blessings of Leviticus 26 are not primarily about material prosperity — they are about God's presence. "I will walk among you." The greatest blessing of the Christian life is not health, wealth, or success but the abiding presence of God. If we have that, we have everything. If we lack that, nothing else satisfies.

4. **The seriousness of covenant.** We live in an age of cheap grace and easy belief. Leviticus 26 reminds us that relationship with God is a serious covenant with real consequences. The New Covenant is no less binding — it is simply better guaranteed, secured by the blood of Christ and the indwelling Spirit.
