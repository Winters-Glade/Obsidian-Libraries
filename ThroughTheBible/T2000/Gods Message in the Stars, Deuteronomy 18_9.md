## 📜 Historical Background

Deuteronomy 18:9-22 addresses two fundamentally opposed sources of spiritual knowledge. The Canaanite nations practiced a wide range of forbidden occult arts, listed with precision in the Hebrew text. Each term carries distinct meaning: קֶסֶם (*qesem*, divination) — the general term for occult forecasting, often involving the casting of lots or arrows (cf. Ezekiel 21:21); מְעוֹנֵן (*me'onnen*, soothsayer/observer of times) — from עָנָן (*anan*, cloud), one who interprets omens from clouds or planetary alignments; נַחַשׁ (*nachash*, enchanter/serpent-diviner) — related to the serpent, suggesting snake-charming or omen-interpretation; מְכַשֵּׁף (*mekhasheph*, sorcerer) — from כָּשַׁף (*kashaf*, to whisper), one who mutters incantations; חוֹבֵר חָבֶר (*chover chaver*, charmer) — one who binds with spells or ties magic knots; שֹׁאֵל אוֹב (*sho'el ov*, consulter of a familiar spirit) — literally "one who inquires of a ghost" (אוֹב, *ov*, was the spirit of the dead, often consulted through a medium); יִדְּעֹנִי (*yidde'oni*, wizard/knowing spirit) — from יָדַע (*yada*, to know), one who claims secret knowledge from a familiar spirit; and דֹּרֵשׁ אֶל־הַמֵּתִים (*doresh el-hametim*, necromancer) — one who seeks the dead for guidance.

These practices were pervasive in the ANE. The Mari tablets (18th century BC, from Tell Hariri on the Euphrates) document extensive divination practices among the Amorites — liver divination (hepatoscopy), dream interpretation, and prophetic ecstatics. The Babylonian creation epic *Enuma Elish* tied the stars to the decrees of the gods, and the *Mul.Apin* tablets (c. 1000 BC) catalogued celestial omens — the very system of astral divination that Deuteronomy condemns. Even in Israel's later history, Manasseh reintroduced these abominations (2 Kings 21:6), and the prophets repeatedly railed against them (Isaiah 47:13-14; Jeremiah 27:9-10; Ezekiel 13:17-23).

In contrast, God promised to raise up a Prophet like Moses from among Israel (Deuteronomy 18:15-19). Moses was unique in speaking to God "mouth to mouth" (Numbers 12:8) and performing authenticating signs. The test of true prophecy was empirical: "when a prophet speaketh in the name of the LORD, if the thing follow not... that is the thing which the LORD hath not spoken" (v. 22). This established Israel's epistemology of revelation — not hidden, esoteric knowledge for the initiated, but public, verifiable, historical words from God through chosen messengers.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus is identified as the ultimate Prophet promised in Deuteronomy 18 — Peter explicitly cites this passage at Pentecost (Acts 3:22-23), declaring that Jesus is the fulfillment of Moses' prophecy. Stephen confirms it in his defense (Acts 7:37). The author of Hebrews opens with the declaration that God 'hath in these last days spoken unto us by His Son' (Hebrews 1:1-2) — where the Canaanites looked to stars, spirits, and sorcery, God has spoken fully and finally in Christ. The psalmist declares that 'the heavens declare the glory of God' (Psalm 19:1) — the stars are God's message, not objects of worship. Paul warns against astrology and elemental spirits (Colossians 2:8) while affirming that creation itself reveals God's eternal power (Romans 1:20). The Bereans are commended for searching the Scriptures daily to verify Paul's teaching (Acts 17:11), embodying the test of true prophecy. John warns to 'try the spirits whether they are of God' (1 John 4:1-3), and Peter commends the 'more sure word of prophecy' (2 Peter 1:19) — the written Word — as a light shining in a dark place.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often addressed the fascination with astrology and the occult in modern culture. He taught that the stars indeed declare God's glory, but they are not to be consulted for guidance. God speaks clearly through His Word and His Son. Seeking hidden knowledge through forbidden means is an expression of unbelief — it shows we don't trust God's revealed truth to be sufficient. Chuck observed that people turn to the occult not because they are evil but because they are spiritually hungry — they want to know the future, they want power, they want connection with the spiritual realm. The gospel answers every legitimate hunger: the future is in God's hands, power comes through the Holy Spirit, and connection with the spiritual realm is through Jesus Christ, the one Mediator between God and man. The true Prophet, Jesus Christ, has spoken the final word. In a culture saturated with horoscopes, tarot cards, and spiritualism, the church must offer a better Word — not merely condemnation of the false, but proclamation of the true. As Chuck often said, 'The stars don't tell you what to do, but they do tell you that God is glorious.'
