## Summary

John 6:66 records a tragic turning point: 'From that time many of His disciples went back, and walked no more with Him.' Jesus had taught hard sayings — eating His flesh and drinking His blood — and many followers left. Pastor Chuck examines the cost of discipleship and the paradox that when Jesus required less, the crowds followed; when He required more, they departed. The twelve remained, not because they understood everything, but because they knew Jesus had the words of eternal life.

## 📖 Research Context

John 6 is a watershed chapter in the Gospel — the feeding of the 5,000, the walking on water, and the Bread of Life discourse. The crowd's enthusiasm after the feeding (they wanted to make Jesus king by force — John 6:15) turned to confusion and offense when Jesus spoke of eating His flesh and drinking His blood. This language deliberately evokes the Cross and the Eucharist — hard sayings that demand a decision. The Greek phrase apēlthon eis ta opisō ('went back into the things behind') suggests a return to the old life, not merely physical departure. Jesus' question to the Twelve — 'Will you also go away?' (John 6:67) — is the most pointed test of loyalty in the Gospels. Peter's reply is both confession and commitment: 'Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life. And we believe and are sure that You are the Christ, the Son of the living God.' This moment foreshadows the even greater sifting at the cross.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Joshua 24:15 (choose this day whom you will serve), 1 Kings 18:21 (how long will you falter between two opinions?), Jeremiah 17:5-8 (curse on those who trust in man, blessing on those who trust in the Lord).

## 📜 Historical Background

John 6 follows the feeding of the five thousand, after which the crowd wanted to make Jesus king by force. Jesus withdrew and walked on water to meet His disciples. The next day, the crowd found Him in Capernaum. His discourse on the Bread of Life — where He said they must eat His flesh and drink His blood — caused many to stumble. The language was intentionally provocative, forcing a decision. The Greek word for 'went back' (apēlthon eis ta opisō) literally means 'went away into the things behind' — returning to their old life. Jesus turned to the twelve and asked the pivotal question: 'Will you also go away?'

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Matthew 16:24 — if anyone will come after Me, let him deny himself, take up his cross, and follow Me. Luke 14:25-33 — count the cost before building. Hebrews 3:12-14 — take care lest there be an evil heart of unbelief in departing from the living God. Hebrews 10:38-39 — if any man draw back, My soul has no pleasure in him; we are not of those who draw back to perdition. 2 Timothy 4:10 — Demas has forsaken me, having loved this present world. 1 John 2:19 — they went out from us because they were not of us.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith observed that Jesus did not soften His words to keep the crowd. When the numbers dwindled, He didn't chase after them with an easier message. Many people want Jesus on their terms, but He must be received on His. Peter's confession — 'Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life' — is the heart of true discipleship. We follow not because we understand everything but because we know the One we follow is the Christ.
