## Summary

Chuck Smith examines one of the most instructive failures in the conquest narrative — the Gibeonite deception. After dramatic victories at Jericho and Ai, Israel faced a new threat: not military opposition but religious deception. The fatal flaw is captured in Joshua 9:14 — 'The men took of their provisions, but they did not ask counsel of the Lord.' They relied on their own senses and reasoning rather than seeking God's direction. This was not a failure of intelligence but a failure of devotion.

## 📜 Historical Background

Joshua 9 occurs after the conquest of Jericho (Joshua 6) and the defeat at Ai followed by victory (Joshua 7-8). Israel had been commanded to destroy all Canaanite nations (Deuteronomy 7:1-2). The Gibeonites were Hivites — one of the seven Canaanite nations marked for destruction — who chose deception over military confrontation.

**The Deception in Detail:** The Gibeonites disguised themselves as ambassadors from a distant land. They wore old, patched sandals and ragged clothes, carried dry, moldy bread, and worn-out wineskins. They claimed, 'This bread of ours was hot when we took it for provision from our houses on the day we left to come to you' (Joshua 9:12). The men of Israel examined the evidence with their senses — they saw the mold, felt the worn leather, tasted the dry bread — and made their judgment based on what they could perceive. The text deliberately contrasts their sensory examination with what they failed to do: 'they did not ask counsel of the Lord.'

**The Means of Divine Guidance:** Israel possessed several means of seeking God's counsel. The Urim and Thummim, worn in the breastplate of the high priest (Exodus 28:30; Numbers 27:21), were specifically designated for inquiring of the Lord. Eleazar the priest stood ready to consult God on Joshua's behalf. But 'the men' — not Joshua alone but presumably the leaders — examined the provisions themselves and bypassed the priestly consultation entirely. This is the second time Joshua made this error; the first was at Ai, where he sent spies without inquiring of the Lord (Joshua 7:2-3), resulting in defeat.

**Ancient Near Eastern Covenant Customs:** In the ancient Near East, once a covenant was made and sworn by the name of a deity, it was irrevocable. The Hittite and Akkadian treaty texts from this period show that treaty oaths were considered absolutely binding, even if obtained by fraud. Israel's leaders, having sworn 'by the Lord God of Israel' (Joshua 9:19), could not break the covenant without violating God's name. This cultural-legal context explains why Israel honored the treaty despite the deception.

**The Ironic Aftermath:** One of the Gibeonite cities listed in the deception — Kirjath-jearim — later became the place where the Ark of the Covenant was kept for 20 years after its return from Philistine captivity (1 Samuel 7:1-2; 1 Chronicles 13:5-6). The very people who deceived Israel into a covenant became the caretakers of God's sacred Ark. Centuries later, when Saul violated Israel's covenant with the Gibeonites by killing some of them, the land suffered famine until David made restitution (2 Samuel 21:1-14). The Gibeonites later became Nethinim (temple servants) in the post-exilic period (Ezra 8:20), serving in the house of God — an unlikely redemption from their deceptive origins.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The principle of seeking the Lord's counsel rather than relying on human wisdom is central to the New Testament. James instructs, 'If any of you lacks wisdom, let him ask of God, who gives to all liberally' (James 1:5). Proverbs 3:5-6 — 'Trust in the Lord with all your heart, and lean not on your own understanding' — could have prevented the Gibeonite disaster. Paul warns that 'the natural man receives not the things of the Spirit of God, for they are foolishness to him' (1 Corinthians 2:14). Jesus Himself modeled the discipline of seeking the Father's direction: 'I do nothing of Myself; but as My Father taught Me, I speak these things' (John 8:28). The failure at Gibeon reminds us that our senses and reasoning are fallible; only God sees the heart.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the most dangerous moment in the Christian life often comes not in times of obvious crisis but in seemingly routine decisions. Israel had just experienced remarkable victories, yet in a moment of complacency they failed to seek God. The fatal flaw was not ignorance but neglect — they had access to divine counsel but did not use it. Every decision, whether great or small, should be brought before the Lord. The consequence of omission — failing to do what we know we should — is often more dangerous than the consequence of commission. Israel's leaders used their natural senses (sight, touch, taste) to evaluate the Gibeonites' story, but the heart cannot be discerned by the senses. Only God 'searches the heart' (Jeremiah 17:10; Revelation 2:23). The lesson for believers is that our most confident judgments — especially those that 'make sense' to our natural minds — must still be brought before the Lord in prayer.
