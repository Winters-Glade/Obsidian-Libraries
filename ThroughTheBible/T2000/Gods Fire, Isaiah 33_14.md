## Summary

'The sinners in [[Zion]] are afraid; fearfulness hath surprised the hypocrites. Who among us shall dwell with the devouring fire? Who among us shall dwell with everlasting burnings?' Pastor Chuck explores the fear of God's [[Holiness of God|holiness]] and the paradox of finding refuge in the consuming fire of God's presence.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[Isaiah 33.md]] is set during the crisis of [[Sennacherib]]'s invasion of [[Judah]] (701 BC). The Assyrian army surrounded [[Jerusalem]], and the people were terrified. But Isaiah contrasts the fear of man with the [[fear of the Lord]]. The 'devouring fire' imagery draws from the wilderness tabernacle and [[Temple]] — the presence of God that consumed the sacrifices on the altar. The question 'who among us can dwell with everlasting burnings?' challenges the complacent: God's [[holiness]] is both terrifying and our only true refuge.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 12_28-29.md]] declares 'Our God is a consuming fire.' But for believers in Christ, the consuming fire becomes a place of purification, not destruction ([[1 Corinthians 3_11-15.md]]). Through Christ, we can dwell with the everlasting burnings because He has become our righteousness. As [[John the Baptist]] said, Christ baptizes 'with the Holy Ghost and with fire' ([[Matthew 3_11.md]]) — fire that purifies rather than destroys.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith noted that the sinners in Zion were afraid because they realized God's presence exposes everything. Hypocrisy cannot survive in God's presence. The good news is that the same fire that consumes sin also provides light and warmth for those who are right with God. The key is being right with God through [[Faith]].
