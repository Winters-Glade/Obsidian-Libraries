## Summary

Matthew 7:21-27 concludes the Sermon on the Mount with Jesus' parable of the wise and foolish builders. Not everyone who says 'Lord, Lord' will enter the kingdom — only those who do the will of the Father. The wise builder hears Jesus' words and acts on them, building on rock that withstands the storms of life. The foolish builder hears but does not act, building on sand that collapses when trials come. Pastor Chuck explores the critical difference between profession and possession — between saying the right things and building a life on the solid rock of genuine obedience.

## 📜 Historical Background

The Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7) is the first of five major discourses in Matthew's Gospel, presenting Jesus as the new Moses delivering the Law from the mountain. This concluding section serves as a dramatic warning: hearing is not enough; obedience is required. In Palestine, building on rock was common sense to experienced builders — the rocky hillsides provided firm foundations, while the wadis (dry riverbeds) looked like flat, convenient building sites but became deathtraps when flash floods came. Jesus' audience would have understood this imagery immediately from their own experience.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** Galilee's landscape is marked by limestone hills and seasonal wadis that run dry in summer but become torrents during winter rains. Building practices in first-century Palestine reflected this reality: houses were typically built of fieldstone with mud mortar, and experienced builders excavated down to bedrock before laying foundations — a labor-intensive but essential practice. Jesus' concluding parable in the Sermon on the Mount would have resonated deeply with an audience familiar with both the convenience of building on level ground and the devastating consequences when the rains came. The Roman presence in Galilee — including the legionary road-building projects that linked the region — meant that construction techniques and engineering standards were a visible part of daily life. Matthew's Gospel, structured as five discourses framing the narrative of Jesus' life, presents these words as the foundation of the new covenant community, with the call to obedience as the test of true discipleship.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

James 1:22-25 echoes this teaching: 'Be ye doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving your own selves.' James compares the hearer-only to a man who looks at his face in a mirror and then forgets what he looks like. Paul warns in 1 Corinthians 3:10-15 about building on the foundation of Christ with materials that will be tested by fire. Jesus' brother Jude (Jude 1:20-21) urges believers to build themselves up in their most holy faith. The image of building on the rock is also developed by Paul in Ephesians 2:19-22, where believers are built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, with Christ Himself as the chief cornerstone.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often warned against the danger of mere religious activity without genuine heart obedience. It is possible to prophesy, cast out demons, and do many wonderful works in Jesus' name and yet not truly know Him (Matthew 7:22-23). The test of true discipleship is not the visibility of our ministry but the quiet, consistent obedience of building our lives on His words. The storms — trials, temptations, and the final judgment — will reveal whether our foundation is real.
