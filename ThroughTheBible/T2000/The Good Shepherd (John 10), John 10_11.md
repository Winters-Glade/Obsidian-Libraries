## Summary

John 10:11 contains Jesus' declaration: 'I am the Good Shepherd: the good shepherd giveth His life for the sheep.' Pastor Chuck explores the beautiful imagery of Christ as the Shepherd who knows His sheep, leads them, protects them, and ultimately lays down His life for them. The Good Shepherd stands in contrast to the hireling who abandons the sheep when danger comes. Jesus' voluntary sacrifice sets Him apart as the true and faithful Shepherd.

## 📖 Research Context

This is the fourth of seven 'I AM' (egō eimi) statements in John's Gospel — a direct claim to divine identity. In the Greek Septuagint, 'I AM' (egō eimi) is the divine name revealed at the burning bush (Exodus 3:14 — 'I AM WHO I AM'). Jesus says 'I AM the Good Shepherd' — not merely 'a good shepherd' but the archetypal, true Shepherd. The context is Jesus' confrontation with the Pharisees after healing the man born blind (John 9). The Pharisees have just acted as false shepherds — casting out the healed man rather than protecting him. Jesus contrasts Himself with the hireling who abandons the sheep: the hireling represents the religious leaders who care only for themselves. The claim 'I lay down My life for the sheep' (John 10:11, 15, 17-18) is explicitly linked to the cross — Jesus' death is a voluntary, substitutionary sacrifice. The phrase occurs three times in this passage, emphasizing the intentionality of the atonement.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Psalm 23 (the Lord is my Shepherd — David's foundational image), Ezekiel 34:1-31 (condemnation of Israel's false shepherds and promise of one Shepherd — My Servant David), Zechariah 11:4-17 (the shepherd rejected and struck), Isaiah 40:11 (He will feed His flock like a shepherd).

## 📜 Historical Background

Shepherding imagery permeates the Old Testament. David — Israel's greatest king — was a shepherd. Psalm 23 begins 'The Lord is my Shepherd.' Ezekiel 34 condemns the shepherds (leaders) of Israel who fed themselves rather than the flock, and God promises to set one Shepherd over them — His servant David (a Messianic prophecy). In first-century Judea, shepherds lived with their flocks, knew each sheep by name, and led them to pasture daily. The sheepfold was a walled enclosure where sheep were gathered at night. Jesus' claim to be the Good Shepherd is a direct claim to divine identity.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Psalm 23 — the Lord is my Shepherd. Ezekiel 34:23 — I will set up one Shepherd over them. Hebrews 13:20-21 — the great Shepherd of the sheep raised from the dead, equip you with every good thing to do His will. 1 Peter 2:25 — returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of your souls. 1 Peter 5:1-4 — shepherd the flock of God, serving as overseers willingly; and when the Chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the crown of glory. Revelation 7:17 — the Lamb who is in the midst of the throne shall shepherd them and lead them to living fountains of waters. Isaiah 53:6 — all we like sheep have gone astray.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that the Good Shepherd knows His sheep individually — not just as a crowd but by name. That personal relationship is the foundation of our security. The hireling serves for pay and abandons at the first sign of danger. Jesus serves because He loves. He didn't just risk His life — He laid it down voluntarily. 'No man takes it from Me, but I lay it down of Myself.' The cross was not an accident but an appointment. We can trust a Shepherd who gave everything for the sheep.
