## Summary

[[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 22.md]] presents one of the most profound and moving narratives in all of Scripture. God commands Abraham to take his beloved son Isaac to the land of Moriah and offer him as a burnt offering. Without hesitation, Abraham rises early the next morning, cuts the wood, and sets out on the three-day journey with Isaac. When Isaac asks where the lamb is for the sacrifice, Abraham speaks one of the most powerful statements of faith ever uttered: "My son, God will provide Himself a lamb." At the moment of obedience, the angel of the Lord stops Abraham's hand, and a ram caught in the thicket by its horns becomes the substitute. Abraham names the place [[Jehovah-Jireh]] — the Lord Will Provide. This event is the supreme Old Testament picture of [[substitutionary atonement]]: a father offering his son, and God providing the sacrifice.

## Key Takeaways

1. The ultimate test of faith is whether we will trust God with what we love most.
2. God never intended for Isaac to die — He was testing Abraham's heart, not requiring child sacrifice.
3. Abraham's faith included the conviction that God could raise Isaac from the dead (Hebrews 11:19).
4. Mount Moriah is the same region as Calvary — the place of substitutionary sacrifice.
5. The ram in the thicket points forward to Christ, the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world.
6. The fellowship of suffering with Christ (our great fellowship) is the highest calling of faith.

## Further Context

### Historical & Cultural Background
Mount Moriah (Gen 22:2) is traditionally identified with the Temple Mount in Jerusalem. Second Chronicles 3:1 explicitly states that Solomon built the Temple on Mount Moriah, where the Lord appeared to David. The three-day journey from Beersheba to Moriah (roughly 70 km/45 miles) gave Abraham ample time to reconsider God's command — his sustained obedience over three days demonstrates the depth of his faith. The Hebrew phrase *Akedah* ("binding") is how Jewish tradition refers to this event. Child sacrifice was practiced by Canaanite cultures (particularly to the god Molech), making God's command culturally understandable but theologically shocking — and the provision of a ram instead becomes a powerful rejection of pagan practice.

### Old Testament Connections
- **Micah 6:6-8** — The prophet asks, "Shall I give my firstborn for my transgression?" and answers that God requires not child sacrifice but justice, mercy, and walking humbly with God — the Moriah event reinterpreted.
- **Exodus 13:1-2, 11-16** — The dedication of the firstborn to God, rooted in the Passover deliverance, echoes the principle that the firstborn belongs to God but is redeemed by a substitute.
- **Leviticus 16** — The Day of Atonement, with its two goats (one sacrificed, one scapegoat), embodies the same principle of substitution established on Moriah.
- **1 Chronicles 21:18-28** — David purchases the threshing floor of Ornan on Mount Moriah to build an altar, explicitly linking the site to Abraham's sacrifice.
- **Hosea 6:6** — "For I desired mercy, and not sacrifice; and the knowledge of God more than burnt offerings" — the heart of God that Abraham understood on Moriah.

### New Testament Connections
- **John 3:16** — "God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son" — the ultimate fulfilment: where Abraham was spared from offering Isaac, God did not spare His own Son.
- **Romans 8:31-32** — "He that spared not his own Son, but delivered him up for us all" — Paul explicitly draws the parallel between Abraham's offering and God's offering of Christ.
- **Hebrews 11:17-19** — "By faith Abraham, when he was tried, offered up Isaac... accounting that God was able to raise him up, even from the dead" — revealing that Abraham believed in resurrection power.
- **James 2:21-23** — Abraham's faith was perfected by works — the offering of Isaac demonstrated the reality of his faith.
- **1 Peter 1:18-19** — "Redeemed... with the precious blood of Christ, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot" — the substitutionary lamb of Moriah fulfilled in Christ.
- **Galatians 3:13-14** — Christ became a curse for us, the ultimate substitute, so that the blessing of Abraham might come on the Gentiles.

### Extra-Biblical Sources
- The *Akedah* (binding of Isaac) is the most commented-upon narrative in Jewish tradition. The *Targum Pseudo-Jonathan* expands the story, portraying Isaac as willing and even asking his father to bind him securely so he would not flinch.
- Philo of Alexandria (*On Abraham* 32) interpreted the Akedah allegorically, seeing Abraham as the mind acquiring virtue and Isaac as joy, offered back to God.
- Søren Kierkegaard's *Fear and Trembling* explores the "teleological suspension of the ethical" — Abraham's faith as a paradoxical relationship with God that transcends moral categories.
- The location of Moriah is confirmed by the Samaritan Pentateuch as "the land of Moriah" and by 2 Chronicles 3:1 as the site of Solomon's Temple.
