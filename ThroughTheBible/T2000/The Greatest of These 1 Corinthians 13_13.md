# 220 The Greatest of These — 1 Corinthians 13:13

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#220](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_fmf21_PxO4)

## Overview

Three things remain: faith, hope, and love — but the greatest of these is love. Paul concludes his great love chapter by elevating love above even the foundational virtues of faith and hope. Love is the ultimate and enduring quality of the Christian life because God Himself is love. Faith and hope are essential for this present age — faith receives God's promises, hope anticipates their fulfillment — but in the age to come, faith will become sight and hope will be realized. Love alone continues into eternity because God is love and we will dwell in His presence forever.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Love | The greatest of the abiding virtues because it partakes of God's own nature — love endures when faith becomes sight and hope is realized |
| Faith | The substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen — essential in this age but surpassed by sight in the age to come |
| Hope | The confident expectation of future glory — sustaining believers now but fulfilled at Christ's return |
| Abiding Virtues | The three foundational Christian virtues that remain when prophecies, tongues, and knowledge pass away |
| Permanence of Love | Unlike spiritual gifts which serve a temporary purpose, love is eternal because it reflects the eternal nature of God |

## Pauline Context

1 Corinthians 13 is often called Paul's greatest literary achievement — a prose poem on love placed strategically between chapters on [[Spiritual Gifts]] and the [[Resurrection]]. Paul's argument is that the Corinthians' obsession with spectacular gifts is misplaced because gifts are temporary while love is eternal. The three abiding virtues — faith, hope, and love — appear together in Paul's letters as a triad (1 Thessalonians 1:3, 5:8; Colossians 1:4-5). Paul's elevation of love above even faith and hope reflects his conviction that love is the very nature of God and the goal of all Christian growth. In his letter to the Romans, Paul writes that "love is the fulfillment of the law" (Romans 13:10), and in Galatians, he affirms that "faith works through love" (Galatians 5:6).
