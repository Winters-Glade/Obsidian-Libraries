## Summary

In the year King [[Uzziah]] died, Isaiah saw the Lord high and lifted up. This vision transformed Isaiah's life and marked his [[Prophecy|prophetic]] calling. The [[seraphim]] cried 'Holy, holy, holy' and the temple shook. Isaiah's response — 'Woe is me, for I am undone' — reveals the profound effect of encountering God's [[Holiness of God|holiness]].

## 📜 Historical Background

King Uzziah's 52-year reign (792-740 BC) was a time of prosperity and military strength for [[Judah]]. His death marked the end of an era and created uncertainty about Judah's future. It was in this moment of political instability that Isaiah received his vision of the true King — the Lord of Hosts. The vision likely took place in the [[Jerusalem]] [[Temple]]. The seraphim (literally 'burning ones') covered their faces and feet in reverence before God's [[Holiness of God|holiness]].

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[John 12_41]] explicitly states that Isaiah saw Christ's glory and spoke of Him. The [[holiness]] of God displayed in [[Isaiah 6]] is the same [[holiness]] that [[Christ]] embodies ([[Hebrews 1_3]]). Isaiah's cleansing with a live coal from the altar prefigures the purification that comes through Christ's sacrifice ([[Hebrews 9_13-14.md]]). The commission 'Whom shall I send?' anticipates the Great Commission ([[Matthew 28_19-20.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that a true vision of God always produces humility. Before we can speak for God, we must first be undone before Him. The sequence is crucial: vision leads to confession, which leads to cleansing, which leads to commission. You cannot effectively serve God until you have first encountered His [[holiness]] and received His cleansing.
