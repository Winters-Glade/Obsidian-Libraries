## Summary

Ezekiel 22:30-31 records God's heartbreaking search: 'I sought for a man among them, that should make up the hedge, and stand in the gap before me for the land, that I should not destroy it: but I found none.' Pastor Chuck explores God's desire for an intercessor — one person who would stand between God's righteous [[Judgment]] and a sinful nation. When no one was found, judgment came.

## 📜 Historical Background

Ezekiel 22 is a comprehensive indictment of Jerusalem's leadership — the princes (v. 6), the priests (v. 26), the prophets (v. 28), and the people (v. 29) are all charged with specific sins. The chapter builds a case for why [[Judgment]] is unavoidable. In verse 30, God shifts from accusation to lament: He wanted to spare the city but could not find a single person to 'stand in the gap' — to intercede effectively before Him. This plays on the imagery of a breached city wall where one person could hold the gap against attackers. The gate was open and no one was willing or able to close it through [[Intercessory prayer|intercession]]. This is a powerful [[Prophecy]] of God's heart for relationship rather than mere religion.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus Christ is the ultimate fulfillment of this passage. He alone could stand in the gap between God and humanity, making intercession for the transgressors ([[Isaiah 53_12.md]]). Paul exhorts Timothy that 'supplications, prayers, intercessions, and giving of thanks, be made for all men' ([[1 Timothy 2_1.md]]), emphasizing the ongoing need for those who will stand in the gap. The writer of Hebrews declares that Christ 'is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him, seeing he ever liveth to make intercession for them' ([[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 7_25.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

This is both a solemn warning and an urgent call. God still searches for those who will stand in the gap — intercessors who will pray for their families, churches, and nations. Chuck Smith often said that the greatest need of any generation is for people who will pray. The tragedy of Ezekiel's day was not that God was unwilling to save but that no one was willing to intercede.
