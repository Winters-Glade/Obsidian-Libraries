# 217 Time Is Short — 1 Corinthians 7:29-31

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#217](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9tIc5ayTYvY)

## Overview

Paul's counsel to the Corinthians is framed by the conviction that 'the time is short.' Those who have wives should be as though they had none, those who weep as though they wept not — not because relationships don't matter, but because the present world order is passing away and undivided devotion to the Lord is paramount. Paul does not despise marriage, sorrow, or joy; he relativizes them in light of the coming age. The urgency is not born of pessimism but of the conviction that the [[Resurrection]] of Christ has inaugurated the last days, and believers must live with eternal perspective.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Eschatology | The conviction that the present age is drawing to a close shapes every aspect of Christian living — the "shortness" of time is a theological and moral reality |
| Urgency | The passing of the present order demands a reordering of priorities — the most important things must come first |
| Detachment | Not indifference to creation but freedom from being consumed by it — holding earthly things with open hands |
| Devotion to the Lord | Undistracted commitment to Christ is the ultimate priority, relativizing even the most legitimate human relationships and pursuits |
| The Present Form Passing | The "schema" (form) of this world is transient — believers must live in light of the permanence of the age to come |

## Pauline Context

1 Corinthians 7:29-31 is part of Paul's extended teaching on marriage and singleness (1 Corinthians 7). Paul's eschatological perspective is the key to understanding his otherwise puzzling statements about living "as though not" — he is not denying the reality of worldly experiences but relativizing their ultimate importance. This passage reflects the "already but not yet" tension central to Pauline theology: the new creation has broken in through Christ's resurrection, but the old age still lingers. Paul's conviction that "the form of this world is passing away" (paragei to schēma tou kosmou toutou) is the basis for his ethical teaching throughout his letters. The same urgency appears in Romans 13:11-12 and Philippians 4:5.
