## Summary

Luke 20:37-38 records Jesus' teaching on the resurrection in response to the Sadducees, who denied the resurrection. Jesus cites Moses' encounter at the burning bush: 'I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob' — not 'I was' but 'I am.' Pastor Chuck explores the profound truth that God is the God of the living, not the dead, and that those who belong to Him live forever in His presence.

## 📖 Research Context

This debate occurs during Jesus' final week in Jerusalem, as the religious leaders attempt to trap Him. The Sadducees' hypothetical scenario about a woman marrying seven brothers (based on the levirate marriage law of Deuteronomy 25:5-6) was designed to make the resurrection seem absurd. Jesus dismantles their argument on two fronts. First, He distinguishes between this age and the age to come — resurrection life is qualitatively different, not merely a continuation of earthly existence. Second, He appeals to the Torah itself (which the Sadducees accepted) — Exodus 3:6, the burning bush passage. The present tense 'I AM' is not grammatical accident but theological revelation. God's covenant relationship with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob transcends death. If God is their God and they are dead, then God is the God of the dead. But He identifies Himself as the God of the living — therefore they live.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Exodus 3:6-15 (I AM the God of your fathers — the foundational covenant identity of Yahweh), Psalm 16:9-11 (You will not abandon my soul to Sheol), Daniel 12:2 (many who sleep in the dust shall awake), Hosea 13:14 (I will ransom them from the power of Sheol).

## 📜 Historical Background

The Sadducees were a Jewish sect that accepted only the Pentateuch (Torah) as authoritative and denied the resurrection, angels, and spirits. They challenged Jesus with a hypothetical scenario based on the levirate marriage law (Deuteronomy 25:5). Jesus responded by citing Exodus 3:6 — the burning bush account — which the Sadducees accepted as Scripture. His argument turns on the tense of the verb: 'I am' not 'I was.' If God is still the God of the patriarchs centuries after their deaths, they must still be alive to Him.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

1 Corinthians 15 — the great resurrection chapter: if Christ be not risen, our faith is vain. John 11:25-26 — I am the Resurrection and the Life; whoever believes in Me, though he were dead, yet shall he live. Philippians 3:20-21 — our citizenship is in heaven, and we await a transformed body. 1 Thessalonians 4:13-18 — the dead in Christ shall rise first. Revelation 21:4 — God shall wipe away every tear; death shall be no more.

1 Corinthians 15:42-44 — sown in corruption, raised in incorruption; sown a natural body, raised a spiritual body. 1 Corinthians 15:50-55 — death is swallowed up in victory; O death, where is your sting? O grave, where is your victory?

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith observed that the Sadducees made the mistake of limiting God by their own understanding. They couldn't imagine how resurrection could work, so they denied it. Jesus points us back to God's own self-revelation: He is not the God of the dead. The implications are staggering — death does not end our relationship with God. Those who have died in faith are not lost but are alive to God. Our hope is not wishful thinking but rooted in the character of the God who is the God of the living.
