## Summary

Ezekiel 26:1-14 pronounces [[Judgment]] on Tyre, the proud Phoenician city-state that rejoiced over Jerusalem's downfall. 'I am the Lord, I have spoken,' declares God. Pastor Chuck explores the certainty of God's prophetic word — what God decrees, He brings to pass. Tyre's judgment was fulfilled through Nebuchadnezzar and later Alexander the Great in a remarkable sequence of events.

## 📜 Historical Background

Tyre was a wealthy Phoenician seaport on the Mediterranean coast, renowned for its maritime trade. When Jerusalem fell in 586 BC, Tyre rejoiced because it saw a commercial rival eliminated (v. 2). Ezekiel's [[Prophecy]] against Tyre (chapters 26-28) contains remarkable detail: Nebuchadnezzar would besiege the mainland city (fulfilled c. 573 BC after a 13-year siege), and later Alexander the Great would destroy the island fortress (332 BC) by building a causeway from the ruins of the mainland city — literally scraping her 'like the top of a rock' (v. 4) and making it a place for drying fishing nets. This is a classic example of fulfilled [[Prophecy]] confirming the reliability of God's word.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus visited the region of Tyre and Sidon ([[Matthew 15_21-28.md]]) and commended the [[Faith]] of a Syrophoenician woman there. Paul found disciples at Tyre on his journey to Jerusalem ([[Acts 21_3-6]]). The fall of Tyre serves as a type of the judgment that awaits all who oppose God's purposes and take pride in their own achievements. The book of Revelation depicts Babylon the Great — a symbolic Tyre — falling under God's judgment ([[_Bible/Revelation/Revelation 18.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith would often point to Tyre as proof that God's Word is absolutely reliable. The specific prophecies against Tyre — that it would be scraped bare, that its stones would be laid in the sea, that it would become a place for drying nets — were fulfilled centuries apart with precision. If God is faithful to His judgments, He is equally faithful to His promises of mercy.
