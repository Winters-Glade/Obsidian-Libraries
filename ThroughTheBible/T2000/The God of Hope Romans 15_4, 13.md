# 213 The God of Hope — Romans 15:4, 13

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#213](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I4gXH4p6jvg)

## Overview

Paul prays that the God of hope would fill believers with all joy and peace in believing, that they might abound in hope through the power of the [[Holy Spirit]]. The Scriptures were written for our learning, that through patience and comfort we might have hope. God Himself is the source and sustainer of true hope. This benediction appears near the conclusion of Romans, capturing the apostle's pastoral heart. The God of hope is the fountainhead — joy and peace flow from believing, hope abounds through the Spirit's power, and Scripture provides the patient endurance and comfort that sustain hope.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Hope | Not wishful thinking but confident expectation anchored in the character of God, the promises of Scripture, and the power of the Spirit |
| Joy | The deep, Spirit-produced gladness that results from believing — joy is not circumstantial but flows from faith |
| Peace | The tranquil confidence that comes from trusting the God of hope, part of the fruit of righteousness |
| Scripture | The Old Testament writings provide instruction, patience, and comfort — they were written for our learning to sustain hope |
| Holy Spirit | The agent through whom hope, joy, and peace are produced in believers — "by the power of the Holy Spirit" |

## Pauline Context

Romans 15:4 and 15:13 are a fitting capstone to Paul's letter, summarizing the key themes that began in Romans 1:16-17: the gospel is the power of God for salvation, the just shall live by faith, and hope is the ultimate expression of that faith. The "God of hope" title is unique to Paul in this passage, reflecting his conviction that hope is not a mere human attitude but a gift from God rooted in His own character. Paul's emphasis on Scripture as the source of patience and comfort reflects his high view of the Old Testament as Christian Scripture — written not just for ancient Israel but for our learning. This passage also connects to Paul's prayer in Ephesians 3:16-19 for believers to be strengthened with power through the Spirit.
