## Summary

Matthew 22:41-46 records Jesus' masterful question to the Pharisees: 'What think ye of Christ? Whose son is He?' They answer, 'The son of David.' Jesus then quotes Psalm 110:1 — 'The Lord said unto my Lord, Sit Thou on My right hand, till I make Thine enemies Thy footstool' — asking how David could call his own son 'Lord.' The Pharisees are silenced, unable to answer. Pastor Chuck explores this pivotal question that stands at the center of Christian faith: Who is Jesus? The answer determines everything — He is both David's son (fully human) and David's Lord (fully divine).

## 📜 Historical Background

This exchange takes place in Jerusalem during Passion Week, after Jesus has already silenced the Sadducees and Pharisees with answers to their trick questions about taxes, resurrection, and the greatest commandment. Now Jesus takes the offensive. Psalm 110 is the most quoted Old Testament passage in the New Testament, cited by Peter (Acts 2:34-35), Paul (1 Corinthians 15:25), and the author of Hebrews (Hebrews 1:13). David's designation of the Messiah as 'my Lord' was understood by Jewish interpreters as referring to the Messiah, but they had not grasped the full implication: the Messiah would be both human descendant of David and divine Lord.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** This debate occurred in the Temple courts during Passion Week — the most volatile time of year in Jerusalem. The city was flooded with hundreds of thousands of Passover pilgrims, and Roman authorities had reinforced the garrison at the Antonia Fortress to suppress any messianic uprising. The question of the Messiah's identity was not merely theological — it was politically explosive. Every Jew hoped for the Messiah, but most expected a military liberator in the mold of Judah Maccabee, who would throw off the Roman yoke. Jesus' question cut to the heart of this expectation: the Messiah is not just David's son (a human king) but David's Lord (a divine figure who transcends earthly politics). Psalm 110, attributed to David, was recognized by first-century rabbis as messianic. By quoting it, Jesus forced the Pharisees to confront a truth they could not harmonize with their political hopes — that the Messiah would be both greater than David and yet David's descendant, a mystery that would only be resolved in the incarnation. Matthew's Gospel, opening with Jesus' genealogy tracing Him to David (Matthew 1:1–17), answers the question from its very first verse.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Peter's confession at Caesarea Philippi (Matthew 16:16) — 'Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God' — is the answer Jesus seeks. John's Gospel opens with the declaration that 'the Word was God' (John 1:1). Paul writes that Christ Jesus, 'being in the form of God, thought it not robbery to be equal with God' (Philippians 2:6). Colossians 2:9 affirms that 'in Him dwelleth all the fullness of the Godhead bodily.' Hebrews 1:8 declares to the Son: 'Thy throne, O God, is for ever and ever.' The question 'What think ye of Christ?' remains the most important question any person can answer.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized that a low view of Christ is the root of every theological error. If Jesus is merely a great teacher or a moral example, He cannot save. But if He is David's Lord — God incarnate — then He is worthy of absolute devotion and trust. The question is not merely academic; it is personal. What we think of Christ determines how we live, what we hope in, and where we spend eternity.
