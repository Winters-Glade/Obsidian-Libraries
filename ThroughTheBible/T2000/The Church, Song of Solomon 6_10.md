## 📜 Historical Background

The Song of Solomon is a poetic celebration of romantic [[Love]], traditionally interpreted as presenting Christ's [[Love]] for the [[church|Church]]. Verse 6:10 uses vivid celestial and military imagery — fair as the moon, clear as the sun, awesome as an army with banners.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul describes Christ's love for the church as a husband loves his wife ([[Ephesians 5_25.md]]). John pictures the Church as the Bride of Christ at the marriage supper of the Lamb ([[Revelation 19_7-9.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

The Song of Solomon reveals the depth of [[Love]] God intends for [[Marriage]], and pictures Christ's [[Love]] for His [[church|Church]]. The [[_Concepts/Doctrines/bride]] is 'terrible as an army with banners' — the [[church|Church]] united under Christ's [[Love]] is a powerful force.

## 📚 Hebrew Roots & Wisdom Context

The Song of Solomon (Hebrew *Shir ha-Shirim*, שִׁיר הַשִּׁירִים — "the Song of Songs," a superlative meaning the greatest of songs) is a unique book in the wisdom corpus: a collection of **Hebrew [[Love]] poetry** celebrating the beauty and intimacy of romantic [[Love]]. The description "fair as the moon, clear as the sun, and terrible as an army with banners" uses three similes moving from gentle beauty through radiant glory to awe-inspiring power. The Hebrew *ayom* (אָיֹם, "terrible") means "awesome, inspiring awe" — the same root used of God in [[Psalm 68_35]].

## 📖 Research Context

### Interpreting the Song of Songs

The Song has been interpreted in three major traditions:
1. **Allegorical** — God's love for Israel (Jewish) or Christ's love for the Church (Christian)
2. **Literal** — A celebration of human romantic love and marriage
3. **Typological** — Human marital love as a divinely ordained type pointing to Christ and the Church

Chuck Smith's interpretation blends literal and typological, consistent with how the New Testament uses [[Marriage]] as a metaphor for Christ and the [[church|Church]] (Ephesians 5:25-32).

### Hebrew Poetic Features

The Song (Hebrew: *Shir Hashirim* שִׁיר הַשִּׁירִים — "the most excellent song") is characterized by:
- **Wasf** — An Arabic poetic form describing the beloved's beauty part by part (Song 4:1-7; 5:10-16; 6:4-10)
- **Refrains** — "I charge you, O daughters of Jerusalem..." (2:7; 3:5; 5:8; 8:4)
- **Comparative particles** — The Hebrew *kaf* (כְּ, "like, as") appears extensively

### Song 6:10 in Tradition

In Jewish tradition, Song 6:10 was interpreted of Israel at the giving of the Law (morning), the Exodus (moon), the wilderness miracles (sun), and Israel's military camps (army with banners). In Christian tradition, the Church is the bride:
- **Fair as the moon** — The Church reflects the light of Christ (the sun)
- **Clear as the sun** — The Church is justified by Christ's righteousness
- **Terrible as an army with banners** — The Church militant, advancing against the gates of hell

The Song is one of the **five Megillot** read at Jewish festivals, recited at Passover. Rabbi Akiva declared: "All the ages are not worth the day on which the Song of Songs was given to Israel; for all the Writings are holy, but the Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies."
