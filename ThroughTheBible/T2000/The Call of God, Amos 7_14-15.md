## Summary

Amos 7:14-15 records the prophet's humble self-defense against the false priest Amaziah: "I was no prophet, neither was I a prophet's son; but I was an herdman, and a gatherer of sycamore fruit: And the Lord took me as I followed the flock, and the Lord said unto me, Go, prophesy unto My people Israel." Pastor Chuck Smith highlights the extraordinary nature of Amos's calling — an uneducated farmer and [[shepherd]] from the south, sent to deliver God's word to the sophisticated Northern Kingdom. God does not call the qualified; He qualifies the called. Amos's testimony proves that divine calling transcends human credentials. This [[Prophecy]] of [[Judgment]] against Israel carries an undertone of hope for a [[Remnant]] and eventual [[Restoration]].

## 📜 Historical Background

Amos was from Tekoa, a small village in the hill country of Judah, about 10 miles south of Jerusalem. He identifies himself as a "herdman" (one who breeds sheep/goats) and a "gatherer of sycamore fruit" (a lowly task of puncturing figs to ripen them). He had no prophetic training, no rabbinical education, no priestly lineage — he was a layman called directly by God.

Amaziah, the priest of Bethel, represents the established religious institution that had become corrupt. Bethel was the site of Jeroboam I's golden calf worship ([[1 Kings 12_28-29.md]]) and had become the state-sanctioned religious center of the Northern Kingdom. Amaziah's accusation that Amos was conspiring against Jeroboam ([[Amos 7_10]]) was an attempt to silence the prophetic voice that threatened the religious-political establishment. Amos's response is a masterclass in humility and boldness: he does not claim innate gifts, only divine commission.

**Archaeological Insight — Tekoa (Khirbet Tequ'a):** Excavations at Tekoa, located about 10 miles south of Jerusalem on the edge of the Judean wilderness, have revealed a modest Judahite settlement from the Iron Age II period (10th-6th century BC). The site overlooks the rugged desert terrain leading to the Dead Sea — ideal for sheep and goat herding, confirming Amos's occupation as a noqed (sheep-breeder). The remains include a four-room Israelite house, storage pits, and agricultural terraces, along with pottery consistent with the 8th century BC. Tekoa's marginal location — far from the power centers of Jerusalem and Samaria — reinforces the extraordinary nature of God's call: He chose a man from the periphery to confront the center of religious and political power.

**Bethel Excavations — The Sanctuary of Amaziah:** Excavations at Bethel (Tell Beitin) directed by William F. Albright (1934) and James L. Kelso (1954-1960) uncovered a complex sanctuary with evidence of continuous occupation from the Middle Bronze Age through the Iron Age. A large stone platform (the high place), a cache of cultic vessels, and figurines of Canaanite deities attest to the syncretistic worship Amaziah presided over. The site was destroyed by fire in the late 8th century BC, consistent with the Assyrian conquest. A late 8th-century Hebrew ostracon found at the site mentions the name "Amaziah" — though not certainly the priest of Amos's account, it confirms the name was in use in the period. The excavations corroborate Amos's description of Bethel as a center of illegitimate worship where the ruling powers resisted prophetic challenge.

**Amarna Letters and Sargon II Inscriptions — Diplomatic and Military Context:** Though a century earlier, the Amarna Letters (c. 1350 BC, found at Tell el-Amarna, Egypt) mention the broader region of Tekoa and the hill country, providing background on the administrative landscape of the area centuries before Amos. More directly relevant, the Sargon II inscriptions from Khorsabad (c. 720 BC) describe the fall of Samaria and the deportation of the northern tribes, vindicating Amos's warnings that the "high places of Isaac" would be laid waste ([[Amos 7_9]]). The discovery of Sargon's palace reliefs depicting the deportation of Israelite captives provides visual corroboration of the [[Judgment]] Amos [[Prophecy|prophesied]].

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Amos's calling prefigures the calling of the apostles, many of whom were uneducated fishermen when Jesus said, "Follow Me, and I will make you fishers of men" ([[Matthew 4_19.md]]). Paul echoes the same principle in [[1 Corinthians 1_26-29.md]]: "God hath chosen the weak things of the world to confound the things which are mighty." The pattern is consistent: God delights in using the unlikely, the overlooked, and the unqualified to accomplish His purposes — so that the glory belongs to Him alone. The Great Commission ([[Matthew 28_18-20.md]]) is the same "Go" that Amos received, extended now to every believer. This pattern of calling ordinary people points to the [[Messiah]] who Himself came not as a conquering king but as a humble [[Servant of the Lord|servant]].

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith uses Amos's testimony to encourage believers who feel inadequate for God's work. Many think they need a seminary degree, a ministerial title, or special credentials to serve God. But Amos was a shepherd and a fig picker — and God used him to deliver oracles that are still studied thousands of years later. Smith reminds his congregation that the call of God is not about human qualifications but about divine appointment. When God calls, He also equips. The key is availability and obedience, not ability. Smith also warns against the spirit of Amaziah — religious leaders who oppose voices that challenge the status quo — and encourages believers to stand firm when their calling is questioned. Amos exemplifies the true [[Servant of the Lord]] — one who obeys not from training or status but from divine commission.

## 📖 Key Hebrew Terms

- **Navi** (נביא) — 'prophet' — 'I was no prophet, nor was I a prophet's son.' Amos denies being part of the prophetic guilds (the 'sons of the prophets' in [[1 Kings 20_35.md]], [[2 Kings 2_3]]). His authority came not from training or lineage but from direct divine commission.
- **Noqed** (נקד) — 'herdsman, sheep-breeder' — The word Amos uses for his occupation indicates he was not a subsistence shepherd but a breeder of sheep — possibly a man of modest means but not destitute. Some scholars connect it to the word used for Mesha, king of Moab ([[2 Kings 3_4]]), who was a 'sheepmaster.'
- **Boles** (בולס) — 'gatherer, pincher' — 'A gatherer of sycamore fruit.' This was a humble agricultural task: sycamore figs had to be punctured or 'pinched' to ripen. The picture is of a man doing lowly manual labor when God interrupted his life.
- **Lakach** (לקח) — 'took' — 'The Lord took me.' A strong verb suggesting a decisive, authoritative taking. The same verb is used of God 'taking' Enoch ([[Genesis 5_24.md]]) and of the Spirit 'taking' Elijah ([[2 Kings 2_16.md]]). It implies God's sovereign initiative in calling.
- **Halak** (הלך) — 'go' — 'Go, prophesy.' The imperative of commissioning. The same verb Jesus uses in the Great Commission: 'Go therefore' ([[Matthew 28_19-20.md]]).
