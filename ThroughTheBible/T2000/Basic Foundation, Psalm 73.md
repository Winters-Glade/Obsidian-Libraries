## Summary

Psalm 73 wrestles with the age-old problem of the prosperity of the wicked. Asaph nearly lost his footing until he 'went into the sanctuary of God' and understood their end. The psalm concludes with: 'Whom have I in heaven but Thee?'

## Key Themes

- **God's goodness**
- **Sanctuary perspective**
- **Envy of the wicked**
- **Eternal perspective**

## 📜 Historical Background

Psalm 73 opens Book III of the Psalms and is attributed to Asaph. It addresses the most difficult challenges to [[Faith]]: the prosperity of the wicked. The turning point comes when he enters the sanctuary — the place of [[Worship]] and divine perspective. There he understands the ultimate end of the wicked.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus warns against laying up treasures on earth ([[Matthew 6_19-21.md]]). Paul writes that 'the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed' ([[Romans 8_18]]). The sanctuary perspective transforms how we view present circumstances.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often taught that when we compare our lives to the wicked who prosper, we lose our footing. But when we enter God's presence, we regain perspective: 'Whom have I in heaven but Thee?'

## 📚 Hebrew Poetry & Wisdom Context

Psalm 73 is a **wisdom psalm** tracing a spiritual journey from doubt to [[Faith]]. Asaph's feet had nearly stumbled — the Hebrew for "footing" (*ashurey*, אֲשֻׁרַי) literally means "steps." The turning point is the **sanctuary** (*miqdash*, מִקְדָּשׁ) — the place of God's presence where divine perspective is regained. The wicked are set "in slippery places" (*chalaqlaqqot*, חֲלַקְלַקּוֹת — a vivid Hebrew reduplicative suggesting polished stones). This psalm bridges the problem of evil (Job) and the conclusion of [[Faith]] (Proverbs).

## 📖 Research Context

### The Sanctuary Perspective — Answering Theodicy

Psalm 73 is one of the most profound treatments of **theodicy** in the Bible. This Asaph psalm begins with a confession the psalmist nearly abandoned when confronted with the prosperity of the wicked (vv. 2-3).

The turning point comes in verse 17: "Until I went into the sanctuary of God; then understood I their end." The Hebrew *miqdash* (מִקְדָּשׁ) — "sanctuary" — refers to the holy place of God's presence. In the sanctuary, the psalmist gains an **eschatological perspective**. He sees not the present prosperity of the wicked but their ultimate end. This is the breakthrough: theodicy cannot be resolved on the horizontal plane of everyday observation; it requires the vertical perspective of God's presence.

### Key Hebrew Words

- **"Envy"** (*qinne* קִנֵּא, v. 3) — The burning anger that arises when others have what we lack
- **"Slippery places"** (*b'halakot* בַּחֲלָקוֹת, v. 18) — The wicked are set on unstable ground

### A Wisdom Psalm

This psalm bridges the wisdom tradition (wrestling with injustice) and the Psalter's theology of [[Worship]]. The sanctuary is not just a building — it is where God's reality reshapes human perception. This anticipates "we walk by [[Faith]], not by sight" ([[2 Corinthians 5_7.md]]). The psalm culminates: "Whom have I in heaven but Thee? and there is none upon earth that I desire beside Thee" (v. 25).
