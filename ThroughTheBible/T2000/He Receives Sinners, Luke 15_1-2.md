## Summary

Luke 15:1-2 sets the stage for three of the most beloved parables — the lost sheep, the lost coin, and the prodigal son. The Pharisees and scribes complained, 'This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them.' Pastor Chuck examines the scandal of grace: that Jesus not only tolerated sinners but welcomed them, ate with them, and sought them out. The religious leaders' complaint reveals more about their hearts than about Jesus' ministry.

## 📖 Research Context

Luke 15 is often called 'the Gospel of the Outcast' — a chapter unique in its concentration of mercy. The three parables (lost sheep, lost coin, prodigal son) form an ascending triad: a shepherd seeks one sheep out of a hundred, a woman seeks one coin out of ten, a father awaits one son out of two. The crescendo is unmistakable — the third parable has no 'search' but the father runs. The Greek word for 'receives' (prosdechetai) in the Pharisees' complaint means 'welcomes' or 'accepts joyfully.' The Pharisees intended it as an accusation; Luke presents it as the heart of the Gospel. The phrase 'eateth with them' indicates table fellowship — the most intimate form of social acceptance in the ancient Near East. To eat with someone was to offer peace, trust, and brotherhood. Jesus' willing table fellowship with tax collectors and sinners was a living parable of grace.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Psalm 51:10-17 (a broken and contrite heart), Micah 7:18-19 (who pardons iniquity and delights in mercy), Hosea 11:8-9 (My heart churns within Me — God's compassionate love for wayward Israel).

## 📜 Historical Background

In first-century Jewish society, tax collectors were despised as collaborators with Rome and often engaged in extortion. 'Sinners' were those who did not observe the oral traditions and purity laws. Eating with someone in that culture signified acceptance and fellowship. The Pharisees' complaint that Jesus 'receives sinners and eats with them' was intended as an accusation, but it perfectly summarizes the Gospel. The three parables that follow each end in celebration — heaven rejoices when one sinner repents.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Romans 5:8 — while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us. 1 Timothy 1:15 — Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners, of whom I am chief. Matthew 9:10-13 — I desire mercy and not sacrifice, for I came not to call the righteous but sinners. 2 Corinthians 5:18-19 — God was in Christ reconciling the world to Himself, not imputing their trespasses. Ephesians 2:4-5 — God who is rich in mercy made us alive together with Christ.

2 Corinthians 5:17-21 — if anyone is in Christ he is a new creation; God made Him who knew no sin to be sin for us that we might become the righteousness of God. 1 John 4:7-10 — in this is love, not that we loved God but that He loved us and sent His Son to be the propitiation for our sins.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith noted that the religious leaders' question 'Why does He receive sinners?' is still asked today. Many churches still struggle with this — they want sinners to come but are uncomfortable when they do. The heart of the Gospel is that Jesus receives sinners, not after they clean up their lives, but as they are. The elder brother in the prodigal son parable represents the Pharisees — righteous in their own eyes but lacking the father's heart of compassion.
