# 197 Turning to the Living God — Acts 14:15

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#197](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v2FF9VLNnHY)

## Overview

After healing a lame man in Lystra, Paul and Barnabas are mistaken for the pagan gods Hermes and Zeus. The priest of Zeus prepares to sacrifice to them. Paul's anguished response redirects the crowd: 'Why are you doing these things? We also are men of the same nature as you, and preach to you that you should turn from these useless things to the living God, who made the heaven, the earth, the sea, and all things that are in them.'

## 📜 Historical Background

Lystra, located in the Lycaonian region of Asia Minor (modern-day Turkey), was a Roman colony with a distinct cultural identity. The local population spoke Lycaonian (Acts 14:11), a native language that survived despite Hellenistic and Roman influence. A local legend — recorded by Ovid in his Metamorphoses — told of Zeus and Hermes visiting this region disguised as mortals. The myth stated that only an elderly couple (Philemon and Baucis) received them, while the rest of the population was destroyed. This explains why the crowd's reaction was so intense: they recognized the miracle as a reenactment of their foundational myth. The priest of Zeus, whose temple stood at the city gate, immediately prepared sacrifices. Paul's anguished response — tearing his clothes, a traditional Jewish expression of horror at blasphemy — shows the apostle's visceral rejection of any honor that belongs to God alone.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul's message to the Lystrans resonates throughout his epistles. His opening line — 'turn from these useless things (mataion — vanities) to the living God' — appears in 1 Thessalonians 1:9-10 as the description of the Thessalonians' conversion. Romans 1:18-25 provides the theological foundation: humanity exchanged the truth of God for a lie and worshipped created things rather than the Creator. The Areopagus address in Acts 17:22-31 develops this same theology of natural revelation for a philosophically sophisticated audience. Paul's argument from creation — 'the living God who made heaven, earth, sea, and all things' — echoes Psalm 115:1-8 and the Old Testament polemic against idols.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith used this passage to expose the timeless nature of idolatry. While modern people may not bow to statues of Zeus, the human heart still manufactures 'useless things' — money, status, relationships, pleasure — that we look to for meaning and security. Smith emphasized that the gospel is not just about adding religious practices but about turning — a decisive, directional change from false sources of life to the living God Himself. The crowd's fickleness — one moment worshiping, the next stoning Paul — illustrates the danger of building faith on spectacular experiences rather than truth.
