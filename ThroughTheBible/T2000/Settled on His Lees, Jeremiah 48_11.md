## Summary

[[Jeremiah 48_11.md]] describes Moab as having 'been at ease from his youth, and he hath settled on his lees, and hath not been emptied from vessel to vessel.' Pastor Chuck unpacks this vivid winemaking metaphor — a warning against spiritual complacency and the danger of remaining undisturbed while the sediment of [[Sin]] settles in our lives.

## 📜 Historical Background

In ancient winemaking, wine was left to settle so the sediment ('lees') would sink to the bottom. If the wine was left undisturbed for too long, it would become thick and syrupy, absorbing the bitter taste of the lees. The proper process was to pour the wine from one vessel to another, leaving the sediment behind. Moab is described as wine that has never been 'emptied from vessel to vessel' — they had enjoyed prolonged peace and prosperity, never experiencing the unsettling discipline that would purify them. Their complacency and pride had become entrenched, and [[Judgment]] was coming.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

[[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 12_5-11.md]] teaches that God disciplines those He loves — if we are without [[_Concepts/Doctrines/discipline]], we are not true children. The 'emptying from vessel to vessel' is a picture of God's refining discipline, removing the dross from our lives. [[Revelation 3_15-17.md]] warns the Laodicean church about the danger of lukewarm complacency: 'Because thou sayest, I am rich, and increased with goods, and have need of nothing; and knowest not that thou art wretched, and miserable, and poor, and blind, and naked.' [[James 1_2-4]] teaches that the testing of faith produces patience and maturity.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often warned against the spiritual danger of becoming too comfortable. When life is easy and undisturbed, we can become 'settled on our lees' — complacent, self-satisfied, and unaware of the impurities accumulating in our lives. God's unsettling interventions — trials, difficulties, transitions — are often His loving work to purify us. The process of being 'emptied from vessel to vessel' may be uncomfortable, but it prevents the sediment of sin from becoming permanent. A life that has never been disturbed is a life that has never been refined.
