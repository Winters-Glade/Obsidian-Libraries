## Summary

John states the purpose of Christ's appearing with stark clarity: 'And ye know that He was manifested to take away our sins; and in Him is no sin.' Christ's incarnation had a specific mission—to deal with the problem of sin. His sinlessness qualified Him to be the perfect sacrifice. Those who abide in Him do not continue in a lifestyle of sin, not because they are perfect, but because His seed (the divine nature) remains in them.

## 📜 Historical Background

First John 3 addresses the practical tension between the Christian's new identity in Christ and the ongoing reality of sin in the believer's life. The Johannine community, situated in Ephesus near the end of the first century, wrestled with false teachers who claimed fellowship with God while living in moral darkness (1 John 1:6). John draws a sharp distinction between occasional sin (which the believer confesses and is cleansed from, 1 John 1:9) and a lifestyle of habitual sin (which demonstrates one does not truly know God). The key Greek verb for 'take away' (airō, αἴρω) means to lift up and carry away — evoking the scapegoat on the Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur) that bore Israel's sins into the wilderness (Leviticus 16:20-22). The related term hilasmos (ἱλασμός, propitiation), used in 1 John 2:2 and 4:10, signifies Christ's sacrifice as the satisfying appeasement of God's righteous wrath against sin. 'In Him is no sin' (hamartia, ἁμαρτία) affirms Christ's absolute sinlessness — an essential qualification for the sin-bearer. The concept of 'His seed' (sperma, σπέρμα) remaining in the believer (1 John 3:9) points to the divine nature imparted at regeneration (2 Peter 1:4), producing a transformed life not through human effort but through the indwelling life of Christ. The epistle's pastoral context combats both antinomianism (the claim that grace permits license) and legalism (the notion that sinless perfection is attainable in this life), steering believers toward the biblical path of honest confession and progressive sanctification.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

John the Baptist declared Jesus as 'the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world' (John 1:29), directly paralleling John's airō (take away) language. Paul writes that God 'made Him to be sin for us, who knew no sin' (2 Corinthians 5:21), echoing the substitutionary logic of 1 John 3:5. The writer of Hebrews describes Christ as 'holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from sinners' (Hebrews 7:26) and emphasizes His once-for-all sacrifice (Hebrews 9:26, 10:12-14). Peter connects the same truth to the believer's practical response: 'Who His own self bare our sins in His own body on the tree, that we, being dead to sins, should live unto righteousness' (1 Peter 2:24). The apostle Paul grounds the purpose of the incarnation in redemption: 'Who gave Himself for our sins, that He might deliver us from this present evil world' (Galatians 1:4), and John in Revelation declares Christ 'loosed us from our sins by His blood' (Revelation 1:5). The abiding motif ('he that abideth in Him sinneth not,' 1 John 3:6) connects to Jesus' vine-and-branches teaching (John 15:4-5), where abiding produces fruit organically rather than through forced effort. The purpose of Christ's coming was not merely to teach or to example but to redeem — to actually take away sin and transform sinners from the inside out.

## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** The purpose of Christ's coming — "to take away [our] sins" — is the central assertion of 1 John's soteriology. The Greek *airō* ("take away," v. 5) means to lift up and carry away, evoking the scapegoat on the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16:20-22) that bore Israel's sins into the wilderness. The statement "in Him is no sin" is absolute — Christ's sinlessness is essential to His efficacy as the sin-bearer. The concept of "His seed" (*sperma autou*, "His seed," v. 9) remaining in the believer refers to the divine nature imparted at regeneration (2 Peter 1:4), which produces a transformed life organically. The present tense "does not sin" (*ouch hamartanei*, v. 6, 9) describes habitual, continuous practice — not the absence of all sin but the direction of one's life. This distinction between occasional sin (which the believer confesses, 1 John 1:9) and habitual sin (which demonstrates one does not know God) is crucial for understanding perseverance.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that Jesus didn't come merely to show us how to live or to be a moral example. He came to solve the sin problem that we could not solve ourselves. We don't take away our sins through self-effort; He takes them away. At the same time, knowing Him changes how we live. The divine nature (the 'seed') within the believer produces a life that increasingly reflects His holiness. We sin less not because we try harder but because His life is being formed in us.
