# 235 Real Living — Philippians 1:21

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#235](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qk-1KUceLT8)

## Overview

"For to me to live is Christ, and to die is gain." This single verse captures the essence of Paul's life and worldview. Christ is the center, the purpose, and the substance of all living. Death is not a loss but gain — entrance into the presence of Christ. Paul expresses the tension between his desire to depart and be with Christ (which is far better) and his continued labor for the sake of the believers.

This verse is perhaps the most concise autobiographical statement in all of Paul's letters. Written from prison (likely Rome, c. AD 60-62), facing possible execution, Paul weighs his options with remarkable clarity. "To live is Christ" means Christ is the source, content, and goal of every aspect of life. "To die is gain" means death is not an enemy to be feared but a door to fuller communion with Christ. Paul describes the tension between the two: his personal desire is to depart and be with Christ, but his pastoral responsibility compels him to remain for the Philippians' progress and joy in the faith. This "Christ-centered hedonism" — finding ultimate joy in Christ alone — is the key to Paul's joy in the midst of suffering, which permeates the entire letter.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Christian Life | The believer's life is defined not by circumstances, achievements, or relationships but by Christ Himself — He is the sum and substance of all living |
| Joy | Paul's joy is not dependent on favorable circumstances but flows from Christ's presence, the gospel's progress, and the Philippians' partnership in the faith |
| Death and Dying | Death is not annihilation but transition — departing to be with Christ is "far better" than the best this life offers |
| Christ-Centered Living | Christ is the center of gravity for every aspect of life — thoughts, motives, relationships, sufferings, and hopes |
| Eternal Perspective | Paul's willingness to suffer and even die for Christ flows from his certainty of [[Resurrection]] and eternal gain |

## Background & Context

Philippians is Paul's most joyful letter, written from imprisonment. The [[church]] at Philippi was the first European [[church]], founded by Paul during his second missionary journey (Acts 16). Despite beatings, imprisonment, and an earthquake, Paul's relationship with the Philippians was marked by exceptional warmth and affection. The letter contains the great Christ-hymn (Philippians 2:5-11), describing Christ's self-emptying (kenosis) and exaltation. Paul's "to live is Christ, to die is gain" echoes themes from his larger theology — union with Christ (Galatians 2:20), the heavenly citizenship (Philippians 3:20), and the confident hope of [[Resurrection]] (1 Corinthians 15). The phrase "to die is gain" (kerdos) uses a business term — death is not a loss of life but a profitable exchange for infinitely better.
