# 196 Man After God's Heart — Acts 13:22

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#196](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_M-nz--4Nzg)

## Overview

In Paul's sermon at Pisidian Antioch, he recounts Israel's history, noting that God removed Saul and raised up David, testifying: 'I have found David the son of Jesse, a man after My own heart, who will do all My will.' This verse captures what distinguishes David — not perfection, but a heart oriented toward God. Paul uses David to transition to the promise of Jesus the Messiah.

## 📜 Historical Background

Paul's sermon in Pisidian Antioch (a Roman colony in Galatia, modern-day southern Turkey) follows the synagogue service pattern: readings from the Law and the Prophets followed by an invitation to speak. Pisidian Antioch was a significant Roman colony settled by veterans of Augustus' legions, with a large Jewish community. Paul's sermon uses Israel's history from the Exodus through the judges and kings to David — the same 'salvation history' pattern Stephen used in Acts 7. David's designation as 'a man after My own heart' (quoting 1 Samuel 13:14) distinguished him from Saul, who had disobeyed God's command. In Jewish tradition, David was Israel's greatest king and the standard against which all subsequent kings were measured. Paul masterfully transitions from David to Jesus — the promised offspring of David whose coming was the fulfillment of Israel's messianic hope (Acts 13:23).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The Davidic covenant (2 Samuel 7:12-16) — God's promise to establish David's throne forever — is the theological foundation Paul uses to introduce Jesus as the promised Messiah. Luke 1:32-33 announces that Jesus will receive 'the throne of His father David.' The phrase 'man after God's own heart' describes not perfection but orientation — David's defining characteristic was that he pursued God even through failure. Romans 4:1-8 uses David's experience of forgiveness (Psalm 32) to illustrate justification by faith. Paul's preaching pattern here — from Scripture to Jesus — becomes the model for apostolic evangelism throughout Acts.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often used David as an example of authentic Christianity — a man who made terrible mistakes but always returned to God with a repentant heart. Smith taught that being 'after God's own heart' is not about flawless performance but about having a heart that seeks God, confesses sin quickly, and remains oriented toward His will. David's psalms reveal a man who poured out his honest emotions before God — joy, lament, anger, praise — without pretense. Smith encouraged believers that God is not looking for perfect people but for people with genuine hearts who will pursue Him above all else. Failure is not final when the heart is right with God.
