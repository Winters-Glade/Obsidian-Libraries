## Summary

Deuteronomy 8:11-20 warns against the peril of prosperity — forgetting the Lord who brought Israel out of Egypt, led them through the wilderness, and gave them the good land. Pastor Chuck explores the spiritual dangers of abundance: pride, self-reliance, and the terrible tragedy of saying in one's heart, 'My power and the might of mine hand hath gotten me this wealth.'

## 📜 Historical Background

Deuteronomy 8 belongs to Moses' second farewell address delivered on the plains of Moab, as the new generation stood poised to enter Canaan. The chapter is structured as a classic ANE (Ancient Near Eastern) suzerain-vassal treaty exhortation: the Great King (Yahweh) rehearses His acts of covenant loyalty — deliverance from Egypt, guidance through the wilderness, provision of manna and water — before issuing stipulations and warnings. This mirrors the pattern found in Hittite and Assyrian treaty texts from the 2nd millennium BC, discovered at Boghazköi (modern Turkey) and Mari (Syria), where the suzerain first recounts his benevolence before demanding exclusive loyalty from his vassal.

The Hebrew verb for "forget" here is שָׁכַח (*shakach*, Strong's H7911) — a covenantal term implying not merely a lapse of memory but active abandonment of relationship. To "forget the LORD" (v. 11) is to repudiate His lordship. The phrase "power and the might of mine hand" (v. 17) uses the Hebrew כֹחַ (*koach*, strength) and עֹצֶם (*otsem*, bone-strength) — language of raw human ability that stands in contrast to divine provision. The chapter's centerpiece is the manna narrative (v. 3, 16), where Israel learned that "man does not live by bread alone but by every word that proceeds from the mouth of the LORD" — a lesson uniquely tied to the wilderness testing.

Archaeologically, the "land flowing with milk and honey" (v. 8) reflects the actual agricultural abundance of Canaan during the Late Bronze Age: wheat, barley, vines, fig trees, pomegranates, olive oil, and honey (likely date honey, *d'vash*). The description of houses they did not build, wells they did not dig, and vineyards they did not plant (v. 12-13) matches the conquest narratives and the transition from nomadic pastoralism to settled agriculture. The warning proved prophetic — the archaeological record of the Iron Age shows Israel's periodic apostasy correlating with times of economic prosperity (e.g., the Omride dynasty), just as the eighth-century prophets later charged.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus echoes this warning in the parable of the rich fool (Luke 12:16-21), who said to his soul, 'Soul, thou hast much goods laid up for many years; take thine ease, eat, drink, and be merry.' But God said, 'Thou fool, this night thy soul shall be required of thee.' Paul warns that 'the love of money is the root of all evil' (1 Timothy 6:10) and instructs the rich 'not to be high-minded, nor trust in uncertain riches, but in the living God' (1 Timothy 6:17). James condemns the rich who trust in their wealth (James 5:1-6). The writer of Hebrews exhorts believers to be 'content with such things as ye have' because God Himself promises, 'I will never leave thee, nor forsake thee' (Hebrews 13:5). Revelation 3:17 rebukes the Laodicean church — 'thou sayest, I am rich, and increased with goods, and have need of nothing' — the very self-deception Moses warned against. Jesus Himself, though rich, became poor for our sake (2 Corinthians 8:9), modeling the humility that prosperity so easily erodes. The warning of Deuteronomy 8 is timeless.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often noted that the wilderness is easier to trust God in than the land of plenty. When we are in need, we pray; when we are full, we forget. The danger of prosperity is not abundance itself but the subtle shift from dependence on God to self-reliance. Moses' remedy is clear: 'Remember the Lord your God.' Gratitude is the antidote to pride. The manna in the wilderness taught daily dependence — they could not store it up; they had to trust God every morning. In seasons of plenty, we must cultivate that same daily dependence through prayer, Scripture, and intentional remembrance of God's past faithfulness. The temptation to say 'my power has gotten me this wealth' is as ancient as Eden and as contemporary as the American Dream. The gospel counters this by declaring that everything we have is a gift — our next breath, our abilities, our opportunities. The truly wealthy person, as Chuck often said, is not the one who has the most but the one who recognizes that all he has comes from God.
