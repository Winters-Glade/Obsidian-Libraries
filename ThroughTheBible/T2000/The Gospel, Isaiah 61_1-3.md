## Summary

'The Spirit of the Lord God is upon me; because the Lord hath anointed me to preach good tidings unto the meek... to proclaim liberty to the captives, and the opening of the prison to them that are bound.' Jesus read this passage in the Nazareth synagogue and declared it fulfilled in their hearing. Pastor Chuck explores the gospel according to Isaiah. This is the pinnacle of [[Messiah|Messianic]] [[Prophecy]], announcing [[Restoration]] and the [[Servant of the Lord|Servant's]] anointed mission.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[_Bible/Isaiah/Isaiah 61.md]] is the pinnacle of [[Messiah|Messianic]] [[Prophecy]] in the Old Testament. The speaker is the [[Servant of the Lord]], anointed by the Spirit to bring good news. The 'acceptable year of the Lord' refers to the Year of Jubilee ([[Leviticus 25.md]]) — every 50th year when debts were forgiven, slaves freed, and land restored. Isaiah uses this economic institution as a metaphor for spiritual redemption. Jesus' reading of this passage in the Nazareth synagogue ([[Luke 4_16-21.md]]) was a direct claim to be the promised [[Messiah]] — He stopped mid-verse at 'the acceptable year of the Lord' because the [[Judgment]] portion ('the day of vengeance of our God') would be fulfilled at His second coming. The themes of [[Restoration]] and redemption here echo the [[Prophecy|prophetic]] ministries of [[Jeremiah]], [[Ezekiel]], and [[Daniel]].

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus explicitly fulfills [[Isaiah 61_1-2.md]] in [[Luke 4_16-21.md]], declaring 'This day is this scripture fulfilled in your ears.' The entire ministry of Christ — healing the sick, freeing the demon-possessed, forgiving sins — embodies this passage. The Gospel is summarized here: good news to the poor, healing to the brokenhearted, liberty to captives, and comfort to those who mourn. This passage is the Old Testament gospel in miniature.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that Jesus intentionally stopped at 'the acceptable year of the Lord' to show that His first coming was about grace, not [[Judgment]]. Judgment will come later (the 'day of vengeance'). The gospel is good news precisely because it addresses humanity's deepest needs: bondage, brokenness, mourning, and despair. Jesus came to 'bind up the brokenhearted' — not just to inform but to heal. The gospel is not a philosophy; it is a divine intervention.
