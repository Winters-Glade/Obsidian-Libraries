## Summary

Matthew 27:22 records Pilate's pivotal question to the crowd: 'What shall I do then with Jesus which is called Christ?' It is the most important question ever asked. Standing before Pilate is the King of the Jews, the Son of God, beaten and bound, while the crowd demands His crucifixion. Pastor Chuck explores the tragedy of Pilate's moment of decision — a man who knew Jesus was innocent, who had been warned by his wife's dream, yet who chose political expediency over moral courage. The question 'What shall I do with Jesus?' is one that every person must answer, and the response determines eternal destiny.

## 📜 Historical Background

Pontius Pilate was the Roman prefect (governor) of Judea from AD 26-36. He was known for his harsh rule and insensitivity to Jewish customs — a background that explains his reluctance to spark another conflict during the volatile Passover festival. The custom of releasing a prisoner at Passover (Matthew 27:15) was a Roman concession to Jewish tradition. Barabbas was a notorious prisoner, described by Mark as a rebel and murderer (Mark 15:7). The choice between Jesus and Barabbas is deeply symbolic: the innocent Son of God exchanged for a guilty insurrectionist. Pilate's wife's dream (Matthew 27:19) adds a striking detail — even a pagan Gentile recognized Jesus' innocence.

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** Pontius Pilate is known from both biblical and extra-biblical sources. The Jewish historian Josephus (Antiquities 18.3–4) and Philo of Alexandria (Embassy to Gaius 38) describe him as inflexible, cruel, and prone to provoking Jewish sensibilities — on one occasion he brought imperial standards with Caesar's image into Jerusalem, sparking massive protests. The Pilate Stone, discovered in Caesarea Maritima in 1961, confirms his title as Prefect of Judea. His governorship ended in AD 36 after he massacred Samaritans on Mount Gerizim, leading to his recall to Rome. Passover in Jerusalem was the most politically charged time of year — the city's population swelled from about 40,000 to several hundred thousand, and Roman reinforcements from Caesarea were stationed at the Antonia Fortress to quell any uprising. Barabbas was likely a Zealot rebel — one of the _sicarii_ (dagger-men) who opposed Roman rule through assassination and insurrection. The crowd's choice of a rebel over the Prince of Peace reflects the explosive political atmosphere: many hoped for a Messiah who would fight, not one who would die. Matthew's Gospel, uniquely, includes Pilate's wife's dream — a detail that echoes the dream-theophany pattern in Matthew's infancy narrative (Matthew 1:20, 2:12, 2:13, 2:19) and emphasizes that even Gentiles recognized Jesus' innocence.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

John 18:33-38 records Pilate's famous question, 'What is truth?' — asked of the One who is the Truth (John 14:6). Peter's sermon in Acts 3:13-15 lays the blame for Jesus' death squarely on the crowd but immediately offers repentance. Paul writes in Acts 17:30-31 that God 'commandeth all men every where to repent' because He 'hath appointed a day, in the which He will judge the world in righteousness by that Man whom He hath ordained.' 2 Corinthians 5:10 reminds us that we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ. The question Pilate faced is the question we all face: what will we do with Jesus?

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often stressed that there is no neutral ground regarding Jesus Christ. Pilate tried to wash his hands of the matter, but he could not escape the decision. Every person must answer the question: what will I do with Jesus? To do nothing is to reject Him. To try to avoid the question is to answer it negatively. The only right response is to receive Him as Lord and Savior — to cry out with Thomas, 'My Lord and my God!' (John 20:28).
