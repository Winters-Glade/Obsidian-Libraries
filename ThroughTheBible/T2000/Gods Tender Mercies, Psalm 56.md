## Summary

Psalm 56 is a cry for [[mercy]] in the midst of fear. Written when the Philistines seized David in Gath, it records his declaration of [[trust]]: 'When I cry unto Thee, then shall mine enemies turn back: this I know; for God is for me.'

## 📜 Historical Background

The inscription places this psalm at one of the most fearful moments of David's life. While fleeing from Saul, David sought refuge in Gath. When recognized, he feigned madness to escape ([[1 Samuel 21_10-15.md]]). Despite the danger, David wrote this psalm declaring 'What time I am afraid, I will [[trust]] in Thee.'

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul's declaration in [[Romans 8_31]] — 'If God be for us, who can be against us?' — echoes David's confidence. The tender mercies of God are described by Paul as 'the Father of mercies, and the God of all comfort' ([[2 Corinthians 1_3.md]]).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often reminded believers that fear is not a sin — what we do with our fear determines its effect. David didn't deny his fear; he directed it toward God. The tender mercies of God are available to us in our moments of greatest weakness.

## 📚 Hebrew Poetry & Worship Context

Psalm 56 is a **Miktam** (מִכְתָּם) — a musical term possibly meaning "engraving" or "golden psalm." The historical superscription places this psalm at the desperate moment when David feigned madness before Achish in Gath (1 Samuel 21:10-15). The Hebrew word *batach* (בָּטַח, "trust") is the psalm's anchor. The repeated refrain "What time I am afraid, I will trust in Thee" uses **inclusio** (bookend repetition). The phrase "put my tears into Thy bottle" (verse 8) reflects an Egyptian custom of collecting mourners' tears in small bottles.

**Worship Significance:** This psalm gives language to the terrified worshipper, transforming fear into trust through prayer and song.


## 📖 Research Context

### Miktam Psalm — David's Flight from Gath

Psalm 56 is a **Miktam** (מִכְתָּם) — a term appearing in superscriptions of Psalms 16 and 56-60, possibly related to atonement or inscription. The superscription places this psalm in the context of David's flight from Saul, when he was seized by the Philistines in Gath (1 Samuel 21:10-15). David feigned madness to save his life — scrabbling on the gate and letting spittle run down his beard. This desperate self-humiliation is the backdrop for: "What time I am afraid, I will trust in Thee" (v. 3).

### Hebrew Chesed (Tender Mercy)

"God's tender mercies" captures the Hebrew **chesed** (חֶסֶד) — one of the richest Old Testament terms: covenant loyalty, steadfast love, lovingkindness, mercy, faithfulness. It is the quality that binds God to His people — a love that is not merely emotional but covenantal and enduring.

### Tears in a Bottle

David uses a powerful image in verse 8: God collects his tears in a bottle (Hebrew *nod* נֹאד — a leather wineskin). This is one of the most tender images of God's compassion in all Scripture — preserving each tear as precious, forgetting not a single one.
