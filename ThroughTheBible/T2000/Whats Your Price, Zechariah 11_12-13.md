## Summary

Zechariah 11:12-13 contains one of the most remarkable prophecies in the Old Testament: the Good Shepherd is valued at thirty pieces of silver — the price of a slave gored by an ox (Exodus 21:32). In a dramatic acted parable, Zechariah plays the role of the rejected shepherd and asks for his wages; the people weigh out thirty pieces of silver. The Lord tells him to cast it to the potter — a vivid foreshadowing of Judas Iscariot's betrayal of Jesus for the same amount and the potter's field purchased with the blood money (Matthew 27:3-10). Pastor Chuck Smith uses this passage to ask a penetrating question: What value do you place on the Good Shepherd?

## 📜 Historical Background

Zechariah 11 is one of the most difficult and profound chapters in the Minor Prophets. It shifts from a tone of restoration (Zechariah 10) to a stark acted parable of rejection. Zechariah is told to become a shepherd to a flock that is doomed to slaughter, representing God's care for His people despite their stubbornness. He takes two staffs — 'Favor' and 'Union' — and breaks them one by one, symbolizing the breaking of the covenant and the division of the nation. When he asks for his wages, the people pay him thirty pieces of silver — a paltry sum that represents the contempt they have for his (and God's) shepherding work. The Lord's response is dripping with irony: 'Cast it unto the potter' — throw this insulting wage to the pottery maker, a common figure in the Valley of Hinnom. Matthew's Gospel explicitly connects this passage to Judas's betrayal and the purchase of the potter's field (Matthew 27:9-10), confirming Zechariah's prophecy as a direct prediction of the Messiah's rejection.

**Thirty Pieces of Silver: The Slave Price (Exodus 21:32).** The valuation of thirty pieces of silver in Zechariah 11:12-13 directly invokes the legal code of Exodus 21:32, where thirty shekels of silver was the compensation for a slave gored by an ox. This was the price of a human life at its lowest valuation — the market price of a slave. In the Persian period, silver coinage began to replace the earlier weight-based system. The Yehud coins, minted in Jerusalem during the Persian period and bearing the inscription "Yehud" in Paleo-Hebrew script, provide archaeological evidence for the monetary economy Zechariah references. These small silver coins (drachms and obols) were the actual currency in which such transactions would have been conducted. The contempt of valuing the Good Shepherd at the slave price is heightened by the archaeological insight that thirty shekels was a deliberately insulting minimum — the people paid God's representative the absolute legal floor.

**Akeldama — The Field of Blood.** The potter's field purchased with the thirty pieces of silver, known in Aramaic as Akeldama (חקל דמא), is traditionally located on the south side of the Valley of Hinnom (Gehenna) in Jerusalem. This valley, associated with child sacrifice to Molech in the Old Testament (Jeremiah 7:31), became a garbage dump and burial ground for strangers in the Second Temple period. Archaeological surveys of the southern Hinnom Valley have revealed numerous burial caves from the Second Temple period, consistent with its use as a burial ground for the poor and foreigners. The irony is multilayered: the money valued the Messiah at a slave's price, was thrown into the temple, deemed unfit for the treasury, and used to buy a cemetery in the very valley associated with judgment — turning the place of curse into a symbol of redemption, as strangers and outcasts would be buried there purchased by the blood money of the Lamb.

**Matthew's Citation and the Potter's Field.** Matthew 27:9-10 attributes the fulfillment to Jeremiah, but the primary reference is clearly Zechariah 11:12-13, with additional echoes of Jeremiah 18:1-6 (the potter's house) and Jeremiah 32:6-15 (the purchase of a field). Some manuscripts of Matthew read "Jeremiah," and early scribes may have confused the prophets, or Matthew may be using a composite quotation technique common in Jewish exegesis. The Dead Sea Scrolls (4QXII) confirm that Zechariah 11 was circulating as part of the Book of the Twelve in the centuries before Christ, showing that this prophetic text was well-known and available to the Gospel writers. The precision of the fulfillment — the exact amount (thirty pieces of silver), the casting to the potter, and the purchase of a field — marks this as one of the most detailed messianic predictions in the Hebrew Scriptures.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The fulfillment of Zechariah 11:12-13 in the New Testament is one of the most precise prophecy fulfillments in all of Scripture. Matthew 26:14-15 records that Judas Iscariot went to the chief priests and asked, 'What will ye give me, and I will deliver Him unto you? And they covenanted with him for thirty pieces of silver.' After Jesus' crucifixion, Judas, stricken with remorse, threw the silver down in the temple (Matthew 27:3-5). The chief priests, unwilling to put blood money into the treasury, used it to purchase the potter's field to bury strangers (Matthew 27:6-10), exactly as Zechariah foresaw. The irony is staggering: the eternal Son of God, the Good Shepherd who lays down His life for the sheep, was valued at the price of a common slave. Peter later uses this imagery when he writes that believers were redeemed 'not with corruptible things, as silver and gold... but with the precious blood of Christ, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot' (1 Peter 1:18-19).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith used the title 'What's Your Price?' as a penetrating question for every believer. The religious leaders of Israel valued Jesus at thirty pieces of silver — the bare minimum. Judas valued Him at the same — the price of his greed. The question Smith pressed home is: What do you value Jesus at? Many Christians profess to love Christ but in practice value Him below their career, their reputation, their comfort, their finances, or their relationships. Smith warned against the insidious danger of 'pricing' Jesus cheaply — of treating Him as an accessory to life rather than as Life itself. He called believers to examine their hearts: Is Jesus worth everything? Have you ever, like Judas, betrayed Him for a price? The good news is that despite being valued at the price of a slave, Christ willingly paid that price — His own life — to purchase His people. The One who was sold for thirty pieces of silver is the One who purchased our salvation with His own blood.

## 📖 Key Hebrew Terms

- **Ra'ah** (רעה) — 'shepherd' — The central metaphor of Zechariah 11. The verb appears repeatedly: 'I will shepherd the flock,' 'Woe to the worthless shepherd.' The shepherd metaphor is one of the most important in Scripture — used of God (Psalm 23), kings (Ezekiel 34), and ultimately Christ (John 10).
- **Sekher** (שכר) — 'wages, hire, reward' — 'Give me my wages (sekher).' The prophet asks for his compensation, and the people give him an insultingly low sum. The word choice emphasizes that the people are paying off the shepherd they reject — they are calculating the value of God's care in cold, mercenary terms.
- **Kesef** (כסף) — 'silver, money' — 'Thirty pieces of silver.' The specific amount was the compensation for a slave gored by an ox (Exodus 21:32). The Good Shepherd is valued at the price of a common slave.
- **Yatsar** (יצר) — 'to form, fashion, pot' — 'Cast it to the potter (yotser).' The potter is a prophetic image of God's sovereign power over nations (Jeremiah 18:1-6). Throwing the silver to the potter suggests it goes to the most menial purpose — confirmation that the people's valuation of God is contemptible.
- **Adonai** (אדני) — 'Lord, Master' — The term used in 'the Lord said to me.' Despite the rejection, God remains sovereign. He is not diminished by His people's low valuation of Him.
- **Na'al** (נעל) — 'worthless' — The worthless shepherd of Zechariah 11:15-17 is the antithesis of the Good Shepherd. This worthless shepherd points to the coming antichrist figure.
- **Segullah** (סגולה) — 'special treasure, valued property' — Though not in this chapter, this word from Malachi 3:17 (and Exodus 19:5) forms the intentional contrast: the people valued the Shepherd at thirty pieces of silver, but God values His people as His treasured possession.
