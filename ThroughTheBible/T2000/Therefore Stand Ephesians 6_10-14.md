# 234 Therefore Stand — Ephesians 6:10-14

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#234](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qCMRE47RB1U)

## Overview

"Finally, my brethren, be strong in the Lord, and in the power of his might. Put on the whole armour of God, that ye may be able to stand against the wiles of the devil." Paul closes his letter with a call to spiritual warfare. The battle is not against flesh and blood but against spiritual forces of wickedness. The believer's defense is the full armor of God — truth, righteousness, the gospel of peace, faith, salvation, and the Word of God.

This passage is Paul's great summons to spiritual conflict. Having laid the doctrinal foundation (chapters 1-3) and the practical instruction (chapters 4-6a), he concludes with the reality of spiritual opposition. The "therefore" (tou loipou) connects back to everything that has gone before: because of all God has done, and because of the walk to which believers are called, they must be prepared for battle. Paul draws on Isaiah 59:17 (the Lord putting on righteousness as a breastplate and a helmet of salvation) and the imagery of a Roman soldier's armor, which would have been a familiar sight to his readers. The key word is "stand" (stēte) — repeated four times. The believer's posture in spiritual warfare is not primarily advancement but holding ground already won by Christ.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Spiritual Warfare | The Christian life is a real battle, not against human opponents but against Satanic forces — the devil, principalities, powers, and world rulers of darkness |
| Armor of God | Six pieces of divine equipment: truth (belt), righteousness (breastplate), gospel of peace (shoes), faith (shield), salvation (helmet), Word of God (sword) — each corresponding to a spiritual reality |
| Standing Firm | The believer's posture is defensive — having done all, stand — because the victory has already been won at the cross |
| The Devil's Schemes | Satan's methods include deception, accusation, temptation, and intimidation — the armor answers each with divine provision |
| Strength in the Lord | The battle is not fought in human power but "in the Lord and in the power of His might" — the same resurrection power that raised Christ |

## Background & Context

Ephesians 6:10-20 is Paul's conclusion to his most comprehensive letter. The imagery of armor would resonate with his audience in Asia Minor, where Roman garrisons were common. Paul himself was likely in chains as he wrote — a "prisoner of the Lord" (Ephesians 4:1) — making the call to spiritual warfare deeply personal. The passage has profoundly shaped Christian teaching on spiritual warfare throughout [[church]] history, from the early church fathers through the Reformation (Luther's "A mighty fortress is our God") to modern spiritual formation literature. Paul's use of the passive imperative — "be strong" (endynamoō) — indicates that strength comes from God's enabling, not self-generated effort. The "wiles of the devil" (methodeias tou diabolou) suggests strategic, systematic opposition, not random attacks.
