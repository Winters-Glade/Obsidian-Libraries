## Summary

King Asa cries out to the Lord as an immense Cushite army of a million men marches against Judah: 'Lord, it is nothing for You to help, whether with many or with those who have no power. Help us, O Lord our God, for we rest on You.' This [[Prayer]] of dependence becomes the turning point of the battle. Asa's [[Faith]] teaches that our resources are irrelevant compared to God's power. The key is not the size of the problem but the object of our trust. Pastor Chuck emphasizes that God delights to show His strength when His people acknowledge their weakness and rest entirely in Him.

## 📜 Historical Background

Asa reigned over Judah from 911-870 BC and did what was right in God's sight, removing idols and restoring worship. The Cushite invasion under Zerah — an army of a million men with 300 chariots — was a severe test of Asa's faith. His prayer acknowledges the disparity ('with many or with those who have no power') but focuses on God's ability, not man's weakness. The victory at Mareshah demonstrates that faith is the victory that overcomes the world. This historical setting shows Judah at a point of spiritual renewal, yet immediately tested.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul's declaration in 2 Corinthians 12:9-10 — 'When I am weak, then I am strong' — captures Asa's confidence. Jesus taught that 'with God all things are possible' (Matthew 19:26). The principle that it is 'nothing for You to help, whether with many or with those who have no power' is echoed in Zechariah 4:6: 'Not by might nor by power, but by My Spirit.' Paul's confidence that 'if God is for us, who can be against us?' (Romans 8:31) is the New Testament expression of Asa's prayer.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Asa's prayer is a masterclass in dependent faith. He acknowledges the problem honestly, confesses his utter dependence, and stakes everything on God's character. Chuck Smith encouraged believers to develop this kind of prayer life — not polished religious rhetoric but honest, desperate dependence on God. The principle is timeless: our resources or lack thereof do not limit God. Whether we have much or little, our help comes from the Lord. 'Lord, it is nothing for You to help' — this is the confidence of faith that moves mountains.

## 📖 Research Flavor

- **Zerah the Cushite — Identity Theories:** Zerah is almost certainly connected to Cush (Ethiopia/Nubia), but the scale of his army (1,000,000 men — the largest field army recorded in the OT) has led some scholars to identify him with Osorkon I (Libyan Pharaoh of the 22nd Dynasty) or Usarkon, a Cushite general serving Egypt. The Cushite (25th) Dynasty of Egypt would later rule the Nile Valley, but an early Cushite-Egyptian alliance in the 10th century BC explains how a million-man army could be raised from Nubian territories. A stela at Thebes mentions a Libyan-Cushite coalition roughly contemporary with Asa.
- **Mareshah and Zephathah Valley Archaeology:** Mareshah (Tell Sandahanna) was a fortified city in the Shephelah. Excavations by the Israel Antiquities Authority (2010–2020) uncovered a massive Hellenistic-era fortification system, but Iron Age II remains beneath confirm the city's strategic importance in Asa's time. The Zephathah Valley (likely the Wadi el-Mukhaddam) was the natural invasion route from the coastal plain into the Judean hill country — a chokepoint where a smaller force could defend against a larger army.
- **Egyptian-Cushite Relations in the 10th Century BC:** The 10th century BC was a period of Egyptian fragmentation following the collapse of the New Kingdom. The 21st Dynasty held only Lower Egypt, while Libyan chieftains and Cushite/Nubian princes controlled the south. A Cushite-led invasion of Judah may reflect a Nubian power play to control the trade routes through Philistia and the Shephelah — the Via Maris and the Ridge Route.
- **Asa's Reforms in Context:** The Chronicler notes that Asa 'did what was good and right in the eyes of the Lord his God' (2 Chr 14:2), removing foreign altars and high places. Archaeological surveys of Judah in the early 9th century BC reveal a dramatic decrease in cultic figurines and pillar figurines at domestic sites — possibly the material trace of Asa's religious reforms.
