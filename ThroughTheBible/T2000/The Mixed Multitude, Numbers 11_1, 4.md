## 📜 Historical Background

Numbers 11 marks a turning point in the wilderness journey. The people begin to complain about their hardships, and the 'mixed multitude' — non-Israelites who joined the Exodus — are identified as the source of discontent. They craved the food of Egypt, forgetting the slavery that accompanied it. Their grumbling spread to the whole congregation, leading them to despise the manna God provided daily. God's anger burned, fire consumed the outskirts of the camp, and later, quail was sent in judgment — until it came out of their nostrils. The place was called Kibroth-hattaavah ('Graves of Lust'). This chapter shows how quickly God's provision can be despised when the heart is set on the world.

**Hebrew Word Studies:**
- *Asafsuf* (אספסוף) — "mixed multitude" (Numbers 11:4). This rare Hebrew term derives from a root meaning "to collect or gather." The LXX renders it *epimiktos* — a mixed crowd of non-Israelites who attached themselves to the Exodus. These were likely Egyptians of lower status, slaves from other nations, and hangers-on who saw opportunity in Israel's liberation but lacked true covenant commitment. The rabbinic tradition calls them the *erev rav* (עֵרֶב רַב) — the "great mixed company" who also came up out of Egypt (Exodus 12:38). They were, as one commentator puts it, "in the camp but not of the camp."
- *Hit'avvu* (הִתְאַוּוּ) — "lusted" or "craved" (Numbers 11:4). This is a strong verb of intense, uncontrolled desire — the same root used for the tenth commandment's prohibition of coveting (*lo ta'chmod*). Their craving was not mere preference but an obsessive longing that blinded them to God's provision.
- *Ta'avah* (תַּאֲוָה) — "lust" — appears in the name Kibroth-hattaavah. The place name itself becomes a memorial: intense desire (*ta'avah*) leads to death (*kivrot*).
- *Manna* (מָן) — "What is it?" — the bread from heaven. The people despised it, calling it "light bread" (Numbers 21:5). The Hebrew *lekhem ha'kelokel* suggests contempt — bread of no account. Yet the Psalms call it "angel's food" (Psalm 78:25).

**ANE Context:** Egyptian food nostalgia was highly selective. The list of desired foods — fish, cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions, garlic (Numbers 11:5) — reflects the diet of the lower classes in the Nile Delta. These were foods readily available to slaves, yet the people remembered them as delicacies. The selective memory of suffering is a hallmark of the discontented heart. In ANE treaty contexts, such grumbling against a suzerain (overlord) would be treated as rebellion. Israel's complaints against God were not merely whining; they were covenant violations — breaches of trust in their divine King.

**The Two Judgments:**
1. **Taberah** (Numbers 11:1-3) — "burning" — fire from the Lord consumed the outskirts of the camp. Moses interceded, and the fire died down.
2. **Kibroth-hattaavah** (Numbers 11:31-34) — "Graves of Lust" — quail was sent in such abundance that it was a plague. While the meat was still between their teeth, the Lord struck the people with a great plague. The place was named for the graves of those who had lusted.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul explicitly uses this incident as a warning to the New Testament church: 'Neither murmur ye, as some of them murmured, and were destroyed of the destroyer' (1 Corinthians 10:10). He brackets this with the broader context of wilderness sins — idolatry, sexual immorality, testing Christ, and grumbling — all of which 'happened unto them for ensamples: and they are written for our admonition, upon whom the ends of the world are come' (1 Corinthians 10:11). Grumbling is treated not as a minor sin but as a serious expression of unbelief and rebellion against God's providential ordering.

James warns that the tongue — though small — sets the course of one's life on fire, and is itself set on fire by hell (James 3:6). The grumbling tongue reveals a heart at war with God's will. The writer of Hebrews warns against an 'evil heart of unbelief, in departing from the living God' (Hebrews 3:12), using the wilderness generation's unbelief as the prime cautionary example.

Jesus Himself connects manna with the true bread from heaven: 'Your fathers did eat manna in the wilderness, and are dead... I am the living bread which came down from heaven' (John 6:49-51). Those who craved Egypt's leeks and garlic rejected God's bread; those who come to Christ receive the true bread that gives eternal life.

The 'mixed multitude' finds its New Covenant counterpart in the parable of the wheat and tares (Matthew 13:24-30). In any visible community of faith, there will be those who are truly converted and those who are among us but not of us (1 John 2:19). The presence of the mixed multitude does not destroy the work of God, but it does create constant spiritual pressure toward worldliness.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often warned about the danger of the 'mixed multitude' — people who are in the church but not truly converted, whose values and desires pull them back to the world. Discontentment is the enemy of gratitude, and ingratitude opens the door to every form of sin. The antidote is to remember what we were saved from and to treasure the daily bread God provides. The manna represents Christ, the true bread from heaven (John 6).

The selective memory of the Israelites is a pastoral warning: when we forget the bitterness of our bondage to sin, we begin to romanticize our life before Christ. The 'leeks and garlics of Egypt' become attractive again — the fleeting pleasures of sin that once enslaved us. The cure is not to deny that the wilderness is hard but to recognize that every step of the journey is sustained by God's daily, supernatural provision.

For the church today, the mixed multitude is both a reality and a caution. Not everyone who travels with the people of God is a child of God. Pastors must lovingly but clearly distinguish the gospel call from cultural Christianity. The complaining tongue must be met with the Word of God, which exposes the thoughts and intentions of the heart (Hebrews 4:12).

Finally, the severity of God's judgment at Taberah and Kibroth-hattaavah reminds us that ingratitude and grumbling are not trivial offenses in a holy God's economy. They are the fruit of unbelief. But the gospel offers a better remedy than quail: Christ, the true bread, and the Spirit who produces in us the fruit of thanksgiving.
