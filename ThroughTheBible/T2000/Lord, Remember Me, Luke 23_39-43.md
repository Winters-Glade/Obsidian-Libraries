## Summary

Luke 23:39-43 records the encounter between the crucified Jesus and the penitent thief. One thief mocked Jesus; the other rebuked his fellow and asked, 'Lord, remember me when You come into Your kingdom.' Jesus' response — 'Today you will be with Me in Paradise' — reveals the immediacy of salvation and the grace that reaches the worst of sinners at the last moment. Pastor Chuck examines the assurance of salvation through faith alone.

## 📖 Research Context

This account is unique to Luke's Gospel — the physician-historian who emphasizes Jesus' compassion for the outcast records the salvation of the penitent thief. The scene is set on Golgotha (the Place of the Skull), a hill outside Jerusalem's walls near a main road where executions served as a public deterrent. Crucifixion was designed by the Romans to be the most agonizing and humiliating death possible — victims were typically scourged first, then nailed through the wrists and feet, dying slowly from asphyxiation and shock. The penitent thief's confession is extraordinary: he acknowledges his own guilt ('justly condemned'), declares Jesus' innocence ('this Man has done nothing amiss'), and recognizes Jesus' kingship ('when You come into Your kingdom'). Jesus' promise of Paradise (Greek paradeisos, from Persian pairidaeza — a walled garden) evokes the Garden of Eden and the hope of restored communion with God. The thief asks to be 'remembered'; Jesus grants far more — immediate presence in Paradise.

Key Old Testament cross-references: Isaiah 53:12 (numbered with the transgressors — literally fulfilled as Jesus is crucified between two criminals), Psalm 22:1-18 (the Psalm of the Cross), Zechariah 12:10 (they shall look on Him whom they pierced).

## 📜 Historical Background

Crucifixion was a Roman punishment reserved for the worst criminals and rebels. The two thieves were likely insurrectionists or bandits. Crucifixion could last for days, but the victims were typically stripped and exposed to public humiliation. The penitent thief's confession is remarkable: he acknowledged his own guilt ('we receive the due reward of our deeds'), Jesus' innocence ('this Man has done nothing amiss'), and Jesus' kingship ('when You come into Your kingdom'). Jesus' promise of Paradise uses a Persian loanword meaning a walled garden or enclosed park — used in the Septuagint for the Garden of Eden and in Jewish literature for the abode of the righteous dead.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the penitent thief is the greatest example of salvation by grace alone in Scripture. He had no time for good works, no opportunity for baptism, no church membership, no sacraments. He could only believe — and that was enough. This teaches us that salvation is never too late and never too hard. As long as there is breath, there is hope. No one is beyond the reach of God's grace if they will simply turn to Christ.
