# 243 The Great Mystery — 1 Timothy 3:16

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#243](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CxU-DdAD7Ko)

## Overview

"And without controversy great is the mystery of godliness: God was manifest in the flesh, justified in the Spirit, seen of angels, preached unto the Gentiles, believed on in the world, received up into glory." This is likely an early [[church]] hymn or creedal statement. It summarizes the entire gospel in six lines — the incarnation, vindication, angelic witness, worldwide proclamation, faith response, and ascension of Christ.

This passage is widely recognized as a fragment of early Christian liturgy — a hymn that the [[church]] sang or recited before the New Testament canon was complete. Six lines arranged in parallel structure tell the story of Christ: (1) His incarnation ("manifest in the flesh"), (2) His vindication by the Spirit (likely the [[Resurrection]], Romans 1:4), (3) His being witnessed by angelic beings (the [[Resurrection]] and ascension), (4) His proclamation to the nations, (5) His reception by faith throughout the world, and (6) His exaltation to glory (the ascension). The "mystery of godliness" is the incarnation itself — the truth that God became man, lived among us, died for us, rose again, and was received into glory. Paul presents this hymn in the context of [[church]] conduct (1 Timothy 3:14-16) — the [[church]] is "the pillar and ground of truth" because it guards and proclaims this great mystery.

## Background & Context

This passage sits within a section on [[church]] order and leadership qualifications (1 Timothy 3:1-13). Paul has described the qualifications for bishops/overseers and deacons — the kind of people who should lead the [[church]]. Then he explains why these matter: the [[church]] is "the household of God" and "the pillar and ground of truth" (1 Timothy 3:15). The hymn that follows (3:16) is the truth the [[church]] upholds. The "mystery of godliness" (to tēs eusebeias mystērion) is Paul's way of summarizing the entire redemptive story in Christ. The hymn structure is clear in the Greek — six aorist passive verbs (was manifested, was vindicated, was seen, was preached, was believed, was received). The text literally reads "He who was manifested in the flesh" though some manuscripts read "God was manifested." Either reading affirms the incarnation as the heart of the faith.
