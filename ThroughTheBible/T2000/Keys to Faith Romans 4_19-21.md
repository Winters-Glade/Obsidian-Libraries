# 206 Keys to Faith — Romans 4:19-21

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#206](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VSwTJ7SUKGM)

## Overview

Abraham's faith is held up as the pattern for all who believe. Despite his advanced age of nearly 100 and Sarah's barrenness, Abraham staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief but was strong in faith, giving glory to God. His example teaches that faith rests on God's ability rather than human circumstances. Paul uses Abraham as the definitive proof that [[justification]] is by faith apart from works — Abraham was counted righteous through faith before circumcision and before the Law. The key to Abraham's faith was not the size of his faith but the object of his faith — God who is able to perform what He has promised.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Faith | Not a vague optimism but a settled conviction that God is able to do what He has promised — faith rests on God's character and power |
| Abraham | The father of all who believe, presented by Paul as the paradigm case of justification by faith, both for the circumcised and uncircumcised |
| Justification | Being declared righteous before God on the basis of faith alone — Abraham believed God and it was counted to him as righteousness |
| The Promise | God's covenant commitment to Abraham was grounded entirely in divine initiative, not human merit; faith simply receives what God has pledged |
| God's Ability | Abraham focused not on the impossibility of his circumstances but on the omnipotence of the promise-maker |

## Pauline Context

Romans 4 is Paul's masterful exegetical argument that [[justification]] by faith is not a new doctrine but the consistent teaching of the Old Testament. Paul demonstrates that Abraham was justified by faith centuries before the Law was given through Moses, proving that the Law is not the means of righteousness. The "staggering not at the promise" (ou diekrithē tē apistia) reflects Paul's contrast between faith and unbelief — faith honors God by taking Him at His word, while unbelief dishonors God by questioning His truthfulness. Paul's argument here is foundational: if Abraham — the father of the Jewish people — was justified by faith, then justification has always been by faith alone. This same argument reappears in Galatians 3, where Paul calls Abraham's children those who are of faith.
