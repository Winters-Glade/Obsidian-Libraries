## Summary

[[Ezekiel 9_8]] captures Ezekiel's anguished cry when he sees the severity of God's [[Judgment]] on Jerusalem: 'Ah Lord God! wilt thou destroy all the residue of Israel in thy pouring out of thy fury upon Jerusalem?' Pastor Chuck explores the tension between God's righteous judgment and the prophet's intercessory heart, and how God's mercy preserves a [[Remnant]] even in the midst of devastating judgment.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[Ezekiel 9.md]] is part of a [[Prophecy|vision sequence]] in chapters 8-11 where [[Ezekiel]], still in [[Babylon]], is transported in spirit to [[Jerusalem]] to witness the abominations being committed in the temple and the subsequent [[Judgment]]. The 'man clothed in linen' with a writer's inkhorn marks the [[faithfulness|faithful]] [[Remnant]] on their foreheads before the executioners destroy the city. This vision predates the final destruction of Jerusalem in 586 BC. The marking of the remnant parallels the Passover blood in [[Exodus 12.md]] and anticipates the sealing of God's servants in [[Revelation 7.md]].

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Ezekiel's cry 'will you destroy all the residue?' echoes Abraham's intercession for Sodom ([[Genesis 18_23-33.md]]). In the New Testament, Paul addresses the [[Remnant]] theme in [[Romans 11_5]] — 'Even so then at this present time also there is a remnant according to the election of grace.' The sealing of the 144,000 in [[Revelation 7_2-3.md]] follows the same pattern of divine marking for preservation through judgment.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

The tension between judgment and mercy is one of the deepest in Scripture. Chuck Smith taught that we can cry out with Ezekiel — 'Ah Lord God!' — bringing our anguish over the lost to God, while trusting that His judgments are always right and that His mercy always preserves a remnant. The man with the inkhorn reminds us that God knows those who are His.
