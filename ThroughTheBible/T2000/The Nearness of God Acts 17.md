## Overview

Paul in Athens, the intellectual capital of the ancient world. His address at the Areopagus (Mars' Hill) represents his most culturally engaged sermon. He quotes Greek poets (Epimenides: 'In Him we live and move and have our being'; Aratus/Cleanthes: 'We are His offspring') to build bridges. The altar 'TO AN UNKNOWN GOD' becomes a point of contact for proclaiming the God who is knowable through Christ.

## 📜 Historical Background

Athens in the first century AD was the intellectual and philosophical center of the Greco-Roman world, still revered for its classical heritage even though its political power had declined. The Areopagus (Mars' Hill) was a rocky outcropping northwest of the Acropolis where the oldest council of Athens met to discuss religious and philosophical matters. Paul encountered two dominant schools: the Epicureans, who believed the gods were distant and uninvolved, and the Stoics, who believed the divine logos permeated the universe. The altar 'TO AN UNKNOWN GOD' (Agnosto Theo) reflects Athens' remarkable religious inclusiveness — they had altars to unknown deities to avoid missing any god. Paul's cultural engagement — quoting Epimenides of Crete ('In Him we live and move and have our being') and Aratus' Phaenomena ('We are His offspring') — demonstrates contextualized evangelism without compromise.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul's Areopagus address is the New Testament's most developed model of contextual evangelism. He begins from common ground (natural revelation, human poetry) and moves to the specific claims of Christ. His declaration that God 'made from one blood every nation of men' (Acts 17:26) undercuts Greek racial superiority while affirming the biblical view of creation. The concept of God's nearness — 'He is not far from each one of us' (Acts 17:27) — resonates with Psalm 139 and Jeremiah 23:23-24. But Paul pivots from God's immanence to the necessity of repentance and judgment through the resurrected Christ. The resurrection is the stumbling block for Greek philosophers (1 Corinthians 1:22-24), as seen in the divided response: some mocked, some wanted to hear more, and some believed.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith used Paul's Athens address to demonstrate that the gospel can engage any culture without being compromised by it. Smith taught that believers should be like Paul — deeply aware of their culture (he even read their poets) but never intimidated by intellectual opposition. The 'Unknown God' represents the deep longing in every human heart for the true God — even those who deny Him have a God-shaped void. Smith emphasized that God's nearness is the most profound truth for those who feel distant from Him: He is not far off, but closer than breath. The resurrection is the non-negotiable center of the gospel — when Paul preached it, some believed and some mocked, but he did not alter his message to make it more palatable.
