## Summary

[[Jeremiah 2_13.md]] captures the heart of Israel's sin: 'For My people have committed two evils; they have forsaken Me, the fountain of living waters, and hewed them out cisterns, broken cisterns, that can hold no water.' Pastor Chuck explores this powerful metaphor of God as the source of living water and the futility of replacing Him with broken human alternatives.

## 📜 Historical Background

Jeremiah ministered during the final decades of Judah's existence before the Babylonian exile (roughly 627-586 BC). Chapter 2 opens God's case against His people like a courtroom [[Judgment|indictment]]. The imagery of living water versus broken cisterns would have been deeply powerful in the arid climate of Judah. Natural springs (living water) were life-sustaining, while man-made cisterns often cracked and leaked, leaving their owners with stagnant, undrinkable water. The indictment is that God's people exchanged the inexhaustible, life-giving spring of God Himself for broken, human-made substitutes.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus picks up this very imagery in [[John 4_10-14.md]] when He speaks to the Samaritan woman at the well, offering 'living water' that becomes a spring welling up to eternal life. In [[John 7_37-39.md]], Jesus stands up at the Feast of Tabernacles and cries out, 'If anyone thirsts, let him come to Me and drink.' The fountain of living waters that Israel forsook is the same living water Christ offers. Paul echoes this in [[_Bible/Romans/Romans 1_21-25.md]], describing how humanity exchanged the truth of God for a lie and served the creature rather than the Creator.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized that the sin of Israel was not merely breaking God's laws but breaking His heart. The two evils — forsaking God and turning to broken substitutes — are always connected. Whatever we turn to instead of God (money, relationships, status) is ultimately a broken cistern that cannot satisfy. The living water of God's presence, grace, and truth is the only source that truly quenches the soul's thirst.
