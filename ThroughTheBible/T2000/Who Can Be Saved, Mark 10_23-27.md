## Summary

Mark 10:23-27 follows the encounter with the rich young ruler who went away sorrowful rather than surrender his wealth. Jesus looks around and declares how hard it is for those who trust in riches to enter the kingdom of God — harder than a camel going through the eye of a needle. The disciples are astonished and ask, 'Who then can be saved?' Jesus answers with one of the most profound statements in Scripture: 'With men it is impossible, but not with God: for with God all things are possible.' Pastor Chuck explores the impossibility of salvation by human effort and the glorious truth that what is impossible for us is possible for God.

## 📜 Historical Background

The camel-through-the-eye-of-a-needle is one of Jesus' most vivid hyperboles. Some have suggested the 'eye of the needle' was a small gate in Jerusalem that camels could pass through only by kneeling — but this interpretation likely originated in later medieval tradition, not in the first century. Jesus' point is deliberate exaggeration: it is completely impossible for a rich person to enter the kingdom by their own resources. Riches create a unique spiritual danger because they foster self-sufficiency — the illusion that we don't need God. The disciples' shock reflects the common Jewish belief that wealth was a sign of God's blessing (Deuteronomy 28). If the rich couldn't be saved, who could?

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Gospel of Mark:** The belief that wealth was a sign of divine favor was deeply rooted in the Old Testament (Abraham, Job, David, Solomon were all wealthy) and reinforced by Deuteronomy's promise of material blessing for obedience. The disciples' astonishment at Jesus' teaching reflects this cultural assumption. Yet the prophetic tradition had long critiqued the wealthy who exploited the poor (Amos 5:11–12, Isaiah 10:1–2), and by the first century, the economic gap in Judea was vast. Under Roman occupation, much of the land was held by a small elite — absentee landlords who collected rents from tenant farmers — while the majority of the population lived at subsistence level. The Herodian aristocracy and the high priestly families controlled enormous wealth, often through collaboration with Rome. Mark's Gospel, written for a persecuted community that had likely experienced confiscation of property (Hebrews 10:34), presents Jesus' radical teaching on wealth as good news: the kingdom is not for the self-sufficient but for those who recognize their complete dependence on God's grace — the one thing impossible with men but possible with God.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul writes in Ephesians 2:8-9: 'For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: not of works, lest any man should boast.' The impossibility of salvation by human effort is the consistent testimony of Scripture. Romans 3:23 declares that 'all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God.' Titus 3:5 states that salvation is 'not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to His mercy.' The rich young ruler's problem was not simply his wealth but his unwillingness to acknowledge that he needed a Savior. The eye of the needle is the great equalizer — no one can squeeze through on their own merits.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized that the question 'Who then can be saved?' is the most important question anyone can ask. The honest answer is: no one — if it depends on human ability. But with God, all things are possible. Salvation is a divine miracle, not a human achievement. The rich young ruler's sorrowful departure shows what happens when we try to hold onto both Jesus and our idols. The key is recognizing our spiritual bankruptcy and throwing ourselves entirely on God's mercy — which is always, miraculously, enough.
