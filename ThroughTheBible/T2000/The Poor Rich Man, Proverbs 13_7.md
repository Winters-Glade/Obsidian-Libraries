## Summary

Proverbs 13:7 presents a spiritual paradox: 'There is that maketh himself rich, yet hath nothing: there is that maketh himself poor, yet hath great riches.' True wealth is not measured by material possessions but by [[Righteousness]] and the [[fear of the Lord]].

## 📜 Historical Background

Proverbs 13 is part of Solomon's collection of wise sayings. Verse 7 encapsulates a theme that runs throughout Scripture: the reversal of worldly values. Those who appear rich may be spiritually bankrupt, while those who appear poor may possess true riches.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus teaches: 'Blessed are the poor in spirit' (Matthew 5:3). Paul describes believers as 'poor, yet making many rich; having nothing, and yet possessing all things' (2 Corinthians 6:10).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

The 'poor rich man' has material wealth but is destitute in what matters most. Those who have Christ may have little of this world's goods but possess great riches — peace, joy, and eternal life.

## 📚 Hebrew Roots & Wisdom Context

Proverbs 13:7 is a classic example of **antithetical parallelism** — the second line contrasts with the first, the most common form in Proverbs. This literary form is the **mashal** (מָשָׁל) — a compressed, memorable, often paradoxical saying. Proverbs 10-22:16 consists almost entirely of two-line proverbs in antithetical or synonymous parallelism, designed for memorization. This proverb challenges the ancient assumption that wealth equals divine favor — a theme also questioned by Job and Ecclesiastes.

## 📖 Research Context

### Antithetic Parallelism in Proverbs

Proverbs 13:7 — "There is that maketh himself rich, yet hath nothing: there is that maketh himself poor, yet hath great riches" — is a textbook example of **antithetic parallelism**, where the second line contrasts with the first. The book of Proverbs is built on such contrasts: wise/foolish, diligent/slothful, humble/proud.

The paradox transcends economic categories, describing:
1. **Outward appearance vs. inner reality** — Those who appear rich but are spiritually bankrupt, and those who appear poor but possess true riches
2. **The spiritual principle** — True wealth is not measured by material possessions

### The Reversal in Wisdom Tradition

This proverb is part of a larger [[wisdom]] theme — the **reversal of expectations**:
- Proverbs 11:24 — "There is that scattereth, and yet increaseth"
- Proverbs 15:16 — "Better is little with the fear of the Lord than great treasure and trouble therewith"
- Proverbs 16:8 — "Better is a little with righteousness than great revenues without right"

The New Testament amplifies this: "Though He was rich, yet for your sakes He became poor" (2 Corinthians 8:9). The cross is the ultimate [[wisdom]] reversal — weakness that is strength, death that brings life.
