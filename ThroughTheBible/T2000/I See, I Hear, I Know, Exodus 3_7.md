## Summary

Exodus 3:7 is the turning point in Israel's history — God declares to Moses from the burning bush, 'I have surely seen the affliction of My people... and have heard their cry.' Pastor Chuck explores the compassion of God who sees, hears, and knows the suffering of His people before He acts. This revelation of God's character precedes the deliverance from Egypt.

## 📜 Historical Background

Exodus 3 marks the commissioning of Moses at the burning bush, one of the most pivotal theophanic encounters in Scripture. The site — Mount Horeb, called "the mountain of God" — is traditionally identified with Jebel Musa in the Sinai Peninsula, though some scholars link it to Jebel al-Lawz in northwestern Arabia. The phrase "the angel of the LORD" (מַלְאַךְ יְהוָה, *mal'akh YHWH*) appears here in a self-identifying context that early Christian interpreters understood as a pre-incarnate Christophany (cf. Justin Martyr, *Dialogue with Trypho* 59).

The Hebrew of Exodus 3:7 uses three verbs in a deliberate, climactic sequence: **רָאֹה רָאִיתִי** (*ra'oh ra'iti* — "I have surely seen," using the infinitive absolute for emphasis), **שָׁמַעְתִּי** (*shama'ti* — "I have heard"), and **יָדַעְתִּי** (*yada'ti* — "I know"). This triadic structure is legally weighted in Semitic rhetoric — it conveys divine certainty and personal engagement. The Canaanite and Egyptian legal traditions of the Late Bronze Age (c. 1550–1200 BC) employed similar tripartite declarations by a suzerain who takes cognizance of a vassal's plight before acting on their behalf.

The Israelites had endured approximately 400 years in Egypt (Genesis 15:13), the latter portion under a pharaoh "who knew not Joseph" (Exodus 1:8). This is widely identified with either Seti I or Ramesses II of the Nineteenth Dynasty, a period of massive state building projects at Pi-Ramesses and Pithom. Archaeological evidence, including the Brooklyn Papyrus (c. 1800 BC) listing Asiatic slaves in Thebes and the reliefs at Medinet Habu depicting Semitic captives making bricks, corroborates the biblical portrait of corvée labor in the Nile Delta region.

The burning bush itself — a thornbush (סְנֶה, *seneh*) aflame but not consumed — is a visual theophany that echoes ANE fire-god imagery (e.g., the Mesopotamian god Girra) while standing in radical contrast: the fire does not destroy, signifying that God's holy presence draws near not to consume but to commission. The command for Moses to remove his sandals reflects ANE temple etiquette — entering sacred space barefoot acknowledged the ground as holy (cf. the Mesopotamian *bit rimki* purification rituals). Horeb thus becomes a sanctuary outside the camp, a mobile holy place anticipating the tabernacle cultus.

Chronologically, this event anchors the birth of Moses to c. 1526 BC (per Ussher) or c. 1391 BC (per early-date Exodus theories). The deliverance that follows becomes the central redemptive paradigm of the Old Testament — the event that later prophets (Hosea 11:1; Isaiah 63:11-14) and psalmists (Psalm 77:14-20; Psalm 106:7-12) repeatedly invoke as the definitive demonstration of Yahweh's saving power.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The compassion of God expressed in Exodus 3:7 finds its ultimate fulfillment in Jesus Christ, who "saw the multitudes and was moved with compassion" (Matthew 9:36; 14:14; 15:32). Where God saw Israel's affliction in Egypt, Jesus sees the harassed and helpless crowds of Israel. The Greek verb **σπλαγχνίζομαι** (*splagchnizomai* — "to be moved in the inward parts") used in the Gospels conveys a visceral, gut-level compassion that echoes the Hebrew emphasis of Exodus 3:7.

The writer of Hebrews declares that we have a High Priest who can be "touched with the feeling of our infirmities" (Hebrews 4:15) — the same God who saw and heard now enters fully into human suffering through the incarnation. The God who sees is the God who saves. In Revelation, the redeemed sing "the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb" (Revelation 15:3), merging the two great deliverances into one continuous redemptive symphony.

Furthermore, Jesus' own self-identification in the "I AM" statements (John 8:58) directly connects to the burning bush revelation where Yahweh declares His name — "I AM THAT I AM" (Exodus 3:14). The call of Moses thus prefigures the Great Commission: as Moses was sent to Pharaoh, so the disciples are sent to the nations (Matthew 28:18-20), and the same promise — "I will be with you" (Exodus 3:12) — is renewed: "I am with you always" (Matthew 28:20).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often emphasized that our circumstances never escape God's attention. When we feel invisible, forgotten, or abandoned, Exodus 3:7 reminds us that God sees, hears, and knows. The sequence is important: God's awareness precedes His action. Before the plagues, before the parting of the Red Sea, before the manna from heaven — God first sees and hears. This is the foundation of faith: we serve a God who is not indifferent to our pain.

There is profound pastoral comfort in the progressive nature of the Hebrew verbs — "I see, I hear, I know." Many believers struggle with feeling that God is distant during seasons of suffering. Yet this text assures us that divine awareness is both intimate and active. God is not a passive observer; His knowledge of our condition moves Him to intervene. The assurance that God knows our situation personally is the starting point of every prayer and the ground of every hope.

Moreover, the burning bush teaches that God often reveals Himself in the midst of ordinary, monotonous labor. Moses was tending sheep — the same work he had done for forty years. It was in the mundane that the miraculous broke through. Christians who feel stuck in routine can take heart: the God of the burning bush meets us in our everyday wilderness and transforms common ground into holy ground through His presence.
