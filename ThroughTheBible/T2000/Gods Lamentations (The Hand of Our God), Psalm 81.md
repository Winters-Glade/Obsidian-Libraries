## Summary

Psalm 81 records God's lament over His stubborn people: 'Oh that My people had hearkened unto Me.' God's heart is revealed as He recalls His deliverance of Israel from Egypt and yearns for their obedience so He could bless them.

## 📜 Historical Background

Psalm 81 is attributed to Asaph and was likely used for the Feast of Tabernacles. The refrain 'Oh that My people had hearkened unto Me' reveals God's heartache over Israel's stubbornness. He longs to bless them but they refuse to walk in His ways.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus laments over Jerusalem in [[Matthew 23_37.md]]: 'O Jerusalem, Jerusalem... how often would I have gathered thy children together... and ye would not!' Paul expresses similar anguish in [[_Bible/Romans/Romans 9_1-3.md]].

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that God's commandments are not arbitrary restrictions but loving directions for our flourishing. When we disobey, we are not breaking rules — we are breaking a relationship.

## 📚 Hebrew Poetry & Worship Context

Psalm 81 is associated with the **Feast of Tabernacles** (Sukkot), one of Israel's three pilgrimage festivals. The blowing of the shofar (verse 3) is central to this feast. The psalm is a **[[Prophecy|prophetic]] oracle** within a hymn, with Asaph delivering God's lament over Israel's stubbornness. The divine refrain &#x22;Oh that My people would hearken unto Me!&#x22; uses the Hebrew *shama* (שָׁמַע) — the root of the *Shema* (Deuteronomy 6:4).

**Worship Significance:** Sung at Sukkot, this psalm reminded Israel that festivals were not merely ritual but an invitation to renewed [[Covenant]] relationship with a God who yearns to bless His children.


## 📖 Research Context

### The Feast of Tabernacles Liturgy

Psalm 81 is an Asaph psalm associated with the **Feast of Tabernacles** (*Sukkot*). The references to "new moon" and "full moon" (v. 3) place it at the 15th of Tishri, when the feast began. The blowing of the *shofar* (שׁוֹפָר, v. 3) was central to Israel's festal calendar ([[Leviticus 23_24.md]]; [[Numbers 29_1.md]]).

The liturgical structure:
1. **Call to [[Worship]]** with music and trumpet (vv. 1-5)
2. **God's voice** recalling the Exodus (vv. 6-7)
3. **The covenant stipulation** — "Hear, O My people" (v. 8)
4. **The divine lament** — "Oh that My people had hearkened" (vv. 13-16)

### The Divine Lament — God's Wounded Heart

The most remarkable feature is the **divine lament** (vv. 13-16). God expresses deep sorrow over Israel's stubbornness — not anger but grief. The Hebrew *lu* (לוּ, "O that, would that") introduces an unreal wish, a window into the divine heart. This passage is the theological counterpart to Jesus' lament: "How often would I have gathered thy children together... and ye would not!" (Matthew 23:37).
