## Summary

Matthew 6:5-15 contains Jesus' teaching on prayer, including the model prayer commonly called the Lord's Prayer. Jesus contrasts genuine prayer with the hypocritical displays of those who pray to be seen by others. He then gives a pattern that begins with addressing God as Father, prioritizes His name and kingdom, acknowledges dependence for daily needs, seeks forgiveness while extending it to others, and asks for deliverance from temptation and evil. Pastor Chuck explores the Lord's Prayer not as a formula to recite but as a framework that shapes our entire approach to communion with God.

## 📜 Historical Background

Jewish prayer customs in the first century included set times of prayer (Daniel 6:10), often accompanied by almsgiving and fasting. Jesus does not condemn public prayer itself — the Temple and synagogues were places of corporate prayer — but the motivation of those who pray 'to be seen of men.' The Lord's Prayer is deeply rooted in Jewish prayers (the Kaddish begins with hallowing God's name and praying for His kingdom). Its brevity and simplicity stand in stark contrast to the verbose prayers of pagans who thought they would be heard for their 'much speaking.'

**Research context — 1st Century Judea & Roman Occupation:** Daily prayer in first-century Judaism followed a structured rhythm — the Shema was recited morning and evening, and the Eighteen Benedictions (Shemoneh Esreh) were prayed three times daily. The Temple in Jerusalem was the center of national worship, but local synagogues served as places of prayer, Scripture reading, and community gathering throughout Galilee and Judea. Under Roman occupation, prayer took on added significance as an expression of Jewish identity and resistance to assimilation. Jesus' use of 'Abba, Father' (an intimate Aramaic term) was striking — it reframed the covenant relationship not as distant reverence but as childlike trust. Matthew's Gospel, with its strong Jewish-Christian perspective, presents this teaching on prayer within the Sermon on the Mount as the new community's rule of life — a counter-cultural way of relating to God that transcends both Pharisaic display and pagan verbosity.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Luke's version of the Lord's Prayer (Luke 11:2-4) is shorter, suggesting Jesus taught this pattern on multiple occasions. Paul emphasizes prayer without ceasing (1 Thessalonians 5:17) and the role of the Spirit in our prayers (Romans 8:26-27). The author of Hebrews speaks of Christ's ongoing intercession for us (Hebrews 7:25). The forgiveness clause is expanded in Matthew 6:14-15, emphasizing that our willingness to forgive others is connected to our experience of God's forgiveness — a theme Paul develops in Ephesians 4:32.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often taught that the Lord's Prayer teaches us more about relationship than ritual. Addressing God as 'Our Father' establishes intimacy with the Creator. The prayer moves from God's glory ('Hallowed be Thy name') to God's purposes ('Thy kingdom come') before it ever gets to our needs — teaching us the proper order of priorities. The petition for daily bread reminds us to live one day at a time, trusting God for today's needs rather than worrying about tomorrow.
