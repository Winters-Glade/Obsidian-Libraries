## Summary

Zechariah 10:7 declares God's promise of restoration: 'And they of Ephraim shall be like a mighty man, and their heart shall rejoice as through wine: yea, their children shall see it, and be glad; their heart shall rejoice in the Lord.' Pastor Chuck Smith teaches that this prophecy speaks of God gathering His scattered people, strengthening them, and filling them with such joy that it overflows to the next generation. The rejoicing is not from alcohol or earthly circumstances but from the Lord Himself — it is the joy of a people who have been sought, found, redeemed, and restored by their God. This is the joy of salvation that comes when God turns captivity and breathes new life into dry bones.

## 📜 Historical Background

Zechariah 10 is part of a section (chapters 9-14) often called 'the Burden of the Word of the Lord' — oracles concerning Israel's future restoration. Zechariah prophesied after the Babylonian exile, when a remnant had returned to Judah but the nation was still subject to Persian rule. The ten northern tribes (Ephraim/Israel) had been scattered by Assyria over a century earlier. The promise that 'they of Ephraim shall be like a mighty man' was extraordinary — Ephraim was weak, scattered, and seemingly lost forever. Yet God promises to strengthen them, gather them, and fill them with joy. This is not merely a return from physical exile but a spiritual revival that transforms weakness into strength and sorrow into rejoicing.

**The Dead Sea Scrolls and Zechariah's Text (4QXII).** Zechariah 10:7 is preserved among the Dead Sea Scrolls, notably in the Minor Prophets scroll from Cave 4 (4QXII). These manuscripts, dating from the 2nd-1st centuries BC, are the oldest surviving copies of Zechariah and demonstrate the remarkable textual stability of the prophetic tradition. The scrolls confirm that Zechariah's oracles concerning Ephraim's restoration were being read, copied, and preserved by the Qumran community — a community that saw itself as a faithful remnant awaiting the fulfillment of these very promises. The 4QXII manuscripts show that the book of the Twelve (the Minor Prophets) circulated as a single scroll by this period, supporting the canonical unity of these texts.

**The Cyrus Cylinder and Persian Restoration Policy.** The Cyrus Cylinder (c. 539 BC, British Museum) documents the Persian policy of repatriating displaced peoples — the very policy that made the restoration Zechariah prophesied historically possible. While the northern tribes (Ephraim) had been scattered by Assyria (722 BC) and did not return as a distinct entity, the Persian period saw the restoration of Judah and the preservation of hope for the full ingathering of all twelve tribes. The Elephantine Papyri (5th century BC) reveal that a Jewish community in Elephantine, Egypt, corresponded with the Persian governor of Judah and the high priest in Jerusalem, showing that the post-exilic community maintained trans-regional connections that sustained hope for the gathering of the dispersed.

**Persian Period Bullae from Jerusalem.** Archaeological excavations in Jerusalem have uncovered dozens of bullae (seal impressions) from the Persian period (6th-4th centuries BC), many bearing Hebrew names of officials and families mentioned in Ezra, Nehemiah, and the post-exilic prophets. These bullae testify to a functioning administrative center in Jerusalem during the very period Zechariah prophesied. The name 'Yehud' (the Persian province name) appears on coins minted in Jerusalem in this period, confirming that the province had its own minting authority — a sign of relative autonomy within the Persian satrap system. Against this backdrop, Zechariah's promise that Ephraim would be restored and rejoice is set in a context where Judah had already experienced a partial restoration, fueling hope for the complete gathering of all Israel.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The joy described in Zechariah 10:7 finds its fulfillment in the New Covenant. Jesus came to bring 'abundant life' (John 10:10) and promised that His joy would remain in His disciples and that their joy would be full (John 15:11). Paul commands believers to 'rejoice in the Lord always' (Philippians 4:4) and describes the fruit of the Spirit as 'joy' (Galatians 5:22). The rejoicing 'as through wine' but not from wine echoes the New Testament contrast between being drunk with wine and being filled with the Spirit (Ephesians 5:18). The Holy Spirit produces a supernatural joy that transcends circumstances — the joy of the Lord that is our strength (Nehemiah 8:10). This joy is also intergenerational, as promised to believers and their children (Acts 2:39).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that true joy is not found in temporary pleasures but in the Lord's saving work. Many people chase happiness through possessions, experiences, or substances, but the joy that Zechariah describes comes from being restored to relationship with God. Smith encouraged believers to recognize that God is actively gathering, strengthening, and rejoicing over His people. The joy of the Lord is a witness to the next generation — when children see their parents rejoicing in God, they are drawn to that same joy. Smith also challenged the misconception that Christianity is a dour, joyless religion. On the contrary, the restored people of God are marked by overflowing gladness, and that joy is both a fruit of the Spirit and a testimony to the world.

## 📖 Key Hebrew Terms

- **Shub** (שוב) — 'to return, restore' — The underlying theme of Zechariah 10. God promises to 'bring them back' and 'gather them.' This is the great reversal — from exile to return, from scattering to gathering, from weeping to joy.
- **Gibbor** (גיבר) — 'mighty man, warrior' — 'They of Ephraim shall be like a mighty man (gibbor).' This is ironic: Ephraim (the Northern Kingdom) was weak, scattered, and demoralized. God promises to transform them into warriors — the same word used for David's mighty men (2 Samuel 23).
- **Susah** (שישה) or **Simchah** (שמחה) — 'joy, gladness' — 'Their heart shall rejoice as through wine.' The joy of restoration is compared to the exhilaration of wine but is produced by the Spirit, not by alcohol. This is the same joy that characterizes the messianic age (Isaiah 25:6).
- **Zera** (זרע) — 'seed, children, offspring' — 'Their children shall see it and be glad.' The promise spans generations — the joy of restoration is not just for the current generation but for their descendants. This is covenant language (God's promises to Abraham's seed).
- **Gal** (גל) — 'to rejoice' — The same root as gil (spinning joy). The repetition of joy words emphasizes that restoration is ultimately about relationship, not just geography — God's people are filled with the joy of His presence.
