# 221 Maranatha! — 1 Corinthians 16:22

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#221](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oXpd0LXaYAA)

## Overview

Paul closes his first letter to the Corinthians with a uniquely Aramaic expression: 'Maranatha' — 'Our Lord, come!' This watchword of the early church combines a declaration of faith ('the Lord is coming') with a prayer of longing ('Come, Lord Jesus'). It is paired with a warning that those who do not love the Lord Jesus are accursed. The juxtaposition of anathema and maranatha captures the tension of Christian eschatology — the coming Lord is both the object of love and the judge of those who reject Him.

## Pauline Context

Maranatha (marana tha in Aramaic) is one of the few Aramaic expressions preserved in the Greek New Testament, demonstrating the early [[church]]'s Jewish worship traditions. Paul likely received this phrase from the Aramaic-speaking [[church]] in Jerusalem and passed it to his Greek-speaking congregations. The pairing of anathema (accursed) with maranatha (come, Lord) reflects Paul's understanding that the same coming of Christ brings salvation to those who love Him and judgment to those who reject Him. This echoes Paul's teaching in 2 Thessalonians 1:6-10 about the coming Lord bringing relief to the afflicted and vengeance to the disobedient. The prayer maranatha appears at the end of the Didache (an early Christian document) and in Revelation 22:20, showing its widespread use in early Christian liturgy.
