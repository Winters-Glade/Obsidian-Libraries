# 204 Believe It or Not — Acts 28:23-24

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#204](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7XMK0eUPNl0)

## Overview

Paul under house arrest in Rome calls together the local Jewish leaders. He reasons with them from morning to evening from the Law of Moses and the Prophets concerning Jesus. The result is a split: 'Some were persuaded by the things which were spoken, and some disbelieved.' This mirrors the pattern throughout Acts — the gospel goes to the Jew first, then to the Gentile. Paul quotes Isaiah 6:9-10, explaining the spiritual blindness of those who reject.

## 📜 Historical Background

Paul's arrival in Rome marked the fulfillment of the promise in Acts 23:11. He was under house arrest (custodia militaris) — a form of Roman custody that allowed the prisoner to rent private accommodation while chained day and night to a Roman soldier. This arrangement allowed him to receive visitors and preach 'with all confidence, without hindrance' (Acts 28:31). Rome's Jewish community was ancient and substantial — estimates range from 20,000 to 50,000 Jews living in the Trastevere district across the Tiber River. They had been expelled under Emperor Claudius (c. AD 49) but had returned after his death. Paul's citation of Isaiah 6:9-10 — the hardening prophecy — is the same passage Jesus used to explain why many rejected His parables (Mark 4:10-12).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul's quotation of Isaiah 6:9-10 ties the rejection of the gospel in Rome to the prophetic tradition of Israel's spiritual blindness. The same passage is quoted by Jesus (Mark 4:10-12), Paul (Romans 11:8), and John (John 12:39-41) — making it one of the most significant Old Testament citations in the New Testament. Romans 9-11 provides Paul's extended reflection on Jewish unbelief and Gentile inclusion: a partial hardening has come upon Israel until the fullness of the Gentiles has come in (Romans 11:25). Acts ends not with Paul's trial or martyrdom but with the unstoppable word — 'preaching the kingdom of God and teaching the things concerning the Lord Jesus Christ with all confidence, without hindrance' (Acts 28:31). The final word is not 'chains' but 'unhindered.'

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often noted that Acts ends on a note of paradox: Paul is in chains, but the word of God is not (2 Timothy 2:9). The response to the gospel is always divided — 'some were persuaded, and some disbelieved' — and Smith taught that believers should not be discouraged by mixed results. Paul's ministry ended not with a triumphant rally but with a divided meeting room. Yet the book closes with 'unhindered' (akolytos), signaling that the gospel's advance cannot be stopped. Smith emphasized that the same [[Holy Spirit]] who opened Lydia's heart (Acts 16:14) also permits some to disbelieve. Our responsibility is not to control the outcome but to faithfully present the message.
