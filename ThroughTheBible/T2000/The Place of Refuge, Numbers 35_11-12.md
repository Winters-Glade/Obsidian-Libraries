## 📜 Historical Background

Numbers 35 concludes the instructions for the inheritance of the tribes, particularly the Levites who received no tribal territory but 48 cities scattered throughout the land. Six of these — three on each side of the Jordan — were designated as 'cities of refuge' (Hebrew: *arei miklat*). These were for those who committed manslaughter (unintentional killing) to flee from the 'avenger of blood' — the nearest male relative of the deceased, responsible for exacting justice. The accused would stand trial before the congregation; if found innocent of intentional murder, they remained in the city of refuge until the death of the high priest, at which point they were free to return home. This system protected the innocent while upholding the sanctity of life.

**Hebrew Word Studies:**
- *Miklat* (מִקְלָט) — "refuge" or "asylum" (Numbers 35:6, 11-15). The root *kalat* (קלט) means to absorb, receive, or take in. A city of refuge was a place that "absorbed" the fugitive, receiving him into its protective boundaries. The noun appears only in the context of these cities. It conveys not just safety but welcome — the city actively receives the one who flees.
- *Goel ha-dam* (גֹּאֵל הַדָּם) — "avenger of blood" (Numbers 35:19, 21, 24-27). *Goel* is a rich legal term from the root *ga'al* (גאל), meaning to redeem, reclaim, or act as a kinsman-redeemer. The same word is used of Boaz, the kinsman-redeemer of Ruth (Ruth 3-4), and of God as the Redeemer of Israel (Isaiah 43:14). The *goel* had both the right and the duty to restore the family's honor and security. In the case of murder, the *goel ha-dam* was responsible for pursuing the killer to ensure justice was done. The system recognized both the sacredness of human life (made in God's image — Genesis 9:6) and the need for due process.
- *Rotze'ach* (רֹצֵחַ) — "murderer" or "manslayer" (Numbers 35:11). The same word covers both intentional murder and accidental killing; the context and trial determined which category applied. The cities existed precisely because the Hebrew language had one word for both — the legal distinction required procedural safeguards.
- *Mishpat* (מִשְׁפָּט) — "judgment" (Numbers 35:12) — the legal proceedings before the congregation. The accused could not simply claim refuge; he had to stand trial. The city of refuge was not a loophole for the guilty but a shield for the innocent.

**The Six Cities:** God designated three cities west of the Jordan (Kedesh in Galilee, Shechem in Samaria, Hebron in Judah) and three east of the Jordan (Bezer in Reuben, Ramoth-gilead in Gad, Golan in Manasseh). They were strategically located on major routes, evenly distributed so that no one in Israel was more than half a day's journey from safety. According to rabbinic tradition (Mishnah Makkot), the roads to the cities of refuge were kept broad and clear of obstacles, signposts at every crossroads read "Miklat! Miklat!" ("Refuge! Refuge!"), and two disciples of the sages were sent to escort the fugitive lest the avenger overtake him before reaching the city.

**ANE Context:** Asylum cities existed elsewhere in the ancient Near East — Egypt, Greece, and Rome had temples and sanctuaries where fugitives could claim protection. But Israel's system was unique in several ways: (1) It distinguished between intentional and unintentional killing through formal trial. (2) Refuge was not in a temple but in Levitical cities — integrating the fugitive into a community of those dedicated to God's service. (3) The refuge was time-limited by the death of the high priest — tying the fugitive's fate to the priest's life. (4) There was no refuge at all for the intentional murderer — the altar of the tabernacle itself could not protect him (Exodus 21:14). This prevented the sanctuary system from becoming a haven for the guilty.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The cities of refuge are a beautiful and multi-layered type of Christ. As the manslayer fled to the city of refuge for safety, the sinner flees to Christ for salvation.

**Christ as the City of Refuge:**
- The writer of Hebrews uses this imagery explicitly: 'That by two immutable things, in which it was impossible for God to lie, we who have fled for refuge might have strong consolation, who have fled for refuge to lay hold of the hope set before us' (Hebrews 6:18). The verb *katapheugo* (καταφεύγω) — "flee for refuge" — evokes the image of the manslayer sprinting toward the city gates.
- The **avenger of blood** is the Law, which pursues the sinner with the just penalty of death. 'The sting of death is sin, and the strength of sin is the law' (1 Corinthians 15:56). The Law, like the *goel ha-dam*, demands justice for the life that has been taken — our sin has brought death, and the Law pursues us.
- The **city of refuge** is Christ, the place where the pursued sinner finds safety. 'There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus' (Romans 8:1). In Him, the avenger cannot touch us.
- The **death of the high priest** sets the fugitive free. In the type, the manslayer remained confined until the sitting high priest died. In the antitype, Christ — our great High Priest — died once for all (Hebrews 9:11-12, 28). His death does not merely mark the end of a term; it procures eternal redemption. When Christ died, the penalty for sin was fully paid, and the prisoner is set free forever.
- The **Levitical cities** were scattered throughout the land, accessible to all — as Christ is accessible to all who call upon Him: 'For whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved' (Romans 10:13).

**Christ as the Goel (Kinsman-Redeemer):**
The same word *goel* describes both the avenger of blood and the Redeemer. Christ is the ultimate *Goel ha-dam* — but in a paradoxical reversal. As our Kinsman-Redeemer, He does not pursue us for vengeance but redeems us from the debt we owe. He steps into our bloodline (the Incarnation), pays the price of our freedom (the cross), and restores our inheritance (resurrection). Boaz redeemed Ruth; Christ redeems His church.

Paul writes that believers are 'hid with Christ in God' (Colossians 3:3). As long as the manslayer remained within the city, he was safe. So the believer who abides in Christ is secure from the judgment that the Law demands. To leave the city was to die; to leave Christ is to be exposed to the full penalty of sin.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often used the cities of refuge to illustrate the security of salvation. The manslayer was safe as long as he remained within the city's boundaries. The moment he left, he was vulnerable. In the same way, our safety is in Christ. We cannot stray outside of Him and expect protection. But within Him, we are secure. The death of the High Priest set the manslayer free — and our High Priest, Christ, died to set us free eternally.

This passage speaks powerfully to those burdened by guilt — including the guilt of genuine failures and sins committed unintentionally or in weakness. The cities of refuge were for those who killed *unawares* (Numbers 35:11, 22-23) — without malice or premeditation. How many of us carry guilt for sins we committed in ignorance, in moments of weakness, or in situations that spiraled beyond our control? Christ is the refuge for such as these. The same grace that covers willful sin repented of also covers the sins we never fully understood.

The illustration of the broad, well-marked roads to the cities of refuge is a picture of the accessibility of the gospel. No obstacle should stand between a sinner and their refuge. The church must be like the roads to the cities — clear, well-maintained, and well-marked with signs that say "Refuge! Refuge! This way to Christ!"

But this passage also warns against presumption. The intentional murderer found no refuge. There is no refuge in Christ for those who willfully persist in sin without repentance. The cities of refuge protected the innocent from vengeance, not the guilty from justice. Hebrews 10:26-27 echoes this: 'For if we sin willfully after that we have received the knowledge of the truth, there remaineth no more sacrifice for sins, but a certain fearful looking for of judgment.' The grace of God is not a license to sin but a refuge from its penalty and power.

Finally, the cities of refuge were Levitical cities — cities dedicated to the service of God. Safety was found not in isolation but in the community of God's servants. The fugitive was brought into proximity with the tabernacle, the priesthood, and the worship of God. So the believer who flees to Christ is not merely saved *from* judgment but saved *to* a community of worship and service — the body of Christ, the church.
