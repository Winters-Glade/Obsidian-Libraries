## Summary

Proverbs 9 presents two invitations: [[Wisdom]]'s banquet and [[Folly]]'s feast. 'The [[fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of [[wisdom]]' is the thesis of the entire book, calling the simple to choose the path of [[Righteousness]] and life.

## 📜 Historical Background

Proverbs 9 is the climax of the introductory section (chapters 1-9). Wisdom and Folly are personified as women who prepare feasts and invite the simple. Wisdom offers nourishing bread and wine; Folly offers stolen water — appealing but deadly.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus invites: 'I am the bread of life' (John 6:35). The contrast between Wisdom's feast and Folly's meal parallels the narrow and broad roads (Matthew 7:13-14).

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Everyone is invited to one of two banquets. [[Wisdom]]'s feast requires humility. [[Folly]]'s feast requires nothing but leads to death. The beginning of [[wisdom]] is making the right choice.

## 📚 Hebrew Roots & Wisdom Context

Proverbs 9 presents the climax of chapters 1-9 through the metaphor of **two invitation feasts**. [[Wisdom]]'s house has **seven pillars** — a detail suggesting completeness (creation week, perfection). The Hebrew *reshit* (רֵאשִׁית, "beginning") means both first in time and first in importance — the foundational principle. This verse is the thesis statement of the entire book. The personification of [[Wisdom]] in Proverbs 8-9 is a high point of Hebrew poetry.

## 📖 Research Context

### Wisdom's Banquet and Folly's Feast

Proverbs 9 presents the climax of the **Wisdom Poems** (chapters 1-9), contrasting two invitations:
- **Lady Wisdom** (vv. 1-6): She has built her house with seven pillars, prepared a feast, and invites the simple: "Come, eat of my bread, and drink of the wine which I have mingled"
- **Lady Folly** (vv. 13-18): She sits at her door, calls to passersby offering stolen water and secret bread — but "the dead are there"

The structural symmetry is deliberate: both women call from the heights, both address the "simple" (*peti* פֶּתִי). Wisdom offers life; Folly offers death. The "seven pillars" of Wisdom's house (v. 1) may refer to perfection or completeness.

### The Refrain of Wisdom

Verse 10 — "The [[fear of the Lord]] is the beginning of [[wisdom]]: and the knowledge of the holy is [[_Concepts/Doctrines/understanding]]" — echoes Proverbs 1:7 and forms the theological center of chapters 1-9. The Hebrew *kedoshim* (קְדֹשִׁים) is a plural of majesty pointing to God's transcendent holiness.

### Christological Fulfillment

Wisdom's invitation is taken up by Jesus: "Come unto Me, all ye that labour... take My yoke upon you" (Matthew 11:28-29). Paul identifies Christ as "the wisdom of God" (1 Corinthians 1:24). Wisdom's feast finds its ultimate expression in the marriage supper of the Lamb (Revelation 19:9).
