# 210 The Riches of His Glory — Romans 9:19-23

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#210](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-k6FjwQRgs)

## Overview

Paul defends God's sovereign right to show mercy on whom He will. Using the potter and the clay metaphor from Jeremiah 18, he argues that God endures with much longsuffering the vessels of wrath fitted for destruction, while making known the riches of His glory on vessels of mercy prepared for glory. This passage is part of Paul's extended treatment of Israel's rejection and God's sovereign purposes in history (Romans 9-11). Paul does not present divine sovereignty as cold determinism but as the ground of mercy — God's right to choose is exercised in patience and compassion toward those who deserve only judgment.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Sovereignty | God's absolute right as Creator to order His creation according to His will — the potter has power over the clay |
| Glory | The ultimate purpose of all things — God makes known the riches of His glory through vessels of mercy |
| Mercy | God's sovereign mercy is the only hope for sinners; He is not obligated to save anyone, yet He chooses to save many |
| Election | God's sovereign choice in salvation, grounded not in human merit but in His own purpose and grace given in Christ before time |
| Divine Patience | God endures vessels of wrath with much longsuffering, demonstrating both His justice and the wealth of His mercy |

## Pauline Context

Romans 9-11 forms the most sustained theological treatment of Israel and God's plan in the New Testament. Paul's argument unfolds in three movements: God's sovereign election (Romans 9), Israel's present unbelief (Romans 10), and God's ultimate plan for Israel's salvation (Romans 11). The potter/clay analogy (from Jeremiah 18) is Paul's definitive answer to the charge of injustice in divine election — the Creator has rights the creature cannot claim. Yet Paul's purpose is not to provoke despair but to magnify mercy: if God owes mercy to none, then His mercy to any is astonishing grace. The passage anticipates Romans 11:32, where Paul concludes that God has consigned all to disobedience that He might have mercy on all.
