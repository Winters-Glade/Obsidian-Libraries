## Summary

[[Jeremiah 50_4-6.md]] speaks of Israel and Judah coming together, weeping as they seek the Lord their God, asking the way to Zion with their faces toward it: 'Come, and let us join ourselves to the Lord in a perpetual covenant that shall not be forgotten.' But the passage also describes God's people as 'lost sheep' whose shepherds have led them astray. Pastor Chuck explores the journey of finding our true resting place in God.

## 📜 Historical Background

[[Jeremiah 50.md]] is part of a series of [[Prophecy|oracles]] against [[Babylon]]. In the midst of [[Judgment]] on Babylon, God inserts a word of [[Restoration|hope]] for His own people, preserving a [[Remnant]]. The picture of Israel and Judah together — the divided kingdom reunited — points to a future [[Restoration]] and ultimately to the [[Messiah]] who would gather His flock. 'Come, and let us join ourselves to the Lord in a perpetual covenant' echoes the language of the new covenant promised in [[_Bible/Jeremiah/Jeremiah 31_31-34.md]]. The 'resting place' is Zion (Jerusalem), but it is also the spiritual rest found in covenant relationship with God. The mention of shepherds leading the flock astray indicts the spiritual leaders who had failed their calling.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Jesus declared Himself the [[John 10_11-16.md]], who gathers His sheep from every fold and makes them one flock under one Shepherd. The 'perpetual covenant' Jeremiah speaks of finds its ultimate expression in the new covenant sealed by Christ's blood ([[Hebrews 13_20-21.md]]). [[Hebrews 4_9-11.md]] speaks of a 'rest' that remains for the people of God — not a geographical place but a rest of faith in Christ's finished work. [[1 Peter 2_25.md]] describes believers as having returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of our souls.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that every human heart is searching for a resting place — a place of peace, security, and belonging. We try many things (success, relationships, possessions) but true rest is found only in God. Like Israel and Judah, we must turn our faces toward Zion — toward God — and seek the way home. The good news is that God is waiting with open arms. The journey back may involve tears of repentance, but it leads to the joy of covenant relationship. And the Shepherd is always seeking His lost sheep.
