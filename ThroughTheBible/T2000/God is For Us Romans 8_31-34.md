# 208 God is For Us — Romans 8:31-34

> **Series:** T2000 Topical Series | **Pastor:** Chuck Smith | **Video:** [#208](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0CtWzAa-2TE)

## Overview

One of the most triumphant passages in Scripture asks: 'If God be for us, who can be against us?' Paul declares the unbreakable chain of salvation from divine foreknowledge to final glorification. Nothing can separate believers from the love of God which is in Christ Jesus our Lord. This passage is the climactic conclusion of Romans 8 — the great chapter on life in the Spirit and the believer's security. Paul's series of rhetorical questions demolishes every possible ground for fear: God is for us, Christ died for us, Christ intercedes for us, and nothing in all creation can separate us from His love.

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Assurance | The confident knowledge that God is on the believer's side, grounded in His sovereign purpose and demonstrated love |
| Security | The believer's eternal safety rests not on human performance but on the finished work of Christ and the unbreakable love of God |
| Justification | If God declares the believer righteous, no opposing accusation can stand — "It is God who justifies" is the ultimate defense |
| Intercession | Christ's present ministry at the right hand of the Father — He lives to make intercession for His people |
| No Condemnation | The triumphant conclusion of Paul's argument: there is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus |

## Pauline Context

Romans 8:31-39 is the doxological conclusion of the great section on [[Sanctification]] and assurance (Romans 5-8). The five rhetorical questions form a legal courtroom drama: Who can be against us? Who will bring a charge? Who will condemn? Who will separate us? The answer to each is a resounding "no one" because of Christ. The golden chain of Romans 8:29-30 — foreknew, predestined, called, justified, glorified — shows that salvation from start to finish is God's work, guaranteeing its completion. This passage mirrors Paul's confidence in Philippians 1:6 that He who began a good work will complete it. The list of potential separators (tribulation, distress, persecution, famine, nakedness, peril, sword) echoes Paul's own sufferings (2 Corinthians 11:23-28) and testifies that none of them can sever the believer from Christ's love.
