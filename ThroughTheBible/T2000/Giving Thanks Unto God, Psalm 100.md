## Summary

Psalm 100 is the classic call to [[Thanksgiving]]: 'Make a joyful noise unto the Lord, all ye lands.' A brief but powerful invitation to enter God's presence with gratitude, acknowledging Him as our Creator and Shepherd, and celebrating His everlasting mercy.

## 📜 Historical Background

Psalm 100 concludes the collection of 'royal psalms' (93-100) celebrating God's kingship. It was likely used in temple worship as a processional hymn. Despite its brevity, it contains the essence of true worship: the Lord is God, we are His people, and His mercy is everlasting.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul commands believers to 'give thanks in everything' ([[1 Thessalonians 5_18.md]]) and to be 'giving thanks always for all things' ([[Ephesians 5_20.md]]). Thanksgiving is not merely a response to blessing but a discipline that opens our hearts to God's presence.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith taught that gratitude is the gateway to God's presence. 'Enter into His gates with [[Thanksgiving]]' — we approach with grateful hearts. When we count our blessings, our perspective shifts from our problems to His provision.

## 📚 Hebrew Poetry & Worship Context

Psalm 100 is the climax of the **Enthronement Psalms** (93-100) celebrating Yahweh as King. The key Hebrew word is *todah* (תּוֹדָה), "thanksgiving" — carrying the richer meaning of confession, praise, and thank-offering. This is a **processional psalm**, likely sung as worshippers ascended the Temple Mount. The three affirmations — God as Creator ("He has made us"), God as Shepherd ("we are His people"), God as Covenant-Keeper ("His mercy is everlasting") — encapsulate Israel's theology of worship.

**Worship Significance:** The quintessential call to worship, it reminds worshippers that access to God is grounded in His character, not their performance. "Enter into His gates with thanksgiving" — gratitude is the gateway to God's presence.


## 📖 Research Context

### Psalm 100 — The Todah Liturgy

Psalm 100 is a **thanksgiving psalm** designed for liturgical entrance into the Temple. The Hebrew *todah* (תּוֹדָה) — "thanksgiving" — shares a root with *yadah* (יָדָה, "to give thanks") and *Yehudah* (Judah). A *todah* offering was presented in the Temple ([[Leviticus 7_12-15.md]]) accompanied by songs of gratitude.

The psalm follows an **entrance liturgy** pattern (similar to Psalms 15, 24, and 95):
1. **Call to [[praise]]** — &#x22;Make a joyful noise&#x22; (v. 1)
2. **Imperatives** — Serve, come, know, enter, be thankful, bless (vv. 2-4)
3. **Reason for praise** — "For the LORD is good" (v. 5)

### Hebrew Imperatives

- **Hari'u** (הָרִיעוּ) — "Make a joyful noise" — a battle cry or shout of triumph
- **De'u** (דְּעוּ) — "Know" — intimate, experiential knowledge (*da'at*)
- **Hodu** (הוֹדוּ) — "Give thanks" — also means "confess," the attitude of humility

### The Eternal Attributes

Verse 5 presents three timeless attributes: **good** (*tov*), **everlasting mercy** (*chesed*), and **truth** (*emunah* — faithfulness) to all generations. This psalm has been a staple of Christian liturgy for centuries.
