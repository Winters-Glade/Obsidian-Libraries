## 📜 Historical Background

Exodus 12 records the institution of the Passover, the defining event of Israel's national identity — one that would be commemorated annually for over three millennia. On the night of the tenth plague — the death of the firstborn — God provided a way of deliverance through the blood of a lamb. The Hebrew word **פֶּסַח** (*pesach*) means "to pass over" or "to spare," deriving from the verb **פָּסַח** (*pasach* — "to skip, to pass over, to spare"). This is not the normal Hebrew word for "to pass through" (עבר, *'avar*), which intensifies the theological nuance: God does not merely pass through but actively *spares* those covered by the blood.

The Passover lamb had specific requirements that demand attention. It was to be:
- **A lamb without blemish** (Exodus 12:5) — the Hebrew **תָּמִים** (*tamim*) denotes physical perfection, anticipating the sinless Christ
- **A male of the first year** — representing the prime and vigor of life
- **Selected on the 10th day and slain on the 14th** — a four-day inspection period, mirroring Christ's examination before His crucifixion
- **Slain "between the evenings"** (בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, *bein ha'arbayim*) — the twilight hours, the same time Jesus died (John 19:14-30)
- **Its blood applied to the doorposts and lintel** — the hyssop branch used for application (Exodus 12:22) had purgative associations in ANE ritual and was later used at Christ's crucifixion (John 19:29)

Archaeologically, the Passover finds no exact parallel in ANE religious practice, which sets it apart as a unique revelation. While Mesopotamian apotropaic rituals (e.g., the *namburbi* incantations) used blood to ward off evil spirits, the Passover blood was not magical but *relational* — it was a sign of obedience and faith in Yahweh's word. The blood did not placate a capricious deity but marked those who trusted God's provision.

The eating of unleavened bread (מַצָּה, *matzah*) signified both the haste of departure (there was no time for leavening) and, symbolically, the removal of sin — leaven representing corruption and pride throughout Jewish tradition (cf. the *bedikat chametz* ceremony preceding Passover). The bitter herbs (מְרֹרִים, *marorim*) recalled the bitterness of slavery, ensuring the ritual was not merely ceremonial but experiential, connecting each generation to the suffering of their ancestors.

The Passover became the foundation of Israel's calendar: "This month shall be unto you the beginning of months" (Exodus 12:2). The month of Abib (later Nisan, March-April) was reset as the first month of the religious year, making the exodus the orienting event of Israel's sacred time. This calendrical transformation parallels how the resurrection of Christ reoriented Christian worship to the first day of the week (Acts 20:7; Revelation 1:10).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

The Passover is the most direct and elaborate Old Testament type of Christ's crucifixion. The parallels are exhaustive and intentional:

- **Selection on the 10th** → Christ's triumphal entry (10th of Nisan) followed by public examination
- **Without blemish** → Christ "who did no sin, neither was guile found in His mouth" (1 Peter 2:22; 1:19)
- **Blood applied** → The blood of Christ "cleanses us from all sin" (1 John 1:7)
- **Not a bone broken** (Exodus 12:46) → Fulfilled in Christ's crucifixion (John 19:33-36)
- **Eaten with unleavened bread** → Christ, the Bread of Life (John 6:35), without the leaven of malice (1 Corinthians 5:8)

John the Baptist's declaration — "Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world!" (John 1:29) — introduces Jesus using the Passover lamb as the controlling metaphor for His mission. Paul makes the direct identification explicit: "Christ our Passover is sacrificed for us" (1 Corinthians 5:7). The Last Supper was a Passover meal (the *seder*), and Jesus transformed the traditional elements — the *afikoman* (the middle matzah, broken, hidden, and brought back) and the third cup (the Cup of Redemption) — to point to His body and blood.

The timing of Christ's crucifixion during Passover week was divinely orchestrated. He was crucified on the 14th of Nisan, at the same hour the Passover lambs were being slaughtered in the Temple courts. As the Mishnah (*Pesachim* 5:3) describes the temple procedure, the priests slaughtered the lambs in three shifts — and Christ, the true Lamb, died at the ninth hour (3 PM), the time the evening lambs began to be offered. Every detail converges to demonstrate that "the Lamb slain from the foundation of the world" (Revelation 13:8) was foreshadowed in the Egyptian Passover.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that the Passover teaches a crucial truth: judgment is universal, but the blood provides a covering. Every house in Egypt either faced the judgment or was covered by the blood. There was no middle ground. In the same way, every person stands under the judgment of sin, but the blood of Christ provides the only covering.

The application matters profoundly — the blood had to be applied, not merely understood or admired. A lamb slain was not enough; the blood had to be on the doorposts. Salvation is not merely intellectual assent to the facts of the gospel; it is the personal application of Christ's blood to the "doorposts" of one's life through faith. As Chrysostom preached: "The blood of the lamb was the safeguard of the houses; the sign of the cross is the safeguard of our souls."

Furthermore, the Passover was eaten in haste — "with your loins girded, your shoes on your feet, and your staff in your hand" (Exodus 12:11). This speaks to the urgency of salvation and the posture of readiness that should mark the believer's life. We are not to linger in Egypt but to be ready to move when God says move. The Christian life is a pilgrimage, not a settlement.

Yet there is also a communal dimension: the lamb was shared among families, and those without a household were to join with neighbors (Exodus 12:4). The Passover was never meant to be eaten alone — and neither is the Christian life. We are called into community, sharing the Lamb together, remembering that our deliverance is both personal and corporate.
