## Summary

Luke 4:1-13 records Jesus' temptation in the wilderness following His baptism. Led by the Spirit into the wilderness, Jesus fasted forty days and was tempted by the devil. Pastor Chuck examines how Jesus overcame each temptation through the Word of God, demonstrating the pattern for spiritual victory. The temptations targeted Jesus' identity, authority, and trust in the Father's plan.

## 📖 Research Context

The Synoptic placement of the temptation immediately after Jesus' baptism is theologically significant. The Father's declaration 'This is My beloved Son' (Luke 3:22) is immediately tested by Satan's 'If You are the Son of God' (Luke 4:3). Jesus relives Israel's wilderness experience — forty days corresponding to Israel's forty years — and succeeds where they failed. Each of Jesus' three quotations comes from Deuteronomy (8:3, 6:16, 6:13), passages where Israel had failed in their own testing. The temptations also mirror Adam's fall in Eden: the lust of the flesh, the lust of the eyes, and the pride of life (1 John 2:16). Where the first Adam fell, the second Adam triumphs (Romans 5:12-19; 1 Corinthians 15:45-49).

Key Old Testament cross-references: Exodus 16 (manna and testing), Exodus 17:1-7 (Massah — testing the Lord), Numbers 14 (Israel's failure in the wilderness), Deuteronomy 8:1-3 (manna taught dependence on God's Word).

## 📜 Historical Background

Jesus' forty-day fast in the wilderness echoes Israel's forty years of wandering. Where Israel failed in testing, Jesus succeeded. The three temptations correspond to the three areas of 1 John 2:16 — the lust of the flesh (stones to bread), the lust of the eyes (kingdoms of the world), and the pride of life (temple pinnacle). Jesus' responses are all from Deuteronomy 8:3, 6:16, and 6:13 — the very passages Israel failed to live by in their wilderness testing.

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Hebrews 4:15 assures us that Jesus was tempted in all points as we are, yet without sin. 1 Corinthians 10:13 promises that God will provide a way of escape in temptation. James 1:12 promises the crown of life to those who endure temptation. Romans 5:18-19 draws the contrast between Adam and Christ — through one man's trespass judgment came to all, but through one act of righteousness justification brings life to all. 1 Corinthians 10:1-13 uses Israel's wilderness failures explicitly as our example — 'these things happened to them as examples, and they were written for our instruction.' Jesus' victory in the wilderness prefigures His ultimate victory over Satan through the cross and resurrection. Ephesians 6:17 calls the Word of God the 'sword of the Spirit' — the same weapon Jesus used.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith emphasized that Jesus didn't use His divine power to overcome temptation — He used the Word, which is available to every believer. The pattern is: the Spirit leads, the Word defends, and obedience follows. Temptation often comes at our points of strength, not weakness, and targets our identity and calling. We overcome not by engaging Satan in debate but by declaring what God has said.
