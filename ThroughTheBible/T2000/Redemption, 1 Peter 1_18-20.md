## Summary

Peter reminds believers that they were not redeemed with corruptible things like silver and gold from their empty way of life handed down from their ancestors, but with the precious blood of Christ, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot. Christ was foreordained before the foundation of the world but was revealed in these last times for our sake.

## 📜 Historical Background

First Peter was written to believers facing persecution and suffering — likely the early stages of Nero's persecution (c. AD 64-68), when Christians were being scapegoated for the burning of Rome and subjected to horrific tortures. Peter writes from 'Babylon' (likely a cipher for Rome, 1 Peter 5:13) to the scattered churches of Asia Minor. The letter is structured as a baptismal homily or liturgy, moving from the new birth (1:3) through holy living and suffering well to final exhortations. The term 'redemption' (Greek: *lytroo*, λυτρόω / *apolytrosis*, ἀπολύτρωσις) carries the specific weight of the Greco-Roman slave market — a price paid to secure freedom for a slave. Silver and gold were the currency of daily commerce, yet they could not purchase spiritual liberation. The Passover lamb imagery (Exodus 12:5 — a lamb without blemish) and the sacrificial system form the Old Testament backdrop. Christ's blood is 'precious' (Greek: *timios*, τίμιος — valuable, costly, held in honor). The phrase 'foreordained before the foundation of the world' (Greek: *proginosko*, προγινώσκω — foreknown) reveals that the cross was no divine afterthought but the centerpiece of God's eternal plan, echoing the concept of the Lamb 'slain from the foundation of the world' (Revelation 13:8).

## ✝️ New Testament Connection

Paul declares that we are 'bought with a price' (1 Corinthians 6:20) and that 'in whom we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins' (Ephesians 1:7). John the Baptist identified Jesus as 'the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world' (John 1:29). The concept of Christ being foreordained before the foundation of the world connects to Revelation 13:8, where He is called 'the Lamb slain from the foundation of the world.' Ephesians 1:4 says we were chosen in Him before the foundation of the world — our redemption was always God's plan. The Old Testament types converge here: the Passover lamb without blemish (Exodus 12:5), the sin offering without defect (Leviticus 4:32-35), and Isaiah's suffering servant 'brought as a lamb to the slaughter' (Isaiah 53:7). The writer of Hebrews makes the same point — Christ 'through the eternal Spirit offered himself without spot to God' (Hebrews 9:14), securing eternal redemption. Peter's own earlier confession — 'Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God' (Matthew 16:16) — frames his understanding of the precious blood he now proclaims. Paul reminds Titus that Christ 'gave himself for us, that he might redeem us from all iniquity' (Titus 2:14), and the heavenly song in Revelation declares: 'Thou wast slain, and hast redeemed us to God by thy blood' (Revelation 5:9).

## 🔍 Further Context

**Faith and Perseverance Theme:** Peter's description of redemption connects directly to the theme of perseverance by grounding it in the eternal purpose of God. The phrase "foreordained before the foundation of the world" (v. 20) uses the Greek *proginosko* (foreknown) — the cross was not a divine afterthought but the centerpiece of God's eternal plan. The "blood of Christ, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot" (v. 19) echoes the Passover lamb (Exodus 12:5) and the daily sacrifices (Leviticus 22:20-21). The Greek *lytroo* (redeem) specifically evokes the slave market — a price paid to secure freedom. The "empty way of life" (*mataias anastrophes*, "vain conversation") contrasts the futile, wandering existence of pre-conversion days with the purposeful life of faith.

**Cross-Batch Connections:** The Passover typology connects to [[_MOC_Batch2_Exodus_Wilderness|Batch 2 — Exodus & Deuteronomy]] (the Passover lamb, Exodus 12:1-13; the exodus redemption from Egypt). The "Lamb of God" declaration of John the Baptist (John 1:29) connects to [[_MOC_Batch10_Luke_John|Batch 10 — The Gospel of John]] and [[_MOC_Batch9_Matthew_Mark|Batch 9 — Matthew & Mark]]. Paul's declaration "bought with a price" (1 Corinthians 6:20, from [[_MOC_Batch12_Romans_Corinthians|Batch 12]]) echoes the same redemption theme. The "Lamb slain from the foundation of the world" (Revelation 13:8) in [[_transcripts/Overcoming The Enemy, Revelation 12_7-12.md|268 — Overcoming The Enemy]] brings the theme to its eschatological fulfillment. The testing-of-faith context (1 Peter 1:6-7) connects to [[_transcripts/Temptation, James 1_2-4.md|256 — Temptation (James 1:2-4)]], where trials test the genuineness of faith.

## 💡 Pastoral Application

Chuck Smith often stressed the costliness of our salvation. If silver and gold could have redeemed us, God could have provided that—He owns all the silver and gold. But only the precious blood of Christ was sufficient. Our redemption was not an afterthought or a Plan B—it was planned before the world began. This should give us security and inspire worship. The empty way of life we were saved from contrasts with the abundant life we are saved to.
