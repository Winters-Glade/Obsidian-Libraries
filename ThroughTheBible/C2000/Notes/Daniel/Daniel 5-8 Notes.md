# Daniel 5-8 — Notes

## Summary

**Daniel 5 — Belshazzar's Feast.** The narrative leaps forward to the final night of the Babylonian Empire. Belshazzar, grandson of Nebuchadnezzar (and son of Nabonidus, the last king of Babylon), holds a great feast for a thousand nobles. In a blasphemous act, he commands that the gold and silver vessels taken from the Jerusalem temple be brought so that he and his guests may drink from them, praising the gods of gold, silver, brass, iron, wood, and stone. Suddenly a hand appears and writes on the palace wall: "MENE, MENE, TEKEL, UPHARSIN." None of the wise men can interpret it. The queen remembers Daniel, who is summoned. Daniel rebukes Belshazzar for his pride — he knew what happened to Nebuchadnezzar yet did not humble himself — and interprets the writing: God has numbered (MENE) the kingdom and finished it; Belshazzar has been weighed (TEKEL) and found wanting; the kingdom is divided (PERES/UPHARSIN) and given to the Medes and Persians. That very night, Belshazzar is slain, and Darius the Mede takes the kingdom. Pastor Chuck Smith highlights how archaeological discoveries, such as the Nabonidus Cylinder and Sir Rawlinson's excavations, confirmed the existence of Belshazzar — whom critics had long claimed was fictional since his name was absent from standard king lists.

**Daniel 6 — Daniel in the Lion's Den.** Under King Darius, Daniel is appointed one of three presidents over the 120 satraps. His excellence provokes jealousy. The other officials trick Darius into signing an irrevocable decree that anyone who petitions any god or man other than the king for thirty days shall be cast into the lions' den. Daniel continues praying toward Jerusalem three times a day as was his custom. He is discovered, and the king — distressed, having been trapped by his own law — is forced to cast Daniel to the lions. Daniel's trust, however, is vindicated: God sends His angel to shut the lions' mouths. Darius issues a decree that all must fear the God of Daniel.

**Daniel 7 — Vision of the Four Beasts.** The second half of the book transitions from historical narrative to apocalyptic vision. In the first year of Belshazzar, Daniel sees four great beasts emerging from the sea: a lion with eagle's wings (Babylon), a bear with three ribs in its mouth (Medo-Persia), a four-headed leopard with four wings (Greece under Alexander and his four successors), and a terrifying fourth beast with iron teeth and ten horns (Rome), from which arises a "little horn" (the Antichrist). The vision then shifts to the throne room of the Ancient of Days, whose judgment destroys the beast. One like the Son of Man comes with the clouds and is given an everlasting dominion. Pastor Chuck Smith emphasizes that the "saints" who are overcome by the little horn are redeemed Israel in the Tribulation, not the Church — because Christ promised the gates of hell would not prevail against His Church. The vision concludes with the saints possessing the kingdom.

**Daniel 8 — Vision of the Ram and Goat.** In the third year of Belshazzar, Daniel sees a ram with two horns pushing westward, northward, and southward (the Medo-Persian Empire). A male goat with a notable horn between its eyes charges from the west, destroying the ram (Alexander the Great's conquest of Persia). The great horn breaks off, and four horns arise in its place (the division of Alexander's empire into four parts). From one of these comes a "little horn" that grows exceedingly great, taking away the daily sacrifice and casting truth to the ground (Antiochus Epiphanes, a type of the coming Antichrist). The vision covers 2,300 days of desolation. Gabriel is commanded to make Daniel understand the vision.

## Key Themes

- **[[Divine Judgment]]** — God's judgment falls on the proud: Belshazzar's kingdom ends in a single night
- **[[Faithfulness Under Pressure]]** — Daniel's unwavering prayer life despite the king's decree
- **[[Divine Protection]]** — The angel shuts the mouths of lions; the fourth man in the furnace
- **[[Sovereignty of God]]** — God raises and deposes kings according to His timeline
- **[[Prophecy Timeline]]** — The four beasts parallel the four metals of Nebuchadnezzar's dream

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Daniel 5-8 spans the transition from Babylonian to Persian rule. The fall of Babylon in 539 BC is one of the best-attested events of antiquity. The Nabonidus Cylinder describes how Cyrus's forces entered Babylon without battle. Belshazzar's feast likely coincided with the night the Persians diverted the Euphrates River and marched under the city walls. The identification of Belshazzar — long a point of critical attack — was vindicated by cuneiform tablets discovered by Sir Henry Rawlinson, which showed Belshazzar was the son of King Nabonidus and served as co-regent. This explains why Belshazzar could offer only the "third" highest position in the kingdom (Daniel 5:16) — he himself was second.

Daniel's visionary chapters (7-8) mark a shift from historical narrative to apocalyptic literature, a genre that flourished in the Persian and Hellenistic periods. The four-empire schema (Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, Rome) became a standard framework for Jewish apocalyptic thought and is found in later writings such as 4 Ezra and 2 Baruch. Antiochus Epiphanes (reigned 175-164 BC), the little horn of Daniel 8, desecrated the Jerusalem temple and outlawed Jewish worship, events commemorated in the festival of Hanukkah and described in 1 and 2 Maccabees.
