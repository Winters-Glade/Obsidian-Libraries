## Summary

**Daniel 9 — Daniel's Prayer and the Seventy Weeks Prophecy.** In the first year of Darius the Mede, Daniel is reading the scroll of Jeremiah (Jeremiah 25:11-12; 29:10) and understands that the seventy years of Jerusalem's desolation are nearly complete. He responds not with celebration but with intense prayer, fasting, sackcloth, and ashes — a profound prayer of confession, identifying himself with the sins of all Israel: "We have sinned and committed iniquity... To the Lord our God belong mercies and forgivenesses, though we have rebelled against Him." While Daniel is still praying, the angel Gabriel appears at the time of the evening oblation and gives him "skill and understanding."

Gabriel then delivers the most detailed messianic prophecy in the Old Testament — the Seventy Weeks (seventy "sevens" of years, i.e., 490 years). The prophecy declares:

- **69 weeks (483 years)** from the decree to restore and rebuild Jerusalem (the decree of Artaxerxes in 445 BC) until "Messiah the Prince" appears.
- After the 69 weeks, "Messiah shall be cut off, but not for Himself," and the city and sanctuary will be destroyed (fulfilled in AD 70).
- A gap (parenthesis) occurs between the 69th and 70th week, during which the Church age is unfolded — a mystery not revealed to Daniel.
- The **70th week** (seven years) is yet future, when a prince (the Antichrist) will make a covenant with many for one week, then break it in the middle, causing the abomination of desolation and great tribulation.

Pastor Chuck Smith follows the dispensational interpretation: the 69 weeks culminated in Christ's triumphal entry; the interval between the 69th and 70th week is the Church age, which Daniel did not see; and the final week is the Tribulation period described in Revelation.

**Daniel 10 — The Vision of the Man in Linen.** In the third year of Cyrus, Daniel mourns for three weeks and receives a final vision. A glorious being appears — a "man clothed in linen, whose loins were girded with fine gold of Uphaz" — a vision of such overwhelming splendor that Daniel's companions flee and Daniel himself loses all strength. The angel explains that he was delayed for 21 days by the "prince of the kingdom of Persia" (a demonic power opposing God's purposes) until Michael, one of the chief princes (the archangel), came to help him. This passage provides extraordinary insight into the reality of spiritual warfare behind earthly events. The angel then declares he must return to fight against the prince of Persia, and afterwards the prince of Greece will come — indicating that the rise and fall of empires is shaped by spiritual conflict.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Daniel 9 is set in approximately 539-538 BC, shortly after Babylon fell to the Medes and Persians. Daniel, now in his 80s, has been in captivity his entire adult life. He turns to Jeremiah's writings — which he evidently possessed in Babylon — and calculates that the 70 years of captivity prophesied by Jeremiah are drawing to a close. The decree to rebuild Jerusalem mentioned in the Seventy Weeks prophecy is widely identified with the decree of Artaxerxes Longimanus to Nehemiah in 445 BC (Nehemiah 2:1-8), though some scholars point to earlier decrees by Cyrus (538 BC) or Darius I (520 BC).

The Seventy Weeks prophecy is one of the most scrutinized passages in biblical literature. The 69 weeks = 483 prophetic years (using a 360-day year) from 445 BC brings us to approximately AD 32-33, the time of Christ's crucifixion. The "gap" or "parenthesis" between the 69th and 70th week is a cornerstone of dispensational eschatology. The final week (seven years) is identified with the Tribulation period described in Revelation 6-19, with the Antichrist's covenant confirming Daniel 9:27.

Daniel 10's depiction of spiritual warfare — angelic princes representing nations — finds echoes in the Dead Sea Scrolls, where the Community Rule speaks of the "Prince of Light" and the "Angel of Darkness" contending for humanity. This passage is foundational for understanding the Pauline concept of "principalities and powers" (Ephesians 6:12).
