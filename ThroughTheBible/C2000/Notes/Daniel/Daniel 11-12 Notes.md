## Summary

**Daniel 11 — The Kings of the North and South.** This chapter contains one of the most detailed prophecies in Scripture, describing the succession of empires and rulers from the Persian period through the Hellenistic era and culminating in the end-time Antichrist. Pastor Chuck Smith emphasizes that critics have long attacked Daniel 11, claiming it was written after the events (ex eventu prophecy), but notes that the Septuagint translation (c. 220 BC) already contained Daniel's prophecies, predating the events critics claim it was written after.

The prophecy unfolds in layers:

- **Three more Persian kings** (Cambyses, Pseudo-Smerdis, Darius Hystaspes) followed by Xerxes (Ahasuerus), who invades Greece.
- **Alexander the Great** (the "mighty king") arises, but his kingdom is divided toward the four winds after his death.
- The **king of the South** (the Ptolemaic dynasty in Egypt) and the **king of the North** (the Seleucid dynasty in Syria) engage in ongoing warfare and marriage alliances.
- **Antiochus Epiphanes** (a type of the end-time Antichrist) arises: he flatters, corrupts, persecutes the Jewish people, sets up the "abomination that makes desolate," and speaks "marvelous things against the God of gods." However, his end comes and no one helps him.
- In the final section (v. 36-45), the prophecy transitions to the end-time **Antichrist himself** — the "willful king" who exalts himself above every god, honors a "god of fortresses," and reaches his catastrophic end at the glorious holy mountain.

**Daniel 12 — The Time of the End.** The final chapter opens with Michael the Archangel standing up for the children of Israel in a time of trouble "such as never was since there was a nation" — the Great Tribulation. Then comes the clearest Old Testament revelation of the resurrection: "Many of them that sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt" (Daniel 12:2). The wise who turn many to righteousness will shine as the stars forever.

Daniel is commanded to shut up the words and seal the book until the time of the end, when knowledge shall increase. This increase of knowledge — particularly in travel and technology — Chuck Smith connects to the modern era. A specific timeline is given: 1,290 days from the cessation of the daily sacrifice, and 1,335 days for the blessed who wait. The chapter ends with Daniel being told to go his way; he will rest and stand in his allotted place at the end of the days.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Daniel 11 provides such precise detail about the wars between the Ptolemies and Seleucids (the "kings of the South and North") that many critical scholars date the chapter to 165 BC — after Antiochus Epiphanes' persecution but before his death. However, the Septuagint (Greek translation of the Old Testament) was begun around 250 BC and completed by 220 BC, well before Antiochus's reign (175-164 BC), and it includes the book of Daniel. Moreover, the prophecy accurately predicts the death of Antiochus "between the sea and the glorious holy mountain" — a detail that contradicts what actually happened historically (Antiochus died in Persia according to 1 Maccabees 6:1-16), suggesting the prophecy is not a mere retelling of history but carries a dual fulfillment pointing to the end-time Antichrist.

The transition from historical type (Antiochus Epiphanes) to the end-time Antichrist occurs at approximately Daniel 11:36, where the "willful king" who "exalts himself above every god" transcends any known historical figure. The references to a "god of fortresses" (military power) and the division of the holy land under the end-time ruler echo the descriptions in Revelation and support a futurist interpretation.

Daniel 12's resurrection passage (v. 2) is the first unambiguous Old Testament reference to the resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked, echoed by Jesus in John 5:28-29. The sealing of the book until the time of the end (v. 4) aligns with the modern era's explosion of knowledge and global travel.
