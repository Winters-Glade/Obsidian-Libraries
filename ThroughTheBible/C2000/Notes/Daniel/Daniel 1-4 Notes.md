# Daniel 1-4 — Notes

## Summary

Pastor Chuck Smith opens the study of Daniel by addressing the "higher criticism" that has attacked the book's authorship, noting that the presence of Greek and Persian words alongside Hebrew and Aramaic only confirms Daniel's genuine role as a wise counselor in the Babylonian court who would have interacted with people from many nations.

**Daniel 1** — Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah are taken captive to Babylon and chosen for the king's training program. They resolve not to defile themselves with the king's rich food and wine, requesting instead a diet of vegetables and water. After ten days they appear healthier than those who ate the king's meat, and God gives them exceptional wisdom and understanding. Daniel specifically receives the ability to interpret all visions and dreams.

**Daniel 2** — Nebuchadnezzar has a troubling dream that his magicians and wise men cannot interpret or even recount. When the king orders all wise men executed, Daniel seeks God in prayer, and the mystery is revealed to him in a night vision. The dream reveals a great statue made of four metals: a head of gold (Babylon), chest and arms of silver (Medo-Persia), belly and thighs of brass (Greece), legs of iron and feet of iron mixed with clay (Rome and its divided form). A stone cut without hands strikes the image and becomes a great mountain filling the whole earth — the kingdom of God that will supersede all human empires. Nebuchadnezzar acknowledges Daniel's God as the "God of gods."

**Daniel 3** — Nebuchadnezzar erects a golden image and commands all to worship it. Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refuse. When brought before the king, they declare: "Our God whom we serve is able to deliver us from the burning fiery furnace, and He will deliver us out of your hand, O king. But if not, let it be known to you, O king, that we do not serve your gods, nor will we worship the gold image which you have set up" (Daniel 3:17-18). This is the essence of true commitment — faith that God can deliver, but submission even if He chooses not to. The furnace is heated seven times hotter, yet the three men are seen walking loose inside with a fourth person "like the Son of God." They emerge without even the smell of smoke.

**Daniel 4** — Nebuchadnezzar's second dream: a great tree reaching to heaven, then cut down by a holy watcher. Daniel interprets it as the king himself, who will be driven from men to live as a beast until he acknowledges that "the Most High rules in the kingdom of men." Twelve months later, the king boasts of his great Babylon, and the judgment falls instantly. He eats grass like an ox for seven years until his reason returns and he blesses the Most High. The chapter ends with Nebuchadnezzar's testimony of praise.

## Key Themes

- **[[Sovereignty of God]]** — God rules over all human kingdoms and raises up and brings down rulers at His pleasure
- **[[Faithfulness Under Pressure]]** — Daniel and his companions' uncompromising commitment to God in a pagan culture
- **[[Commitment]]** — The fiery furnace demonstrates faith that trusts God even when deliverance does not come (Daniel 3:16-18)
- **[[Prophecy Timeline]]** — The four metals of Nebuchadnezzar's dream outline successive world empires
- **[[Divine Revelation]]** — God reveals mysteries to those who seek Him in prayer
- **[[Exile]] and [[Captivity]]** — God's people serving faithfully in a foreign land
- **[[Pride]] and [[Humility]]** — Nebuchadnezzar's humbling from king to beast and back

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

The events of Daniel 1-4 take place during the Neo-Babylonian period (c. 605-562 BC). Nebuchadnezzar II was the most powerful ruler of his age, having defeated the Egyptian army at Carchemish (605 BC) and subjugated Judah. The first deportation of Jewish captives — including Daniel and his friends — occurred in 605 BC. These young men, likely teenagers from noble or royal families, were deliberately indoctrinated into Babylonian culture through a three-year program of language, literature, and palace service. The Babylonian court was cosmopolitan, and the presence of Greek and Persian loanwords in Daniel reflects the multicultural environment in which he served.

Nebuchadnezzar's grandeur is well attested archaeologically: the Ishtar Gate, the Hanging Gardens (one of the Seven Wonders), and vast palace complexes. His seven-year madness — though not explicitly documented in Babylonian annals (which would naturally omit royal humiliation) — is consistent with the ancient Near Eastern pattern of kings being struck with a form of illness described as lycanthropy. The Nabonidus Prayer and the "Prayer of Nabonidus" from Qumran (4QPrNab) contain parallel motifs of a Babylonian king being afflicted for seven years and then restored upon acknowledging the true God.
