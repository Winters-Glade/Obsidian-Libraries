## Historical Context
These prophecies were delivered during the [[Babylonian Exile]] (605-536 BC). Ezekiel spoke to the captives in Babylon, while Daniel served in the royal court of Babylon. Both addressed God's sovereignty over history and the future restoration of Israel.

## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Daniel, chapters 1-12.

**Daniel:** Daniel, taken captive in the first deportation to Babylon, served in the courts of Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, Darius, and Cyrus. The first half of the book contains narratives of faithfulness under pressure - the fiery furnace, the lion's den, and Daniel's refusal to defile himself. The second half contains apocalyptic visions of world empires and the ''Seventy Weeks'' prophecy, outlining God's timeline for Israel and the coming of the Messiah.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

The Book of Daniel is set in the Neo-Babylonian and early Persian periods (c. 605–536 BC). Archaeological discoveries have repeatedly confirmed the historical framework of Daniel's narratives:

- **Nebuchadnezzar II** (r. 605–562 BC) is one of the best-documented kings of antiquity. The **Babylonian Chronicles** detail his military campaigns, including the siege of Jerusalem in 597 BC (BM 21946). The **East India House Inscription** and numerous building texts describe his massive construction projects in Babylon — the Ishtar Gate, the Processional Way, and the Etemenanki ziggurat (likely the Tower of Babel's successor).
- **Belshazzar** (Daniel 5) was long thought by critics to be a fictional character since no Babylonian king lists mention him. However, the **Nabonidus Cylinder** and other cuneiform texts revealed that Belshazzar was the son of King Nabonidus, who ruled as co-regent while Nabonidus was in Arabia. This explains why Belshazzar could offer only the "third highest" position in the kingdom (Daniel 5:16) — he himself was second.
- **Darius the Mede** (Daniel 5:31) has been the subject of scholarly debate, with various identifications proposed including Cyrus himself, a governor named Gubaru (recorded in the Nabonidus Chronicle), or a throne-name for Cyrus.
- The **Cyrus Cylinder** (c. 539 BC) describes Cyrus the Great's policy of repatriating displaced peoples — the historical context for Cyrus's decree allowing the Jews to return (Ezra 1; cf. Daniel's prayer in Daniel 9).
- The **Dead Sea Scrolls** include multiple copies of Daniel from Cave 4 at Qumran (4QDan<sup>a</sup>, 4QDan<sup>b</sup>, 4QDan<sup>c</sup>, 4QDan<sup>d</sup>), among the earliest extant biblical manuscripts, demonstrating the text's antiquity and careful preservation.

## Eschatological Significance

Daniel is arguably the most important Old Testament book for eschatology. Its influence on New Testament apocalyptic and Christian end-times theology is immense:

- **Nebuchadnezzar's Dream** (Daniel 2): The statue of four metals representing successive world empires (Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, Rome) culminating in the kingdom of God — a stone cut without hands that destroys them all. Chuck Smith taught this as the foundation for understanding world history as God's prophetic timeline.
- **The Seventy Weeks Prophecy** (Daniel 9:24-27): One of the most detailed messianic prophecies in Scripture. It presents a timeline of 70 "sevens" (weeks of years) from the decree to rebuild Jerusalem to the coming of the Anointed One, with a gap (parenthesis) before the final week — a period widely associated with the future tribulation. Chuck Smith's teaching follows the dispensational interpretation of a gap between the 69th and 70th week, with the final week corresponding to the Great Tribulation.
- **The Son of Man** (Daniel 7:13-14): Jesus' most frequent self-designation, drawn directly from Daniel's vision of "one like a son of man" coming with the clouds to receive an everlasting kingdom.
- **The Resurrection** (Daniel 12:2): The clearest Old Testament reference to the resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked — "many of them that sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt."

Chuck Smith linked Daniel's prophecies directly to modern events: the re-establishment of Israel (1948) as the fig tree generation, the revival of the Roman Empire in European unification, and the rise of a global leader.

## New Testament Connections

- **Daniel 7:13** — "Son of Man" coming with clouds → [[Mark 14_62]], [[Matthew 24_30.md]], [[Revelation 1_7.md]]; used by Jesus at His trial before the Sanhedrin.
- **Daniel 9:24-27** — The Seventy Weeks → Jesus' ministry "cut off" in the middle of the final week; the "abomination of desolation" referenced by Jesus in [[Matthew 24_15.md]] and [[Mark 13_14]].
- **Daniel 12:1** — Michael the archangel → [[Revelation 12_7.md]] where Michael fights against the dragon.
- **Daniel 12:2** — Resurrection → [[John 5_28-29.md]] where Jesus speaks of the resurrection of life and judgment.
- **Daniel 3** — The fiery furnace → [[Hebrews 11_34.md]] (faith quenched the violence of fire); 1 Peter 1:7 (faith tested by fire).
- **Daniel 6** — The lion's den → [[Hebrews 11_33.md]] (stopped the mouths of lions).

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Daniel and [[Ezekiel]]** — Contemporaries in Babylon. While Ezekiel ministered to the exiles, Daniel served in the palace. Both emphasize God's sovereignty over pagan rulers (Nebuchadnezzar's dreams in Daniel; God's judgment on the nations in Ezekiel 25-32).
- **Daniel and [[Zechariah]]** — Zechariah draws from Daniel's apocalyptic imagery in his night visions. The four horsemen (Zechariah 1; 6) echo Daniel's four beasts. Zechariah 14's depiction of the nations gathered against Jerusalem parallels Daniel's final vision.
- **Daniel and [[Revelation 19]]** — The parallels are structural: Daniel's four beasts → Revelation's beasts; Daniel's little horn → Revelation's Antichrist; Daniel's 70th week → Revelation's tribulation; Daniel's Son of Man → Revelation's glorified Christ; Daniel's resurrection → Revelation's first and second resurrections.
- **Daniel and [[Isaiah]]** — Isaiah 13, 21 (the burden of Babylon), and the promise of Cyrus by name (Isaiah 44:28–45:1) provide background to Daniel's Persian context.
