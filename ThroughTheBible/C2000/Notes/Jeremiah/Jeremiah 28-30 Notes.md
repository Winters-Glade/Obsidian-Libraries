## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Jeremiah 28 records the confrontation between Jeremiah and the false prophet **Hananiah**, who broke the yoke from Jeremiah's neck and prophesied a quick return of the exiles within two years. Jeremiah's response — "The prophets who preceded you and me from ancient times prophesied war, disaster, and plague against many countries and great kingdoms" — underscores the test of true prophecy: it must come to pass (Deuteronomy 18:21–22). Hananiah died within the year.

Chapter 29 contains Jeremiah's famous **letter to the exiles** (29:4–14), instructing them to build houses, plant gardens, marry, and seek the peace of Babylon — for their return would come only after 70 years. The promise of **Jeremiah 29:11** — "For I know the plans I have for you, declares the Lord, plans for welfare and not for evil, to give you a future and a hope" — remains one of the most beloved verses in Scripture.

Chapter 30 introduces the **Book of Comfort** (chapters 30–33), a collection of restoration promises.
