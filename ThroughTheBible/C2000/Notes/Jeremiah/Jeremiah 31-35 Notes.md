## Summary

This time, shall we turn in our Bibles to Jeremiah 31. Now, there are those who say that God has cast off Israel as a nation forever and that all of the blessings, all of the covenants, and all of the promises that God made to this nation are now fulfilled in the church. That we have become Israel after the Spirit, and being spiritual Israel, God has forsaken the nation itself and is now pouring out all of the blessings that he had promised through his covenant upon the church.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Jeremiah 31–35 contains the theological high point of the book: the **New Covenant** prophecy (31:31–34). Jeremiah declares that the days are coming when God will make a new covenant with Israel and Judah, "not like the covenant I made with their fathers" at Sinai — a covenant written not on stone tablets but on their hearts, with forgiveness of sins at its center.

Chapter 32 records Jeremiah's remarkable act of buying a field from his cousin Hanamel at Anathoth while the Babylonian siege of Jerusalem was underway — a prophetic sign that "houses, fields, and vineyards will again be bought in this land." Chapter 35 recounts the faithfulness of the **Rechabites**, a clan who obeyed their forefather's commands to abstain from wine — held up as a contrast to Judah's disobedience to God.

## ✝️ New Testament Fulfillment

**Jeremiah 31:31–34** — The **New Covenant** prophecy is the most significant Old Testament passage for understanding the theology of the New Testament:

- Jesus explicitly references the new covenant at the **Last Supper**: "This cup is the **new covenant** in My blood" (**Luke 22:20**, **1 Corinthians 11:25**).
- **Hebrews 8:7–13** quotes the entire passage (31:31–34) and argues that the new covenant in Christ has made the first covenant "obsolete."
- **Hebrews 9:15** describes Christ as the "mediator of a new covenant."
- **Hebrews 10:16–17** re-cites the promise that God will "put My laws in their hearts" and "remember their sins no more," grounding the believer's confidence in Christ's once-for-all sacrifice.

The internalization of the law ("written on their hearts") anticipates the work of the Holy Spirit, as described by Paul in **2 Corinthians 3:3–6** — "you are a letter of Christ... written not with ink but with the Spirit of the living God, not on tablets of stone but on tablets of human hearts."
