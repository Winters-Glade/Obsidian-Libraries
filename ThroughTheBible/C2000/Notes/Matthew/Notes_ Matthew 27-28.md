## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### The Trial Before Pilate
[[Pontius Pilate]] was the Roman prefect of [[Judea]] (AD 26–36), known from both the Gospels and non-Christian sources. The [[Pilate Stone]] (discovered at [[Caesarea Maritima]] in 1961) confirms his title as "Prefect of [[Judea]]." Philo and Josephus describe him as stubborn and cruel. The custom of releasing a prisoner at the feast (Matthew 27:15-26) — while not attested outside the Gospels — may reflect a Roman practice of amnesty during festivals.

### The Crucifixion
[[Crucifixion]] was a Roman punishment reserved for slaves, rebels, and the worst criminals. The victim was first scourged (a brutal lashing with a *flagrum*), then forced to carry the *patibulum* (crossbeam) to the execution site outside the city wall. The site [[Golgotha]] (Aramaic for "skull") was likely a small hill near a road for maximum visibility. Archaeological confirmation of crucifixion comes from the discovery of a heel bone with a nail still embedded (the [[Jehohanan]] ossuary, found at [[Givat HaMivtar]] in 1968).

### Burial and Resurrection
Joseph of [[Spiritual Gifts.md]]'s offer of a tomb fulfills [[Isaiah 53_9]] ("He was assigned a grave with the wicked, but with the rich in his death"). The tomb was a rock-hewn bench tomb typical of wealthy first-century [[Jerusalem]] families. The stone rolled before the entrance was a disk-shaped stone (*golel*) that rolled in a channel.

### The Empty Tomb
The earliest Jewish response to the empty tomb (Matthew 28:11-15) — that the disciples stole the body — is preserved in Matthew's Gospel itself, indicating this was an early controversy. The [[Great Commission]] (Matthew 28:16-20) concludes Matthew's Gospel with a universal mandate: "Go therefore and make disciples of all nations." This Trinitarian baptismal formula ("in the name of the Father, Son, and [[Holy Spirit]]") reflects early Christian liturgical practice.
