## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### The [[Sermon on the Mount]]
The [[Sermon on the Mount]] (Matthew 5–7) is the first of five major discourses in Matthew's Gospel, reflecting his structure as a "new Torah" — Jesus, as the new Moses, delivers the law of the [[Kingdom of Heaven]] from a mountain. The traditional site, the Mount of Beatitudes (near [[Capernaum]] on the northwestern shore of the [[Sea of Galilee]]), offers a natural amphitheater setting.

### Beatitudes in Jewish Context
The Beatitudes echo themes from the Hebrew Bible, particularly [[_Bible/Isaiah/Isaiah 61.md]] (the year of the Lord's favor) and the Psalms. "Blessed" (makarios in Greek) corresponds to the Hebrew *ashrei*, a formula found throughout the [[_Concepts/Biblical_References/Psalms]] (e.g., [[Psalm 1_1]], [[Psalm 32_1]]). The emphasis on the "poor in spirit" reflects the *anawim* tradition — the faithful remnant who trust wholly in God.

### First-Century Galilean Life
[[Galilee]] in the first century was a region of agrarian villages and fishing towns. The population was predominantly Jewish, with a strong oral tradition and deep devotion to the Torah. The Greek cities of the Decapolis lay to the east, while [[Epaphras.md]] (a Hellenistic city being rebuilt by [[Herod Agrippa.md]] during Jesus' youth) was only 4 miles from [[Nazareth]], exposing Galilean Jews to Greek and Roman culture.

### Synoptic Parallels
Luke's "Sermon on the Plain" ([[Luke 6_17-49.md]]) is the Synoptic parallel, though shorter and set on a level place. Luke includes four beatitudes and four corresponding woes, whereas Matthew has eight beatitudes. This is a key example of how Matthew and Luke edited their common source material (likely Q) for different theological emphases.

### Archaeological Notes
Excavations at the site commonly identified as the Mount of Beatitudes (near [[Tabgha]]) have uncovered a Byzantine-era church commemorating the [[Sermon on the Mount]]. Fourth-century pilgrim Egeria described the site. The nearby ancient synagogue at [[Capernaum]] (a later 4th–5th century building on earlier first-century foundations) gives insight into Galilean synagogue worship.
