## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### Jewish Practices of Piety
Matthew 6 addresses three core practices of Jewish piety: almsgiving (tzedakah), prayer (tefillah), and fasting (ta'anit). These were the "three pillars" of righteous living in [[Second Temple]] Judaism, as emphasized in the [[Talmud]] and earlier writings like the [[Book of Life.md]]. Jesus does not reject these practices but redirects their motivation from public display to private devotion.

### The Lord's Prayer
The [[Prayer]] (Matthew 6:9-13) is structured as a Jewish *berakhah* (blessing) with an Aramaic original that can be retroverted from the Greek. Its petitions echo the [[Kaddish]] prayer in synagogues ("Exalted and hallowed be His great name...") and focus on the coming of God's kingdom. Luke's shorter version ([[Luke 11_2-4]]) is placed in a different context, suggesting Jesus taught the prayer on multiple occasions or that Matthew and Luke drew from different traditions.

### Treasure in Heaven
The concept of storing up "treasures in heaven" (Matthew 6:19-20) reflects the Jewish teaching that acts of charity and obedience to God's commandments accrue merit before God (cf. [[Sirach 29:11]]). The "eye as the lamp of the body" (Matthew 6:22-23) is a common ancient Mediterranean metaphor — a "good eye" (ayin tovah) in Hebrew idiom meant generosity, while an "evil eye" (ayin ra'ah) meant stinginess.

### OT Background
The prohibition against worry (Matthew 6:25-34) draws on the wilderness tradition of manna ([[Exodus 16.md]]), where Israel had to trust God for daily provision. The "lilies of the field" and "birds of the air" exemplify God's providential care, a theme found throughout the [[_Concepts/Biblical_References/Psalms]] ([[Psalm 104.md]], [[Psalm 145.md]]).
