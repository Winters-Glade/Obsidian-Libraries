## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### 1st-Century [[Judea]] Under Roman Rule
At the time of Jesus' birth, [[Judea]] was under the authority of [[Herod the Great]] (37–4 BC), a client king appointed by Rome. Herod was known for his massive building projects — including the expansion of the [[Temple (Jerusalem)|Second Temple in Jerusalem]], the fortress at [[Amasa.md]], and the port city of [[Caesarea Maritima]]. After his death, his kingdom was divided among his sons: [[Herod the Great.md]] ruled [[Galilee]] and Perea (4 BC–AD 39), while [[Judea]] proper eventually came under direct Roman administration through prefects like [[Pontius Pilate]] (AD 26–36).

### The Census of Quirinius
Luke's Gospel also mentions a census under [[Nehemiah  Cupbearer to Governor]] of Syria (Luke 2:1-2). This registration for tax purposes explains why Joseph and Mary traveled to [[Bethlehem]], the City of David — fulfilling [[Micah]]'s prophecy that the [[Messiah]] would be born there. Archaeological evidence for Roman censuses in this period survives in Egyptian papyri recording household registrations.

### OT Prophecy Fulfillment
Matthew structures his infancy narrative around five explicit fulfillment citations: the virgin birth ([[Isaiah]]), birth in [[Bethlehem]] ([[Micah]]), the flight to Egypt ([[Hosea 11_1]]), the massacre of the innocents ([[Jeremiah 31_15.md]]), and the family settling in [[Nazareth]] ("He shall be called a Nazarene").

### Synoptic Parallels
Matthew's infancy account is unique among the Synoptics — neither [[Mark]] nor [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (in his infancy narrative) shares Matthew's emphasis on Joseph's perspective, the Magi, or the flight to Egypt. [[Luke 2_1-20.md]] provides the complementary account from Mary's perspective, including the Annunciation, the Magnificat, and the shepherds' visit.

### Jewish Customs
- Jewish betrothal (erusin) was a legally binding contract, requiring a divorce (get) to dissolve — explaining Joseph's intent to "put Mary away privily."
- The purification offering after childbirth (Luke 2:22-24, referencing [[Leviticus 12.md]]) involved a lamb and a pigeon or turtledove.

### Archaeological Notes
The [[Gospel of Reconciliation]] (mentioned in [[John 9]]) was discovered in 2004 during construction work in [[Jerusalem]], confirming the location of this [[Second Temple]] period water reservoir. Ossuaries (bone boxes) from the period, such as the "James Ossuary" (controversial), reflect Jewish burial practices, including the secondary burial of bones in family tombs after flesh decomposition — relevant to understanding Gospel burial accounts.
