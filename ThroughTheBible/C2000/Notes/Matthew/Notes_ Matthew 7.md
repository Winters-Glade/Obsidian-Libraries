## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### Rabbinic Teaching Methods
Matthew 7 contains Jesus' teaching using several methods common to first-century Jewish rabbis. The use of hyperbole ("beam" and "mote," Matthew 7:3-5) was a recognized teaching technique (*guzma* in Aramaic). The "Golden Rule" (Matthew 7:12) — "whatever you wish that others would do to you, do also to them" — finds parallels in the teaching of [[Gamaliel.md]] ("What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow," [[Talmud Shabbat 31a]]), though Jesus states it in the positive form.

### The Narrow Gate
The image of the narrow gate and the two ways (Matthew 7:13-14) is rooted in the [[Deuteronomy 30.md]] choice between life and death, blessing and curse. This "two ways" motif appears throughout Jewish wisdom literature, including [[Psalm 1]] and the [[Didache]] (an early Christian manual) which opens with the "Two Ways of Life and Death."

### False Prophets
Warnings against false prophets "in sheep's clothing" (Matthew 7:15) reflect the reality that first-century [[Judea]] had numerous prophetic and messianic figures. Josephus records several: [[Theudas]], the Egyptian prophet, and various sign prophets who led followers into the wilderness.

### Wise and Foolish Builders
The concluding parable of the wise and foolish builders (Matthew 7:24-27) is a distinctly Jewish parable form (*mashal*). The contrast between building on rock vs. sand would resonate in a region where seasonal flash floods (*wadi* floods) were a regular hazard, washing away poorly constructed homes.

### Synoptic Parallels
Luke 6:43-49 contains a parallel version of this teaching, but with the Sermon on the Plain setting. The same parable of the builders appears in both, but Matthew's version emphasizes doing the words of Jesus, while Luke's concludes with the house's destruction when the flood comes.
