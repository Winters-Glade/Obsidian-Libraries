## Historical & Contextual Background

> [!QUESTION]- This enrichment needs QT verification

**The Tongue & Wisdom:** James 3's teaching on taming the tongue draws on both Jewish wisdom literature (Proverbs 10:19; 15:1–2; Sirach 28) and Hellenistic moral philosophy, which also discussed the power and danger of speech. The contrast between earthly and heavenly wisdom (3:13–18) reflects a Jewish two-ways tradition (cf. the Dead Sea Scrolls' Community Rule, which presents the spirit of truth vs. the spirit of falsehood).

**Poverty & Wealth:** James's strong condemnation of the rich (5:1–6) reflects his concern for the poor — a hallmark of the Jerusalem church, which collected relief funds for the needy (Acts 11:27–30; Galatians 2:10). The social context likely included wealthy landowners exploiting laborers, a situation common in 1st-century Palestine and the diaspora.

**Prayer & Healing:** The call to anoint the sick with oil (5:14) reflects Jewish medicinal practice and early Christian sacramental tradition. The prayer of Elijah (5:17–18) as a model of effective prayer would resonate with Jewish readers familiar with 1 Kings 17–18.

**Martin Luther's Assessment:** Luther famously called James an "epistle of straw" due to its perceived contradiction with Paul's teaching on justification. Modern scholarship recognizes James and Paul as addressing different aspects of the faith-works relationship.
