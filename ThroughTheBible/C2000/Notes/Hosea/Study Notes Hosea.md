## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Hosea, chapters 1-14.

**Hosea:** Hosea's marriage to Gomer serves as a living parable of God's relationship with unfaithful Israel. Through this personal tragedy, Hosea proclaims God's covenant lawsuit against the Northern Kingdom while also revealing the depth of God's unfailing love. The book oscillates between judgment and restoration, ultimately climaxing in a promise of healing and restoration.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Hosea prophesied to the Northern Kingdom of Israel during the 8th century BC (c. 755–715 BC), overlapping with the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah of Judah, and Jeroboam II of Israel. This was a period of relative prosperity under Jeroboam II's long reign, but also of deep spiritual corruption:

- The **Tel Dan Stele** (9th century BC) provides extra-biblical evidence of the "House of David" dynasty, confirming the historical reality of the divided monarchy that Hosea addressed.
- The **Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III** (c. 841 BC) depicts Jehu, king of Israel, bowing before the Assyrian king — showing the tribute relationship between Israel and Assyria that Hosea warned about (Hosea 5:13; 7:11).
- The **Samaria Ostraca** (8th century BC) — inscribed pottery fragments from the Northern Kingdom — record shipments of wine and oil, providing a window into the economic activity and administrative systems of Hosea's day.
- The later **Sargon II Prism** documents the Assyrian conquest of Samaria in 722 BC, fulfilling Hosea's warnings of judgment.

## New Testament Connections

- **Hosea 6:6** — "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" — quoted twice by Jesus: [[_Bible/Matthew/Matthew 9_13.md]] (after calling Matthew, in response to the Pharisees) and [[Matthew 12_7.md]] (in defense of His disciples plucking grain on the Sabbath). Jesus uses this verse to prioritize compassion over ritual.
- **Hosea 11:1** — "Out of Egypt I called my son" — quoted by Matthew in [[Matthew 2_15.md]] as a prophecy of the Holy Family's return from Egypt. Matthew reads Hosea's reference to the Exodus as a typological pattern fulfilled in Christ.
- **Hosea 1:10** / [[Hosea 2_23]] — "You are not my people" changed to "sons of the living God" — quoted by Paul in [[Romans 9_25-26.md]] to explain God's inclusion of the Gentiles, and by Peter in [[1 Peter 2_10.md]].
- **Hosea 13:14** — "O death, where are your plagues? O grave, where is your destruction?" — quoted by Paul in [[1 Corinthians 15_55.md]] in his triumphant declaration of Christ's victory over death.

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Hosea and [[Amos]]** — Both prophesied to the Northern Kingdom in the 8th century. Both use [[Covenant Lawsuit]] language. Where Amos focuses on social justice, Hosea emphasizes the intimate relationship between God and Israel as a marriage.
- **Hosea and [[Jeremiah]]** — Jeremiah repeatedly echoes Hosea's marriage metaphor (Jeremiah 2-3), using the imagery of an unfaithful wife forsaking her husband.
- **Hosea and [[Ezekiel]]** — Ezekiel 16 and 23 use the extended allegory of unfaithful wives (Oholah and Oholibah), building on Hosea's foundational marriage metaphor.
- **Hosea and [[Jonah]]** — Both reveal God's heart of compassion — Hosea toward Israel, Jonah (reluctantly) toward Nineveh.
