## Overview

Now, Naaman was the captain of the host of Syria. He was a great man with his master. He was honorable because the Lord had actually helped him to subdue many nations. He was a mighty man and very brave, but he was a leper ([[2 Kings 5_1]]). And the Syrians had gone out by companies and brought away captives out of the land of Israel. And a little maid happened to be the servant of his wife. And she said, it's too bad your husband isn't with a prophet there in Israel because he could heal him of his leprosy ([[2 Kings 5_3]]). And so it was told to Naaman and he told the king that there was a prophet in Israel that could cure him of his leprosy. And so the king of Syria, Ben-Hadad, sent a letter. He sent a formal letter to the king of Israel and sent Naaman down ([[2 Kings 5_5-6.md]]). And in the letter it said, I want you to heal my general of this leprosy. So when the king of Israel saw Naaman come in, knew who he was, this captain of the Syrian host and all, and when he gets this note, I want you to heal this guy of his leprosy, the king just became excited and concerned. He said, look how this guy's trying to pick a fight. Am I God that I can heal him of his leprosy? ([[2 Kings 5_7]]). And so the king had torn his clothes in distress. But [[Elisha]] heard of it and sent word: Let him come to me, and he shall know that there is a prophet in Israel ([[2 Kings 5_8]]). And so Naaman came to [[Elisha]]'s house, and [[Elisha]] sent a messenger out saying, Go and wash in the Jordan seven times, and your flesh shall be restored ([[2 Kings 5_10.md]]). Naaman was angry at first — he expected a dramatic healing — but his servants persuaded him to obey ([[2 Kings 5_11-13.md]]). So he dipped seven times in the [[Jordan River]] and was completely healed ([[2 Kings 5_14.md]]). Naaman then declared, "There is no God in all the earth but in Israel" ([[2 Kings 5_15.md]]). [[Elisha]] continued his ministry with further miracles: making an axe head float ([[2 Kings 6_1-7.md]]), revealing the Syrian king's battle plans to the king of Israel ([[2 Kings 6_8-12.md]]), and opening the eyes of his servant to see the angelic army surrounding them at Dothan ([[2 Kings 6_15-17.md]]). The Syrian army was struck blind and led into [[Samaria]], where [[Elisha]] instructed the king to feed them rather than kill them ([[2 Kings 6_18-23.md]]). Later, when [[Samaria]] was besieged by Syria, causing a terrible famine, [[Elisha]] prophesied its sudden end ([[2 Kings 6_24-33.md]], [[2 Kings 7_1-20.md]]).

## Key Themes

- God's grace extends beyond Israel to the Gentiles, as demonstrated in Naaman's healing ([[2 Kings 5_1-19.md]])
- Humility and obedience are prerequisites for receiving God's healing and blessing ([[2 Kings 5_9-14.md]])
- The power of a faithful witness: a young Israelite slave girl points Naaman to the prophet ([[2 Kings 5_2-4.md]])
- [[Elisha]]'s ministry demonstrates that God's power is not limited by human circumstances ([[2 Kings 6_1-23.md]])
- The unseen spiritual reality: the angelic army that protects God's servants ([[2 Kings 6_15-17.md]])
- God's mercy even toward enemies: the Syrian soldiers fed and released rather than destroyed ([[2 Kings 6_21-23.md]])

## Connections

- Jesus references Naaman in [[Luke 4_27]] — noting that there were many lepers in Israel, but only Naaman the Syrian was healed, demonstrating God's concern for the Gentiles
- Naaman's healing prefigures baptism — washing in the Jordan for cleansing ([[Mark 1_9-11]])
- The angelic army at Dothan echoes [[2 Kings 2_11.md]] (chariots of fire with Elijah) and the heavenly armies seen in [[Psalm 34_7]], [[Psalm 91_11-12.md]], and [[Hebrews 1_14.md]]
- Gehazi's greed and leprosy serves as a warning against covetousness and misrepresenting God ([[1 Timothy 6_9-10.md]])
- The healing of Naaman illustrates the gospel going to the Gentiles ([[Acts 10_1-48.md]], [[Romans 3_29-30.md]])
- The multiplication of loaves ([[2 Kings 4_42-44.md]]) and God's provision in famine prefigure Jesus' miracles

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- The Divided Kingdom period continues (ca. 850-560 BC)
- Elisha's ministry (2 Kings 1-8): double-portion miracles, the Shunammite woman, Naaman the Syrian
- The Jehu Revolution (2 Kings 9-10) — archaeology: the Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III (depicts Jehu bringing tribute, ca. 841 BC) — the only known image of an Israelite king
- The Fall of Samaria (2 Kings 17, ca. 722 BC): Assyrian conquest under Shalmaneser V and Sargon II. The fate of the Ten Lost Tribes. Assyrian resettlement policies
- Hezekiah's reforms and the Assyrian siege of Jerusalem (2 Kings 18-19, ca. 701 BC): Sennacherib's Prism (Taylor Prism) documents the siege, Hezekiah's Tunnel (Siloam Tunnel inscription), the Broad Wall in Jerusalem
- Manasseh's long reign (2 Kings 21): Assyrian vassalage, apostasy
- Josiah's reform (2 Kings 22-23): the discovery of the Book of the Law, the Deuteronomic reform. The Tel Dan Stele, evidence of cultic reform at Arad
- The Fall of Jerusalem (2 Kings 24-25, ca. 586 BC): Nebuchadnezzar's destruction, the Babylonian Chronicles, the Lachish Letters (ostraca showing the final days of Judah)
- The Exile: deportation to Babylon, the Babylonian Captivity
