## Overview

[[2 Kings 17.md]]. In the 17th chapter, we come to the death of the northern kingdom, the nation of Israel. In the 12th year, when Ahaz was the king in Judah, that's the king of the southern kingdom, Hosea began to reign in [[Samaria]] over Israel. He reigned for nine years. He did that which was evil in the sight of the Lord ([[2 Kings 17_1-2.md]]). And so, unfortunately, Israel did not have one single king of which it was not testified that he did evil in the sight of the Lord. Not one king of Israel followed after the Lord from the very beginning of [[Jeroboam]]. When the kingdom was divided into the northern and southern kingdom, from [[Jeroboam]] onward, all of the kings did evil in the sight of the Lord ([[2 Kings 17_7-23.md]]). It is interesting that as the king goes, so went the nation so often. And the nation's following after God or turning from God was largely dependent upon the influence of the king. And so, the Assyrians came up against them. Shalmaneser, the king of Assyria, and Hosea became a servant and paid tribute ([[2 Kings 17_3.md]]). But the king of Assyria found them conspiring, for they had sent to the king of Egypt for help ([[2 Kings 17_4.md]]). So the Assyrians came again and laid siege to [[Samaria]] for three years, and the city fell, and Israel was carried away into [[Exile|exile]] to Assyria and placed in Halah and in Habor by the river of Gozan, and in the cities of the Medes ([[2 Kings 17_5-6.md]]). The rest of 2 Kings chronicles the final years of Judah: the righteous reign of [[Hezekiah]] and the miraculous deliverance from [[Sennacherib]]'s Assyrian army ([[2 Kings 18_1-37.md]], [[2 Kings 19_1-37.md]]), the wicked reign of [[Manasseh]] who led Judah into deeper [[idolatry|idolatry]] ([[2 Kings 21_1-18.md]]), the reform of [[Josiah]] who rediscovered the Book of the Law ([[2 Kings 22_1-20.md]], [[2 Kings 23_1-30.md]]), and the final decline and fall of [[Jerusalem]] to [[Nebuchadnezzar]] of [[Babylon]] ([[2 Kings 24_1-20.md]], [[2 Kings 25_1-30.md]]), culminating in the destruction of the [[Temple|temple]] and the [[Exile|exile]] of Judah.

## Key Themes

- The fall of the northern kingdom (Israel) as divine judgment for persistent [[idolatry|idolatry]] and rejection of the [[Covenant|covenant]] ([[2 Kings 17_7-23.md]])
- The faithful remnant in Judah: [[Hezekiah]] and [[Josiah]] as models of [[Repentance|repentance]] and reform ([[2 Kings 18_1-6.md]], [[2 Kings 22_1-2.md]], [[2 Kings 23_1-25.md]])
- God's miraculous deliverance of [[Jerusalem]] from [[Sennacherib]] in answer to [[Hezekiah]]'s prayer and [[Isaiah]]'s [[Prophecy|prophecy]] ([[2 Kings 19_14-37.md]])
- The devastating consequences of [[idolatry|idolatry]] under [[Manasseh]] ([[2 Kings 21_1-16.md]])
- The rediscovery of God's Word as the catalyst for national revival under [[Josiah]] ([[2 Kings 22_8-20.md]], [[2 Kings 23_1-25.md]])
- The [[Exile|exile]] of Judah as the fulfillment of prophetic warnings ([[2 Kings 24_1-4.md]], [[2 Kings 25_1-21.md]])
- The [[Babylon]]ian captivity as both judgment and the beginning of a new chapter in God's redemptive plan

## Connections

- The fall of [[Samaria]] fulfills the warnings of [[Prophecy|prophets]] like [[Amos]] ([[Amos 5_26-27.md]], [[Amos 7_11-17.md]]), [[Hosea]] ([[Hosea 1_4-7]], [[Hosea 9_1-7]]), and [[Isaiah]] ([[Isaiah 7_17-25.md]], [[Isaiah 28_1-4.md]])
- [[Hezekiah]]'s deliverance is celebrated in [[Isaiah 36_36-39.md]] (the parallel account) and referenced in [[Psalm 46]] and [[Psalm 76]]
- [[Isaiah]]'s prophecies of [[Sennacherib]]'s downfall ([[Isaiah 37_21-38.md]])
- [[Josiah]]'s Passover ([[2 Kings 23_21-23.md]]) connects to the original Passover in [[Exodus 12_1-51.md]] and is the greatest celebration since the days of the judges
- The [[Exile|exile]] of Judah to [[Babylon]] fulfills the covenant curses of [[Deuteronomy 28_49-68.md]] and sets the stage for the post-exilic return ([[Ezra 1_1-11]], [[Nehemiah 1_1-11.md]])
- Jesus references the destruction of Jerusalem in [[Matthew 24_15.md]] (the abomination of desolation) and [[Luke 19_41-44.md]]
- The fall of [[Jerusalem]] and the [[Temple|temple]] destruction foreshadow the Roman destruction in AD 70 ([[Matthew 24_12.md]])
- The promise of restoration after [[Exile|exile]] anticipates the new [[Covenant|covenant]] ([[_Bible/Jeremiah/Jeremiah 31_31-34.md]], [[Ezekiel 36_24-28.md]])

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- The Divided Kingdom period continues (ca. 850-560 BC)
- Elisha's ministry (2 Kings 1-8): double-portion miracles, the Shunammite woman, Naaman the Syrian
- The Jehu Revolution (2 Kings 9-10) — archaeology: the Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III (depicts Jehu bringing tribute, ca. 841 BC) — the only known image of an Israelite king
- The Fall of Samaria (2 Kings 17, ca. 722 BC): Assyrian conquest under Shalmaneser V and Sargon II. The fate of the Ten Lost Tribes. Assyrian resettlement policies
- Hezekiah's reforms and the Assyrian siege of Jerusalem (2 Kings 18-19, ca. 701 BC): Sennacherib's Prism (Taylor Prism) documents the siege, Hezekiah's Tunnel (Siloam Tunnel inscription), the Broad Wall in Jerusalem
- Manasseh's long reign (2 Kings 21): Assyrian vassalage, apostasy
- Josiah's reform (2 Kings 22-23): the discovery of the Book of the Law, the Deuteronomic reform. The Tel Dan Stele, evidence of cultic reform at Arad
- The Fall of Jerusalem (2 Kings 24-25, ca. 586 BC): Nebuchadnezzar's destruction, the Babylonian Chronicles, the Lachish Letters (ostraca showing the final days of Judah)
- The Exile: deportation to Babylon, the Babylonian Captivity
