## Overview

Now as we finished 1 Kings, we were dealing with basically the northern kingdom under [[Ahab]] with his wicked wife [[Jezebel]] who had brought the northern kingdom of Israel into its lowest state morally and spiritually as they led the people into [[idolatry|idolatry]] and more specifically the worship of Baal which was introduced by [[Jezebel]] who was from the area of Sidon ([[1 Kings 16_31.md]]), and that was one of the major gods of Sidon and so she introduced that to the children of Israel in their worship and thus Israel was sinking into a state of great suffering and spiritual apostasy. Now at the end of 1 Kings we came to the death of [[Ahab]] ([[1 Kings 22_34-40.md]]) and his son Ahaziah ascending to the throne who reigned for only two years over Israel ([[1 Kings 22_51.md]]). And during the time of Ahaziah, Moab, which of course is across the Jordan River — the present area known as Jordan — who were vassals and tributaries to the king of Israel, rebelled against Israel ([[2 Kings 1_1]]). And Ahaziah fell down through a lattice in his upper chamber that was in [[Samaria]] ([[2 Kings 1_2]]), so he had an accident and fell down through this lattice work from the upper chamber and was injured, and he ordered his servants to go to inquire of the god of Ekron which was Beelzebub — now the word Beel is a word that means lord and so the people were worshipping the lord but the lord wasn't God, it was their lord, and Beelzebub is actually lord of the flies ([[2 Kings 1_2-3.md]]). Instead of seeking the Lord God of Israel, Ahaziah sought a pagan oracle. [[Elijah]] intercepted the messengers and pronounced the king's death ([[2 Kings 1_3-4.md]]). This opening sets the stage for the transition of prophetic authority from [[Elijah]] to [[Elisha]]. [[Elijah]] is taken up to heaven in a whirlwind ([[2 Kings 2_1-11.md]]), and [[Elisha]] picks up his mantle, requesting a double portion of his spirit ([[2 Kings 2_9-15.md]]). [[Elisha]] then begins his ministry with miracles: healing the waters of [[Jericho]] ([[2 Kings 2_19-22.md]]), cursing the mockers at [[Bethel]] ([[2 Kings 2_23-24.md]]), multiplying the widow's oil ([[2 Kings 4_1-7.md]]), raising the Shunammite woman's son from the dead ([[2 Kings 4_18-37.md]]), and purifying the poisonous pottage ([[2 Kings 4_38-41.md]]).

## Key Themes

- The transfer of [[Prophecy|prophetic]] authority from [[Elijah]] to [[Elisha]] ([[2 Kings 2_1-15.md]])
- [[idolatry|Idolatry]] and apostasy in the northern kingdom as the root of national decline ([[1 Kings 16_30-33.md]])
- God's power displayed through miracles as a sign of His presence among His people ([[2 Kings 2_19-22.md]], [[2 Kings 4_1-44.md]])
- The failure of kings to seek the Lord leads to seeking false gods ([[2 Kings 1_2-6.md]])
- The double portion of God's spirit upon Elisha, fulfilling his request ([[2 Kings 2_9-15.md]])
- The faithfulness of humble individuals (the Shunammite woman, the widow) contrasted with unfaithful leaders

## Connections

- Elijah's translation prefigures Christ's ascension ([[Acts 1_9-11]])
- Elisha's double portion echoes the inheritance of the firstborn ([[Deuteronomy 21_17.md]])
- The Shunammite woman's son raised from the dead foreshadows Jesus raising the widow's son at Nain ([[Luke 7_11-17.md]])
- The multiplication of loaves in 2 Kings 4:42-44 prefigures Jesus feeding the 5,000 ([[Matthew 14_13-21.md]], [[John 6_1-14]])
- The Moabite rebellion connects to the [[archaeology|Mesha Stele (Moabite Stone)]] which records these events
- The healing of Jericho's waters symbolizes spiritual restoration and [[Repentance|repentance]]
- [[Jezebel]]'s influence continues to corrupt the northern kingdom until the judgment of [[Jehu]] (2 Kings 9-10)

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- The Divided Kingdom period continues (ca. 850-560 BC)
- Elisha's ministry (2 Kings 1-8): double-portion miracles, the Shunammite woman, Naaman the Syrian
- The Jehu Revolution (2 Kings 9-10) — archaeology: the Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III (depicts Jehu bringing tribute, ca. 841 BC) — the only known image of an Israelite king
- The Fall of Samaria (2 Kings 17, ca. 722 BC): Assyrian conquest under Shalmaneser V and Sargon II. The fate of the Ten Lost Tribes. Assyrian resettlement policies
- Hezekiah's reforms and the Assyrian siege of Jerusalem (2 Kings 18-19, ca. 701 BC): Sennacherib's Prism (Taylor Prism) documents the siege, Hezekiah's Tunnel (Siloam Tunnel inscription), the Broad Wall in Jerusalem
- Manasseh's long reign (2 Kings 21): Assyrian vassalage, apostasy
- Josiah's reform (2 Kings 22-23): the discovery of the Book of the Law, the Deuteronomic reform. The Tel Dan Stele, evidence of cultic reform at Arad
- The Fall of Jerusalem (2 Kings 24-25, ca. 586 BC): Nebuchadnezzar's destruction, the Babylonian Chronicles, the Lachish Letters (ostraca showing the final days of Judah)
- The Exile: deportation to Babylon, the Babylonian Captivity
