## Overview

[[2 Kings 9_1|Chapter 9]]. [[Elisha]] is an extremely colorful character. And we are coming towards the end of the career of [[Elisha]], a [[Prophecy|prophet]] to the northern kingdom of Israel ([[2 Kings 9_1]]). And [[Elisha]] commanded one of the young prophets to go to Jezreel and there to take a cruse of oil and call [[Jehu]], who was a captain of the host of Israel, into another room secretly and there anoint him with the oil to be king over Israel and then get out of there as fast as you can ([[2 Kings 9_1-3.md]]). So this young man came to Jezreel where [[Jehu]] was sitting with a bunch of the officers. And he said, I have a message for you, O [[Jehu]]. And he said, which one of us? And he said, you. And so he went into the other room and he said, thus saith the Lord. He has anointed you to be king over Israel to replace the house of [[Ahab]] ([[2 Kings 9_6-8.md]]). And he poured this cruse of oil over [[Jehu]] and then he took off. When [[Jehu]] came out with the rest of the officers, they asked him what the wild-looking prophet was about. And [[Jehu]] said, he anointed me with oil and told me I would be king ([[2 Kings 9_11-12.md]]). And they quickly proclaimed him king ([[2 Kings]]). [[Jehu]] then launched a bloody purge, killing [[Joram]] ([[2 Kings 9_14-26.md]]), [[Jezebel]] ([[2 Kings 9_30-37.md]]), the seventy sons of [[Ahab]] ([[2 Kings 10_1-11.md]]), and the prophets of Baal ([[2 Kings 10_18-28.md]]). However, [[Jehu]] did not depart from the sins of [[Jeroboam]] — he maintained the golden calves at [[Bethel]] and [[Dan]] ([[2 Kings 10_29-31.md]]). The subsequent chapters chronicle the decline of both kingdoms: the reign of Athaliah in Judah ([[2 Kings 11_1-16.md]]), the repair of the [[Temple|temple]] under Joash ([[2 Kings 12_1-16.md]]), and the long succession of kings in Israel — Jehoahaz, Jehoash, Jeroboam II, and others — who continued in the [[idolatry|idolatry]] of [[Jeroboam]] ([[2 Kings 13_1-25.md]], [[2 Kings 14_1-29.md]]). The northern kingdom continued its spiral toward the judgment that would culminate in the Assyrian [[Exile|exile]].

## Key Themes

- Divine judgment upon the house of [[Ahab]] and [[Jezebel]] through [[Jehu]]'s revolution ([[2 Kings 9_1-37.md]], [[2 Kings 10_1-28.md]])
- The incomplete [[Repentance|repentance]] of [[Jehu]]: he destroyed Baal worship but kept the golden calves ([[2 Kings 10_29-31.md]])
- The persistent [[idolatry|idolatry]] of the northern kingdom from [[Jeroboam]] onward ([[2 Kings 13_2.md]], [[2 Kings 14_24.md]], [[2 Kings 15_9.md]], [[2 Kings 15_18.md]], [[2 Kings 15_28.md]])
- God's mercy even in judgment: He saw the affliction of Israel and provided deliverers ([[2 Kings 13_4-5.md]], [[2 Kings 14_26-27.md]])
- The [[Covenant|covenant]] faithfulness of God to the house of [[David]] in Judah despite the unfaithfulness of many kings ([[2 Kings 11_1-21.md]], [[2 Kings 12_1-3.md]], [[2 Kings 14_3-4.md]])
- The [[Prophecy|prophetic]] word as the standard by which kings are measured and judged

## Connections

- Jehu's purge fulfills the prophecies of Elijah against Ahab and Jezebel ([[1 Kings 21_17-24.md]], [[1 Kings 21_29.md]])
- The Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III depicts Jehu bringing tribute, providing archaeological confirmation of his reign
- Athaliah's destruction of the royal seed parallels the attempt to destroy the Davidic line (see also [[Exodus 1_15-22.md]], [[Matthew 2_16-18.md]])
- Joash's repair of the temple prefigures later reforms under [[Hezekiah]] and [[Josiah]]
- The death of [[Elisha]] (2 Kings 13:14-21) — even his bones brought life — points to the power of God beyond the prophet's lifetime and prefigures Christ's resurrection power
- The Assyrian threat against Judah under Ahaz (2 Kings 16) sets the stage for the later deliverance under [[Hezekiah]] and [[Isaiah]]
- [[Jeroboam]] II's reign brought prosperity but not spiritual reform — echoing [[Amos]] and [[Hosea]]'s contemporary warnings
- The spiral of [[idolatry|idolatry]] in the north parallels the [[Exile|exile]] warnings of the [[Prophecy|prophets]]

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- The Divided Kingdom period continues (ca. 850-560 BC)
- Elisha's ministry (2 Kings 1-8): double-portion miracles, the Shunammite woman, Naaman the Syrian
- The Jehu Revolution (2 Kings 9-10) — archaeology: the Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III (depicts Jehu bringing tribute, ca. 841 BC) — the only known image of an Israelite king
- The Fall of Samaria (2 Kings 17, ca. 722 BC): Assyrian conquest under Shalmaneser V and Sargon II. The fate of the Ten Lost Tribes. Assyrian resettlement policies
- Hezekiah's reforms and the Assyrian siege of Jerusalem (2 Kings 18-19, ca. 701 BC): Sennacherib's Prism (Taylor Prism) documents the siege, Hezekiah's Tunnel (Siloam Tunnel inscription), the Broad Wall in Jerusalem
- Manasseh's long reign (2 Kings 21): Assyrian vassalage, apostasy
- Josiah's reform (2 Kings 22-23): the discovery of the Book of the Law, the Deuteronomic reform. The Tel Dan Stele, evidence of cultic reform at Arad
- The Fall of Jerusalem (2 Kings 24-25, ca. 586 BC): Nebuchadnezzar's destruction, the Babylonian Chronicles, the Lachish Letters (ostraca showing the final days of Judah)
- The Exile: deportation to Babylon, the Babylonian Captivity
