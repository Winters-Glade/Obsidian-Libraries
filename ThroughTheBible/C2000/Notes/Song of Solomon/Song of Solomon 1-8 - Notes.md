### Ancient Near Eastern Parallels

The Song of Solomon belongs to an ancient tradition of [[Love]] from the [[Ancient Near East]]. Egyptian love poetry from the [[Ram]] (c. 1300–1075 BCE) — such as the [[Chester Beatty Papyrus.md]] — features lovers addressing each other as "brother" and "sister," praising each other's physical beauty in language strikingly similar to the Song's. Mesopotamian [[Marriage]] texts ([[Qumran.md]] and [[Akkadian]]) celebrate the union of the king with the goddess [[Manna.md]]/[[Ishtar]], blending romantic and cultic language.

What distinguishes the Song from its ANE counterparts is its uncompromising [[Monotheism]]: unlike the Mesopotamian sacred marriage texts, the Song never invokes deities or cultic settings. It celebrates human love as God's good gift, not as a mythological reenactment.

### Hebrew Genre & Structure

The Song is a collection of lyric love poems in dramatic dialogue, set primarily in and around [[Jerusalem]] and the countryside of northern [[Israel]]. Its structure moves through stages of desire, separation, reunion, and celebration. Key refrains — "Do not stir up nor awaken love until it pleases" (2:7, 3:5, 8:4) — structure the book as a call to let love develop on its own timing.

### Authorship & Dating

The book opens with "The Song of Songs, which is [[Solomon]]'s" (1:1), traditionally attributing authorship to [[Solomon]] (c. 970–931 BCE). The superlative "Song of Songs" means the finest of his 1,005 songs (1 Kings 4:32). Modern scholarship suggests a later date ([[Persia.md]] or [[Hellenistic Period]], 5th–3rd centuries BCE) due to linguistic features, though the traditions it draws on may be ancient. The attribution to Solomon functions as a claim to wisdom literature status and royal authority.

### New Testament & Christian Interpretation

Christian tradition has long read the Song as an [[Allegory]] of the love between [[Christ]] and His [[_Concepts/Doctrines/Head of the Church]] (following the Jewish tradition of reading it as an allegory of God's love for [[Israel]]):
- **[[Ephesians 5_25–33.md]]** — Paul's teaching on marriage as a picture of Christ and the Church provides the hermeneutical key for the allegorical reading
- **[[Revelation 21_2.md]]** — The [[New Jerusalem]] descends as a bride adorned for her husband, echoing the Song's wedding imagery
- **[[2 Corinthians 11_2.md]]** — Paul describes the Church as a pure virgin presented to Christ
- Medieval commentators ([[_Concepts/Doctrines/House of Israel]], [[John]]) produced extensive spiritual commentaries on the Song
