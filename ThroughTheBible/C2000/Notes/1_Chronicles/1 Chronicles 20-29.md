## Overview

[[1 Chronicles 20.md]]. And it came to pass after the year was expired and the time that the kings go out to battle, Joab led forth the power of the army and he wasted the country of the children of Ammon and came and besieged [[Rabbah]]. But [[David]] tarried at [[Jerusalem]] and Joab smote Rabbah and destroyed it. And [[David]] took the crown of their king from off his head and found it to weigh a talent of gold and there were precious stones in it and it was set upon [[David]]'s head and he brought also exceeding much spoil out of the city and he brought out the people that were in it and cut them with saws and with harrows of iron and with axes. Even so dealt [[David]] with all the cities of the children of Ammon and [[David]] and all the people returned to [[Jerusalem]]. Now there is seemingly a discrepancy in the story here. First of all, the time of the year when they went out to war. There were times of the year which were better for fighting than other times of the year and so they just had times, this is the time to go to war. They had appointed times for warfare. We just fight all the time. But in those days there were just circumstances and certain periods, you know, they get the crops all in, everything is all set, now let's go out and fight for a while, you...

This concluding section of 1 Chronicles covers David's later military victories (ch. 20), the costly census and the purchase of the [[Temple Mount|threshing floor of Ornan]] (ch. 21), David's extensive preparations for the [[Temple|temple]] (ch. 22), the organization of the [[Levites]] and [[priesthood|priesthood]] (ch. 23-26), the civil and military administration of the kingdom (ch. 27), and David's final charge to [[Solomon]] and the generous offerings for the [[Temple|temple]] (ch. 28-29). The Chronicler portrays David as the ideal king who, despite his failings, established the pattern of [[Worship|worship]], organized the [[priesthood|priesthood]], and made exhaustive preparations for the [[Temple|temple]] that [[Solomon]] would build.

## Key Themes

- **Preparation for the Temple**: David's extensive preparations — materials, plans, and organization — for the [[Temple|temple]] that [[Solomon]] would build ([[1 Chronicles 22.md]], [[C2000/Notes/1_Chronicles/1 Chronicles 20-29.md]])
- **Levitical Worship**: The detailed organization of the [[Levites]], singers, gatekeepers, and priests as ministers of [[Worship|worship]] ([[1 Chronicles 23_23-26.md]])
- **Sin and Repentance**: David's sinful census and his [[Repentance|repentance]], demonstrating the Chronicler's theme of immediate divine response to sin ([[1 Chronicles 21.md]])
- **The Temple Mount**: The purchase of the threshing floor of [[Temple Mount|Ornan the Jebusite]] as the site of the future [[Temple|temple]] ([[1 Chronicles 21_18-30.md]], [[1 Chronicles 22_1.md]])
- **Generous Giving**: The leaders and people's joyful, willing offerings for the [[Temple|temple]], and David's example of sacrificial [[Worship|worship]] ([[1 Chronicles 29_1-9.md]])
- **David's Charge to Solomon**: David's exhortation to [[Solomon]] to serve God faithfully and build the [[Temple|temple]] ([[1 Chronicles 22_6-19.md]], [[1 Chronicles 28_9-10.md]])

## Connections

- **2 Samuel 24 (David's Census)**: The Chronicler's account of David's census parallels [[2 Samuel 24.md]] but emphasizes the [[Temple Mount|site of the future Temple]] (the threshing floor of Ornan) and omits details that might diminish David's role as the Temple-preparer
- **1 Kings 1-2 (Solomon's Succession)**: The Chronicler omits the court intrigues around Solomon's accession, presenting a unified, peaceful transition of power
- **Ezra-Nehemiah (Temple Restoration)**: The detailed organization of [[Levites]] and [[priesthood|priesthood]] in chapters 23-26 directly served the post-[[Exile|exile]] community's need to reinstate proper [[Worship|worship]] at the restored [[Temple|temple]] in [[Jerusalem]]
- **The Temple Mount**: David's purchase of Ornan's threshing floor ([[1 Chronicles 21.md]]) connects to [[2 Chronicles 3_1.md]] (Solomon begins building on that site) and ultimately to [[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 22.md]] (Abraham's offering of Isaac on [[Mount Moriah]])
- **Giving to God**: David's call for freewill offerings ([[1 Chronicles 29.md]]) is echoed in [[Exodus 35.md]] (offerings for the Tabernacle) and [[2 Corinthians 9.md]] (cheerful giving)
- **The Davidic Covenant**: David's charge to Solomon in [[1 Chronicles 28_9-10.md]] echoes the conditions of the [[Davidic covenant]] from [[1 Chronicles 17.md]]

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- Written ca. 450-400 BC, after the Babylonian exile, likely by Ezra the scribe (Jewish tradition)
- Chronicles retells the history of Israel from a post-exilic, priestly perspective — emphasizing the Davidic Covenant, the Temple, and proper worship
- The genealogies (1 Chronicles 1-9) emphasize the continuity of God's people from Adam through David, crucial for a post-exilic community re-establishing its identity
- The focus is on Judah (the southern kingdom) and David's dynasty — the northern kingdom is largely ignored
- David's preparations for the Temple (1 Chronicles 22-29): the centrality of worship in the Chronicler's theology
- The Chronicler's perspective differs from Samuel/Kings: he omits David's sin with Bathsheba and focuses on positive aspects
- Archaeological context: the Chronicler had access to sources now lost ("the books of the kings of Israel and Judah," "the commentary of the book of the kings")
