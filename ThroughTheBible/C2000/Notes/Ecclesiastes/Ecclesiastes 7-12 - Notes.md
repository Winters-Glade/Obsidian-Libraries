# Ecclesiastes 7-12 - Notes

## Summary

Chuck Smith explores Solomon's reflections in Ecclesiastes — the Preacher's search for meaning "under the sun." Through examining pleasure, wealth, wisdom, labor, and companionship, Solomon concludes that all is vanity apart from God, ultimately pointing to the fear of God and keeping His commandments as the whole duty of man.

### Ancient Near Eastern Parallels

Ecclesiastes stands within an international tradition of skeptical or "vanity" literature. The Mesopotamian [[Ballad of Early Rulers]] (2nd millennium BCE) laments "where are those kings of former times?" — the same [[Memento Mori]] theme that pervades Kohelet's reflections on death. The Sumerian [[Nig-Nam Nu-Kal]] ("Nothing is of Value") declares the vanity of all human achievement. Egyptian [[Harpers Songs]] (c. 2000 BCE) alternate between hopeful confidence in the afterlife and skeptical [[Carpe Diem]]: "Make holiday, and weary not thereof! For none may take his goods with him." This same tension between enjoying God's gifts and facing death's finality runs through Ecclesiastes.

The [[Dialogue of Pessimism]] (Akkadian) and the [[Dispute Between a Man and His Ba]] (Egyptian) both explore the futility of human striving — themes Kohelet transposes into the framework of [[Yahwistic]] faith, concluding not with despair but with the [[Fear of the Lord]].

### Hebrew Genre & Structure

Ecclesiastes uses a [[Frame Narrative]]: a third-person narrator introduces and concludes (1:1–2, 12:9–14), while [[Qoheleth]] speaks in first person for the main body (1:12–12:8). The book's structure follows an [[Inclusio]] — bracketed by the refrain "[[Vanity]] of vanities, all is vanity" (1:2, 12:8). The central section (1:12–6:9) presents Qoheleth's investigation of life under the sun, followed by his conclusions (6:10–11:6), framed by an opening poem on the cycles of nature (1:3–11) and a closing poem on youth and old age (11:7–12:8).

The Hebrew word [[Hevel]] (הבל) — translated as "vanity" — literally means "vapor" or "breath," suggesting not meaninglessness but [[Transience]]: life is fleeting, elusive, impossible to grasp and hold.

### Historical Background

The book identifies its author as [[Qoheleth]] ("one who speaks before an assembly") — traditionally [[Solomon]], writing in old age. Most critical scholars date the book to the [[Persian Period|Persian]] or early [[Hellenistic Period]] (c. 450–250 BCE), based on [[Aramaic]] loanwords, Persian administrative terms, and its philosophical resonance with Greek [[Stoicism]] (the acceptance of fate) and [[Epicureanism]] (the embrace of simple pleasures). The epilogue (12:9–14) is widely regarded as a later scribal addition that reorients the book's skepticism toward orthodox piety.
