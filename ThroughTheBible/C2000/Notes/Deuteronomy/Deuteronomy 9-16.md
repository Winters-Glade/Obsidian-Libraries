## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith covers Moses' warnings against [[Self-Righteousness]], the [[Golden Calf]] incident, and the question "What does the Lord require of you?" — answered by reverencing God, walking in His ways, loving Him, and serving Him with all the heart. Moses calls for [[Circumcision of the Heart]], sets blessings on [[Mount Gerizim]] and curses on [[Mount Ebal]], and instructs on worship in [[The Central Sanctuary|the appointed place]], [[Dietary Laws|dietary laws]], the [[The Year of Release|Year of Release]], [[Bond Slave by Choice|bond slaves by choice]], [[False Prophets]], and the [[The Three Annual Feasts|three annual feasts]]. The theme is wholehearted devotion to God alone.

## Summary

Moses addresses the new generation on the verge of crossing the [[Jordan River|Jordan]]. He warns them not to think their own righteousness earned the land — it is because of the wickedness of the [[Canaanites]] and God's promise to the fathers. He recounts the [[Golden Calf]] and Israel's repeated rebellions, showing they are a stiff-necked people. In chapter 10, he asks the profound question: "What does the Lord require of you?" — to fear God, walk in His ways, love Him, serve Him with all the heart and soul, and keep His commandments. He calls for [[Circumcision of the Heart]] — cutting off the flesh life. Chapter 11 sets the [[Choice]] before Israel: blessing on Gerizim for [[Obedience]], curse on Ebal for disobedience. Chapters 12-16 detail the laws for worship in the place God chooses, the destruction of pagan religious sites, dietary distinctions, the [[The Year of Release|Year of Release]] (every seventh year debts are forgiven), the [[Bond Slave by Choice|bond slave]] who chooses to serve for life (ear pierced with an awl), and the [[The Three Annual Feasts|three annual feasts]] where every male must appear before the Lord. [[False Prophets]] who lead people to other gods — even if they work signs — are to be put to death. Judges are warned against bribery, and the people are commanded to rejoice before the Lord.

## Key Takeaways

1. God's requirement of humanity is to fear Him, walk in His ways, love Him, and serve Him with wholehearted devotion. This is the divine ideal — but when we fail, God's solution is faith in His Son.
2. Self-righteousness is a constant danger. When God uses us, we must not take credit — it is not our holiness but His grace and the wickedness of those He judges.
3. Circumcision of the heart is the true mark of God's people — the outward ritual is empty without inward reality.
4. God sets before every person a choice between blessing and curse. The choice is ours; God warns and woos, but He will not override our will.
5. False prophets may perform genuine signs — the test is not the miracle but whether they lead people to the true God. We must not be drawn by supernatural phenomena alone.
6. The bond slave by choice (ear pierced with an awl) is a beautiful picture of the believer who voluntarily declares, "I love my Master and will not go free" — a picture of our commitment to Christ.

## Historical & Cultural Context
The golden calf incident (Deut 9:7-21) echoes the broader ancient Near Eastern pattern of calf/bull worship. The [[Apis Bull]] in Egypt and the [[Baal]] cult in Canaan both featured bovine imagery as symbols of fertility and divine power. Jeroboam I later erected golden calves at [[Dan]] and [[Bethel]] ([[1 Kings 12_28-30.md]]), using the same language: "These are your gods, O Israel, which brought you up out of the land of Egypt." The ceremony on [[Mount Gerizim]] and [[Mount Ebal]] (Deut 11:26-32; 27:1-26) reflects the ancient Near Eastern practice of covenant ratification ceremonies. The bond slave with the pierced ear (Deut 15:12-18) parallels the [[Code of Hammurabi]] (c. 1750 BC, Law #117-118) which regulated debt-slavery but limited it to three years — Israel's law of the seventh-year release was a more humane extension of this concept. The dietary laws of Deuteronomy 14 bear striking similarities to [[Ugaritic]] and [[Phoenician]] food taboos, though Israel's were distinctively tied to holiness theology.

## Archaeological Evidence
The [[Deir Alla Inscription]] (c. 800 BC), found in modern Jordan, mentions the prophet [[Balaam]] son of Beor — the same figure who appears in [[Numbers 22_22-24.md]] — providing remarkable extra-biblical confirmation of a prophet otherwise known only from Scripture. This inscription describes Balaam as a "seer of the gods," matching the biblical portrait of a non-Israelite prophet who could receive revelation from Yahweh. Excavations at [[Mount Ebal]] by archaeologist [[Adam Zertal]] (1980s) uncovered a large stone structure identified by some scholars as Joshua's altar described in Deuteronomy 27:5-7 — an altar of uncut stones with evidence of animal sacrifice dating to the early Iron Age (c. 1250 BC). The [[Mesad Hashavyahu Ostracon]] (c. 630 BC) reflects Israelite legal practices concerning debt and garment pledges that align with Deuteronomy 15:7-11 and 24:10-13, showing real-world application of these laws. The [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] (4QDeut) contain confirmation of the textual integrity of this section.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
[[Justin Martyr]] (c. 100-165 AD) in his *Dialogue with Trypho* used Deuteronomy 10:16 ("circumcise the foreskin of your heart") to argue that the Old Testament itself pointed beyond physical circumcision to spiritual transformation, which he identified with Christian conversion. [[Clement of Alexandria]] (c. 150-215 AD) interpreted the dietary laws of Deuteronomy 14 allegorically, seeing the clean and unclean animals as types of virtuous and vicious souls. [[Jerome]] (c. 347-420 AD) in his *Commentary on Deuteronomy* noted that the law of the bond slave (Deut 15) prefigured Christ, who "did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped" but took the form of a servant. [[The Venerable Bede]] (c. 673-735 AD) wrote an allegorical commentary on Deuteronomy, applying the three annual feasts — Passover, Pentecost, and Tabernacles — to the Christian liturgical year and the stages of spiritual growth.

## New Testament Connections
The question "What does the Lord require of you?" (Deut 10:12-13) finds its New Testament answer in [[Micah 6_8]] and is summed up by Jesus in [[Matthew 22_37-40.md]]: love God and love your neighbor. [[Paul the Apostle]] directly uses the concept of "circumcision of the heart" in [[Romans 2_28-29.md]], arguing that a true Jew is one inwardly circumcised by the Spirit, not merely by the letter. The bond slave choosing to serve his master forever (Deut 15:16-17) is applied by Paul to Christ in [[Philippians 2_7.md]] — Jesus "took upon Him the form of a servant" — and serves as a model for the believer's voluntary devotion ([[Romans 6_16-18.md]]). The law against false prophets (Deut 13:1-5) is echoed in [[Matthew 7_15-20.md]] where Jesus warns against false prophets who come in sheep's clothing but inwardly are ravening wolves — to be known by their fruits. The three annual feasts — [[Passover]], [[Pentecost]], [[Tabernacles]] — find their fulfillment in Christ ([[_Bible/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 5_7.md]], [[Acts 2_1-4]], [[Zechariah 14_16-19.md]]).
