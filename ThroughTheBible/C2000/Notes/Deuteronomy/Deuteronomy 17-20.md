## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith continues through Moses' final instructions, covering rules for future kings, the provision for priests and Levites, warnings against [[Occult Practices]], a major [[Messianic Prophecy]] about a [[Prophet Like Moses]], the test for [[False Prophets]], [[Cities of Refuge]], and laws for warfare. God anticipates Israel's future demand for a king and provides regulations to limit royal excess. The prophecy of a coming Prophet like Moses is identified by Peter in Acts as pointing to [[Jesus Christ]], though the Jews still await a human Messiah, which opens the door for the [[Antichrist]].

## Summary

In chapter 17, Moses instructs that no blemished sacrifice may be offered — God is honored by gifts that cost us something. Idolatry is punishable by death, requiring two or three witnesses. Hard cases are to be brought to the priests. God anticipates Israel's future demand for a king and prescribes limits: the king must not multiply horses (trust in military might), multiply wives (lest his heart be turned away), nor greatly multiply silver and gold. The king must write his own copy of the [[Law]] and read it daily. Chapter 18 provides for the priest's portion and warns against the abominable practices of the nations — divination, observing times (astrology), enchantment, witchcraft, spiritism, and necromancy. God then gives a great prophecy: "I will raise them up a [[Prophet Like Moses|Prophet from among their brethren, like unto thee]], and will put My words in His mouth." Peter identifies this as [[Jesus Christ]]. Chapter 19 covers [[Cities of Refuge]] for accidental manslaughter and laws against removing landmarks and bearing false witness. Chapter 20 addresses warfare: the priest encourages the army not to fear horses and chariots because God fights for them. Those who have built a house, planted a vineyard, or are betrothed are exempted from battle; the fearful are also sent home. When besieging cities, peace is offered first. The seven [[Canaanite Nations]] are to be utterly destroyed because their practices were so vile and infectious — like a rabid dog that must be put down to protect the innocent. Fruit trees are not to be cut down during siege.

## Key Takeaways

1. God is honored when we give Him our best — not our cast-offs. David's refusal to offer to God that which cost him nothing is the standard for our giving.
2. The king's three restrictions (horses, wives, wealth) were all violated by Solomon — proving that God's warnings are always relevant and ignoring them brings certain consequences.
3. The command for the king to write and read the law daily is a model for all leaders: success comes from meditating in God's Word.
4. The prophecy of a Prophet like Moses points to Jesus Christ, but Israel still awaits a human Messiah — a misunderstanding that will lead to their acceptance of the Antichrist.
5. God's order to destroy the Canaanite nations was an act of mercy toward Israel, protecting them from the infectious, self-destructive practices of those corrupt cultures — analogous to putting down a rabid dog to protect children.
6. Fear in battle is dispelled by the consciousness of God's presence — "If God be for us, who can be against us?" The exemptions from battle show God's care for the personal concerns and emotional readiness of His people.

## Historical & Cultural Context
The restrictions placed on the king in Deuteronomy 17:14-20 are unique in ancient Near Eastern law codes — no other known document from this period limits royal power in this way. The prohibitions against multiplying horses (particularly from Egypt) reflect the geopolitical reality that Egypt controlled the primary source of chariot horses, and reliance on Egypt was a recurring temptation for Israelite kings (cf. [[Isaiah 31_1]]). Solomon's violation of all three restrictions — accumulating 1,400 chariots (horses), 700 wives (including foreign women), and vast quantities of gold ([[1 Kings 10_14-11_8.md]]) — is a striking historical confirmation of this prophecy's relevance. The "cities of refuge" (Deut 19:1-13) find parallels in ancient Near Eastern asylum practices, such as the sanctuary at [[Anatolia]] and the temples in [[Egypt]] and [[Greece]] that provided refuge for fugitives. The warfare laws of Deuteronomy 20, including the offer of peace before siege and the exemption of fruit trees, reflect a humanitarian standard unmatched in ancient Near Eastern military codes such as the [[Assyrian]] annals, which typically boasted of total devastation.

## Archaeological Evidence
The [[Tel Dan Stele]] (c. 840 BC) mentions the "House of David" and provides the first extra-biblical evidence of the Davidic dynasty, confirming the historical reality of the kingship that Deuteronomy 17 anticipates. The [[Taylor Prism]] (Sennacherib's Annals, c. 690 BC) describes the Assyrian king's siege of Jerusalem in 701 BC during Hezekiah's reign, directly illustrating the warfare context of Deuteronomy 20 — the "nation from afar" coming "as the eagle flies" (Deut 28:49). Sennacherib's annals record his conquest of 46 fortified cities of Judah, a vivid example of the siege warfare described in Deuteronomy. The [[Ketef Hinnom Silver Amulets]] (c. 600 BC) contain the Priestly Blessing, demonstrating the ongoing cultic and priestly traditions that Deuteronomy 18 regulates. Excavations at [[Hazor]], [[Gezer]], and [[Megiddo]] — cities Solomon fortified — have revealed Solomonic-era gates and administrative buildings, confirming the historical reality of the monarchy period Deuteronomy describes.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
[[Justin Martyr]] (c. 100-165 AD) in his *First Apology* and *Dialogue with Trypho* extensively argued that the Prophet like Moses (Deut 18:15) was [[Jesus Christ]], pointing to the parallels between Moses and Jesus — both were prophets who performed miracles, delivered God's law, and were rejected by their own people. [[Origen of Alexandria]] (c. 185-254 AD) in *Against Celsus* used the cities of refuge as types of Christ, to whom the sinner flees from the avenger (Satan) for safety. [[Augustine of Hippo]] (354-430 AD) in *The City of God* interpreted the laws of warfare in Deuteronomy 20 as establishing just war principles, arguing that peace should always be offered first and that war must be a last resort. [[Eusebius of Caesarea]] (c. 260-340 AD) in his *Demonstratio Evangelica* argued that the prophecy of a Prophet like Moses was fulfilled in Jesus, who alone matched Moses in being a mediator of a new covenant, a lawgiver, and the leader of God's people. [[John Calvin]] (1509-1564) in his *Harmony of the Law* treated the king's duty to write and read the law daily as the model for all Christian rulers.

## New Testament Connections
[[_Bible/Acts/Acts 3_22-23.md]] records Peter's explicit identification of the Prophet like Moses (Deut 18:15-19) with [[Jesus Christ]]: "For Moses truly said unto the fathers, A prophet shall the Lord your God raise up unto you of your brethren, like unto me; him shall ye hear in all things whatsoever he shall say unto you." [[Acts]] repeats this identification in Stephen's speech before the Sanhedrin. Jesus Himself alludes to this prophecy in [[John 5_43-47.md]] when He says, "If you believed Moses, you would believe Me, for he wrote about Me." The cities of refuge (Deut 19:1-13) are interpreted in [[Hebrews 6_18.md]] as a type of the hope set before us in Christ — "fled for refuge to lay hold upon the hope set before us." The warfare principle that "the Lord your God is He who goes with you, to fight for you against your enemies, to save you" (Deut 20:1-4) is applied spiritually in [[Romans 8_31]]: "If God be for us, who can be against us?"
