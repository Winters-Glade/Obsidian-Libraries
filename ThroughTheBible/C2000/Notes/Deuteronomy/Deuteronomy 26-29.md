## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on the [[Covenant Renewal]] section of Deuteronomy, where Moses brings his final sermons to a climax. Chapter 26 gives the liturgy for [[Firstfruits]] and [[Tithing]]. Chapters 27-28 present the dramatic ceremony on [[Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal]] — the blessings of obedience and the terrifying curses of disobedience. Chapter 29 recounts the covenant made in [[Moab]], warning against apostasy and self-deception. Throughout, Moses emphasizes that [[Obedience]] to God's commandments is the condition for blessing in the land.

## Summary

Chapter 26 contains the liturgical confession to be recited when bringing firstfruits to the sanctuary, rehearsing the history from "a Syrian ready to perish was my father" (Jacob) through the sojourn in Egypt, the exodus, and the entry into the Promised Land. It also gives the prayer of the third-year tithe. God commands His people to rejoice in every good thing He has given. Chapter 27 instructs the building of an altar of uncut stones on Mount Ebal, where the law is to be written on plaster, and the tribes divide on Mount Gerizim (blessings) and Mount Ebal (curses). The Levites pronounce twelve specific curses, each answered by "Amen." Chapter 28 is the great chapter of blessings and curses — the blessings for obedience are comprehensive (blessed in the city, field, basket, store, coming in, going out), but the curses for disobedience are even more extensive, including disease, drought, military defeat, madness, blindness, siege so severe that parents eat their own children, and finally worldwide dispersion. Chapter 29 renews the covenant in Moab, reminding the people of God's miraculous provision over 40 years and warning against any heart that secretly turns to other gods.

## Key Takeaways

1. God commands His people to rejoice — joy is not optional but an act of obedience and a powerful witness to the world.
2. The blessings and curses of Deuteronomy 28 are not abstract; they were fulfilled in Israel's history — the blessings in the days of David and Solomon, the curses in the Assyrian and Babylonian captivities and the Roman destruction of Jerusalem.
3. The Mosaic covenant was conditional on obedience; the New Covenant in Christ is based on His finished work, accessed by faith, not law-keeping.
4. A nation that honors God is blessed; a nation that turns from God experiences the curses of moral and social decay — this principle applies to any nation, including the United States.
5. Worship should be simple and focused on God, not distracted by ornate buildings or human artistry.
6. The secret things belong to God, but what is revealed is for us and our children to obey — we are responsible for what God has made known.

## Historical & Cultural Context
The ceremony on [[Mount Gerizim]] and [[Mount Ebal]] (Deut 27) follows the format of ancient Near Eastern covenant renewal ceremonies. The twelve curses pronounced by the Levites parallel the curse sections of Hittite and Assyrian suzerainty treaties. The description of the altar of uncut stones (Deut 27:5-6) reflects an ancient taboo against using iron tools on sacred stones, a practice found among many ancient cultures including the [[Canaanite]] *massebot* (standing stones) worship sites. The liturgical confession in Deuteronomy 26:5-10 — "A wandering Aramean was my father" — is one of the oldest creedal statements in the Bible, summarizing Israel's redemptive history from Jacob through the Exodus. This confession was later incorporated into the Passover seder liturgy (*Maggid*), demonstrating its enduring liturgical significance. The covenant in Moab (Deut 29) is distinguished from the covenant at Horeb (Sinai), suggesting a renewal and expansion of the original covenant for the new generation about to enter the land.

## Archaeological Evidence
The [[Taylor Prism]] (Sennacherib's Annals, c. 690 BC) provides dramatic confirmation of the siege curses in Deuteronomy 28:49-57. Sennacherib's description of his siege of Jerusalem in 701 BC matches the prophecy of a "nation from afar, as the eagle flies" — the Assyrian campaign symbols included the eagle. The [[Lachish Reliefs]] (from Sennacherib's palace at Nineveh, now in the British Museum) graphically depict the siege and fall of Lachish, showing the horror of warfare described in Deuteronomy 28 — people fleeing, impaled captives, and the utter devastation of a fortified city. Josephus ([[Josephus]], *The Jewish War*, Book 5-6) provides a first-hand account of the Roman siege of Jerusalem in 70 AD, where the curses of Deuteronomy 28 were fulfilled in graphic detail — starvation so severe that mothers ate their children, as prophesied in verse 53. The [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] include the [[Temple Scroll]] (11Q19) which develops the Deuteronomy 27 altar law in detail. Excavations of Mount Ebal by Adam Zertal revealed a large structure interpreted by some as the altar described in Deuteronomy 27.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
[[Origen of Alexandria]] (c. 185-254 AD) in his *Homilies on Deuteronomy* interpreted the blessings and curses of Deuteronomy 28 allegorically, seeing in them the spiritual rewards for virtue and the consequences of sin. [[John Chrysostom]] (c. 347-407 AD) preached a series of homilies on Deuteronomy emphasizing that the curses of the law were intended to drive people to seek God's mercy — a precursor to the argument that the law was a "schoolmaster to bring us to Christ." [[Theodoret of Cyrus]] (c. 393-460 AD) noted that the firstfruits liturgy (Deut 26) expresses the proper posture of humility and gratitude before God. [[Jonathan Edwards]] (1703-1758), the great American theologian, preached his famous sermon "[[Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God]]" from Deuteronomy 32:35 — "Their foot shall slide in due time" — using the imagery of divine judgment from Deuteronomy's curses to call listeners to repentance. [[John Calvin]] (1509-1564) wrote extensively on the blessings and curses, arguing that they demonstrate God's moral governance of nations.

## New Testament Connections
The New Covenant prophesied through Jeremiah ([[_Bible/Jeremiah/Jeremiah 31_31-34.md]]) and established by Christ ([[Luke 22_20]]) is the direct fulfillment of the covenant theology of Deuteronomy. Where the Mosaic covenant promised temporal blessings for obedience and curses for disobedience, the New Covenant offers eternal life through faith in Christ's finished work. Paul contrasts the two covenants in [[Galatians 3_10-14.md]], noting that "cursed is every one that continues not in all things which are written in the book of the law to do them" (quoting Deut 27:26) — showing that the law's curse points us to Christ's redemption. The principles of firstfruits and joyful giving (Deut 26) are carried into the New Testament in [[2 Corinthians 9_7.md]] — "God loves a cheerful giver" — and in [[1 Corinthians 15_20.md]] where Christ is called the "firstfruits of them that slept." The warning against a heart that "turns away from the Lord" (Deut 29:18-19) is applied in [[Hebrews 12_15.md]] — "looking diligently lest any man fail of the grace of God; lest any root of bitterness springing up trouble you."
