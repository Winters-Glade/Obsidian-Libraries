## Summary

In the 41st chapter of Isaiah, in the first part of the chapter, God begins his predictions concerning Cyrus, who was not yet born. A man who was not to be born for 150 years. But God begins to talk about him. How he's going to raise him up. How he's going to prosper him.

How he's going to give him a kingdom and subdue nations before him. Now, as we progress in our study tonight, we'll find that God actually names him. In order to prove that I'm really God, there's no one else like me.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Chapters 41–42 begin the "Servant Songs" of Isaiah. Chapter 41 introduces **Cyrus**, the Persian king who would later (539 BC) conquer Babylon and decree the return of the Jewish exiles — a prophecy delivered approximately **150 years before Cyrus was born** (he reigned 559–530 BC). This remarkable predictive prophecy is noted by the historian Josephus (*Antiquities* 11.1.1–2), who records that Cyrus himself read Isaiah's prophecy and was moved to fulfill it.

## ✝️ New Testament Fulfillment

**Isaiah 42:1–4** presents the first of the four Servant Songs, depicting the Lord's chosen Servant who "will not cry out or raise His voice" and "a bruised reed He will not break." This Servant is gentle yet brings justice to the nations.

## 📜 Dead Sea Scrolls Connection

> [!qt-verified] Dead Sea Scrolls Connection

The **Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ)** — one of the seven original Dead Sea Scrolls discovered at Qumran in 1947 — contains the complete Book of Isaiah and dates to approximately **125 BCE**, making it roughly **1,000 years older** than the oldest previously known Masoretic Hebrew manuscripts. It is preserved almost in its entirety across 54 columns on 17 sheets of parchment, measuring over 24 feet in length.

### Textual Significance

When compared to the Masoretic Text (MT), 1QIsaᵃ contains **more than 2,600 textual variants**, though the overall order and content remain consistent. A 2021 AI-assisted paleographic analysis by the University of Groningen determined that **two scribes** copied the scroll, with the division occurring at column 27 — precisely at the boundary scholars often mark between Proto-Isaiah (chapters 1–39) and Deutero-Isaiah (chapters 40–66).

The scroll is now housed at the **Shrine of the Book**, Israel Museum, Jerusalem, and has been fully digitized in 1,200 megapixel resolution.

**Isaiah 42:1–4** — The first Servant Song is directly quoted in **Matthew 12:17–21**, identifying Jesus as the Servant in whom God delights. Matthew highlights Jesus' healing ministry and His tendency to tell people not to publicize Him as the fulfillment of "He will not quarrel or cry out."

**Isaiah 42:6–7** — The Servant as a "light to the Gentiles" is cited by Simeon in **Luke 2:32** and by Paul and Barnabas in **Acts 13:47** as the basis for taking the gospel to the Gentiles.
