## Summary

When men made chapter and verse divisions, they did make mistakes. The Word of God is divinely inspired. It's inerrant. But men, for the sake of helping us to find scriptures and to memorize passages, divided the Bible into chapter and verses, and it's a very convenient way to reference. However, many times they made the divisions in the wrong place, and in our reading we are prone to read to an end of a chapter and then quit until the next reading.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

**Isaiah 53** is the fourth and most famous of the Servant Songs, depicting the Suffering Servant who "was pierced for our transgressions" and "crushed for our iniquities." This passage has been central to Jewish-Christian dialogue for two millennia. The **Septuagint** (Greek translation, c. 200 BC) translation of this chapter was especially influential in early Christian understanding of Jesus' atoning death.

Chapter 54 promises restoration and an everlasting covenant of peace — "For the mountains may depart and the hills be removed, but My steadfast love shall not depart from you" (54:10). Chapter 55 issues the great invitation: "Come, everyone who thirsts, come to the waters" — a universal call to salvation.

### The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ) on Isaiah 53
The Dead Sea Scrolls preserve this chapter with remarkable fidelity. One notable variant: in 1QIsaᵃ, the phrase "he made intercession for the transgressors" (53:12, MT) includes a grammatical form that some scholars argue strengthens the interpretive reading of the Servant as an individual (rather than the nation of Israel). The scroll's preservation of this passage confirms that the Suffering Servant interpretation was present in the textual tradition well before the Christian era (c. 125 BCE).

## 📜 Dead Sea Scrolls Connection

> [!qt-verified] Dead Sea Scrolls Connection

The **Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ)** — one of the seven original Dead Sea Scrolls discovered at Qumran in 1947 — contains the complete Book of Isaiah and dates to approximately **125 BCE**, making it roughly **1,000 years older** than the oldest previously known Masoretic Hebrew manuscripts. It is preserved almost in its entirety across 54 columns on 17 sheets of parchment, measuring over 24 feet in length.

### Textual Significance

When compared to the Masoretic Text (MT), 1QIsaᵃ contains **more than 2,600 textual variants**, though the overall order and content remain consistent. A 2021 AI-assisted paleographic analysis by the University of Groningen determined that **two scribes** copied the scroll, with the division occurring at column 27 — precisely at the boundary scholars often mark between Proto-Isaiah (chapters 1–39) and Deutero-Isaiah (chapters 40–66).

The scroll is now housed at the **Shrine of the Book**, Israel Museum, Jerusalem, and has been fully digitized in 1,200 megapixel resolution.

## ✝️ New Testament Fulfillment

**Isaiah 53** is the most quoted Old Testament chapter in the New Testament after the Psalms:

- **Matthew 8:17** cites 53:4 — "He took up our infirmities and carried our sorrows" — in the context of Jesus' healing ministry.
- **John 12:38** cites 53:1 — "Who has believed our message?" — as a summary of Israel's unbelief.
- **Acts 8:26–40** recounts Philip's encounter with the Ethiopian eunuch reading Isaiah 53, leading to the eunuch's baptism after Philip explained that the passage referred to Jesus.
- **Romans 10:16** cites 53:1 again regarding the Gentile response to the gospel.
- **1 Peter 2:22–25** directly alludes to 53:4–12, describing Christ as the Shepherd who "by His wounds you have been healed" (53:5).
- **Hebrews 9:28** echoes 53:11–12 — Christ "appeared once for all at the culmination of the ages to do away with sin by the sacrifice of Himself."

**Isaiah 55:1–3** — The invitation to "come, buy wine and milk without money" is echoed in **Revelation 22:17**: "Let the one who is thirsty come; and let the one who wishes take the free gift of the water of life."
