## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### The Olivet Discourse
Mark 13, known as the "Little Apocalypse," is Jesus' prophecy about the destruction of the [[Temple (Jerusalem)|Temple]] and the end of the age. The disciples' admiration of the Temple (Mark 13:1) — "what manner of stones and what buildings!" — reflects the massive scale of [[Herod the Great|Herod's]] Temple, whose stones could be up to 40 feet long. The [[Mediterranean.md]] (the *Kotel*) is part of this Herodian retaining wall.

### The Abomination of Desolation
The "abomination of desolation standing where it ought not" (Mark 13:14) references [[Daniel 9_27]], [[Daniel 11_31.md]], and [[Daniel 12_11.md]]. The aorist participle *hestēkota* ("standing") suggests a specific historical event. Many scholars identify this with the Roman army surrounding [[Jerusalem]] in AD 66–70, or possibly Caligula's attempt to place his statue in the Temple (AD 40). The parenthetical "let the reader understand" suggests Mark is writing for a community under existential threat.

### False Messiahs
The warning against false christs and false prophets (Mark 13:21-23) was pertinent in first-century Judaism. Josephus records numerous messianic figures: [[Theudas]] (AD 44–46), the Egyptian prophet (AD 52–58), and Simon bar Giora (AD 69–70). The early church also faced deceivers claiming apostolic authority.

### The Fig Tree Parable
The parable of the fig tree (Mark 13:28-31) teaches discernment of the times. Jesus' statement "this generation shall not pass away until all these things are done" (Mark 13:30) is one of the most debated verses — "generation" (*genea*) could mean the contemporary generation that would see [[Jerusalem]]'s fall, or the Jewish people as a nation, or the generation that sees the final signs.

### Synoptic Parallels
The Olivet Discourse appears in all three Synoptics ([[_Bible/Matthew/Matthew 24.md]], [[Luke 21_5-36.md]]). Luke's version is notable for specifying the "times of the Gentiles" and predicting [[Jerusalem]]'s trampling. Mark's version is considered the earliest and most apocalyptic.
