## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### Marriage and Divorce
The teaching on divorce (Mark 10:1-12) presents Jesus' most stringent view — no divorce allowed, and remarriage constitutes adultery. Mark lacks the Matthean exception clause (*porneia*). This absolute standard reflects the original creation order ([[Genesis 1_27.md]], [[Genesis 2_24.md]]). Roman and Jewish law both permitted divorce, making Jesus' teaching counter-cultural.

### The Rich Young Ruler
The encounter with the rich man (Mark 10:17-31) raises the question of "good teacher" — Jesus redirects: "No one is good except God alone." The command to sell all highlights the incompatibility of wealth and whole-hearted discipleship. The disciples' astonishment reflects the common Jewish belief that wealth was a sign of God's favor (the "retribution principle" in [[_Bible/Deuteronomy/Deuteronomy 28.md]]).

### The Triumphal Entry
The [[Triumphal Entry]] (Mark 11:1-11) fulfills [[Zechariah 9_9.md]]. Jesus enters [[Jerusalem]] from the [[Mount of Olives]] via the eastern gate, along the route prescribed for the [[The Red Sea Crossing]] ceremony and the scapegoat on [[Yom Kippur]]. The crowds spread cloaks and branches, echoing the reception of kings (cf. [[2 Kings]]).

### Cleansing the Temple
The cleansing of the Temple (Mark 11:15-19) is placed by Mark immediately after the entry, and the fig tree narrative (Mark 11:12-14, 20-25) is used as a "sandwich" around it — the barren fig tree symbolizing the fruitless Temple establishment.

### The Authority Question
The chief priests, scribes, and elders challenge Jesus' authority (Mark 11:27-33). Jesus' counter-question about John's baptism places them in a dilemma: if they say "from heaven" they must explain why they didn't believe John; if "from man," they fear the crowd who held John as a prophet.
