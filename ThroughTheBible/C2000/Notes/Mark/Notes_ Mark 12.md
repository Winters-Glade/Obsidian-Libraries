## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### The Parable of the Tenants
The parable of the wicked tenants (Mark 12:1-12) echoes [[Isaiah 5_1-7.md]] (the Song of the Vineyard). The servants sent to collect fruit represent the prophets; the "beloved son" represents Jesus. The tenants' rejection mirrors Israel's history of rejecting God's messengers. The quotation of [[Psalm 118_22-23.md]] ("the stone the builders rejected") was a key early Christian text.

### Tribute to Caesar
The question about paying taxes (Mark 12:13-17) was a trap: if Jesus said yes, he would lose popular support; if no, he could be reported to Rome for sedition. The [[Darius.md]] with Caesar's image and inscription was itself offensive to Jewish sensibilities (the image violated the second commandment). Jesus' response — "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's, and to God the things that are God's" — establishes a principle of dual loyalty.

### The Resurrection Debate
The [[Sadducees]]' question about the woman with seven husbands (Mark 12:18-27) was designed to make resurrection look absurd. Jesus' response appeals to [[Exodus 3_6]] ("I am the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob"), arguing that God is the God of the living, not the dead — implying the patriarchs are alive to God. This use of the [[Torah]] (the only Scriptures the Sadducees accepted) was masterful.

### The Great Commandment
The [[Shema]] ([[Deuteronomy 6_4-5.md]]) combined with [[Leviticus 19_18.md]] (Mark 12:28-34) represents the heart of Jewish ethics. The scribe's positive response — agreeing that love is "more than all whole burnt offerings and sacrifices" — echoes the prophetic tradition ([[Hosea]], [[Micah 6_6-8]]).

### The Widow's Mite
The widow's offering (Mark 12:41-44) contrasts with the scribes who "devour widows' houses" (Mark 12:38-40). The [[Election.md]] (mite) was the smallest Jewish bronze coin, worth about 1/128 of a denarius. The widow's gift of her entire livelihood (*holos ton bion*) is praised over the large sums of the wealthy.
