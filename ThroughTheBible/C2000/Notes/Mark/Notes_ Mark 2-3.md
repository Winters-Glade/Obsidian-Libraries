## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### Healing the Paralytic
The healing of the paralytic (Mark 2:1-12) is notable for the crowds so dense that the roof was dug open. Galilean homes of this period typically had flat roofs made of thatch, mud, and cross-beams, accessible by an external staircase — making the roof-opening both plausible and vivid. The declaration "Your sins are forgiven" would have been seen as blasphemous to Jewish ears, as only God could forgive sins (cf. [[Isaiah 43_25.md]]).

### Calling Levi (Matthew)
The call of [[Levi]] the tax collector (Mark 2:13-17) — identified with [[Paul the Apostle.md]] — reflects the despised status of tax collectors (*publicani*) in Jewish society. They were considered traitors who collaborated with the Roman occupation and were often dishonest, collecting more than required. Eating with "tax collectors and sinners" violated the Pharisaic standards of table fellowship.

### The Sabbath Controversies
Mark 2:23-28 and 3:1-6 present two Sabbath controversies in quick succession. The dispute about David eating the showbread ([[1 Samuel 21_1-6.md]]) uses a rabbinic interpretive method. The healing of the man with the withered hand on the Sabbath raises the principle of *pikuach nefesh* — the Jewish law that saving a life overrides the Sabbath.

### Choosing the Twelve
The appointment of the [[The twelve apostles.md]] (Mark 3:13-19) on a mountain deliberately echoes Moses summoning the twelve tribes at [[Mount Sinai]]. The number 12 symbolizes the restoration of Israel. The list includes [[Judas Iscariot]] — "Iscariot" likely meaning "man of Kerioth" (a town in [[Judea]]), identifying him as the only non-Galilean among the Twelve.
