## Historical & Cultural Context

### The Parable of the Sower
The parable of the sower (Mark 4:1-20) is the first of Mark's parables, and Jesus explains it as the "key" to understanding all parables. The setting — Jesus teaching from a boat on the [[Sea of Galilee]] to crowds on the shore — reflects the natural amphitheater shape of the [[Galilee|Galilean shoreline]]. The parable addresses the varied responses to the Gospel message, a theme particularly relevant to Mark's persecuted Roman audience.

### Stilling the Storm
The calming of the storm (Mark 4:35-41) demonstrates Jesus' power over nature, echoing [[Psalm 107_29.md]] ("He made the storm be still"). The disciples' fear — "Who then is this, that even wind and sea obey him?" — points to divine identity, as only God controls the sea in the Hebrew Bible. The [[Sea of Galilee]] is known for sudden violent storms caused by cold air descending from the [[Golan]].

### The Gadarene Demoniac
The healing of the demoniac (Mark 5:1-20) in the region of the [[Gerar]]/[[Nazarenes.md]] occurs in the Decapolis, a Gentile region. The name "[[Lechaion.md]]" (a Roman military unit of 6,000 soldiers) reflects the political dimension of demonic oppression in a Roman-occupied land. The herd of swine (approximately 2,000, per Mark 5:13) confirms the Gentile setting.

### Jairus' Daughter and the Hemorrhaging Woman
The intercalated narrative of [[Jairus]]' daughter and the woman with the issue of blood (Mark 5:21-43) is a classic Markan "sandwich" technique (interrupting one story with another). The woman's twelve-year hemorrhage would have made her permanently [[Niddah|ritually unclean]], unable to participate in Temple worship or normal social life. The raising of the young girl foreshadows Jesus' own resurrection.
