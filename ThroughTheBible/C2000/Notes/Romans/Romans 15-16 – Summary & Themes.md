# Romans 15-16 – Summary & Themes

## Overview

So we turn to the 15th chapter of Paul's epistle to the Romans. In the 14th chapter, Paul was dealing with the subject of walking in love within the [[The Body of Christ|Body of Christ]]. Recognizing that we have differences of feelings, convictions, or opinions on the different issues involving the Christian walk, Paul said those that are strong in the faith can eat meat. Those who are weak in the faith have convictions against eating meat, so they eat vegetables.

### The Roman Church: Composition and Context
Paul wrote his letter to the [[Romans 8_1|Romans]] around AD 57 from [[Corinth]] (likely during his three-month stay in Greece, Acts 20:2-3), before his final journey to Jerusalem. The letter was delivered by [[Rhoda (biblical figure).md]] of [[Cenchreae]] (16:1-2). The [[church]] had existed for perhaps a decade before Paul wrote — it likely originated from the return of Jewish Christians after the [[Council of Jerusalem]] (AD 48-49) and from travelers from Jerusalem bearing the gospel. The community included both [[Christ|Jewish]] and [[Christ|Gentile]] believers, with tensions between them. The edict of [[Claudius]] (c. AD 49) expelling Jews from Rome (noted by [[Suetonius]], ''[[_Concepts/Doctrines/House of Israel]]'' Claudius 25.4) had displaced Jewish Christians (including [[Aquila and Priscilla]], Acts 18:2), and their return after Claudius's death (AD 54) created tensions in the church about status and identity.

### Archaeological Context of Neronian Rome
Paul wrote Romans during the latter part of [[Nero]]'s reign (AD 54-68). The city of Rome was experiencing massive urban growth, with the emperor's extravagant building projects (including the [[Domus Aurea]], Golden House) funded by increased taxation of the provinces. The mention of "those who are of the household of [[_Concepts/Doctrines/House of Israel|Aristobulus]]" (16:10) and "those of the household of [[Narcissus (freedman)|Narcissus]]" (16:11) references actual households in the imperial court — Aristobulus was a grandson of [[Herod the Great]], and Narcissus was a powerful freedman under Claudius. 

### The Reformation Significance of Romans
[[Martin Luther]] described Romans as "the chief part of the New Testament and the purest gospel." [[Romans 1_17]] — "The righteous shall live by faith" (quoting [[Habakkuk 2_4.md]]) — was the spark that ignited the [[Protestant Reformation]]. Luther's study of Romans led him to reject the medieval system of [[indulgences]] and works-righteousness. [[John]]'s experience on [[Aldersgate Street]] (May 24, 1738), when he felt his "heart strangely warmed" while hearing Luther's preface to Romans read aloud, launched the [[Methodist]] revival. [[Augustine of Hippo]]'s conversion (AD 386) was precipitated by reading [[Romans 13_13-14.md]]. [[Earth]]'s landmark commentary ''[[Epistle to the Hebrews.md]]'' (1919/1922) shattered 19th-century [[Pauline Theology.md]] and launched the [[neo-orthodoxy|neo-orthodox]] movement.

### Paul's Missionary Plans (Romans 15)
Paul explains his apostolic calling as "a minister of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles" (15:16). His quotation of [[Isaiah 52_15.md]] — "those who have never been told of him will see" (15:21) — underscores his pioneering missionary strategy: he aims to preach where Christ had not yet been named (15:20). His travel plans — to deliver the collection for the Jerusalem saints (15:25-28), then visit Rome on his way to [[Sin.md]] (15:24, 28) — reveal the geographic scope of his ambition. Spain, at the western edge of the known world, represented the "ends of the earth" for a 1st-century Roman.

### Personal Greetings (Romans 16)
Chapter 16 provides an invaluable window into the early Roman church — a community as diverse as the empire itself. Paul greets 26 individuals by name, plus several households and groups. The prominence of women is striking: [[Rhoda (biblical figure).md]] is commended as a "deacon" (diakonos) and "patron" (prostatis, 16:1-2); [[Aquila and Priscilla.md]] is named before her husband [[Aquila and Priscilla.md]] (16:3); [[Junia]] is "prominent among the apostles" (16:7) — a woman apostle. The greeting "the whole church meets in their house" (16:5) reveals the house-church structure of early Roman Christianity. The closing doxology (16:25-27) celebrates "the mystery that was kept secret for long ages but has now been disclosed" through Paul's gospel.
