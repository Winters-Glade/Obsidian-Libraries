## Overview

This book presents the history, the personal history of [[Samuel]], who was the last of the [[judges|judges]], and it ushers in the beginning of the period of the [[monarchy|monarchy]] (the kings) among the children of Israel. There was a certain man of Ramathame Zophim ([[Ramah]]) on [[Mount Ephraim]]. His name was Elkanah. He had two wives. One was [[Hannah]], the other was Peninnah. Peninnah had children. [[Hannah]] had no children. This man went out of the city yearly to worship and to sacrifice unto the Lord of Hosts in [[Shiloh]], which at that time was the religious center of the nation. And the two sons of [[Eli]], who was the high priest at that time, Hophni and Phinehas, the priests of the Lord, were there. And it came when the time was... But Elkanah offered, he gave to Peninnah and her children portions, but unto [[Hannah]] he gave a goodly or an extra portion. Because he loved [[Hannah]] very much, but God had shut up her womb and that she was barren. So the scene is set. A man living in polygamy, two wives. One he loved more than the other. One had many children. But the one he really loved could not have children. And so her adversary, that is [[Hannah]]'s adversary, or the other wife. So there was friction in the house between the two wives. As they vied for the attention and the love of the one man. As I said this morning,...

## Key Themes

- **Transition from Judges to Monarchy**: [[Samuel]] serves as the last [[judges|judge]] and first [[Prophecy|prophet]], bridging the era of charismatic leadership to the institutional [[monarchy|monarchy]] ([[1 Samuel 8_4-9.md]])
- **Hannah's Prayer and [[Covenant]] Faithfulness**: [[Hannah]]'s vow ([[1 Samuel 1_10-11.md]]) and her song of praise ([[1 Samuel 2_1-10.md]]) model devout prayer and trust in God's sovereignty
- **The Call of [[Samuel]]**: God's revelation to the young Samuel at [[Shiloh]] establishes the [[Prophecy|prophetic]] office ([[1 Samuel 3_1-21.md]])
- **The Ark Captured and Returned**: The [[Philistines]] capture the Ark of the [[Covenant|covenant]], but God's power is displayed through the Dagon idol's fall and the plagues ([[1 Samuel 4_1-7_2.md]])
- **Israel Demands a King**: The people's desire to be "like all the nations" leads to the rejection of God's direct rule and the request for a king ([[1 Samuel 8_1-22.md]])
- **[[Anointing]] of [[Saul]]**: Samuel anoints Saul as the first king of Israel at [[Gilgal]] ([[1 Samuel 9_1-10_27.md]])
- **God's Sovereignty in Human History**: Despite human failure, God's purposes advance through His chosen instruments

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

The events of **1 Samuel 1–10** take place during the transition from the period of the Judges to the early monarchy in Israel (ca. 1100–1050 BC). The narrative opens at **[[Shiloh]]**, the central sanctuary where the Tabernacle resided. Archaeological excavations at Khirbet Seilun (Shiloh) reveal a destruction layer dating to ca. 1050 BC, consistent with the Philistine victory and capture of the Ark described in [[1 Samuel 4.md]].

**[[Samuel]]** emerges as the last judge and the first of the classical [[Prophecy|prophets]], bridging the era of charismatic leadership (judges) and the institutional [[monarchy|monarchy]]. His birth narrative ([[1 Samuel 1.md]]–[[1 Samuel 2.md]]) mirrors other Old Testament barren-wife birth accounts (Sarah, Rebekah, Rachel) and foreshadows the birth of John the Baptist in the New Testament.

The period is marked by **[[Philistines|Philistine]] domination**. The Philistines, part of the "Sea Peoples" who settled the coastal plain of Canaan ca. 1200 BC, possessed superior military technology — iron chariots, iron weapons ([[_Bible/1 Samuel/1 Samuel 13_19-22.md|1 Sam 13:19–22]]), and a centralized army — while Israel still fought as a loose tribal confederation with improvised weapons. This asymmetry explains the people's demand for a king "like all the nations" ([[1 Samuel 8_5.md]]).

**International context:** Egypt was weakened during the Third Intermediate Period, Assyria was in a quiet phase (not yet the Neo-Assyrian Empire), and the great powers of the Late Bronze Age had collapsed. This power vacuum allowed local states like [[Philistines|Philistia]], Ammon, Moab, and a nascent Israel to emerge and compete for territory.

**[[Covenant]] vs. [[monarchy|kingship]] tension** runs throughout these chapters: [[Samuel]] warns that a king will take sons, daughters, fields, and flocks ([[1 Samuel 8_10-18.md]]), yet God permits the monarchy as a concession to the people's hardness of heart — a pattern that continues through the rest of 1–2 Samuel and into the prophetic books.
