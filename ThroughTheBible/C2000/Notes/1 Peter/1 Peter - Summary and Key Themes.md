## Historical & Contextual Background

> [!QUESTION]- This enrichment needs QT verification

**Household Codes:** 1 Peter 3:1–7 reflects the Greco-Roman household code tradition (*Haustafel*), which regulated relationships between wives and husbands, slaves and masters. Similar codes appear in Ephesians 5:21–6:9, Colossians 3:18–4:1, and in Stoic moral philosophy. Peter adapts these to a context where Christian wives may have unbelieving husbands, urging conduct "without a word" as a quiet evangelistic witness.

**Suffering & Witness:** The instruction to "always be prepared to give a defense" (3:15) uses the legal term *apologia* — suggesting Christians were being called before magistrates to explain their faith. This reflects the situation described in Pliny the Younger's letters to Trajan (c. AD 112), where Christians in Bithynia (one of the provinces addressed) were interrogated and executed simply for being Christian.

**Shepherd Imagery:** The call to elders to "shepherd the flock" (5:1–4) echoes Jesus's commission to Peter (John 21:15–17). "Shepherd and Overseer" (2:25) applies the Old Testament title for God (Psalm 23; Ezekiel 34) to Christ.

**Neronian Persecution:** Peter's death is traditionally linked to Nero's persecution following the Great Fire of Rome (AD 64). Tradition holds he was crucified upside-down at his own request. If the letter was written from Rome using the code name "Babylon," it was composed at the epicenter of imperial persecution.
