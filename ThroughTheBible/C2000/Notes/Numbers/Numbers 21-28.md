## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on Numbers 21-28, covering Israel's final journey around [[Edom]] toward the [[Promised Land]], the [[T2000/The Serpent of Brass, Numbers 21_5-9.md|bronze serpent]], the defeat of [[Sihon]] and [[Og]], the fascinating account of [[Balaam]] and [[Barak.md]], the sin of [[Baal]], the [[Jesus.md]], the appointment of [[Joshua]] as Moses' successor, and the laws concerning daily and festival sacrifices. ## Summary

Nearly 40 years have passed. Miriam and Aaron are dead, and the old generation has perished. As Israel journeys around [[Edom]], the people again complain against God and Moses. In judgment, God sends fiery [[Stephen.md]] among them. When the people repent, God instructs Moses to make a [[T2000/The Serpent of Brass, Numbers 21_5-9.md|bronze serpent]] on a pole — whoever looks at it lives. This becomes a powerful foreshadowing of [[Christ]]'s crucifixion. Israel defeats [[Sihon]] the [[Amorites|Amorite]] and [[Og]] of [[Bashan]], possessing their lands east of the [[Jordan River|Jordan]]. King [[Barak.md]] of [[Moab]], terrified, hires the prophet [[Balaam]] to curse Israel. Though Balaam seeks to comply, God puts only blessings in his mouth, declaring "God is not a man that He should lie" and "He has not beheld iniquity in Jacob." Unable to curse Israel directly, Balaam advises Balak to entice Israel into idolatry and sexual sin at [[Baal]], resulting in a plague that kills 24,000. [[Phinehas]]' zealous act stops the plague. A [[Jesus.md]] is taken, comparing tribal numbers. [[Spirit of Adoption]] successfully petition for inheritance rights. [[Joshua]] is publicly commissioned as Moses' successor. The chapter concludes with detailed instructions for daily, Sabbath, monthly, and festival sacrifices.

## Key Takeaways

1. The bronze serpent is a clear foreshadowing of Christ on the cross — sin judged, and whoever looks in faith lives. Salvation is as simple as looking to Jesus.
2. Balaam demonstrates that God's blessing on His people is irrevocable — no curse or enchantment can prevail against those whom God has blessed.
3. The permissive will of God is a dangerous place: God may allow what He does not desire, and leaving the center of His will leads to all kinds of problems.
4. The doctrine of Balaam — leading God's people into compromise through worldly enticement — remains a present danger in the church.
5. Phinehas' zeal illustrates that God honors those who take a stand for righteousness, even when that stand requires decisive action.
6. The transition from Moses to Joshua shows the importance of raising up the next generation of spiritual leadership, passing the mantle of authority.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The [[T2000/The Serpent of Brass, Numbers 21_5-9.md]] (Hebrew *nehash nehoshet*, Numbers 21:8–9) is rooted in the ANE tradition of magical/healing serpent symbolism. In Egyptian iconography, the uraeus (cobra) symbolized royalty and divine protection; the Babylonian *Mushussu* serpent-dragon was associated with Marduk. Israel's bronze serpent stands out because the healing agent is not the object itself but the obedient act of looking in faith toward God's provision — and it was later destroyed by [[Hezekiah]] when it became an object of idolatry called [[_MOC_by_Theme.md]] ([[_Bible/2 Kings/2 Kings 18_4.md]]).
- [[Balaam]] (Numbers 22–24) is attested in extrabiblical sources: the [[Deir Alla Inscription]] (c. 800 BC), discovered in modern-day Jordan, mentions "Balaam son of Beor" as a visionary prophet from Mesopotamia who received night oracles. This remarkable parallel confirms that Balaam was a historically recognized diviner figure in the Transjordanian region.
- The kingdoms of [[Sihon]] the Amorite and [[Og]] of Bashan (Numbers 21:21–35) occupied territories that later became the inheritance of [[Reuben]], [[Gad]], and the half-tribe of [[Manasseh]]. Archaeological surveys in the Transjordan (the [[Balaam.md]] region and the [[Golan]]) have identified extensive Iron Age settlements consistent with the biblical description of fortified Amorite kingdoms.
- [[Baal]] (Numbers 25) refers to the god [[Baal]] worshipped at Mount Peor (possibly modern Jebel en-Numeira). The cult involved ritual sexual practices typical of Canaanite fertility religion, which explains the text's emphasis on "harlotry" and intermarriage with Moabite women. The coupling of idolatry and sexual sin is a recurring theme in prophetic literature ([[Hosea]], [[Ezekiel]]).
- The second census (Numbers 26) served both military and land-distribution purposes — typical of ANE administrative practices where the census preceded both conscription and the assignment of fiefs. The slight population decline from the first census (603,550 to 601,730) underscores the devastating toll of the wilderness judgment.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Deir Alla Inscription]] (c. 800 BC), found in 1967 at Tell Deir Alla in Jordan, is a plaster wall inscription recounting a vision of Balaam son of Beor, a "seer of the gods." It testifies to Balaam's reputation as a legitimate prophet in the Transjordan region independent of the biblical tradition, lending historical credibility to his role in Numbers.
- Excavations at [[Dan.md]] (northern Israel) and [[Hazor]] have uncovered bronze serpents and serpentine cultic objects from the Late Bronze and Iron Ages, confirming the prevalence of serpent iconography in Canaanite religion and providing material context for the Nehushtan tradition.
- The territory of [[Og]] king of Bashan is associated with the region of the [[Golan]], where megalithic structures called *Rujm el-Hiri* (the "Stone Circle of the Giants") and the ancient city of [[Ashtoreth.md]] (Tell Ashtara) have been excavated. The [[Ephraim.md]] — a people group associated with Og — appear in Ugaritic texts as semi-divine beings, suggesting a long cultural memory of a race of giants dwelling in Bashan.
- The [[Moabites.md]] (Mesha Stele, c. 840 BC) — discovered at Dhiban, Jordan in 1868 — records the Moabite king Mesha's rebellion against Israel after the death of Ahab and his restoration of sites in the region of Moab. While it post-dates the Numbers narrative by centuries, it confirms the historicity of Moab as a political entity and the biblical geography of the Arnon River region.
- Surveys of the [[Arabah]] and the [[King's Highway]] (the ancient trade route running north-south through Transjordan, mentioned in Numbers 20:17) confirm a well-established network of fortified sites and waystations in the Late Bronze Age, supporting the biblical account of Israel's request to pass through Edomite and Amorite territory.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Justin Martyr]] (c. AD 100–165) in his *Dialogue with Trypho* (ch. 94) was among the first Christian writers to identify the [[T2000/The Serpent of Brass, Numbers 21_5-9.md]] as a type of the Cross: "The bronze serpent was erected so that those who were bitten by the serpents might look at it and be healed. In the same way, the Son of Man was lifted up on the cross so that those who believe in Him might be saved from the bite of the ancient serpent, the devil."
- [[Tertullian]] (c. AD 155–220) in *Against Marcion* used the Balaam narrative to argue that God's blessing of Israel was irreversible and that the same God who blessed Israel is the God revealed in Christ — a polemic against Marcion's rejection of the Old Testament.
- [[Augustine of Hippo]] in *The City of God* (Book 16) interpreted Balaam's prophecies (especially the "Star out of Jacob" in Numbers 24:17) as direct messianic predictions fulfilled in Christ, noting that even a false prophet could speak true prophecy under divine constraint.
- [[Thomas Aquinas]] (c. AD 1225–1274) in the *Summa Theologiae* discussed [[Phinehas]]'s act of zeal (Numbers 25:7–8) under the category of just punishment, arguing that his slaying of Zimri and Cozbi was an act of divinely-inspired fervor for God's honor, not personal vengeance — a key text in the development of just-war and punishment theory.
- [[Martin Luther]] (AD 1483–1546) wrote extensively on the Balaam narrative in his *Lectures on Numbers*, emphasizing that Balaam's inability to curse Israel demonstrated the unconditional nature of God's covenant promises — an important foundation for his doctrine of justification by grace through faith.

## New Testament Connections

- [[John 3_14–15.md]] is the most direct NT connection: "And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in Him should not perish but have eternal life." Jesus explicitly identifies the [[T2000/The Serpent of Brass, Numbers 21_5-9.md]] as a type of His own crucifixion — the judgment for sin displayed on a pole, offering life to all who look in faith.
- [[Revelation 2_14.md]] condemns the church in [[Pergamum.md]] for holding to "the doctrine of [[Balaam]], who taught Balak to put a stumbling block before the children of Israel, to eat things sacrificed to idols, and to commit sexual immorality." The same strategy of corruption — compromise through idolatry and immorality — threatens the New Covenant church.
- [[2 Peter 2_15–16.md]] and [[Jude 1_11]] both cite Balaam as a negative exemplar: he "loved the wages of unrighteousness" and "ran greedily after the error of Balaam for profit." These passages warn against false teachers who prostitute spiritual gifts for material gain.
- [[Hebrews 11_32.md]] mentions [[Gideon]], [[Barak]], [[Samson]], and [[Jephthah]] — but not Phinehas directly. However, Phinehas's zeal is reflected in the NT theme of righteous zeal for God's house (cf. [[John 2_17]] quoting [[Psalm 69_9]]).
- Balaam's oracles contain messianic prophecy: "A Star shall come out of Jacob, and a Scepter shall rise out of Israel" (Numbers 24:17). This is traditionally interpreted as a prediction of [[Jesus Christ]] — the [[City Gate of Bethlehem.md]] ([[Matthew 2_1–2.md]]) and the royal scepter of [[Judah]] ([[Genesis 49_10.md]]). [[Ra]] also interpreted this verse messianically, both in the [[Targums]] and in the writings of [[Philo of Alexandria]] and [[Josephus]].
