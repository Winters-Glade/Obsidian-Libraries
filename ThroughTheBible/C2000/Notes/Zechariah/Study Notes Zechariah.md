## Historical Context
These prophecies date to the [[Post-Exilic Period]] after the return from Babylon (c. 520-430 BC). Haggai and Zechariah encouraged the rebuilding of the temple; Malachi addressed spiritual decline a generation later.

## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Zechariah, chapters 1-14.

**Zechariah:** Zechariah, a contemporary of Haggai, provides the most extensive messianic prophecies of any minor prophet. His eight night visions encourage the rebuilding of the temple and the restoration of Jerusalem. He foretells the Messiah as both King and Priest, riding on a donkey, betrayed for 30 pieces of silver, pierced by His people, and returning to establish His kingdom.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Zechariah ministered alongside Haggai in 520–518 BC, during the second year of Darius I of Persia:

- The **Cyrus Cylinder** provides the policy background for the return from exile, but the specific context of Zechariah's ministry is the return under Zerubbabel and Joshua the high priest (the Persian period, c. 538–430 BC).
- The **Elephantine Papyri** (c. 495–399 BC) mention the temple at Elephantine and correspondence with the Jerusalem temple authorities, including references to figures named Sanballat and Johanan, who appear in Nehemiah and Zechariah — confirming the historical framework of the post-exilic period.
- The **Darius Palace Inscription** at Persepolis and the **Behistun Inscription** document the reign of Darius I, confirming the chronology of Zechariah's ministry.
- Archaeological excavations at **Jerusalem** in the "City of David" and the **Ophel** have uncovered Persian-period remains, including seal impressions bearing names of individuals mentioned in the post-exilic biblical books — demonstrating that the community of returned exiles was historically real.

## Eschatological Significance

Zechariah is second only to Daniel in its eschatological importance among Old Testament books. Its prophecies span the first and second comings of Christ:

- **Zechariah 9:9** — The King coming on a donkey (fulfilled at the [[Triumphal Entry]]). Chuck Smith taught this as a literal prophecy of Jesus' humility at His first coming.
- **Zechariah 12:10** — "They will look on Me whom they have pierced" — seen as a prophecy of the crucifixion, with a future national repentance of Israel when they recognize their pierced Messiah.
- **Zechariah 13:1** — "A fountain will be opened" — the cleansing from sin, applied to salvation in Christ.
- **Zechariah 14** — The Lord's return to the Mount of Olives, the splitting of the mountain, and the establishment of His kingdom. In Chuck Smith's eschatology, this is the most detailed Old Testament description of the second coming, describing Jesus standing on the Mount of Olives, the topographical changes, and the Feast of Tabernacles in the millennial kingdom.
- **Zechariah 6:12-13** — The Branch who will build the temple of the Lord, sitting as Priest on His throne — a vision combining both kingly and priestly roles, which Chuck Smith interpreted as Christ's millennial reign.

## New Testament Connections

Zechariah is the most frequently messianically-quoted Minor Prophet in the New Testament:

- **Zechariah 9:9** — "Behold your King comes to you, righteous and having salvation, lowly and riding on a donkey" — directly quoted in all four Gospels ([[Matthew 2_15.md]]; [[Mark 11_7]]; [[Luke 19_35]]; [[John 12_15]]) describing the Triumphal Entry.
- **Zechariah 11:12-13** — "Thirty pieces of silver" thrown to the potter — quoted in [[Matthew 27_9-10.md]] in connection with Judas's betrayal and the purchase of the potter's field.
- **Zechariah 12:10** — "They shall look on Me whom they have pierced" — quoted in [[John 19_37]] after the soldier pierced Jesus' side, and referenced in [[Revelation 1_7.md]].
- **Zechariah 13:7** — "Strike the Shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered" — quoted by Jesus in [[Matthew 26_31.md]] and [[Mark 14_27]] on the night of His arrest, predicting the disciples' abandonment.
- **Zechariah 14:5** — "The Lord my God will come, and all the holy ones with Him" — echoed in [[1 Thessalonians 3_13.md]] and [[Jude 1_14]] regarding the second coming with the saints.
- **Zechariah 14:7** — "At evening time there shall be light" — the imagery of a unique day without day or night reflects [[Revelation 21_23-24.md]] where the New Jerusalem has no need of sun or moon.

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Zechariah and [[Haggai]]** — Contemporaries in the post-exilic period. Haggai addresses the practical need to rebuild; Zechariah provides the visionary encouragement through night visions.
- **Zechariah and [[Ezekiel]]** — Both describe visions of the temple, the cleansing fountain, and God's glory returning. Ezekiel's river of life and temple vision (Ezekiel 40-48) are paralleled in Zechariah's description of the living waters flowing from Jerusalem (Zechariah 14:8).
- **Zechariah and [[Daniel]]** — Zechariah's four horsemen and night visions draw on Daniel's apocalyptic imagery. Zechariah 14's battle of the nations echoes Daniel's end-times visions.
- **Zechariah and [[Isaiah]]** — The image of the [[Ruach.md]] (Zechariah 3:8; 6:12) is a messianic title that Isaiah also uses (Isaiah 4:2; 11:1). The universal worship of God (Zechariah 14:16-19) extends Isaiah's vision of all nations coming to Jerusalem.
- **Zechariah and [[Malachi]]** — Malachi's closing promise of Elijah (Malachi 4:5-6) complements Zechariah's picture of the coming King.
