## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on the renewal of the covenant after the golden calf. These chapters are among the most profound in Scripture — Moses pleads for God's presence, requests to see God's glory, and receives a revelation of God's character. The covenant is renewed, and Moses' face shines from being in God's presence.

## Summary

After the golden calf, God tells Moses to lead the people to Canaan but says He will not go with them lest He consume them. Moses intercedes, pleading for God's presence. He sets up the Tabernacle of Meeting outside the camp. God speaks to Moses face to face as a friend. Moses makes a bold request: "Show me Your glory." God places him in the cleft of the rock, covers him with His hand, and passes by proclaiming His name and character: "The Lord, the Lord God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering and abundant in goodness and truth." Moses cuts new tablets and returns to Sinai. The covenant is renewed with warnings against idolatry and commands for the feasts. When Moses descends, his face shines with glory. He veils his face when speaking to the people but removes the veil when speaking with God.

## Key Takeaways

1. God's presence is what distinguishes His people from all others — Moses refused to move without it.
2. Moses' request to see God's glory was answered not with a vision of power but a revelation of character — God's glory is His goodness, mercy, grace, and truth.
3. The cleft of the rock is a picture of our salvation in Christ — we are hidden in the Rock while God's glory passes by.
4. The shining of Moses' face was a reflected glory that faded, unlike the glory of the New Covenant in Christ which is permanent and transforming (2 Corinthians 3).
5. The veil on Moses' face symbolizes the veiled understanding of the Old Covenant — in Christ, the veil is taken away.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The [[Tent of Meeting]] (Exodus 33:7–11) set up "outside the camp" — before the Tabernacle was built — represents a period of separation after the golden calf. The fact that God would only meet with Moses outside the camp signified that God's presence had been withdrawn from the midst of the people due to their sin. Yet the pillar of cloud at the tent entrance demonstrated that God had not abandoned them entirely.
- The revelation of God's name in Exodus 34:6–7 is one of the most theologically dense passages in the Old Testament. The phrase "The Lord, the Lord God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering and abundant in goodness and truth" (Hebrew: *Yahweh, Yahweh El, rachum v'chanun, erech appayim v'rav chesed v'emet*) became the foundational description of God's character, echoed throughout the Old Testament (Psalm 86:15, 103:8, 145:8; Joel 2:13; Jonah 4:2; Nehemiah 9:17).

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Mesha Stele]] (Moabite Stone, c. 840 BC) uses the name [[Yahweh]] in a Moabite inscription, showing that the divine name was known outside Israel in the 9th century BC. This provides extrabiblical attestation of Israel's God.
- The [[Lachish Letters]] (c. 587 BC) contain letters from a Judean military outpost that include personal names compounded with *Yah* (the shortened form of Yahweh), confirming the usage of the divine name in everyday life during the First Temple period.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Cyril of Alexandria]] (c. 376–444 AD) in his *Glaphyra on Exodus* interpreted the cleft of the rock (Exodus 33:22–23) as a type of Christ: "The rock is Christ" (1 Corinthians 10:4). To be placed "in the cleft of the rock" is to be hidden in the wounds of Christ, where we are protected when God's glory passes by and His judgment falls on sin.
- [[John of Damascus]] (c. 675–749 AD) in his *Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith* used Moses' shining face (Exodus 34:29–35) to illustrate the doctrine of theosis (deification) — as Moses reflected God's glory, believers are transformed into God's likeness by participation in His divine nature (2 Peter 1:4).

## New Testament Connections

- [[2 Corinthians 3_7-18.md]] — Paul provides the most extensive NT commentary on Moses' veil. He contrasts the "ministry of death, written and engraved on stones" (the Old Covenant) with the "ministry of the Spirit" (the New Covenant). The veil on Moses' face symbolized the veiled understanding of Israel when reading the Old Covenant. But "when one turns to the Lord, the veil is taken away" (2 Corinthians 3:16). Believers, with unveiled face, behold "the glory of the Lord" and "are being transformed into the same image from glory to glory."
- [[_Bible/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 10_4.md]] — "That Rock was Christ." The cleft of the rock where Moses was hidden is identified as Christ Himself. Just as Moses was sheltered in the rock to see God's glory, believers are sheltered in Christ to receive God's mercy.
- [[1 Peter 1_10–12.md]] — The revelation of God's character (mercy, grace, faithfulness) that Moses received is described as the same Spirit of Christ that "testified beforehand the sufferings of Christ and the glories that would follow."
