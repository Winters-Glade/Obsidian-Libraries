## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith covers the final ordinances, the three annual feasts, the confirmation of the covenant, and the beginning of the Tabernacle instructions. The transition from law to worship is significant — God gave them the standard of righteousness, then provided a way to dwell among them through the Tabernacle.

## Summary

Chapter 23 continues laws of justice — no false reports, no partiality. The Sabbath year is instituted. Three annual feasts are commanded: Unleavened Bread (Passover), Harvest (Firstfruits/Pentecost), and Ingathering (Tabernacles). God promises an Angel with His name to lead them. In chapter 24, the covenant is confirmed with blood — Moses sprinkles the people and they pledge obedience. Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and seventy elders see God. Moses ascends the mountain for 40 days. Chapter 25 begins the Tabernacle instructions: a voluntary offering, then detailed plans for the Ark of the Covenant with its mercy seat and cherubim, the Table of Showbread, and the Golden Lampstand (Menorah). All must be made according to the pattern shown on the mountain.

## Key Takeaways

1. The three annual feasts are prophetic — they prefigure the work of Christ and the coming kingdom.
2. The Angel of the Lord who bears God's name is a pre-incarnate appearance of Jesus Christ.
3. The blood of the covenant points to the blood of Christ, which establishes the New Covenant.
4. The Tabernacle is a picture of Christ — every detail points to some aspect of His person and work.
5. The pattern on the mountain indicates that earthly worship reflects heavenly reality.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The [[three annual feasts|three annual feasts]] (Exodus 23:14–17) — Unleavened Bread (*Matzot*), Harvest/Firstfruits (*Shavuot*/Pentecost), and Ingathering (*Sukkot*/Tabernacles) — correspond to Israel's agricultural cycle: barley harvest (spring), wheat harvest (summer), and fruit harvest (autumn). Each feast also had profound historical (Exodus) and prophetic (Messianic) significance. Every male Israelite was required to appear "before the Lord" three times a year.
- The [[Angel of the Lord]] (Exodus 23:20–23) who "bears My name" is distinguished from created angels by possessing the divine name Yahweh in Himself. This is widely understood by Christian interpreters as a pre-incarnate appearance of the Son of God (a Christophany). The Angel's promise to "not pardon your transgressions" (Exodus 23:21) indicates divine authority to forgive sins.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Gezer Calendar]] (c. 10th century BC), a limestone tablet from the time of Solomon, lists the agricultural seasons of Israel, providing background for understanding the agricultural basis of Israel's feast calendar.
- The [[Siloam Inscription]] and other Israelite stonework from the First Temple period confirm the sophistication of Israelite construction, providing context for the Tabernacle's advanced craftsmanship described in Exodus 25.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Clement of Alexandria]] (c. 150–215 AD) in his *Stromata* interpreted the Tabernacle as an allegory of the cosmos: the Outer Court = the material world, the Holy Place = the spiritual realm, and the Most Holy Place = the divine presence. The furnishings represented stages of spiritual ascent to God.
- [[Theodoret of Cyrus]] (c. 393–457 AD) in his *Questions on Exodus* provided detailed typological interpretations of the Tabernacle furniture, seeing the [[Ark of the Covenant]] as a type of Christ's humanity (acacia wood) united with His divinity (gold), and the [[Mercy Seat]] as Christ Himself, the place of atonement.

## New Testament Connections

- [[Hebrews 8_1–6.md]] — The entire Tabernacle system is presented as "a copy and shadow of the heavenly things." Jesus is the minister of the "true tabernacle which the Lord erected, not man." The pattern shown to Moses on the mountain (Exodus 25:40) is the heavenly reality that Christ fulfills.
- [[Hebrews 9_1–14.md]] — Christ entered "not into the holy places made with hands, which are copies of the true, but into heaven itself." His blood, not of goats and calves, obtains eternal redemption.
- [[John 1_14]] — "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us" — literally "tabernacled" (*skenoo*) among us. Jesus Christ is the true Tabernacle — God dwelling with humanity in person.
- [[Revelation 11_19.md]] — John sees "the temple of God in heaven opened, and the ark of His covenant appeared in His temple" — the heavenly reality of which the earthly Ark was a copy.
