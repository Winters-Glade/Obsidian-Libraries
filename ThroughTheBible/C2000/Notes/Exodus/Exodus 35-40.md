## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on the actual construction and erection of the Tabernacle. After the tragedy of the golden calf and the renewal of the covenant, the people respond with willing, generous hearts. The Tabernacle is built exactly according to God's pattern, and the glory of the Lord fills it. This concludes the book of Exodus.

## Summary

Moses gathers Israel and repeats the Tabernacle commands, beginning with the Sabbath. The people respond with overwhelming generosity — men and women bring offerings of gold, silver, bronze, yarn, linen, skins, wood, spices, and precious stones. They bring so much Moses must restrain them. The women spin with their hands. Bezalel and Oholiab lead the craftsmen. The Tabernacle is constructed piece by piece — the curtains, boards, bars, veil, Ark, table, lampstand, altar of incense, altar of burnt offering, laver, and court. The priestly garments are made. Moses inspects the work and blesses the people. On the first day of the first month, the Tabernacle is erected. The cloud covers the Tent of Meeting, and the glory of the Lord fills the Tabernacle. From that point forward, when the cloud lifts, Israel journeys; when it stays, they remain.

## Key Takeaways

1. The people gave willingly and abundantly — when the heart is right, giving is a joy, not a burden. God loves a cheerful giver.
2. The Tabernacle was built exactly according to God's pattern — obedience in the details matters to God.
3. The glory of the Lord filling the Tabernacle was the ultimate purpose — God desired to dwell among His people.
4. The cloud and fire guided Israel's journeys — we must also be sensitive to the Spirit's leading, moving when He moves and staying when He stays.
5. The Tabernacle points to Jesus Christ who "tabernacled" among us (John 1:14) and to the future when God will dwell with His people forever (Revelation 21:3).
6. Exodus ends with God's presence — the story that began in bondage and oppression concludes with the glory of God filling His dwelling place.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The construction of the Tabernacle — a portable sanctuary that could be disassembled and carried through the wilderness — reflects the nomadic context of Israel's journey. The materials (acacia wood, gold, silver, bronze, linen, goat hair, ram skins, and dolphin/badger skins) were either brought from Egypt (Exodus 12:35–36) or obtained in the wilderness. Acacia wood was the only durable wood available in the Sinai desert.
- The role of women in the Tabernacle construction is noteworthy: they "spun with their hands" and brought their mirrors for the bronze laver (Exodus 38:8). The "wise-hearted women" who spun the fine linen (Exodus 35:25–26) were specifically noted for their skill — a recognition of women's contribution to Israel's worship that was unusual in the ancient world.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Tabernacle of Amun]] at [[Karnak]] — a ritual tent used by Egyptian pharaohs on military campaigns — provides a structural parallel to the Israelite Tabernacle. Fluted poles, linen curtains, and a courtyard arrangement are attested in Egyptian reliefs from the 19th Dynasty, demonstrating that tent sanctuaries were a known concept in the ancient Near East.
- The [[Pithom Stele]] (c. 3rd century BC) describes building projects in the eastern Delta, confirming the ongoing tradition of monumental construction in the region. While post-dating the Exodus, it demonstrates the continuity of building practices.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Ephrem the Syrian]] (c. 306–373 AD) in his *Hymns on the Nativity* compared the construction of the Tabernacle to the incarnation of Christ. Just as the Tabernacle was built by human hands yet filled with divine glory, so Christ's humanity was built from the substance of Mary yet filled with the fullness of God.
- [[Bede the Venerable]] (c. 673–735 AD) in his *On the Tabernacle* provided a comprehensive allegorical interpretation: the outer court = the visible world, the Holy Place = the church, the Most Holy Place = heaven. The veil represented the separation of the heavenly from the earthly, which Christ removed.

## New Testament Connections

- [[John 1_14]] — "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us" — the Greek verb *skenoo* literally means "to pitch a tent" or "to tabernacle." John explicitly frames the incarnation as the ultimate fulfillment of the Tabernacle: Jesus Christ is God dwelling with humanity in person, the true Tabernacle of which the Mosaic structure was a shadow.
- [[Hebrews 9_11–12.md]] — "Christ came as High Priest of the good things to come, with the greater and more perfect tabernacle not made with hands." The earthly Tabernacle was a copy; Christ entered the original.
- [[_Bible/Revelation/Revelation 21_3.md]] — "Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men, and He will dwell with them." The Exodus narrative that began with God's people in bondage ends with God's glory filling His dwelling place — and Revelation promises this pattern reaches its ultimate fulfillment in the new creation.
- [[2 Corinthians 8_1–5.md]] — The willing generosity of the Israelites in giving materials for the Tabernacle is held up as an example for New Testament giving: "They gave according to their ability, yes, and beyond their ability, freely willing."
