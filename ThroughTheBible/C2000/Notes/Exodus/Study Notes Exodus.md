# Study Notes — Exodus

## Key Themes
[[Exodus]] [[Moses]] [[Ten Plagues]] [[Passover]] [[Red Sea Crossing]] [[Ten Commandments]] [[Tabernacle]] [[Law of Moses]]

## Historical Context
These books (traditionally attributed to [[Moses]]) cover the foundational history of Israel: creation, the patriarchs, the exodus from Egypt, the giving of the Law at Sinai, and the wilderness journey to the border of the Promised Land. They were written during the wilderness period (c. 1440-1400 BC).

## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Exodus, chapters 6-10.

**Exodus:** The story of Israel's deliverance from Egyptian bondage under Moses. From the burning bush to the ten plagues, the crossing of the Red Sea, and the giving of the Law at Mount Sinai, Exodus establishes Israel as a nation under God. The second half details the construction of the Tabernacle — God's dwelling place among His people.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The book of Exodus was written against the backdrop of the New Kingdom Egyptian 19th Dynasty (c. 1290–1213 BC according to the late-date chronology), a period of massive building projects and imperial expansion under [[Ramesses II]]. The mention of the store cities Pithom and Raamses ([[Exodus 1_11]]) ties the narrative to Ramesside building programs, and the name "Moses" (Egyptian *msy* — "son" or "born of") reflects authentic Egyptian naming conventions of the period.
- For those who hold to the early date (c. 1446 BC, based on [[1 Kings 6_1]]), the pharaoh of the oppression would be [[Thutmose III]] (or possibly [[Amenhotep II]]), placing the Exodus during the 18th Dynasty rather than the 19th. Both dating frameworks recognize the historical plausibility of a Semitic population residing in the eastern Delta (the land of [[Goshen]]) during the Second Intermediate Period and New Kingdom.
- The Ten Plagues (Exodus 7–12) constitute a sustained polemic against the pantheon of Egypt. Each plague targeted a specific Egyptian deity: the Nile ([[Hapi]]) turned to blood, frogs ([[Heqet]]), darkness against [[Ra]] the sun-god, and the death of the firstborn against Pharaoh himself (considered a living god). This theological dimension is explicitly stated in [[Exodus 12_12.md]]: "against all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgment."
- The crossing of the [[Red Sea]] (Hebrew *Yam Suph* — "Sea of Reeds") and the destruction of Pharaoh's army at the sea (Exodus 14–15) functioned as the definitive act of divine deliverance, celebrated in Israel's earliest poetic tradition (the [[Song of the Sea]], Exodus 15). This event became the paradigmatic salvation event in Israel's memory, echoed throughout the Psalms and the Prophets.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Merneptah Stele]] (c. 1208 BC) provides the earliest extrabiblical reference to "Israel," confirming their presence in Canaan by the late 13th century BC. This inscription, erected by Pharaoh Merneptah, states: "Israel is laid waste, his seed is not" — a propagandistic boast that nonetheless testifies that Israel was a recognized people group in Canaan within a generation or two of the late-date Exodus.
- The [[Ipuwer Papyrus]] (sometimes dated to the Second Intermediate Period) describes a series of catastrophes in Egypt — the Nile turned to blood, darkness, pestilence, and widespread death — that some scholars (e.g., [[David Rohl]], [[Immanuel Velikovsky]]) have connected to the biblical plagues, though mainstream scholarship treats the papyrus as a literary lament rather than a historical chronicle.
- The [[Brooklyn Papyrus 35.1446]] (c. 1700 BC) lists the names of Semitic slaves in Egypt, including names like ''Shiphrah'' (matching one of the Hebrew midwives in [[Exodus 1_15]]), providing evidence of a Semitic presence in Egypt consistent with the biblical narrative.
- [[The Harvard excavations at Tell el-Dab'a]] (ancient [[Avaris]]/later [[Pi-Ramesses]]) have uncovered evidence of a substantial Semitic population in the eastern Nile Delta during the 13th–12th Dynasties, including houses built in Levantine style and burials with Asiatic grave goods. This site is widely identified with the region where the Israelites would have settled.
- While no direct Egyptian record of the Exodus events themselves has survived (consistent with the Egyptian practice of omitting humiliating defeats), the cumulative archaeological picture — Semitic presence in the Delta, forced-labor projects, and the sudden appearance of a distinct Israelite culture in the highlands of Canaan around 1200 BC — fits the biblical framework remarkably well.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Augustine of Hippo]] in *The City of God* (Book 18) treated the Exodus as the paradigmatic act of divine deliverance in the Old Testament, prefiguring the redemption accomplished by Christ. He argued that the plagues demonstrated God's sovereign power over all false gods and that the hardening of Pharaoh's heart illustrated the mystery of divine sovereignty and human responsibility.
- [[Origen of Alexandria]] in his *Homilies on Exodus* developed a thoroughgoing allegorical interpretation of the book, seeing the journey from Egypt to the Promised Land as an allegory of the soul's journey from sin to salvation. His typological reading deeply influenced later patristic and medieval exegesis.
- [[John Chrysostom]] delivered homilies on Exodus that emphasized the moral lessons of the book, particularly the obedience of faith and the danger of unbelief (as exemplified by Pharaoh). He also drew attention to the typological significance of the Tabernacle as a shadow of heavenly realities.
- [[Martin Luther]] described Exodus as "the most glorious book of the Old Testament" because it contains "the figure and image of our redemption." His lectures on Exodus emphasized justification by faith (the Passover lamb), the role of the Law (as tutor to Christ), and the centrality of God's covenant mercy revealed in the divine name [[Yahweh]].
- [[John Calvin]]'s commentaries on Exodus (in his *Harmony of the Last Four Books of Moses*) focused on the book's covenantal structure, the sovereignty of God displayed in the plagues and the Exodus, and the relationship between Law and Gospel. He saw the Tabernacle as a visible demonstration of God's desire to dwell among His people.

## New Testament Connections

- The entire New Testament is built on Exodus typology: Jesus is the new [[Moses]] (deliverer and lawgiver), the [[Passover Lamb]] (sacrifice, [[_Bible/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 5_7.md]]), the [[Bread from Heaven]] (manna, [[John 6_31-35.md]]), the [[Rock]] (water, [[_Bible/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 10_4.md]]), and the true [[Tabernacle]] (God with us, [[John 1_14]]). The Gospel narratives deliberately echo Exodus themes to present Jesus as the fulfillment of Israel's story.
- [[Luke 9_31]] — At the Transfiguration, Moses and Elijah spoke with Jesus about His "decease" (Greek *exodos*) which He was about to accomplish at Jerusalem. The word choice is deliberate: Jesus' death, resurrection, and ascension constitute a new Exodus — a deliverance from the bondage of sin and death that surpasses the first.
- [[John 1_14]] — "The Word became flesh and *tabernacled* among us" (Greek *eskēnōsen*, literally "pitched his tent") directly echoes the Tabernacle imagery of Exodus, presenting Jesus as the ultimate dwelling place of God with humanity.
- [[Hebrews 3_1–4_13.md]] — The author of Hebrews contrasts the faithfulness of Jesus with the unfaithfulness of the wilderness generation, warning believers not to harden their hearts as Israel did at [[Meribah]] and urging them to enter God's "rest" — a rest prefigured by Canaan but fulfilled in Christ.
- [[_Bible/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 10_1-13.md]] — Paul interprets Israel's wilderness journey as a typological warning to the church: baptism (the Red Sea), spiritual food and drink (manna and water from the rock), and the danger of idolatry and unbelief. He explicitly identifies Christ as the rock that followed them.
- [[_Bible/Revelation/Revelation 15_3.md]] — The redeemed sing "the song of Moses, the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb," fusing the Exodus deliverance with the final deliverance accomplished in Christ, showing that the Exodus story finds its ultimate fulfillment in the new creation.
