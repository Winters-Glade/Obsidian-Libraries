## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on the civil and social ordinances given to Israel. These "judgments" apply the Ten Commandments to specific situations in daily life, covering servant laws, personal injuries, property rights, and social responsibilities. They reveal God's heart for justice, mercy, and order.

## Summary

God gives specific case laws applying the Ten Commandments. Hebrew servants serve six years and go free in the seventh. The servant who chooses to stay has his ear pierced — a sign of willing servitude. Laws protect human life with capital punishment for murder, but distinguish accidental killing. The lex talionis principle (eye for eye) limits retaliation to proportionate punishment. Laws address personal injury, property damage, theft, and restitution — typically double or more. God shows special concern for the vulnerable: widows, orphans, and the poor. Interest (usury) on loans to the poor is forbidden. The Sabbath year for land rest is established.

## Key Takeaways

1. God's law covers every aspect of life — not just religious ceremonies but civil, social, and personal conduct.
2. The "eye for eye" principle was not a license for personal vengeance but a guideline for judicial punishment — the punishment must fit the crime.
3. The ear-piercing of the willing servant is a beautiful type of Christ, who willingly gave Himself for His bride.
4. God has a special heart for the vulnerable — widows, orphans, the poor, and strangers. His people must reflect that heart.
5. Restitution teaches that sin has consequences requiring restoration, pointing ultimately to the restoration Christ accomplishes.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The laws of [[Exodus 3_21-22.md]] are often compared to the [[Code of Hammurabi]] (c. 1754 BC), the most famous ancient Near Eastern law collection. While there are parallels (both have laws about goring oxen, slave treatment, and property damage), the differences are striking: biblical law values human life more highly (e.g., no death penalty for theft, as in Hammurabi), provides for the poor (no interest on loans), and grounds law in God's character rather than royal authority.
- The Hebrew [[bondservant|bondservant]] (*eved ivri*) who serves six years and goes free in the seventh (Exodus 21:2) reflects the Jubilee principle of redemption. The ear-piercing ceremony (Exodus 21:6) at the doorpost — a ritual still practiced in some Jewish communities today — symbolized the servant's permanent attachment to the household, performed "before God" (at the sanctuary or in a legal context).

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Code of Hammurabi]] (Louvre Museum, Paris) is a stele with 282 laws inscribed in Akkadian cuneiform. Its prologue claims the god Shamash gave the laws to Hammurabi — a striking parallel to Moses receiving the Law from God. The lex talionis principle appears in both codes, but the biblical version is unique in applying equally to all social classes, whereas Hammurabi's code prescribes different penalties for nobles, commoners, and slaves.
- The [[Mishpatim]] (covenant judgments) of Exodus 21–22 share structural similarities with other ANE legal collections from [[Nuzi]], [[Mari]], and [[Eshnunna]], suggesting a common legal tradition adapted by Israel under divine revelation.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Tertullian]] (c. 155–220 AD) in *Against Marcion* defended the Law of Moses against Marcion's claim that it was harsh and incompatible with the Gospel. He argued that the "eye for eye" principle was a limitation on vengeance, not a command for it — a merciful check on human wrath.
- [[Thomas Aquinas]] (1225–1274) in the *Summa Theologica* (I-II, Q. 95–105) classified the Mosaic judicial precepts as specific applications of the natural law, binding in their historical context but not necessarily binding on all nations. He distinguished the moral law (Ten Commandments, permanent), the ceremonial law (types of Christ, fulfilled), and the judicial law (civil ordinances of Israel, not binding on other polities).

## New Testament Connections

- [[Matthew 5_38-42.md]] — Jesus directly addresses the *lex talionis* ("eye for eye"), teaching His disciples not to resist an evil person but to turn the other cheek. This does not abolish the judicial principle (which was for courts, not personal vendettas) but calls His followers to a higher standard of grace in personal relationships.
- [[Philippians 2_9–11.md]] — The willing servant whose ear is pierced (Exodus 21:5–6) is a profound type of Christ, who "made Himself of no reputation, taking the form of a bondservant" (Philippians 2:7). Christ's willing submission to the Father echoes the servant's declaration: "I love my master... I will not go out free."
- [[James]] — "Pure and undefiled religion before God and the Father is this: to visit orphans and widows in their trouble." This echoes the special care for widows and orphans mandated in Exodus 22:22–24.
