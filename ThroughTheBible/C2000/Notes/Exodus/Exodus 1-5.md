## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on the opening chapters of Exodus, covering the oppression of Israel in [[Egypt]], the birth and calling of [[Moses]], and the beginning of his confrontation with [[Pharaoh]]. This section sets the stage for the entire Exodus narrative — God's [[Deliverance]] of His people from [[Slavery|bondage]].

## Summary

The children of Israel multiply greatly in Egypt, causing fear in Pharaoh who enslaves them and commands male infants be killed. Moses is born, hidden, discovered by Pharaoh's daughter, and raised in the palace. After killing an Egyptian, he flees to [[Midian]] for 40 years. [[God]] calls him from [[The Burning Bush|the burning bush]] in a [[theophany|divine theophany]], revealing His name [[Yahweh]] and commissioning him to deliver Israel. Moses objects repeatedly, so God appoints Aaron as his spokesman. They confront Pharaoh, who responds by increasing Israel's burdens, causing the people to despair.

## Key Takeaways

1. God sees the affliction of His people and is moved to action. He is not distant from our suffering.
2. God's name "I AM" speaks to His eternal, self-existent nature — He is the God who is always present.
3. Moses' excuses reveal human inadequacy, but God equips those He calls.
4. Pharaoh's hardness of heart foreshadows the greater spiritual battle between God's kingdom and the powers of this world.
5. God's deliverance begins not when circumstances improve but when they get worse — He works through apparent setbacks.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The oppression of Israel described in Exodus 1 matches the building projects of the 19th Dynasty (c. 1290 BC). The cities Pithom and Raamses ([[Exodus 1_11]]) have been identified with Tell el-Retabeh and Qantir/Pi-Ramesses, the capital of Pharaoh Ramesses II. Mudbrick production with straw was standard in ancient Egyptian construction, as depicted in tomb paintings from Thebes.
- The Egyptian practice of forced labor by foreign captives (the *ḥmw* or "captives" class) is well documented in New Kingdom texts. The [[Brooklyn Papyrus 35.1446]] (18th Dynasty) lists over 80 slaves, many with Semitic names, including a female slave named "Shiphrah" — the same name as one of the Hebrew midwives ([[Exodus 1_15]]).

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Ipuwer Papyrus]] (Egyptian Museum, Leiden) contains striking parallels to the plagues described in Exodus — "the river is blood," "gates, walls, and columns are consumed by fire," and "the land is deprived of light." While Ipuwer may describe internal Egyptian chaos rather than the Exodus directly, the parallels are notable.
- The name [[Moses]] is Egyptian in origin (Egyptian *msy* = "son of" or "born of," as in Thutmose = "born of Thoth"), fitting his upbringing in Pharaoh's household. No direct archaeological evidence of Moses exists, which is consistent with the absence of non-royal Egyptian records of slaves or foreigners in official inscriptions.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Origen]] (c. 185–254 AD) saw in Moses' flight to Midian a pattern of the soul withdrawing from worldly temptations to encounter God. He described the burning bush as a type of the incarnation — God's presence in humble matter.
- [[Augustine of Hippo]] (354–430 AD) in his *Contra Faustum* interpreted the plunder of the Egyptians ([[Exodus 3_21-22.md]]; [[Exodus 12_35-36.md]]) as a legitimate use of worldly wealth for God's purposes, which he applied allegorically to Christians "plundering" pagan learning for the service of the Gospel.

## New Testament Connections

- [[Stephen]]'s speech in [[Acts 7_17-44.md]] recounts the story of Moses as the climax of Israel's history, emphasizing that Moses was "mighty in words and deeds" ([[Acts 7_22]]) before his calling — a detail not explicit in Exodus but preserved in Jewish tradition.
- [[Matthew]]'s infancy narrative ([[Matthew 2_13-23.md]]) deliberately parallels Moses' infancy — both are threatened by a murderous king, both are preserved by divine intervention, and both come out of Egypt. Matthew presents Jesus as the new and greater Moses.
- [[Hebrews 11_23-29.md]] lists Moses among the heroes of faith, noting that he "refused to be called the son of Pharaoh's daughter" and "esteemed the reproach of Christ greater riches than the treasures of Egypt."
