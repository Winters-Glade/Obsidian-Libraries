## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on the giving of the Law at Mount Sinai. This is one of the most important sections in all of Scripture — the establishment of the Old Covenant and the giving of the Ten Commandments. The awesome display of God's presence at Sinai demonstrates His holiness and the seriousness of His covenant.

## Summary

Israel arrives at Sinai in the third month. God offers the covenant: if they obey His voice and keep His covenant, they will be a peculiar treasure, a kingdom of priests, and a holy nation. The people agree. God descends on Sinai in fire, smoke, earthquake, and trumpet blast. The people tremble. God speaks the Ten Commandments directly. The first four commands govern humanity's relationship with God: no other gods, no idols, no vain use of God's name, keep the Sabbath. The remaining six govern relationships with others: honor parents, no murder, no adultery, no theft, no false witness, no coveting. The people, terrified, ask Moses to mediate. Moses draws near to God.

## Key Takeaways

1. The Law was given to a people already redeemed — grace precedes law. God saved them first, then gave them His standards.
2. The Ten Commandments summarize God's moral law and are divided into love for God (commandments 1-4) and love for neighbor (commandments 5-10).
3. The fear of the Lord is the proper response to encountering a holy God. It is not terror but reverential awe.
4. The people's request for a mediator shows their need for someone to stand between them and God — this points to Jesus, our Mediator.
5. The Law cannot save; it reveals sin and drives us to Christ. "By the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified in His sight" (Romans 3:20).

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The covenant structure at Sinai follows the pattern of ancient Near Eastern [[suzerainty treaties|suzerainty treaties]], particularly Hittite treaties from the 2nd millennium BC. The elements include: preamble ("I am the Lord your God"), historical prologue ("who brought you out of Egypt"), stipulations (the commandments), deposition (the tablets placed in the Ark), witnesses, and blessings/curses. This demonstrates that the biblical covenant is not an arbitrary list of rules but a formal treaty between the Great King (Yahweh) and His vassal people (Israel).
- The theophany at Sinai — fire, smoke, earthquake, trumpet blast (Exodus 19:16–19) — echoes the language of volcanic eruption, though Sinai is not a volcano. Similar imagery is used in Judges 5:4–5, Psalm 68:7–8, and Habakkuk 3:3–6 to describe God's presence. The "trumpet" (Hebrew *shofar*) was a ram's horn, its sound associated with theophany and later with the [[Day of Atonement]] (Leviticus 25:9).

## Archaeological Evidence

- The location of [[Mount Sinai]] is debated. The traditional site at [[Jebel Musa]] (Arabic for "Mountain of Moses") in the southern Sinai Peninsula has been venerated since the 4th century AD, housing [[St. Catherines Monastery]]. Alternative proposals include [[Jebel al-Lawz]] in Saudi Arabia (based on Galatians 4:25) and various sites in the Negev or Edom.
- The [[Nash Papyrus]] (c. 2nd century BC), discovered in Egypt, contains a version of the Ten Commandments with slight textual variations, demonstrating the early textual transmission and liturgical use of the Decalogue.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] (c. 130–202 AD) in *Against Heresies* argued that the Law was given not to save but to expose sin and restrain evil, preparing humanity for the Gospel. He saw the Ten Commandments as the "natural law" engraved on stone because it had been effaced from human hearts.
- [[John Calvin]] (1509–1564) in his *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (Book 2, Chapters 7–8) articulated the threefold use of the Law: (1) a mirror to reveal sin, (2) a restraint on evil in society, and (3) a guide for Christian living (the "third use" of the Law).

## New Testament Connections

- [[Matthew 5_17-48.md]] — Jesus repeatedly says "You have heard that it was said... but I say to you," intensifying the demands of the Law. He does not abolish the Law but fulfills it, showing that its true standard goes far beyond external compliance to the inner disposition of the heart.
- [[Galatians 3_19–25.md]] — Paul argues that the Law was a "tutor" (*paidagogos*) to lead us to Christ. The Law was added "because of transgressions" until the Seed (Christ) should come. It was never intended as a means of justification.
- [[Hebrews 12_18–24.md]] contrasts Mount Sinai (physical, terrifying, the Old Covenant) with Mount Zion (heavenly, joyful, the New Covenant). The contrast highlights the superiority of the New Covenant mediated by Christ.
