## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith covers the departure from Egypt, the consecration of the firstborn, the miraculous crossing of the Red Sea, and the Song of Moses. This section contains one of the most dramatic acts of deliverance in Scripture and the first great song of praise recorded in the Bible.

## Summary

Moses sanctifies the firstborn to the Lord. God leads Israel via the wilderness route to avoid war with the Philistines. The pillar of cloud by day and fire by night guides them. Pharaoh pursues with his army, trapping Israel at the sea. The people panic, but Moses declares, "Stand still and see the salvation of the Lord." The angel and pillar move behind Israel, giving light to them and darkness to Egypt. Moses stretches his rod over the sea; the waters part. Israel crosses on dry ground. The Egyptians pursue and are drowned when the waters return. Moses and Israel sing a song of triumph — "The Lord is my strength and song." Miriam leads the women in dance. At Marah, bitter waters are made sweet by a tree. At Elim, they camp by twelve wells and seventy palm trees.

## Key Takeaways

1. God leads His people in unexpected ways — the longer route through the wilderness was for their protection.
2. The Red Sea crossing is the defining miracle of the Old Testament — it demonstrates God's power over creation and His commitment to His people.
3. "Stand still and see the salvation of the Lord" — sometimes our part is to trust and watch God work.
4. The Song of Moses is the first worship song recorded — worship is the proper response to deliverance.
5. The tree that sweetened the waters at Marah is a type of the cross — Christ's sacrifice transforms bitterness into blessing.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The crossing of the [[Red Sea]] (Hebrew *Yam Suph* — "Sea of Reeds" or "Sea of End") has been debated as to location. The traditional site at the [[Gulf of Suez]] and alternative proposals at the [[Serbonian Bog]] (Lake Sirbonis) or the [[Bitter Lakes]] region each have strengths. The narrative describes a "strong east wind" (Exodus 14:21) driving the waters back — a natural phenomenon employed by a supernatural act of God.
- The [[Song of Moses]] (Exodus 15) is one of the oldest poems in the Hebrew Bible, preserving archaic linguistic features. It follows the structure of ancient Near Eastern victory hymns (cf. the Song of [[Deborah]] in Judges 5 and the Ugaritic [[Baal Cycle]]). The refrain "The Lord is a man of war; the Lord is His name" (Exodus 15:3) parallels Canaanite epithets for Baal as a divine warrior.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Merneptah Stele]] (c. 1208 BC), discovered by Flinders Petrie in 1896, contains the earliest extrabiblical reference to Israel as a people in Canaan: "Israel is laid waste — his seed is no more." This proves that Israel was established in Canaan by the late 13th century BC, providing key evidence for the chronology of the Exodus.
- The [[Berlin Pedestal]] (c. 13th century BC) from the reign of [[Ramesses II]] or [[Merneptah]] may contain the name "Israel" alongside other Canaanite place names, offering additional early attestation of Israel's presence in Canaan.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Gregory of Nyssa]] (c. 335–395 AD) in his *Life of Moses* interpreted the Red Sea crossing as the archetypal Christian baptism — the Egyptians (sins) are drowned, and Israel (the believer) passes through water to new life. He saw the pillar of cloud and fire as the guidance of the Holy Spirit.
- [[Origen]] in his *Homilies on Exodus* expanded the typology: the Red Sea crossing prefigures baptism (1 Corinthians 10:1–2), the destruction of Pharaoh's army represents the defeat of Satan, and the Song of Moses and Miriam represents the worship of the redeemed in heaven.

## New Testament Connections

- [[1 Corinthians 10_1–2.md]] explicitly identifies the Red Sea crossing as a typological precursor to Christian baptism: "All were baptized into Moses in the cloud and in the sea." Paul uses this as a warning against presumption and a call to faithful perseverance.
- The [[Song of Moses]] appears again in [[_Bible/Revelation/Revelation 15_3.md]], sung by those who "have victory over the beast." This connects the first great redemption (Exodus from Egypt) with the final great redemption (deliverance from the end-times beast).
- The bitter water made sweet by a tree at [[Marah]] (Exodus 15:23–25) is a type of the cross of Christ — the tree (cross) transforms the bitterness of sin and judgment into the sweetness of salvation. [[John 19_29]] echoes this when Jesus is offered vinegar (bitter) on a hyssop branch (associated with Passover).
