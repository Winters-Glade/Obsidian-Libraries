## Summary

Chapter 26: The Tabernacle is constructed with 10 linen curtains with cherubim, covered by goat hair, ram skins, and badger skins. Acacia wood boards overlaid with gold are set in silver sockets. The veil separates the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place. Chapter 27: The bronze altar for burnt offerings sits in the outer court. The lampstand is to burn continually with pure olive oil. Chapter 28: Holy garments are made for Aaron — the ephod with shoulder stones bearing the twelve tribes, the breastplate of judgment with twelve precious stones, the robe with bells and pomegranates, and the turban inscribed "Holiness to the Lord." The Urim and Thummim are placed in the breastplate for discerning God's will. Chapter 29: The consecration ceremony for priests involves washing, anointing, sin offerings, burnt offerings, and seven days of consecration. The daily burnt offering — a lamb morning and evening — is instituted.

## Key Takeaways

1. The Tabernacle structure, with its courts, veil, and Most Holy Place, teaches that approaching God requires specific preparation and atonement.
2. The veil, separating man from God's presence, was torn at Christ's death — the way into the Holiest is now open through His blood.
3. The High Priest's garments — bearing Israel on his shoulders (strength) and on his heart (love) — beautifully picture Christ's intercession for us.
4. "Holiness to the Lord" on the priest's forehead reminds us that those who serve God must be consecrated to Him.
5. The daily burnt offering (morning and evening) points to the continual worship and sacrifice that God's people should offer.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The Tabernacle structure — a rectangular tent with a courtyard, divided into Holy Place and Most Holy Place by a veil — follows the pattern of [[Egyptian]] and [[Canaanite]] tent sanctuaries, yet transforms the concept. Unlike pagan temples where the god's image was housed, the [[Holy of Holies]] contained no image — only the Ark with the tablets of the Law, signifying that Yahweh is not to be represented by any physical form.
- The [[High Priests garments]] (Exodus 28) — [[ephod|ephod]], [[breastplate|breastplate]], [[robe|robe]], [[tunic|tunic]], [[turban|turban]], and [[sash|sash]] — were made "for glory and for beauty" (Exodus 28:2). The [[breastplate of judgment|breastplate of judgment]] contained twelve precious stones, each engraved with the name of a tribe. This corresponds to the twelve foundation stones of the New Jerusalem (Revelation 21:19–20). The [[Urim and Thummim]] ("lights and perfections") were probably sacred lots used to discern God's will.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[tomb of Tutankhamun|tomb of Tutankhamun]] (c. 1323 BC) revealed remarkable craftsmanship in gold, acacia wood, and precious stones from the approximate period of the Exodus (if an early date). The skill of Egyptian artisans in overlay work, weaving, and gem setting provides context for the Tabernacle's elaborate construction.
- The [[Ketef Hinnom silver scrolls]] (c. 600 BC), containing the Aaronic blessing (Numbers 6:24–26), demonstrate the importance of priestly blessing formulas in ancient Israel and the early use of Scripture in amulets.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[John Chrysostom]] in his *Homilies on Hebrews* (Homily 15) expounded on the veil of the Tabernacle, explaining that the tearing of the veil at Christ's death (Matthew 27:51) signified that the way into God's presence was now open to all, not just the High Priest once a year.
- [[Jerome]] (c. 347–420 AD) in his *Letters* (Epistle 64) provided a detailed allegorical interpretation of the High Priest's garments: the ephod's two onyx stones (shoulders) represent Christ's divine nature bearing the church; the twelve stones (breastplate) represent the apostles; and the bells on the robe represent the sound of preaching.

## New Testament Connections

- [[Hebrews 9_1–14.md]] — The author carefully describes the Tabernacle's two compartments and argues that Christ entered the "greater and more perfect tabernacle not made with hands" with His own blood, securing eternal redemption. The earthly High Priest entered annually with animal blood; Christ entered once with His own blood.
- [[Hebrews 4_14-16.md]] — Jesus is the "great High Priest who has passed through the heavens," enabling us to "come boldly to the throne of grace." The High Priest's role as mediator is fulfilled perfectly in Christ.
- [[Matthew 27_51.md]] — At the moment of Christ's death, "the veil of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom." This signified that the barrier between God and humanity — symbolized by the Tabernacle veil (Exodus 26:31–33) — was removed forever through Christ's atoning work.
