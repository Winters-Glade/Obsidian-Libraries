## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith teaches on the climactic tenth plague — the death of the firstborn — and the institution of the Passover. This is the most theologically significant section in Exodus, establishing the foundational event of Israel's redemption and a profound type of Christ's atoning work.

## Summary

Moses warns Pharaoh of the final plague: at midnight all firstborn in Egypt will die. Then the Lord institutes the Passover. Each household must take a lamb without blemish, kill it, and apply its blood to the doorposts and lintel. They are to eat the lamb roasted with unleavened bread and bitter herbs, ready to depart. The blood is a sign — when the Lord sees it, He will pass over that house. At midnight, judgment falls. Every firstborn in Egypt dies — from Pharaoh's palace to the dungeon. Pharaoh urgently commands Israel to leave. About 600,000 men plus women and children depart, having plundered the Egyptians. The Passover becomes a perpetual ordinance for Israel.

## Key Takeaways

1. The Passover is the central Old Testament picture of atonement — a substitute dies, blood is applied, and judgment passes over.
2. Salvation is by blood — the lamb's death, not the Israelites' merit, secured their deliverance.
3. The blood must be applied — the lamb's death alone was not enough; the blood had to be on the doorposts (faith applied).
4. The Passover directly prefigures Christ — He is the Lamb of God without blemish, whose blood delivers us from the judgment of sin.
5. The "mixed multitude" reminds us that God's salvation is open to all who will identify with His people.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The Passover ceremony draws on ancient Near Eastern shepherding traditions. The smearing of blood on doorposts and lintel ([[Exodus 12_7]]) mirrors ANE apotropaic rituals — blood applied to doorways to ward off evil. However, the biblical meaning is transformed: the blood is not a magical charm but a sign of faith in God's provision of a substitute.
- The Hebrew term *pesach* (פסח) likely means "to pass over" or "to spare." The lamb was to be roasted whole (not boiled), eaten with unleavened bread (*matzah* — symbolizing haste, as there was no time for the dough to rise), and bitter herbs (*maror* — recalling the bitterness of Egyptian bondage). These elements remain central to the Jewish Seder to this day.
- The "mixed multitude" ([[_Bible/Exodus/Exodus 12_38.md]]) that left Egypt with Israel — a *erev rav* — indicates that the Exodus was not exclusively ethnic but included Egyptians and others who chose to identify with Yahweh. This foreshadows the inclusion of Gentiles in the people of God.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Elephantine Papyri]] (5th century BC) from a Jewish military colony in Egypt attest to the observance of Passover outside Jerusalem centuries after the Exodus, confirming the enduring significance of the festival.
- The tomb of [[Ramesses II]]'s son, [[Khaemwaset]], survived — but no Egyptian record mentions a mass death of firstborns, as Egyptian scribes typically suppressed humiliating events. The absence of such a record is consistent with ancient historiographical practice.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Melito of Sardis]] (c. 180 AD) in his *Peri Pascha* (On the Passover) wrote one of the earliest Christian theological treatments of the Passover, arguing that the lamb's blood prefigured Christ's blood and that the Exodus from Egypt typified the Christian's deliverance from sin and death.
- [[Justin Martyr]] (c. 150 AD) in his *Dialogue with Trypho* argued extensively that the Passover lamb was a type of Christ, noting that the lamb was to be "without blemish" and roasted with its head and entrails — pointing to the perfection of Christ and the completeness of His sacrifice.

## New Testament Connections

- [[John the Baptist]]'s declaration, "Behold the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29), directly alludes to the Passover lamb. The chronology of John's Gospel places Jesus' crucifixion at the same time the Passover lambs were being slaughtered in the Temple (John 19:14).
- [[_Bible/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 5_7.md]] explicitly states: "Christ our Passover is sacrificed for us." Paul follows this with a call to "keep the feast" with unleavened bread of sincerity and truth — applying Passover imagery to Christian moral purity.
- The [[Last Supper]] was a Passover Seder (Matthew 26:17–30). Jesus reinterpreted the elements — the bread as His body, the cup as His blood of the New Covenant — transforming the Passover from the memorial of physical redemption from Egypt to the celebration of spiritual redemption from sin.
