## Overview

Pastor Chuck Smith continues through the narrative of [[The Ten Plagues]]. This section covers plagues one through nine (blood, frogs, lice, flies, livestock disease, boils, hail, locusts, darkness). Each plague demonstrates God's power over the [[Gods of Egypt|gods of Egypt]] and progressively judges [[Pharaoh]]'s hardened heart.

## Summary

God reassures Moses and reveals Himself as [[Yahweh]], the covenant-keeping God. The genealogies establish Aaron and Moses' credentials. The plagues begin as a direct challenge to Pharaoh and the [[Gods of Egypt|gods of Egypt]]. Each plague escalates in severity: water turned to blood (judgment on the Nile god Hapi), frogs (Heqet), lice, flies, livestock disease (Hathor), boils, hail and fire (Nut/Seth), locusts, and darkness (Ra). The magicians initially mimic the miracles but are unable to replicate the later plagues and confess "This is the finger of God." [[Goshen]] is repeatedly spared, demonstrating God's ability to protect His people. Pharaoh alternates between relenting and hardening his heart.

## Key Takeaways

1. The plagues are not random acts of destruction — they are targeted judgments against specific Egyptian deities, demonstrating Yahweh's supremacy.
2. There is a progression in judgment — each plague intensifies, giving Pharaoh opportunity to repent.
3. God draws a clear line of distinction between His people and the world — Goshen is protected.
4. Hardness of heart is both something Pharaoh does ([[Exodus 8_15|Exodus 8:15]], [[Exodus 8_32|32]]) and something God does ([[Exodus 9_12]]) — a mystery of divine sovereignty and human responsibility.
5. The magicians' inability to replicate later plagues shows that satanic power has limits.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- Each of the ten plagues targeted a specific Egyptian deity: the Nile ([[Hapi]] turned to blood; frogs ([[Heqet]]); lice from dust ([[Geb]], the earth god); flies ([[Khepri]], the scarab god); livestock disease ([[Hathor]]/[[Apis]]); boils ([[Sekhmet]]/[[Imhotep]]); hail and fire ([[Nut]], sky goddess, and [[Seth]]); locusts ([[Nepri]], grain god); darkness ([[Ra]], the sun god); and the death of the firstborn ([[Osiris]]/[[Pharaoh]] himself as divine). This systematic dismantling of Egypt's pantheon was a direct assault on Egyptian theology.
- The magicians of Egypt mentioned in [[Exodus 7_1-8_32.md]] are named [[Jannes and Jambres]] in Jewish tradition ([[2 Timothy 3_8.md]]). Egyptian magical papyri (such as the [[Papyrus Harris]] and the [[Westcar Papyrus]]) describe magicians performing similar feats — serpent charming and water manipulation — confirming that such practices were well-established in Pharaonic court life.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Ipuwer Papyrus]] (c. 19th-18th century BC) from the Egyptian Museum in Leiden contains descriptions that eerily parallel the plagues: "the river is blood" (cf. [[Exodus 7_20]]), "the land is without light" (cf. [[Exodus 10_22.md]]), and "cattle are left to stray" (cf. [[Exodus 9_3]]). Skeptics note Ipuwer may describe internal collapse, but the parallels remain striking.
- The [[Berlin Block]] and the [[Avaris]] excavations (Tell el-Dab'a) show evidence of Semitic populations in the eastern Nile Delta during the Second Intermediate Period, providing archaeological context for the Israelite presence in [[Goshen]].

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Augustine]] in *The City of God* (Book 12) used the plagues as an extended allegory of divine judgment on sin. He noted that the magicians could mimic the first plagues but not the later ones — demonstrating that demonic power is derivative and limited.
- [[John Chrysostom]] (c. 347–407 AD) preached that Pharaoh's alternating repentance and hardening exemplified the danger of temporary repentance without genuine heart transformation.

## New Testament Connections

- The plagues are echoed in the [[Revelation 19]] judgments (the trumpet and bowl judgments in [[Revelation 8_1-16_21.md]]), which follow a similar pattern of escalating judgment giving opportunity for repentance. Hail and fire (Exodus 9) appears in [[Revelation 8_7.md]]; darkness (Exodus 10) in [[Revelation 16_10.md]]; water turned to blood (Exodus 7) in [[Revelation 16_3-4.md]].
- [[Paul]] references Jannes and Jambres in [[2 Timothy 3_8-9.md]] as examples of those who "resist the truth" — a pattern of false teachers whose folly will be exposed.
- [[Romans 9_17–18.md]] uses Pharaoh's hardening as a key illustration of divine sovereignty and human responsibility in salvation.

## For Further Study
