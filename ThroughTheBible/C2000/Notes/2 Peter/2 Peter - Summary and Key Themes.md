## Historical & Contextual Background

> [!QUESTION]- This enrichment needs QT verification

**Authorship & Dating:** 2 Peter is among the most disputed New Testament books for authorship. It claims to be written by "Symeon Peter" (1:1), but most critical scholars consider it pseudepigraphal (c. AD 100–150). Reasons: sophisticated Greek different from 1 Peter, dependence on Jude, reference to Paul's letters as "scripture" (3:16), and a tone addressing post-apostolic concerns. Evangelical scholars who defend Petrine authorship often date it c. AD 64–67, shortly before Peter's death.

**Relationship to Jude:** 2 Peter 2 is closely parallel to Jude 4–18, with most scholars concluding that either Peter used Jude or both drew from a common source. The overlap includes: angels who sinned, Sodom and Gomorrah, Balaam's error, false teachers' characteristics, and the mockers in the last days.

**False Teachers:** The opponents appear to be early Gnostic-influenced teachers who denied Christ's second coming, lived licentiously, and exploited believers. The skepticism about the Parousia ("Where is the promise of His coming?" — 3:4) suggests a later generation was growing impatient with the delay of Christ's return.

**The Day of the Lord:** Peter's defense of the delayed Parousia (3:8–10) — "one day is as a thousand years" (Psalm 90:4) — has become a foundational proof-text for the doctrine of divine timelessness. The prediction that the elements will be "dissolved with fire" echoes Stoic *ekpyrosis* (world-conflagration) language while grounding it in biblical prophetic tradition (Isaiah 34:4; Malachi 4:1).
