## Historical & Contextual Background

> [!QUESTION]- This enrichment needs QT verification

**Authorship & Context:** The author identifies himself as "Jude, a servant of Jesus Christ and brother of James" — making him the brother of James the Just and thus a brother (or half-brother) of Jesus himself (Matthew 13:55; Mark 6:3). The letter is fiercely polemical, written to urge believers to "contend for the faith" against false teachers who had "crept in unnoticed" (v. 4).

**Apocryphal Literature Quotations:** Jude is remarkable for quoting non-canonical Jewish literature as authoritative:

- **1 Enoch:** Jude 14–15 directly quotes 1 Enoch 1:9 — "Behold, the Lord comes with ten thousands of his holy ones, to execute judgment." 1 Enoch was a widely circulated Jewish apocalypse (complete copies survive in Ethiopic) and heavily influenced early Christian angelology and eschatology.
- **Assumption of Moses:** Jude 9 refers to Michael the archangel contending with the devil over the body of Moses — an episode not found in the canonical Old Testament but preserved in the lost Assumption of Moses (a 1st-century Jewish apocryphon). This tradition is also referenced in the Testament of Moses.
- **Cain, Balaam, Korah:** The three Old Testament examples (v. 11) represent the pattern of false teachers: Cain's envy, Balaam's greed, and Korah's rebellion against God's appointed authority (Numbers 16).

**Relationship to 2 Peter:** Jude shares extensive material with 2 Peter 2, and 70–80% of Jude's content appears in 2 Peter. Most scholars believe 2 Peter used Jude as a source, expanding and generalizing Jude's specific warnings.

**The Doxology:** Jude's closing doxology (vv. 24–25) is considered one of the finest doxologies in the New Testament — "Now to him who is able to keep you from stumbling and to present you blameless before the presence of his glory with great joy." It is frequently used in Christian liturgy.

**Canonical Journey:** Jude was among the "disputed books" (*Antilegomena*) of the New Testament canon. Its use of apocryphal literature raised questions about its authority, but it was eventually accepted into the canon due to its apostolic connections and widespread use.
