## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Micah, chapters 1-7.

**Micah:** Micah, a contemporary of Isaiah, prophesies to both Israel and Judah. He condemns corrupt leaders, false prophets, and social injustice. The book contains the famous prophecy of Bethlehem as the birthplace of the Messiah (Micah 5:2) and the timeless summary: ''What does the Lord require of you but to do justice, love mercy, and walk humbly with your God'' (Micah 6:8).

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Micah prophesied during the reigns of Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah of Judah (c. 735–700 BC) — a period of upheaval as Assyria conquered the Northern Kingdom and threatened Judah:

- The **Sennacherib Prism** (Taylor Prism, c. 691 BC) records the Assyrian king Sennacherib's invasion of Judah in 701 BC, claiming to have besieged 46 fortified cities and shut up Hezekiah "like a bird in a cage" in Jerusalem — a direct historical witness to the events Micah prophesied. The prism confirms that Jerusalem was not captured, consistent with Micah's hope for the remnant.
- The **Lachish Reliefs** (from Sennacherib's palace at Nineveh, now in the British Museum) depict the siege of Lachish, one of Judah's key fortresses. These reliefs provide a visual record of the Assyrian military campaigns that Micah warned about, showing siege ramps, battering rams, and the deportation of Judean captives.
- The **Silwan Inscription** (c. 700 BC), found in the Kidron Valley near Jerusalem, is associated with the tomb of Shebna, a royal steward mentioned in [[Isaiah 22_15.md]] — a contemporary of Micah. This inscription shows the Hebrew script of the period.
- The **Hezekiah's Tunnel Inscription** (the Siloam Inscription, c. 701 BC) documents the construction of the water tunnel described in [[2 Kings 20_20.md]], likely a response to the Assyrian siege preparations that Micah and Isaiah were involved with.

## Eschatological Significance

- **Micah 5:2** — The [[Prophecy]] is one of the most specific messianic prophecies in the Hebrew Bible, declaring the Messiah's birthplace. This was cited by the chief priests and scribes when Herod inquired where the Christ was to be born ([[Matthew 2_4-6.md]]). In Chuck Smith's teaching, this demonstrates the literal fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy in Jesus of Nazareth.
- **Micah 4:1-4** — The vision of the mountain of the Lord's house established above the mountains, with nations streaming to it and weapons beaten into plowshares — this is a prominent vision of the millennial kingdom in Chuck Smith's eschatology. "They shall sit every man under his vine and under his fig tree" (Micah 4:4) is a picture of peace in God's future kingdom.

## New Testament Connections

- **Micah 5:2** — "But you, Bethlehem Ephrathah" — quoted directly in [[Matthew 2_5-6.md]] in connection with Jesus' birth, establishing Micah as a key messianic prophet.
- **Micah 6:8** — "What does the Lord require of you" — while not directly quoted, this verse deeply influenced NT ethics. It resonates with [[James]] (pure religion is caring for orphans and widows) and [[_Bible/Matthew/Matthew 23_23.md]] (Jesus' rebuke of Pharisees for neglecting "the weightier matters of the law: justice, mercy, and faithfulness").
- **Micah 7:6** — "A man's enemies are the members of his own household" — quoted by Jesus in [[Matthew 10_35-36.md]] as He warned of the divisions the gospel would bring.
- **Micah 7:18-19** — God's compassion and forgiveness ("who pardons iniquity and passes over transgression") — reflected in [[Romans 5_20]] ("where sin increased, grace abounded all the more").

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Micah and [[Isaiah]]** — Contemporaries in the 8th century BC. There is a striking parallelism between Micah 4:1-3 and [[Isaiah 2_2-4.md]] (the mountain of the Lord / swords into plowshares), suggesting a shared prophetic oracle or mutual influence.
- **Micah and [[Amos]]** — Both denounce social injustice among the wealthy and call for ethical religion as the true expression of covenant faithfulness.
- **Micah and [[Hosea]]** — Both emphasize God's reliable love (chesed) — Hosea through the marriage metaphor, Micah through the famous summary of God's requirements.
- **Micah and [[Zechariah]]** — Both prophesy about the Messiah. Micah identifies Bethlehem as the birthplace; Zechariah provides extensive details about His coming and rejection.
