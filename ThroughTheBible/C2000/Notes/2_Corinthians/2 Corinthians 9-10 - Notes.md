## Flavor Text

### The City of Corinth
Corinth was a Roman colony refounded by Julius Caesar in 44 BC, rising from the ashes of its destruction by Lucius Mummius in 146 BC. By Paul's day, it was the capital of the province of Achaia and one of the wealthiest commercial centers in the Mediterranean. Its strategic location on the narrow Isthmus of Corinth gave it two thriving ports — Lechaeum on the Corinthian Gulf and Cenchreae on the Saronic Gulf — making it the crossroads of north-south and east-west trade.

The city had a reputation for moral laxity so notorious that the Greek verb *korinthiazesthai* ("to act like a Corinthian") meant to practice sexual immorality. The Temple of Aphrodite on the Acrocorinth (the city's acropolis) employed a thousand temple prostitutes, though modern scholarship debates the extent of sacred prostitution in the Roman period. Horace quipped that "not everyone is able to go to Corinth" — a reference to its exorbitant cost of living and moral temptations.

### Archaeological Highlights
Excavations by the American School of Classical Studies (beginning 1896) have uncovered:
- The **Bema** (judgment seat), a raised stone platform in the forum where Paul likely stood before Gallio (Acts 18:12-17)
- The **Erastus Inscription**, a first-century paving stone naming "Erastus, commissioner of public works" — almost certainly the same Erastus Paul calls "the city's director of public works" (Romans 16:23, written from Corinth)
- The **Temple of Apollo** (c. 550 BC), whose seven remaining Doric columns still dominate the archaeological site
- The **Lechaion Road**, the marble-paved thoroughfare leading to the western port
- The **Fountain of Peirene**, an ancient water source adorned with marble facades
- The **Synagogue inscription**, a stone lintel fragment reading "Synagogue of the Hebrews," likely from the synagogue where Paul preached (Acts 18:4)

### Greco-Roman Cultural Background
- **Citizenship & Social Status:** As a Roman colony, Corinth granted its free citizens Roman citizenship with full legal rights. This created a sharp divide between citizens, non-citizen residents (*peregrini*), freedpersons (*liberti*), and the large enslaved population. Paul's gospel message that in Christ "there is neither slave nor free" (Galatians 3:28) directly challenged these social hierarchies.
- **The Isthmian Games:** Second only to the Olympics, these games were held at the Isthmus of Corinth every two years. Paul likely drew on this imagery in 1 Corinthians 9:24-27, where he speaks of athletes competing for a perishable wreath — the Isthmian crown was made of withered celery or pine.
- **Wealth Disparity:** The population of about 200,000 included a mix of Roman colonists, Greeks, Jews, freed criminals, and a massive enslaved workforce. The Lord's Supper in Corinth was marred by class division, with the rich eating and drinking while the poor went hungry (1 Corinthians 11:20-22).

### Cross-Reference to Acts
Acts 18:1-18 records Paul's first visit to Corinth during his second missionary journey (c. AD 50-52). He arrived from Athens, stayed with Aquila and Priscilla (fellow tentmakers recently expelled from Rome by Claudius' edict), and preached in the synagogue every Sabbath. When opposition arose, Paul famously shook out his garments and declared, "Your blood be on your own heads! I am innocent. From now on I will go to the Gentiles" (Acts 18:6), moving his ministry next door to the house of Titius Justus. The Lord appeared to Paul in a vision, saying "Do not be afraid; speak and do not be silent, for I am with you" (Acts 18:9-10), resulting in an 18-month stay — longer than any other stop on that journey. The proconsul Gallio (Lucius Junius Gallio Annaeanus, brother of the philosopher Seneca) dismissed Jewish charges against Paul, providing a key date fixed by the Delphi inscription (AD 51-52).

### Connections to Other Pauline Letters
- Paul likely wrote **1 Thessalonians** from Corinth during this stay (c. AD 51).
- The **Epistle to the Romans** was later written from Corinth during Paul's third missionary journey (c. AD 57), as indicated by his mention of Phoebe from Cenchreae (Romans 16:1).
- The Corinthian correspondence is intimately connected to the **collection for the Jerusalem saints** (1 Corinthians 16:1-4; 2 Corinthians 8-9), a project Paul also urged upon the Galatian and Macedonian churches.
- Issues addressed in Corinth — [[Spiritual Gifts]], the [[Resurrection]], church unity — form the most detailed pastoral teaching in the Pauline corpus on these subjects.

### The City of Corinth

[[Corinth]] was one of the most prominent cities of Roman Greece. Destroyed by the Roman consul Mummius in 146 BC, it was refounded as a Roman colony (Colonia Laus Iulia Corinthiensis) by [[Julius Caesar]] in 44 BC. By Paul's time, it had become the capital of the Roman province of [[Achaia]] (modern southern Greece) and a bustling commercial center with two major ports — [[Cenchreae]] to the east and [[Lechaion]] to the west — controlling trade routes between Italy and Asia.

The city was notorious even in the pagan world for its moral laxity. The Greek verb *korinthiazesthai* meant "to act like a Corinthian" — to practice fornication. The Temple of [[Aphrodite]] atop the [[Acrocorinth]] — a towering 1,886-foot citadel — was said to employ a thousand temple prostitutes, though modern scholarship questions the extent of cultic prostitution in the Roman period.

### Paul in Corinth (Acts 18)

Paul arrived in Corinth during his second missionary journey (c. AD 50-52), after his time in [[Athens]]. He lodged with [[Aquila and Priscilla]], Jewish tentmakers who had recently been expelled from Rome under Claudius's edict (AD 49). Paul ministered there for **18 months** — one of his longest stays in any city — and was brought before the [[Bema]] (judgment seat) of [[Proconsul Gallio]], an event that provides one of the firmest chronological anchors for Paul's life (Gallio is known from the Delphi Inscription to have been proconsul in AD 51-52).

### Archaeological Discoveries

- **The Erastus Inscription** — A first-century pavement inscription discovered near the theater of Corinth reads "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense." This almost certainly refers to the [[Erastus]] mentioned in Romans 16:23 as the "city treasurer" of Corinth, confirming the presence of high-ranking Roman officials in the early church.
- **The Bema (Judgment Seat)** — The large bema platform in the center of the Roman forum, where Paul was brought before Gallio (Acts 18:12-17), is still visible today.
- **The Gallio Inscription** — A fragmentary inscription from Delphi (now at the Delphi Archaeological Museum) mentions Gallio as proconsul of Achaia, dating his term to AD 51-52.
- **The Fountain of Peirene** — A monumental public fountain that was the city's main water supply, located in the agora.

### Greco-Roman Cultural Background

- **Rhetoric and Wisdom** — Paul's Corinthian audience prized Greek rhetorical skill and philosophical wisdom. The Corinthians' fascination with eloquent speakers (1 Corinthians 1-4) reflects the broader Hellenistic culture where traveling sophists competed for followers.
- **Temple Meats (Idolothyta)** — The debate over food sacrificed to idols (1 Corinthians 8-10) was especially acute in Corinth, where the city was filled with temples and trade guilds held feasts in temple precincts.
- **Spiritual Gifts and Ecstasy** — The Corinthian enthusiasm for ecstatic spiritual experiences may have been influenced by ecstatic practices of pagan mystery religions (Dionysian cults, the cult of Cybele).
- **The Lord's Supper as a Communal Meal** — The abuses Paul addresses (1 Cor 11:17-34) reflect the practice of the *agape* feast in private homes, where social stratification between wealthy patrons and poorer members created division.


### The Blessing of Generosity and Paul's Defense (Chapters 9-10)

Paul continues his appeal for the Jerusalem collection (chapter 9), promising that God is able to "make all grace abound toward you" (2 Cor 9:8). In chapter 10, the tone shifts as Paul begins to defend his apostolic authority against "false apostles" (2 Cor 11:13). His description of spiritual warfare — "the weapons of our warfare are not of the flesh but have divine power to destroy strongholds" (2 Cor 10:4) — has inspired generations of believers.
