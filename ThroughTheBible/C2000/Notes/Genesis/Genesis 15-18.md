## Overview
Pastor Chuck Smith covers God's [[Abrahamic Covenant|covenant with Abram]] (chapter 15), the birth of [[Ishmael]] (chapter 16), the establishment of [[Circumcision|circumcision]] and the change of names (chapter 17), and the visit of the three angels with the promise of [[Isaac]] and the [[intercession|intercession]] for [[Sodom and Gomorrah|Sodom]] (chapter 18).

## Summary
Pastor Chuck Smith walks through four pivotal chapters in Abraham's life. In chapter 15, God speaks to Abram in a vision, declaring "I am thy shield and exceeding great reward." Despite Abram's childlessness, God promises descendants as numerous as the stars, and Abram believes God—counted as [[Righteousness by Faith|righteousness]]. God formalizes the covenant with a smoking furnace and burning lamp passing between the animal pieces, and reveals that Abram's descendants will be slaves in [[Egypt]] for 400 years. In chapter 16, Sarah's impatience leads to the Hagar plan, producing [[Ishmael]]—a man whose descendants would be a constant problem. Chapter 17 brings the [[Covenant of Circumcision|covenant of circumcision]] and name changes: Abram (exalted father) becomes [[Abraham]] (father of multitudes), and Sarai becomes [[Sarah]] (princess). God promises [[Isaac]] within a year. Chapter 18 describes the visit of three angels to Abraham's tent, the promise of Isaac's birth, and Abraham's bold [[intercession|intercession]] for [[Sodom and Gomorrah|Sodom]], bargaining from 50 down to 10 righteous.

## Historical & Cultural Context
- The covenant ceremony in [[Genesis 15_7–21.md]]—cutting animals in half and passing between the pieces—reflects an ANE treaty-making ritual known from the [[Mari Tablets]] and [[Jeremiah 34_18.md]]. The contracting parties would walk between the divided carcasses, symbolically saying, "May I become like these animals if I break the covenant." Strikingly, only God (represented by the smoking furnace and burning lamp) passes through, making this an unconditional covenant.
- The practice of a barren wife giving her maidservant to her husband to bear children ([[Genesis 16_1–4.md]]) is attested in the [[Nuzi Tablets]] (c. 1500–1400 BC), which record marriage contracts stipulating that if a wife is barren, she must provide a maidservant to produce an heir. This confirms that Sarah's actions followed the legal customs of the time.

## Archaeological Evidence
- The [[Nuzi Tablets]] (c. 1500–1400 BC), discovered at [[Yorghan Tepe]] (ancient Nuzi) in Iraq, contain numerous legal and social parallels to the patriarchal narratives: the adoption of a household servant as heir when childless (cf. [[Genesis 15_2–3.md]]), the provision of a maidservant for procreation (cf. [[Genesis 16_1–4.md]]), and oral deathbed blessings that had legal force (cf. [[Genesis 27.md]]).
- The [[Covenant of Circumcision]] ([[Genesis 17_10–14.md]])—the removal of the foreskin—was practiced in ancient Egypt (as attested by tomb reliefs at [[Saqqara]]) and other ANE cultures, though only in Israel did it carry theological significance as the sign of God's covenant relationship.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[Augustine]] in *The City of God* (Book 16) extensively analyzed the Abraham narrative, focusing on [[Genesis 15_6.md]] ("he believed in the Lord, and He counted it to him for righteousness") as the foundational prooftext for justification by faith—a passage that would later become central to the Reformation.
- [[John Chrysostom]] in his *Homilies on Genesis* (Homily 38–40) praised Abraham's intercession for Sodom ([[Genesis 18_16–33.md]]) as a model of bold yet reverent prayer, noting how Abraham's persistence demonstrated intimacy with God while maintaining humility ("I who am but dust and ashes").

## New Testament Connections
- [[Romans 4_1–25.md]]—the keystone of Pauline theology—cites [[Genesis 15_6.md]] to prove that justification is by faith apart from works of the law. Paul argues that Abraham was counted righteous before circumcision, making him the father of all who believe, whether Jew or Gentile.
- [[Galatians 4_22–31.md]] presents [[Hagar]] and [[Sarah]] as an allegory of two covenants: Hagar represents Mount Sinai (bondage to the law), while Sarah represents the Jerusalem above (freedom in Christ). This allegory was central to Paul's argument that believers are children of promise, not of the flesh.
- [[James 2_21–23.md]] provides the complementary perspective: Abraham's faith was "perfected" by his works, specifically his willingness to offer Isaac, demonstrating that genuine faith produces obedience. This reconciles the justification by faith (Paul) with faith demonstrated by works (James).
- [[John 8_56]] records Jesus's statement that "Abraham rejoiced to see My day, and he saw it and was glad," indicating that the patriarch had prophetic insight into the coming Messiah.

## For Further Study
- The relationship between faith and works in the Abraham narrative is one of the most important theological themes in Scripture. For Reformed perspectives, see John Murray's *The Epistle to the Romans* (on Romans 4); for a Lutheran perspective, see the *Luther's Works* volume on Genesis.
- The [[400-Year Prophecy]] of Egyptian bondage ([[Genesis 15_13–14.md]]) was fulfilled to the year ([[Exodus 12_40–41.md]]). This period is discussed in *Chronological Aspects of the Life of Christ* by Harold W. Hoehner.
