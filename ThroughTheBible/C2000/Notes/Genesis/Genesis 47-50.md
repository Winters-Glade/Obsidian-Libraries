## Overview
Pastor Chuck Smith covers the final chapters of Genesis: [[Jacob]] and his family settling in [[Egypt]], Jacob's blessings on [[Ephraim]] and [[Manasseh]], his [[Prophecy|prophecies]] concerning each of his twelve sons, his death and burial, and Joseph's final years and death. The book ends with hope and [[Faith|faith]] in God's [[Covenant|promise]].

## Summary
Pastor Chuck Smith concludes the book of Genesis with sober and significant moments. [[Jacob]] and his family settle in the land of [[Goshen]] in [[Egypt]]. Jacob blesses [[Pharaoh]], and Joseph manages the severe [[Famine|famine]] wisely. As Jacob's death approaches, he extracts a promise from Joseph to bury him in [[Canaan]] with his fathers. Jacob then blesses Joseph's two sons, [[Ephraim]] and [[Manasseh]], crossing his hands to place the greater blessing on the younger Ephraim—again demonstrating God's sovereign choice. Jacob then gathers his twelve sons and pronounces prophetic [[blessing|blessings]] over each. The highlight is the [[Blessing of Jacob.md]]: "The scepter shall not depart from Judah, nor a lawgiver from between his feet, until [[Shiloh]] come"—a clear [[Messianic Prophecy|prophecy of the Messiah]]. After Jacob dies, Joseph weeps and reassures his fearful brothers, reiterating "you intended evil against me, but God meant it for good." Joseph lives to see his great-grandchildren and makes his brothers swear to carry his bones to the Promised Land. The book ends with Joseph's bones in a coffin in Egypt, awaiting fulfillment.

## Historical & Cultural Context
- The settlement of Jacob's family in [[Goshen]] (the eastern Nile Delta region, also called the "land of Rameses" in [[Genesis 47_11.md]]) was excellent pastoral land, suitable for shepherds. The Egyptians considered shepherds "abominable" ([[Genesis 46_34.md]]), which kept Israel separate and preserved their distinct identity during their 400-year sojourn.
- Egyptian [[embalming|embalming]] practices mentioned in [[Genesis 50_2–3, 26.md]] involved removing the internal organs, desiccating the body with [[natron|natron]] (a natural salt), wrapping it in linen bandages, and covering it with resin. The 70-day embalming period mentioned for Jacob matches the 70-day cycle documented in Egyptian sources (40 days for desiccation, 30 days for wrapping and rituals).
- Jacob's blessing on [[Ephraim]] and [[Manasseh]] ([[Genesis 48_13–20.md]]) with crossed hands—placing the right hand on the younger—reflects the ANE significance of the right hand as the hand of greater blessing. This continues the biblical pattern of the younger being chosen over the elder ([[Abel]] over [[Cain]], [[Isaac]] over [[Ishmael]], [[Jacob]] over [[Esau]], [[David]] over his brothers).

## Archaeological Evidence
- The [[land of Goshen|land of Goshen]] has been identified with the [[Wadi Tumilat]] region in the eastern Nile Delta. Archaeological surveys by the [[Egypt Exploration Society]] have identified settlement patterns consistent with a Semitic population in that area during the Second Intermediate Period (c. 1800–1550 BC).
- The mention of [[Pharaoh]] in the Joseph narrative without a specific name is consistent with Egyptian literary conventions, but the details—Egyptian names ([[Zaphnath-Paaneah]], [[Asenath]], [[Poti-Pherah]]), court protocol, administrative titles—are authentically Egyptian. The title "Pharaoh" itself was used as a respectful address during the New Kingdom.
- [[Josephs tomb]] is traditionally located at [[Shechem]] (modern [[Nablus]]), where [[_Bible/Joshua/Joshua 24_32.md]] records that the Israelites buried his bones. The site is marked by a traditional tomb (Qabr Yusuf), though archaeological verification of the specific location is not possible.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[Augustine]] in *The City of God* (Book 16) devoted extensive commentary to Jacob's prophetic blessings ([[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 49.md]]), particularly the blessing of Judah. He interpreted "Shiloh" ([[Genesis 49_10.md]]) as a clear prophecy of Christ, the one to whom the nations would be gathered—a prediction fulfilled in the worldwide spread of the gospel.
- [[John Chrysostom]] in his *Homilies on Genesis* (Homily 67–68) marveled at Joseph's faith in commanding his bones to be carried to the Promised Land ([[Genesis 50_25.md]]), seeing it as a demonstration that Joseph considered God's promise more real than the present reality of Egypt. He noted that the book of Genesis ends with "a coffin in Egypt" to show that the patriarchs died in faith, not having received the promises but seeing them from afar.

## New Testament Connections
- [[Hebrews 11_21–22.md]] cites both Jacob blessing the sons of Joseph and Joseph's command concerning his bones as examples of faith: "By faith Jacob, when he was dying, blessed each of the sons of Joseph... By faith Joseph, when he was dying, made mention of the departure of the children of Israel, and gave instructions concerning his bones."
- [[Revelation 5_5.md]] identifies Jesus as the "Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David," directly fulfilling Jacob's prophecy in [[Genesis 49_9–10.md]] that Judah would be a lion's whelp and that the scepter would not depart from him until Shiloh comes.
- [[Matthew 19_28.md]] speaks of the twelve apostles sitting on twelve thrones judging the twelve tribes of Israel, connecting the twelve sons of Jacob to the eschatological governance of God's people.
- [[John 4_5]] mentions the parcel of land Jacob gave to Joseph near Sychar—the same land where Jesus would later speak to the Samaritan woman—connecting the patriarchal gift to the ministry of Christ.
- [[Acts 7_15–16.md]] recounts the burial of Jacob and the twelve patriarchs in the tomb that Abraham had purchased at Shechem, confirming the tradition of the patriarchal burial site.

## For Further Study
- The [[Blessing of Jacob]] ([[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 49.md]]) is one of the most important prophetic passages in the OT. For a detailed verse-by-verse analysis, see *Genesis: An Introduction and Commentary* by [[Derek Kidner]] and *The Message of the Twelve Sons* by [[Walter C. Kaiser Jr.md]].
- The messianic prophecy of [[Shiloh]] ([[Genesis 49_10.md]]) has been interpreted as "he whose right it is" ([[Ezekiel 21_27.md]]), "the one to whom tribute belongs," or as a proper name for the Messiah. For the history of interpretation, see *Messianic Prophecy* by [[E. W. Hengstenberg]].
- Joseph's bones being carried out of Egypt ([[Exodus 13_19.md]]) and buried at Shechem ([[_Bible/Joshua/Joshua 24_32.md]]) bookend the exodus narrative, showing that faith in God's promises outlasts generations of waiting.
