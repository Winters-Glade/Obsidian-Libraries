## Overview
Pastor Chuck Smith covers Joseph's story in [[Egypt]]—his service in [[Potiphar|Potiphar's]] house, the temptation by [[Potiphars Wife|Potiphar's wife]], imprisonment, [[Dreams|interpretation of dreams]] for the butler and baker, and his elevation to prime minister after interpreting [[Pharaoh|Pharaoh's]] dreams. The theme of "the Lord was with Joseph" runs throughout.

## Summary
Pastor Chuck Smith traces God's [[Providence|providence]] in Joseph's life. Sold as a slave in [[Egypt]], Joseph is purchased by [[Potiphar]], and the Lord is with him, causing everything he does to prosper. Potiphar puts him in charge of his entire household. However, [[Potiphars Wife|Potiphar's wife]] repeatedly tries to seduce Joseph, who refuses, declaring it would be sin against God. Eventually, she falsely accuses him, and Joseph is imprisoned. Yet even in prison, "the Lord was with Joseph," and he gains favor with the prison keeper, eventually running the prison. While there, he interprets the [[Dreams|dreams]] of Pharaoh's butler and baker—the butler is restored but forgets Joseph. Two years later, [[Pharaoh]] has troubling dreams of seven fat cows devoured by seven lean cows, and seven full ears of grain devoured by seven thin ears. The butler remembers Joseph, who is brought before Pharaoh. Joseph interprets the dreams as seven years of plenty followed by seven years of [[Famine|famine]], and counsels Pharaoh to appoint a wise man to manage storage. Pharaoh appoints Joseph as prime minister over all Egypt. Joseph marries [[Asenath]] and has two sons: [[Manasseh]] (forgetfulness) and [[Ephraim]] (fruitfulness).

## Key Takeaways
- The repeated phrase "the Lord was with Joseph" is the key to understanding his success and resilience
- Joseph's integrity in the face of temptation is a model of faithfulness
- God's timing is not always quick—Joseph spent 13 years as a slave/prisoner before elevation
- Joseph's faith was consistent whether in Potiphar's house, in prison, or in the palace
- His humility ("It is not in me") shows his dependence on God
- The names Manasseh and Ephraim reflect Joseph's journey from forgetfulness of pain to fruitfulness in affliction
- Joseph is a clear type of Christ: beloved son, betrayed, sold, falsely accused, then exalted to rule

## Historical & Cultural Context
- [[Potiphar]]'s title "captain of the guard" (Hebrew: *sar hatabbachim*, literally "chief of the executioners") indicates he was head of Pharaoh's security forces, likely the commander of the royal bodyguard and prison system. His position in Pharaoh's court and his trust in Joseph reflect the Egyptian administrative structure where foreigners could rise to significant positions.
- Dreams were taken very seriously in ancient Egypt. The Egyptians produced elaborate [[dream interpretation manuals|dream interpretation manuals]], the most famous being the [[Chester Beatty Papyrus]] (c. 1250 BC), which classifies dreams as good or bad and interprets their symbols. Joseph's declaration "Do not interpretations belong to God?" ([[Genesis 40_8.md]]) marked a sharp contrast with Egyptian divination practices.
- The seven-year famine cycle described in [[Genesis 41.md]] fits Egyptian agricultural realities dependent on the annual [[Nile]] flood. A series of low floods could produce precisely the pattern of seven lean years. Egyptian records from the [[Famine Stela]] (on the island of [[Sehel]]) describe a seven-year famine during the reign of [[Djoser]] (c. 2700 BC), suggesting this pattern was known in Egyptian tradition.

## Archaeological Evidence
- The [[Famine Stela]] (Ptolemaic period, but claiming to record events from the Old Kingdom) describes a seven-year famine in Egypt and the appointment of a wise man ([[Imhotep]]) to advise Pharaoh. While legendary, it demonstrates that the concept of a seven-year famine was deeply embedded in Egyptian cultural memory.
- [[Egyptian administrative records]] from the New Kingdom document the role of a "overseer of the granaries" and the construction of large-scale storage facilities—exactly the kind of position Joseph would have held. The title *Ab-hener* ("overseer of the granaries") is attested in Egyptian inscriptions.
- The practice of giving a foreign name and wife to an elevated official ([[Genesis 41_45.md]]) is attested in Egyptian texts. Joseph's Egyptian name [[Zaphnath-Paaneah]] (meaning uncertain, possibly "the god speaks and he lives") and his marriage to [[Asenath]], daughter of [[Poti-Pherah]] priest of [[On]] (Heliopolis), reflect authentic Egyptian customs of naturalizing foreign administrators.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[John Chrysostom]] in his *Homilies on Genesis* (Homily 62–64) emphasized Joseph's faithfulness in every circumstance—whether in Potiphar's house, in prison, or in the palace. He noted that Joseph's refusal of Potiphar's wife was motivated by his fear of God, not merely fear of human consequences.
- [[Ambrose of Milan]] in *On Joseph* treated Joseph as the supreme Old Testament type of Christ, tracing parallels between Joseph's betrayal, his suffering, his exaltation, and his provision of grain (bread) for the world—connecting to Christ as the Bread of Life.

## New Testament Connections
- The typology between [[Joseph]] and [[Jesus]] is extensive: both were beloved sons of their fathers ([[Genesis 37_3.md]]; [[Matthew 3_17.md]]); both were sent to their brothers and rejected ([[Genesis 37_23–28.md]]; [[John 1_11]]); both were sold for silver ([[Genesis 37_28.md]]; [[Matthew 26_15.md]]); both were falsely accused ([[Genesis 39_17–18.md]]; [[Matthew 26_59–60.md]]); both suffered with criminals ([[Genesis 40_1–3.md]]; [[Luke 23_32]]); both were exalted after suffering ([[Genesis 41_41–44.md]]; [[Philippians 2_9–11.md]]); and both provided salvation for those who had wronged them.
- [[Acts 7_9–10]] explicitly summarizes Joseph's story in Stephen's speech, emphasizing that "God was with him."
- [[1 Corinthians 10_11.md]] states that these events were written as "examples" (*typoi*) for us, providing the hermeneutical basis for seeing Christ in the Joseph narrative.
