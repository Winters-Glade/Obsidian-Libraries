## Overview
Pastor Chuck Smith covers the events leading up to the flood, including the controversial [[sons of God|"sons of God"]] passage, the [[Sin|wickedness]] of mankind, [[Noah|Noah's]] [[Righteousness|righteousness]], the building of the [[Ark]], and the great flood itself. He discusses evidence for a worldwide flood and defends its historicity against uniformitarian geology.

- The Nephilim (giants) in the earth

## Summary
Pastor Chuck Smith tackles the difficult passage about the "[[Sons of God]]" taking daughters of men as wives, explaining that this likely refers to fallen angels who intermarried with humans, producing [[the Nephilim|the Nephilim]] (giants). He connects this to the New Testament reference of angels who kept not their first estate. As wickedness spread, God determined to judge the earth but found [[Grace|grace]] in Noah—the first use of the word "grace" in Scripture. Smith discusses Noah's [[Righteousness|righteousness]] and his 120-year project of building the [[Ark]], warning of coming judgment. He defends a worldwide flood against uniformitarian geology, citing evidence from Velikovsky's "Earth in Upheaval" of mass graves, mixed fossil deposits, and polystrate fossils. The flood narrative itself describes the breaking up of the fountains of the great deep and the opening of the windows of heaven, covering the highest mountains. Smith notes continuing reports of the ark's remains on [[Mount Ararat]].

## Historical & Cultural Context
- The flood narrative in Genesis shares structural parallels with earlier ANE flood accounts, most notably the [[Epic of Gilgamesh]] (Tablet XI, c. 1300 BC) and the [[Atrahasis Epic]] (c. 1700 BC). Key parallels include a divine decision to destroy humanity, a favored man building a boat, the preservation of animal life, a bird sent out to test for dry land, and a sacrifice after the flood. However, Genesis differs radically in theology: a single sovereign God acts for moral reasons (human wickedness), not capricious divine whim.
- The "sons of God" (bene ha-Elohim) in [[Genesis 6_1–4.md]] is a debated phrase. In the Hebrew Bible, it consistently refers to angelic beings ([[Job 1_6]], [[Job 38_7]], [[Psalm 82_6]]). This interpretation—that fallen angels intermarried with human women—was the dominant view in pre-Christian Judaism ([[Book of Enoch]] 6–7) and the early church ([[Justin Martyr]], [[Irenaeus]], [[Tertullian]]).

## Archaeological Evidence
- Over 270 flood traditions exist worldwide across diverse cultures, from the Babylonian to the Chinese to Native American traditions. This near-universal flood memory is frequently cited by flood proponents as evidence of a historical deluge, though skeptics attribute it to common human experiences of local flooding.
- The [[Dur-Sharrukin]] (Khorsabad) and [[Nineveh]] excavations have uncovered cuneiform tablets containing the Gilgamesh flood story, discovered by [[Austen Henry Layard]] and [[George Smith]] in the 19th century. Smith's 1872 announcement of the Babylonian flood account caused a sensation and sparked modern comparative ANE studies.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[Augustine]] in *The City of God* (Book 15) addressed the "sons of God" passage, ultimately favoring the interpretation that the "sons of God" were the righteous line of Seth mixing with the "daughters of men" from Cain's line—a view that gained prominence after him. However, he acknowledged the angelic interpretation was held by many.
- [[John Chrysostom]] in his *Homilies on Genesis* emphasized Noah's righteousness as the basis for his salvation, noting that Noah preached righteousness for 120 years without a single convert—a testament to his perseverance in faith despite universal opposition.

## New Testament Connections
- [[2 Peter 3_3–7.md]] uses the flood as a type of the coming judgment by fire, arguing that scoffers willfully forget that "the world that then existed perished, being flooded with water." The flood thus becomes a paradigm for eschatological judgment.
- [[Matthew 24_37–39.md]] records Jesus's own words: "As the days of Noah were, so also will the coming of the Son of Man be," connecting the suddenness and universality of the flood to the unexpectedness of Christ's return.
- [[1 Peter 3_20–21.md]] draws a typological connection between Noah's salvation through water and Christian baptism, which "now saves us" as an appeal to God for a good conscience through the resurrection of Jesus Christ.
- [[Jude 6]] and [[2 Peter 2_4]] refer to angels who "did not keep their proper domain" and are "reserved in everlasting chains under darkness for the judgment of the great day"—commonly linked to the Genesis 6 "sons of God" passage.

## For Further Study
- The [[Nephilim]] ([[Genesis 6_4]]) are mentioned again in [[Numbers 13_33.md]] as the inhabitants of Canaan that intimidated the Israelite spies. The relationship between the antediluvian Nephilim and the post-flood Nephilim is a subject of ongoing discussion.
- The [[Water Canopy]] theory, advocated by Chuck Smith and popularized by [[Joseph C. Dillow]] (*The Waters Above*), posits a thermal vapor blanket surrounding the pre-flood earth. While widely held in conservative evangelical circles, it is not supported by mainstream atmospheric science and is rejected by many young-earth creationists.
