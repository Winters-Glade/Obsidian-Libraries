## Overview
Pastor Chuck Smith covers Abram's return from [[Egypt]], the separation from [[Lot]], God's renewed [[Covenant|promise]], and the battle of the kings involving Abram's rescue of Lot. The highlight is the appearance of [[Melchizedek]], king of Salem, who receives [[Tithe|tithes]] from Abram.

## Summary
Pastor Chuck Smith describes Abram's return to [[Bethel]] after his Egyptian detour. Wealthy in cattle, silver, and gold, conflict arises between Abram's and Lot's herdsmen. In a generous display of [[Faith|faith]], Abram allows [[Lot]] to choose the land first. Lot chooses the lush Jordan plain, pitching his tent toward [[Sodom]]—a spiritually disastrous decision. God then reaffirms His promise to Abram to give him all the land he can see. Chapter 14 shifts to a regional war: four kings from the east defeat five kings of the plain, taking Lot captive. Abram arms 318 trained servants and pursues, defeating the kings and rescuing Lot. Upon return, Abram encounters [[Melchizedek]], king of [[Salem]] (Jerusalem) and priest of the Most High God, to whom Abram gives a [[Tithe|tenth]] of all. Smith interprets Melchizedek as a [[Typology|type of Christ]]—a king-priest. Abram then refuses any reward from the king of Sodom, demonstrating his trust in God alone for blessing.

## Historical & Cultural Context
- The battle of the kings in [[Genesis 14_1–16.md]] reflects the geopolitical landscape of the Middle Bronze Age (c. 2000 BC), when regional coalitions of kings waged war for control of trade routes and resources. The four eastern kings (Chedorlaomer of Elam, Amraphel of Shinar, Arioch of Ellasar, Tidal of Goiim) parallel known powers of the era: Elam was a major kingdom east of Babylon; Shinar is the biblical name for Sumer/Babylon; Ellasar is likely Larsa.
- Abram's pursuit of the kings as far as [[Dan]] (northern Canaan) and the mention of his 318 trained servants ([[Genesis 14_14.md]]) reflects the patriarchal household military structure found in ANE texts like the [[Mari Tablets]], where a clan chieftain could muster a small, loyal militia.

## Archaeological Evidence
- The [[Mari Tablets]] (c. 1800 BC), discovered at Tell Hariri on the Euphrates, document a complex system of tribal confederations, migrations, and city-state politics in the Middle Bronze Age that closely parallels the world of the patriarchs. They mention names like Abram, Jacob, and Laban in similar contexts.
- The name [[Melchizedek]] means "king of righteousness" (Hebrew: *malki-tsedeq*). A similar name, *Salkim-tsedeq* ("Salkim is righteous"), appears in [[Ebla Tablets]], suggesting that "righteous king" was a known theophoric name in the region.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[Augustine]] in *The City of God* (Book 16) and [[Jerome]] in his *Hebrew Questions on Genesis* both saw [[Melchizedek]] as a historical figure who prefigures Christ. The mysterious nature of his priesthood—appearing without genealogy (as described in [[Hebrews 7_3]])—made him a rich type of Christ's eternal priesthood.
- [[Clement of Alexandria]] (c. AD 150–215) in *Stromata* saw Melchizedek as a righteous Gentile who worshipped the true God, demonstrating that knowledge of the Most High God existed outside the Abrahamic line.

## New Testament Connections
- [[Hebrews 7_1–28.md]] provides the fullest NT exposition of Melchizedek, arguing that his priesthood is superior to the Levitical priesthood because [[Abraham]]—the ancestor of Levi—paid tithes to Melchizedek. Christ is declared "a priest forever according to the order of Melchizedek" (quoting [[Psalm 110_4]]), indicating a priesthood not based on genealogy but on the power of an indestructible life.
- [[Hebrews 7_2]] notes that Melchizedek's name means "king of righteousness" and his title "king of Salem" (peace), making him a type of Christ who is both King of Righteousness and King of Peace—a point Chuck Smith highlights in his teaching.
- [[Psalm 110_4]], the most quoted OT verse in the NT, declares the Melchizedekian priesthood of the Messiah, establishing the theological foundation for Christ's unique high priestly role.

## For Further Study
- The figure of [[Melchizedek]] has been interpreted variously in Jewish tradition: as [[Shem]] (Noah's son), as Job, or as a unique priest-king. The [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] (11QMelch) depict Melchizedek as a heavenly, angelic figure who will execute eschatological judgment.
- The location of [[Salem]] is almost certainly [[Jerusalem]] ([[Psalm 76_2]] explicitly identifies Salem with Zion). See "Salem" in the *Anchor Bible Dictionary*.
