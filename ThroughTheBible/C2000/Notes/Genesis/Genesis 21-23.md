## Overview
Pastor Chuck Smith covers the birth of [[Isaac]], the expulsion of [[Hagar]] and [[Ishmael]], Abraham's covenant with [[Abimelech]] at [[Beersheba]], and the ultimate test of Abraham's [[Faith|faith]]—the [[Akedah|offering of Isaac]] on [[Mount Moriah]]. The section ends with the death and burial of [[Sarah]].

## Summary
Pastor Chuck Smith celebrates the birth of [[Isaac]]—God keeps His word, even after a 13-year wait. The tension between [[Sarah]] and [[Hagar]] leads to the expulsion of [[Ishmael]] and Hagar. God provides for them in the wilderness, promising to make Ishmael a great nation. Abraham makes a covenant with [[Abimelech]] at [[Beersheba]], naming the well as a witness. Then comes the ultimate test: God commands Abraham to offer Isaac as a burnt offering on [[Mount Moriah]]. Without hesitation, Abraham obeys, believing God could raise Isaac from the dead. At the crucial moment, God provides a ram caught in a thicket—a powerful picture of [[substitutionary atonement|substitutionary sacrifice]]. God reaffirms His covenant, swearing by Himself to bless all nations through Abraham's seed. The section concludes with Sarah's death at 127 years old and Abraham's purchase of the [[Cave of Machpelah]] as a burial site—the first piece of the Promised Land owned by Abraham.

## Historical & Cultural Context
- The binding of Isaac (the [[Akedah]], [[Genesis 22_1–19.md]]) is set on [[Mount Moriah]], which [[2 Chronicles 3_1.md]] identifies as the site of [[Solomons Temple]] in Jerusalem. Jewish and Christian traditions also connect it to [[Golgotha]], the site of Jesus's crucifixion—the same "land of Moriah" where Abraham offered Isaac.
- The purchase of the [[Cave of Machpelah]] from [[Ephron the Hittite]] ([[Genesis 23_3–20.md]]) follows Hittite legal customs preserved in Hittite law codes: the seller first offers both the cave and the field as a package (a "gift" that must be purchased), then names an exorbitant price, and the buyer pays the full amount in public at the city gate. This negotiation reflects authentic Hittite land-transfer procedures.

## Archaeological Evidence
- The [[Cave of Machpelah]] in [[Hebron]] is enclosed by a massive Herodian-era structure (the Tomb of the Patriarchs, built by [[Herod the Great]]), the second holiest site in Judaism. The Herodian masonry matches that of the [[Temple Mount]] in Jerusalem. The site has been venerated continuously for over two millennia.
- [[Beersheba]] ([[Genesis 21_31.md]]), meaning "well of the oath," has been extensively excavated by [[Yohanan Aharoni]] and others. The site contains a well-preserved water system and a horned altar that may indicate worship practices from the patriarchal period. The well at Beersheba remains a landmark to this day.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[Augustine]] in *The City of God* (Book 16) and *Against Faustus* treated the [[Akedah]] as a profound type of the atonement: Isaac carrying the wood prefigures Christ carrying the cross; the ram substituted for Isaac prefigures Christ's substitutionary death.
- [[Origen of Alexandria]] (c. AD 184–253) in his *Homilies on Genesis* (Homily 8) emphasized Abraham's faith as the key to the Akedah, noting that Abraham's statement "God will provide Himself a lamb" was prophetic—Abraham saw the mystery of Christ's sacrifice by faith. He also noted that Isaac's willing submission (no struggle mentioned) prefigured Christ's willing sacrifice.
- [[John Chrysostom]] in his *Homilies on Genesis* (Homily 47–48) focused on Abraham's emotional struggle, noting that the command came when Isaac was around 25–37 years old, not a small child—making Abraham's obedience even more remarkable.

## New Testament Connections
- [[Hebrews 11_17–19.md]] declares that Abraham "offered up Isaac" by faith, "accounting that God was able to raise him up, even from the dead, from which he also received him in a figurative sense"—the first clear statement of resurrection faith in Scripture.
- [[James 2_21–23.md]] cites the Akedah as the supreme demonstration that Abraham's faith was "perfected" by works: "Was not Abraham our father justified by works when he offered Isaac his son on the altar?"
- [[John 8_56]] records Jesus saying, "Your father Abraham rejoiced to see My day, and he saw it and was glad"—likely referring to Abraham's prophetic glimpse of the Messiah in the Akedah.
- [[Romans 8_32]] echoes the Akedah: "He who did not spare His own Son, but delivered Him up for us all," using language reminiscent of [[Genesis 22_12.md]] where God says, "You have not withheld your son, your only son."

## For Further Study
- The [[Akedah]] (binding of Isaac) is a central text in Jewish tradition, read on [[Rosh Hashanah]] (the Jewish New Year). It is extensively developed in rabbinic literature and in the writings of [[Abraham Joshua Heschel]].
- [[Mount Moriah]] and the [[Temple Mount]] are explored in detail in *Jerusalem: The Temple Mount* by [[Leen Ritmeyer]] and *The Temple of Jerusalem* by [[Simon Goldhill]].
