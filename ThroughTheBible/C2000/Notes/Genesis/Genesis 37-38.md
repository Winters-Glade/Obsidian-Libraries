## Overview
Pastor Chuck Smith covers the beginning of the [[Joseph]] narrative (chapter 37)—his [[Dreams|dreams]], his brothers' hatred, and his being sold into slavery—and the interlude of [[Judah]] and [[Tamar]] (chapter 38). These chapters show God's sovereignty working through human evil and failure.

- The coat of many colors (or long-sleeved tunic)
- Joseph's dreams of sheaves and stars bowing
- His brothers' jealousy and hatred
- The plot to kill Joseph
- Reuben's attempt to rescue him

- The interlude: Judah and Tamar
- Tamar's deception and Judah's confession
- The birth of Pharez and Zarah

## Summary
Pastor Chuck Smith introduces the Joseph story, noting [[Jacob|Jacob's]] favoritism toward [[Joseph]] (son of his beloved [[Rachel]]) which alienates his brothers. The [[_Concepts/Doctrines/House of Israel|coat of many colors]] (actually a long-sleeved tunic, indicating status) and Joseph's [[Dreams|dreams]] of dominance fuel their hatred. When Joseph is sent to check on his brothers at Shechem, they plot to kill him. [[Reuben]] dissuades them from murder, suggesting throwing him into a pit. [[Judah]] later suggests selling Joseph to a passing [[Ishmaelites|Ishmaelite]] caravan for 20 pieces of silver. They dip his coat in goat's blood and present it to Jacob, who mourns inconsolably. Chapter 38 is a surprising interlude focusing on Judah. He separates from his brothers and marries a Canaanite woman. His eldest son Er dies, then Onan refuses to provide offspring for [[Tamar]] and also dies. Tamar, promised to Shelah but never given, disguises herself as a prostitute and conceives twins by Judah himself. When confronted, Judah confesses, "She is more righteous than I." The twins, [[Pharez]] and [[Zarah]], continue the line through which the Messiah would come.

## Key Takeaways
- God's sovereignty works even through human jealousy and betrayal—Joseph's dreams would be fulfilled
- Jacob's favoritism repeated the family pattern of parental preference
- Joseph's coat and dreams set the stage for God's larger plan
- Judah's moral failure with Tamar is recorded, yet through this line the Messiah came

## Historical & Cultural Context
- The "coat of many colors" ([[Genesis 37_3.md]], KJV) is more accurately translated as a "long-sleeved tunic" or "ornamented robe" (Hebrew: *ketonet passim*). Such a garment was a mark of status and favor in the ANE, typically worn by royalty or nobility (cf. [[2 Samuel 13_18.md]], where Tamar, a princess, wore the same type of garment). This explains why the brothers' jealousy was so intense—Jacob was effectively designating Joseph as the heir.
- Joseph being sold for 20 pieces of silver ([[Genesis 37_28.md]]) reflects the slave trade prices of the early second millennium BC. The [[Code of Hammurabi]] (c. 1750 BC) sets the price for a slave at 20 shekels of silver, precisely matching the Genesis account and confirming the historical accuracy of the price.
- The [[Levirate marriage]] custom in [[Genesis 38_8–10.md]]—where a brother was required to produce offspring for his deceased brother's widow—is codified in [[_Bible/Deuteronomy/Deuteronomy 25_5-10.md]] and parallels ANE practices from [[Hittite law]] and [[Assyrian law]].

## Archaeological Evidence
- [[Egyptian tomb paintings]] from the Middle Kingdom (c. 2000–1700 BC) at [[Beni Hasan]] depict Semitic traders with multicolored garments entering Egypt—exactly the kind of caravan (Ishmaelites/Midianites) that bought Joseph. These paintings provide visual confirmation of the biblical description of trade caravans traveling between Canaan and Egypt.
- The price of slaves in Mesopotamia is documented in numerous cuneiform texts, with the standard price for a male slave being 20 shekels during the Old Babylonian period (c. 2000–1600 BC)—confirming the accuracy of the price mentioned in Genesis.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[John Chrysostom]] in his *Homilies on Genesis* (Homily 61–62) focused on Joseph's character even as a 17-year-old: his obedience to his father (going to check on his brothers despite knowing their hatred), and his brothers' murderous jealousy. He noted that God permitted Joseph's suffering because He had a greater purpose.
- [[Augustine]] in *The City of God* (Book 16) saw the [[Tamar]] and Judah episode as a demonstration of God's grace—working through deeply flawed human beings to bring about His redemptive plan. The fact that the messianic line included Tamar (a Canaanite woman involved in a scandal) shows that God's grace transcends human categories of righteousness.

## New Testament Connections
- [[Matthew 1_3]] lists [[Phares]] (Pharez) and [[Zara]] (Zarah) in the genealogy of Jesus Christ—the twins born to Judah and Tamar. Tamar is one of only four women named in Matthew's genealogy (alongside Rahab, Ruth, and Bathsheba), highlighting the prominent role of unexpected women in the messianic line.
- [[Acts 7_9–10]] summarizes Joseph's story: "The patriarchs, becoming envious, sold Joseph into Egypt. But God was with him and delivered him out of all his troubles, and gave him favor and wisdom in the presence of Pharaoh, king of Egypt."
- [[Matthew 27_9–10.md]]—Jeremiah's prophecy about the 30 pieces of silver (a development of the 20 pieces of silver Joseph was sold for) connects the betrayal narratives of Joseph and Jesus.

## For Further Study
- The typology between [[Joseph as a Type of Christ]] is one of the richest in Scripture: beloved son, sent by the father, rejected by his brothers, sold for silver, falsely accused, suffered unjustly, then exalted to rule over all, providing salvation for those who rejected him. See *Joseph and the Gospel of God* by [[John M. Roper]].
- The story of [[Judah and Tamar]] is set within the broader context of the messianic line. The genealogy leading to [[David]] and ultimately to [[Jesus]] passes through Pharez ([[Ruth]]), making this sordid story part of God's redemptive tapestry.
