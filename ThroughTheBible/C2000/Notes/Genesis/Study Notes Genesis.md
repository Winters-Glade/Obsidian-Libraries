# Study Notes — Genesis

## Key Themes

## Historical Context
These books (traditionally attributed to [[Moses]]) cover the foundational history of Israel: creation, the patriarchs, the exodus from Egypt, the giving of the Law at Sinai, and the wilderness journey to the border of the Promised Land. They were written during the wilderness period (c. 1440-1400 BC).

## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Genesis, chapters 8-9.

**Genesis:** The book of beginnings. Genesis traces God's creation of the world, the fall of man, the flood, and the call of Abraham. It follows the patriarchs — Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph — and establishes the foundational covenants that shape the rest of Scripture. Key events include the creation week, the Garden of Eden, Noah's ark, the Tower of Babel, Sodom and Gomorrah, and Joseph's rise to power in Egypt.

## Historical & Cultural Context
- Genesis was written in the context of the ancient Near East (c. 2000-1400 BC), a world dominated by polytheistic cultures (Egyptian, Mesopotamian, Canaanite). The Genesis narratives consistently subvert ANE mythology: creation by divine fiat rather than cosmic battle (contrast *Enuma Elish*), the flood as moral judgment rather than capricious divine whim (contrast *Gilgamesh*), and humanity as God's image-bearers rather than slaves created to serve the gods (contrast the *Atrahasis Epic*).
- The patriarchal narratives display authentic cultural details of the Middle Bronze Age (c. 2000-1550 BC): nomadic pastoralism, tribal law, covenant ceremonies, marriage customs (bride-price, levirate marriage), and legal procedures (oral blessings with binding force, household gods for inheritance rights). These details match ANE texts from [[Mari]], [[Nuzi]], and [[Sela.md]].

## Archaeological Evidence
- The [[Merneptah Stele]] (c. 1208 BC) provides the earliest extra-biblical reference to "Israel" as a people in Canaan, confirming that the nation descending from the patriarchs existed by the late 13th century BC.
- The [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] (c. 250 BC-AD 68) contain the oldest extant manuscripts of Genesis, demonstrating the remarkable textual preservation of the book over two millennia.
- The [[Nuzi Tablets]] (c. 1500-1400 BC), [[Mari Tablets]] (c. 1800 BC), and [[Ebla Tablets]] (c. 2500-2250 BC) provide extensive legal, social, and cultural parallels that confirm the authenticity of the patriarchal narratives.
- The [[Ketef Hinnom]] silver amulets (c. 600 BC) containing the [[Priestly Blessing]] ([[Numbers 6_24-26.md]]) demonstrate the antiquity of the religious and textual traditions reflected in the Pentateuch.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- The church fathers universally accepted Mosaic authorship of Genesis and treated the patriarchs as historical figures. [[Augustine]]'s *The City of God* (Books 15-16) and [[John Chrysostom]]'s *Homilies on Genesis* provide the most extensive patristic treatments of Genesis. [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] developed the doctrine of "recapitulation" (Christ as the second Adam) from Genesis 1-3.
- During the Reformation, [[Martin Luther]] and [[John Calvin]] both wrote extensive commentaries on Genesis, emphasizing justification by faith (from [[Genesis 15_6.md]]), covenant theology (from the Abrahamic covenant), and the historical-grammatical method of interpretation. Luther's *Lectures on Genesis* (1535-1545) remains one of the most detailed Lutheran expositions of the book.

## New Testament Connections
- Genesis is the most quoted OT book in the New Testament (after Psalms and Isaiah). Key passages include: creation ([[John 1_1-5]]; [[Colossians 1_15-17.md]]), the Fall ([[Romans 5_12-21.md]]; [[1 Corinthians 15_21-22, 45-49.md]]), Abraham's faith ([[Romans 4_1-25.md]]; [[Galatians 3_6-14.md]]; [[James 2_21-23.md]]), Melchizedek ([[Hebrews 5_6, 10.md]]; [[Hebrews 7_1-28.md]]), Sodom ([[Luke 17_28-33.md]]; [[2 Peter 2_6-8.md]]), and Joseph ([[Acts 7_9-15]]).
- Jesus directly referenced Genesis: the creation of male and female ([[Matthew 19_4-6.md]]), Noah and the flood ([[Matthew 24_37-39.md]]), Lot's wife ([[Luke 17_32]]), Abraham and Isaac ([[John 8_56]]), and Jacob's ladder ([[John 1_51]]).
