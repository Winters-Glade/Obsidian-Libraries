## Summary

Chapter 16 describes the [[Day of Atonement]] in detail. The high priest wore plain linen garments (not his glorious robes), first offered for his own sins, then entered the [[Holy of Holies]] with blood. The two goats — one sacrificed to the Lord, one sent into the wilderness as the [[Scapegoat]] — picture both [[Atonement]] and removal of [[Sin]]. No equivalent exists in Christ for the priest's own sin offering, since [[Jesus Christ|Jesus]] was sinless. Chapter 17 establishes the foundational principle that blood makes atonement for the soul. Chapters 18-20 cover [[Sexual Morality]], interpersonal ethics, and penalties for sin, with the repeated refrain "I am the Lord your God." Chuck Smith applies these passages directly to contemporary Hollywood, the judicial system, and moral decay.

## Key Takeaways

1. The [[Day of Atonement]] required the high priest to bathe five times and slaughter some 30 animals — a stark contrast to Christ's single, sufficient sacrifice.
2. [[Jesus Christ|Jesus]] had no need to offer for His own sins — He was sinless, unlike [[Aaron]].
3. The blood principle is foundational: without the shedding of blood, there is no remission.
4. The [[Scapegoat]] pictures God removing our sins completely ([[Psalm 103_12.md]]).
5. God's moral laws are not optional — they reflect His [[Holiness|holy]] character.
6. Chuck Smith applies the passages on [[Sexual Morality]] directly to contemporary entertainment and cultural decay.
7. [[Capital Punishment]] is presented as God's standard for grave sins, with the principle that the punishment must fit the crime.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur) was the only day the high priest could enter the Holy of Holies, and only with blood. The Mishnah (tractate [[Yoma]]) describes the elaborate preparations: the high priest was sequestered seven days prior, and a substitute priest stood ready to replace him if he became disqualified. The ritual's uniqueness underscores the gravity of approaching a holy God on behalf of the nation.
- The identity of [[Azazel]] (the scapegoat) is disputed. The Hebrew *la-azazel* may mean "for complete removal" or refer to a desert demon. The [[Book of Enoch]] (1 Enoch 6-10) identifies Azazel as a fallen angel who taught forbidden knowledge, creating a rich interpretive background in Second Temple Judaism. The Mishnah records that the goat was led to a cliff and pushed backward, ensuring it never returned.
- The sexual prohibitions of Leviticus 18 are framed as a rejection of the practices of Egypt and Canaan. Egyptian culture (especially among royalty) permitted sibling marriage, while Canaanite religion involved temple prostitution and, in the cult of [[Molech]], child sacrifice — practices Israel was commanded to abhor.
- The blood prohibition in Leviticus 17:11 ("the life of the flesh is in the blood") reflects an ancient Semitic understanding of blood as the life-principle belonging to God alone. This theological rationale distinguishes Israel's law from mere hygiene: blood is sacred because God appoints it for atonement.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Prophet.md]] sites at Carthage and other Phoenician colonies contain thousands of urns with cremated infant remains, accompanied by votive inscriptions to [[Baal]] (the Phoenician equivalent of Molech). These grim finds provide concrete archaeological evidence for the child sacrifice practices that Leviticus 18:21 and 20:2-5 explicitly condemn.
- The [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] (4Q180-181, the Pesher on the Periods) interpret Azazel in connection with the fallen angels of Genesis 6, demonstrating that Second Temple Jewish readers understood the scapegoat ritual against a rich angelological and demonological backdrop.
- The [[Temple Scroll]] (11QT, from Qumran) contains expanded Yom Kippur regulations that elaborate on the biblical text, showing the ongoing development and interpretation of the Day of Atonement ritual in the centuries before Christ.
- The [[Ketef Hinnom silver scrolls]] (c. 600 BC), containing the priestly blessing of Numbers 6:24-26, demonstrate the antiquity of the priestly tradition in which the atonement rituals of Leviticus were embedded.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Origen of Alexandria]] (*Homilies on Leviticus*, Homily 9) interpreted the Day of Atonement as a type of Christ's two advents: the slain goat represents Christ's first coming in suffering, the scapegoat His second coming in judgment. Origen's typological method profoundly shaped Christian interpretation of the Old Testament ritual system.
- [[John Chrysostom]] (c. 347-407 AD) argued that the very repetition of the Day of Atonement proved its insufficiency — if the sacrifices truly cleansed, they would not need to be repeated. He drew a stark contrast between the annual blood of bulls and goats and the once-for-all blood of Christ, a theme later central to the Epistle to the Hebrews.
- [[Augustine of Hippo]] (354-430 AD) in *The City of God* and *Against Faustus* defended the continued value of the Old Testament ceremonial laws as prophetic signs, even while insisting their literal observance was superseded. He read the scapegoat as a figure of Christ bearing the sins of the world into the wilderness of death and resurrection.
- [[John Calvin]] (1509-1564) in his *Institutes of the Christian Religion* treated the Levitical sacrifices primarily as pedagogical tools designed to drive Israel to Christ by demonstrating the costliness of sin and the inadequacy of animal blood.

## New Testament Connections

- [[Hebrews 9_6-14.md]] provides the most extended NT treatment of the Day of Atonement, contrasting the high priest's annual entry with animal blood to Christ's once-for-all entry with His own blood into the heavenly sanctuary. The passage explicitly states that the Holy Spirit was signifying that "the way into the Holiest of All was not yet made manifest" while the earthly tabernacle stood.
- [[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 10_19-22.md]] exhorts believers to "enter the Holiest by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way" — every believer now has the access that only the high priest had on Yom Kippur.
- [[Romans 3_25]] describes Jesus as the "propitiation" (Greek *hilasterion*, the same word used in the LXX for the mercy seat) — Christ Himself is the place of atonement where God's justice and mercy meet.
- [[1 John 2_2]] declares Jesus "the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the whole world," extending the atoning work of Yom Kippur beyond Israel to all humanity.
- [[1 Corinthians 6_9-11.md]] echoes the moral prohibitions of Leviticus 18, listing sexual immorality, idolatry, and homosexuality among the sins that exclude from the kingdom, while affirming "such were some of you" — the cleansing power of Christ transforms those who were once defined by these practices.
- [[1 Peter 1_15-16.md]] directly quotes Leviticus 19:2 ("Be holy, for I am holy") as the foundation for Christian ethical conduct, demonstrating that the holiness code retains its moral force under the New Covenant.

## For Further Study

- The relationship between the Yom Kippur ritual and the theology of atonement in the [[Epistle to the Hebrews]] is one of the richest intertextual studies in the Bible. See [[Hebrews 9-10]] for the fullest development.
- The identity of [[Azazel]] continues to be debated. For the earliest Jewish traditions, see [[Enoch.md]] 6-10 and the [[Dead Sea Scrolls]]. Modern scholarship has proposed readings ranging from "the goat that departs" to a place name to a demonic figure.
