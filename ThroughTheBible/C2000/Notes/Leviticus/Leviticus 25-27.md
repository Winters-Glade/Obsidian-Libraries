## Overview

Chuck Smith teaches through the closing chapters of Leviticus, covering the [[Sabbath Rest.md]], the [[Gospel of Reconciliation]], the [[Covenant]] blessings and curses, and the laws of [[Vows]] and [[Tithe|Tithes]]. The Jubilee is presented as a type of [[Jesus Christ|Christ's]] redemptive work. Chapter 26's blessings and curses are applied to Israel's history and to eschatology, including the millennial [[Sabbath]] rest.

## Summary

Chapter 25 establishes the [[Sabbath Rest.md]] (land rests every seventh year) and the [[Gospel of Reconciliation]] (every fiftieth year: liberty proclaimed, property returned, slaves freed). God promised a triple crop in the sixth year to carry through the Sabbath year. The law of the [[kinsman-redeemer]] is introduced — a picture of [[Jesus Christ|Christ]] who became man to redeem us. Chapter 26 is one of the most important covenant chapters in the Bible, listing blessings for obedience (rain, harvest, peace, victory, [[God|God's]] presence) and five escalating stages of judgment for disobedience (terror, drought, wild beasts, war/exile, worldwide dispersion). Even in judgment, God promises to remember His [[Covenant]] with [[Abraham]], [[Isaac]], and [[Jacob]]. Chuck Smith connects the 6,000 years of human history to the 7th millennium as the coming Sabbath rest — the [[Millennial Kingdom]]. Chapter 27 covers [[Vows]] and [[Tithe|Tithes]], with the tithe belonging wholly to the Lord.

## Key Takeaways

1. The [[Sabbath Rest.md]] was a test of faith — trusting God's promise of a triple crop in the sixth year.
2. The [[Gospel of Reconciliation]] is a powerful type of the gospel: Christ proclaims liberty and restores our inheritance.
3. The [[kinsman-redeemer]] (Goel) prefigures Christ, who became a man to redeem fallen humanity.
4. [[_Bible/Leviticus/Leviticus 26.md]] provides a prophetic outline of Israel's history, including the Babylonian exile and worldwide dispersion.
5. Even in judgment, God remembers His [[Covenant]] with the patriarchs — guaranteeing a future for Israel.
6. Chuck Smith connects the 6,000 years of human history to a coming 7th millennium of Sabbath rest (the [[Millennial Kingdom]]).
7. The [[Tithe]] belongs to God — it is holy to the Lord, not a gift but what is due.
8. The book of Leviticus ends where it began: "These are the commandments which the Lord commanded [[Moses]]" — God's authority undergirds the entire book.

## Historical & Cultural Context

- The Sabbath year (shemittah) and the Year of Jubilee (yovel) are unique to Israel's covenant framework. While ANE kings occasionally proclaimed misharum edicts (debt cancellations and liberty proclamations), these were ad hoc royal decrees, not divinely mandated, recurring institutions. The Jubilee's 50-year cycle (seven weeks of years plus one) embedded economic justice into the fabric of Israel's calendar, preventing the permanent concentration of land wealth.
- The kinsman-redeemer (goel) was a well-established institution in Israelite law (cf. [[Ruth]] 3-4, [[Jeremiah]] 32:6-15). The goel had four responsibilities: redeem a poor relative's property, redeem a relative sold into slavery, avenge a relative's murder (blood avenger), and marry a childless widow to preserve the family line. The goel concept provides the legal backdrop for understanding Christ's redemptive work.
- The covenant blessings and curses of Leviticus 26 follow the structure of ANE suzerain-vassal treaties (especially the Hittite and Neo-Assyrian treaty forms): preamble, historical prologue, stipulations, blessings, curses, and witnesses. The five-stage progression of judgment (terror, drought, wild beasts, war, exile) mirrors the escalating sanctions found in treaties like the [[Spirit of Adoption]].
- Vows (nedarim) were a common feature of ANE religion, where individuals would promise gifts or services to a deity in exchange for favor. Leviticus 27 provides a system for redeeming vowed persons, animals, or property, ensuring that vows made in the heat of devotion or desperation could be fulfilled without exploitation.

## Archaeological Evidence

- The [[Siloam Inscription]] (c. 701 BC) records Hezekiah's water tunnel project and demonstrates the historical context of the Judean monarchy, against which the covenant promises of Leviticus 26 (rain and prosperity for obedience) can be read as conditional upon covenant fidelity.
- The [[Babylonian Chronicle]] and [[Nebuchadnezzars Dream.md]] provide extra-biblical confirmation of the Babylonian exile described in Leviticus 26:33-39, documenting the destruction of Jerusalem and the deportation of the Judean elite in 597 and 586 BC.
- The [[Cyrus Cylinder]] (c. 539 BC) records the Persian king's policy of repatriating deported peoples — including the Jews — which directly enacted the reversal of the exile curses of Leviticus 26 and enabled the return to the land. Some scholars see Cyrus's proclamation as a partial fulfillment of Jubilee principles.
- The [[Ruth]] scroll itself (the [[Ketef Hinnom amulets]] feature the goel theology indirectly through the priestly blessing) and the [[Book of Enoch.md]] manuscript from the Dead Sea Scrolls (4QRuth) attest to the ongoing significance of the kinsman-redeemer institution through the Second Temple period.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation

- [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] (c. 130-202 AD) in *Against Heresies* read the Jubilee as a type of Christ's recapitulation of all things — the restoration of what was lost in Adam. The fiftieth year of liberty prefigured the "acceptable year of the Lord" proclaimed by Christ ([[Luke 4_16-21.md]]), which Irenaeus understood as the full restoration of humanity in the age to come.
- [[Augustine of Hippo]] in *The City of God* (Book 20) interpreted the seven millennia schema (6,000 years of history followed by a 1,000-year Sabbath rest) as drawn from the creation week and the Jubilee cycle. While he allegorized the Millennium as the current age of the Church, he acknowledged the literal interpretation held by many — a debate that continues in eschatology.
- [[Martin Luther]] (1483-1546) in his *Lectures on Leviticus* treated the Sabbath year as a lesson in trusting God's provision and the Jubilee as a foreshadowing of the spiritual liberty won by Christ. He emphatically rejected any literal observance of these laws by Christians but prized them as catechetical tools pointing to the gospel.
- [[John Calvin]] (1509-1564) in his *Harmony of the Last Four Books of Moses* interpreted the Jubilee as a figure of the spiritual restoration accomplished in Christ — the remission of debts representing the forgiveness of sins, the return of property the restoration of our heavenly inheritance, and the liberty proclaimed the freedom from bondage to sin and death.

## New Testament Connections

- [[Luke 4_16-21.md]] records Jesus's inaugural sermon in Nazareth, where He reads from [[Isaiah 61_1-2.md]] and declares, "Today this Scripture is fulfilled in your hearing." The Isaiah passage directly echoes the Jubilee proclamation of Leviticus 25 — "the acceptable year of the Lord" — and Jesus explicitly identifies Himself as the One who brings spiritual and ultimate liberation.
- [[Acts 2_44-45.md]] describes the early church's practice of selling property and sharing with the needy — a community-level enactment of Jubilee principles, where no one claimed private ownership but held all things in common (cf. [[Acts 4_32-35.md]]).
- [[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 4_1-11.md]] develops the theology of sabbatismos (Sabbath rest) for the people of God, drawing on the creation Sabbath (Gen 2:2), the weekly Sabbath (Ex 20:8-11), and the land Sabbath (Lev 25) to promise an eschatological rest that remains for believers.
- [[Ephesians 1_7, 14.md]] presents Christ's redemption as the fulfillment of the goel's work — "In Him we have redemption through His blood" and the Holy Spirit as the "guarantee of our inheritance until the redemption of the purchased possession."
- [[Romans 8_19-23.md]] describes creation itself waiting for "the redemption of our body" — the final Jubilee when the entire created order is liberated from its bondage to decay.
- [[_Bible/Galatians/Galatians 3_13.md]] states that "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law, having become a curse for us" — directly addressing the covenant curses of Leviticus 26, which Christ bore on behalf of His people to secure the blessings of Abraham.
- [[2 Corinthians 9_6-7.md]] — "God loves a cheerful giver" — provides New Covenant principles for giving that build on the tithe legislation of Leviticus 27, emphasizing the heart attitude over the legal requirement.

## For Further Study

- The relationship between the Jubilee and the [[Millennial Kingdom]] is a key theme in dispensational eschatology. See the discussion of the 7th millennium in [[Chuck Smith]] and the broader tradition of millennial chronology.
- The year 1994 marked the 50th anniversary of the UN's partition plan for Palestine and was proclaimed a "Jubilee" by some Christian and Jewish groups. The history of Jubilee observance in the Jewish tradition is traced in the [[Mishnah]] tractate [[Arakhin]] (valuations).
- The [[kinsman-redeemer]] as a type of Christ is richly developed in the [[Book of Enoch.md]]. See [[Ruth 4]] for the legal procedures of the goel and their theological application in the genealogy of David and ultimately of Christ.
