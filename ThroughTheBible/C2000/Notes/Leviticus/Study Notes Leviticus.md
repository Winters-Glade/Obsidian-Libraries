## Historical Context
These books (traditionally attributed to [[Moses]]) cover the foundational history of Israel: creation, the patriarchs, the exodus from Egypt, the giving of the Law at Sinai, and the wilderness journey to the border of the Promised Land. They were written during the wilderness period (c. 1440-1400 BC).

## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Leviticus, chapters 25-27.

**Leviticus:** God's manual for holiness. Leviticus outlines the sacrificial system, the priesthood, dietary laws, moral regulations, and the Day of Atonement. The key verse is 'Be holy, for I am holy.' It reveals God's standard of purity and the means of approach to a holy God through sacrifice.

## Historical & Cultural Context
- The book of Leviticus takes its name from the Greek *Leuitikon* ("relating to the Levites") via the Latin Vulgate. In Hebrew tradition it is called *Vayikra* ("And He called") from its opening word. The book is entirely set at Mount Sinai during the first year after the Exodus, presenting a comprehensive system of worship, purity, and holiness for the newly formed nation of Israel.
- The sacrificial system of Leviticus 1-7 stands in contrast to ANE sacrificial practices attested in Ugaritic, Hittite, and Mesopotamian texts. While pagan sacrifices were often apotropaic (warding off evil) or aimed at feeding the gods, Israel's sacrifices emphasized moral confession, substitutionary atonement, and voluntary devotion — reflecting the covenantal relationship between Yahweh and His people.
- The [[Holiness of God.md]] (Leviticus 17-26) is recognized by modern biblical scholarship as a distinct literary stratum within the Pentateuch, characterized by the repeated formula "I am the Lord" and "I am the Lord your God." This section integrates moral, ceremonial, and judicial law under the overarching call to imitate God's holiness.

## Archaeological Evidence
- The [[Lachish Reliefs]] (from Sennacherib's palace at Nineveh, c. 701 BC) depict the siege of Lachish and the deportation of Judean captives, providing visual evidence for the covenant curses of Leviticus 26 — the exile and dispersion that followed Israel's disobedience.
- The [[Kuntillet Ajrud inscriptions]] (c. 800 BC) mention "Yahweh of Samaria and his Asherah," attesting to the syncretistic practices that the Levitical laws against idolatry (Lev 19:4, 26:1) were designed to combat. These findings illuminate the religious environment in which the Levitical code operated.
- The [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] include multiple copies of Leviticus (e.g., 4QLev-Num, 11QpaleoLev), demonstrating the careful preservation of the text. The paleo-Hebrew script of 11QpaleoLev suggests the text was considered especially sacred. The Qumran community also produced interpretive documents (the [[Temple Scroll]], the [[Damascus]]) that expanded on Levitical law.

## Patristic & Historical Interpretation
- [[Origen of Alexandria]] (c. 185-254 AD) — the first great Christian exegete of Leviticus — preached sixteen homilies on the book, interpreting its rituals as allegories of Christ and the soul's journey to God. His hermeneutical principle, "the letter kills but the Spirit gives life" (2 Cor 3:6), guided Christian reading of Leviticus for centuries, though it sometimes minimized the historical meaning of the text.
- [[Theodoret of Cyrus.md]] (c. 350-428 AD), representing the Antiochene school, rejected excessive allegorization and insisted that the literal and historical meaning of Leviticus be understood first, with typology reserved for clear NT warrant (e.g., the Day of Atonement in Hebrews). His approach anticipated modern grammatical-historical exegesis.
- [[Transformation.md]] brought renewed attention to Leviticus. [[John Calvin]]'s *Harmony of the Last Four Books of Moses* provided a detailed commentary that treated the ceremonial law as pedagogically useful for Israel and typologically fulfilled in Christ, while the moral law remained binding. Calvin rejected allegorical excess while affirming that "the whole service of the law was a shadow of the reality which is now manifest in Christ."

## New Testament Connections
- [[Hebrews 8_8-10.md]] is the NT's most sustained engagement with Leviticus, presenting Christ as the High Priest "after the order of Melchizedek," the mediator of a better covenant, and the perfect sacrifice who enters the heavenly Holy of Holies once for all. The book argues that the Levitical system was "a copy and shadow of the heavenly things" (Heb 8:5).
- [[1 Peter 1_14-19.md]] directly applies the Levitical call to holiness (Lev 11:44-45, 19:2) to Christian conduct: "Be holy in all your conduct" because you have been redeemed "with the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or defect."
- [[Romans 12]] transforms the sacrificial system into a call for personal surrender: "present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable to God, which is your reasonable service."
- [[Revelation 5_9-10.md]] depicts the Lamb that was slain as the one who "redeemed us to God by Your blood out of every tribe and tongue and people and nation" — the ultimate fulfillment of the atonement theme that runs through the entire book of Leviticus.

## For Further Study
- Leviticus is often neglected in Christian reading plans. An excellent devotional commentary is [[Andrew]]'s *Leviticus: A New Translation with Notes* (1846), which combines close textual reading with christological application.
- For the documentary hypothesis and the identification of the Holiness Code (H) as distinct from the Priestly Code (P), see [[Spirit of Adoption]] and the works of Israel Knohl (*The Sanctuary of Silence*) and Jacob Milgrom (*Leviticus: A Continental Commentary*).
- For Jewish interpretive traditions on Leviticus, the [[Ra]] (Vayikra Rabbah) provides extensive rabbinic homilies on the book, and the [[Mishnah]] tractates (especially *Yoma*, *Zevachim*, *Menachot*, *Nega'im*) document the practical observance of its laws in the Second Temple period and beyond.
