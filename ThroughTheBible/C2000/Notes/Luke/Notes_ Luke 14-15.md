## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### Healing on the Sabbath
The healing of the man with dropsy (Luke 14:1-6) — a condition causing fluid retention — again raises the Sabbath question. The question about "whose son shall fall into a well" reflects the rabbinic principle of saving life on the Sabbath.

### Parables of the Banquet
The parables of the great banquet (Luke 14:15-24) and the invited guests reflect the social customs of formal meals in the ancient Near East. Invitations were issued in two stages: an initial announcement and then a summons when the feast was ready. The excuses — real estate, oxen, marriage — are deliberately flimsy. The "poor, crippled, blind, and lame" as guests (Luke 14:21) echoes Luke's theme of God's inclusion of the outcasts.

### Cost of Discipleship
The sayings about counting the cost (Luke 14:25-35) use illustrations from building a tower and a king going to war — practical examples that Jesus' audience would readily understand. The demand to "hate" one's family (Luke 14:26) is a Semitic hyperbole meaning "love less" (cf. [[Genesis 29_31.md]]).

### The Parables of Mercy (Chapter 15)
Luke 15 contains three of the most beloved parables — the [[Parable|Lost Sheep]], the [[Parable|Lost Coin]], and the [[The prodigal son.md]] — all defending Jesus' association with sinners. The lost sheep parable also appears in [[Matthew 18_12-14.md]], but the lost coin and the prodigal son are unique to Luke. The parable of the prodigal son is the longest and most developed of Jesus' parables, with the elder brother representing the Pharisees and scribes who objected to Jesus' ministry.

### Jewish Background
Shepherding imagery has deep roots in the Hebrew Bible ([[Psalm 23]], [[Ezekiel 34.md]], [[Isaiah 40_11.md]]). The woman searching for a lost coin reflects the value of ten silver [[drachma]] (the Greek equivalent of a denarius, a day's wage). The younger son's journey to a "far country" — likely one of the Decapolis cities — represents complete alienation from his covenant community.
