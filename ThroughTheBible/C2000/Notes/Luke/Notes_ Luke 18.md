## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### The Parable of the Persistent Widow
The parable of the unjust judge and the persistent widow (Luke 18:1-8) teaches perseverance in prayer. The judge "who neither feared God nor respected man" represents the opposite of ideal judges in Israel (cf. [[Deuteronomy 1_16-17.md]]). Widows in the ancient world were among the most vulnerable members of society — with no husband to represent them, they were dependent on the justice system.

### The Pharisee and the Tax Collector
The parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector (Luke 18:9-14) contrasts self-righteousness with humility. The Pharisee's prayer lists his credentials — not an extortioner, unjust, adulterer, fasting twice a week, tithing — all good things, but prayed "with himself" not to God. The tax collector's prayer — "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" — uses the Greek *hilaskomai* (propitiation/atonement language), asking for mercy on the basis of sacrifice.

### Children and the Kingdom
Jesus' welcome of children (Luke 18:15-17) in a culture where children had low social status reinforces the theme of humility. The reign of God is received, not earned.

### The Rich Young Ruler
The encounter with the rich ruler (Luke 18:18-30) raises the question of eternal life. Jesus' response — "Sell all that you have" — is particularly difficult in a culture where wealth was seen as a sign of God's blessing (the "retribution principle" of [[_Bible/Deuteronomy/Deuteronomy 28.md]]). The disciples' astonishment reflects this assumption.

### The Blind Man at Jericho
The healing of the blind man (Luke 18:35-43) occurs as Jesus approaches [[Jericho]]. The city was an [[Osiris.md]] in the [[Jordan Valley]], a wealthy center of trade and a priestly city. The blind man calls Jesus "Son of David" — a messianic title — and follows Jesus "glorifying God."
