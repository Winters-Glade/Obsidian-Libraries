## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### The Parable of the Shrewd Manager
The parable of the unrighteous steward (Luke 16:1-13) is one of the most difficult parables. The manager's actions — reducing debts by writing new contracts — may represent legitimately reducing his own commission (the manager's profit from overcharging) rather than outright fraud. The point is not the dishonesty but the shrewd, decisive action. Jesus calls his followers to be equally shrewd in using worldly wealth for eternal purposes. The saying "You cannot serve God and [[Mammon|Money]]" (Luke 16:13) personifies wealth as a competing master.

### The Rich Man and Lazarus
The parable of the rich man and [[Lazarus]] (Luke 16:19-31) reflects a common folk-tale motif but is transformed by Jesus into a serious teaching about judgment. The rich man's purple (the color of royalty and extreme wealth, from [[Murex]] snail dye) and fine linen contrast with Lazarus' ulcerated poverty. [[Gehenna]] — the place of torment — was originally the [[Valley of Shadow.md]] (southwest of [[Jerusalem]]). The final verse — "neither will they be convinced if someone should rise from the dead" — is ironic in light of Jesus' own resurrection.

### Causing Sin and Faith
The warnings about causing "little ones" to sin (Luke 17:1-4) reflect the high value placed on children and new believers. The call to forgive "seven times in the day" expands on the teaching about forgiveness.

### The Ten Lepers
The healing of the ten lepers (Luke 17:11-19) is unique to Luke, emphasizing gratitude and the inclusion of a [[Samaritans.md]] (the only one who returned to give thanks). Leprosy (*tzara'at*) required ritual isolation ([[_Bible/Leviticus/Leviticus 13.md]]).

### The Coming of the Kingdom
The teaching on the kingdom (Luke 17:20-37) addresses the Pharisees' question about when the kingdom would come. Jesus' answer — "the kingdom of God is in the midst of you" (*entos hymon*) — may mean "within you" (internal/spiritual) or "among you" (present in Jesus' person). The reference to "the days of [[Noah]]" and "the days of [[Lot]]" compares the suddenness of the eschaton to the flood and the destruction of [[Sodom]] ([[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 3_6-7.md]], [[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 19.md]]).
