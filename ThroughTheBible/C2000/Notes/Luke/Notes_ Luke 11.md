## Historical & Cultural Context

> [!NOTE] QT Verification
> The enrichment below has been added by the Batch 6 Research workflow. Verify facts before using in teaching.

### The Lord's Prayer
Luke's version of the [[Prayer]] (Luke 11:1-4) is shorter than Matthew's (Matthew 6:9-13). The prayer begins simply — "Father" — using the Aramaic "Abba" (an intimate term). The petition "Give us each day our daily bread" uses the unusual Greek word *epiousios*, possibly meaning "for the coming day" or "for existence." Luke's version lacks the closing doxology and the petition "Your will be done."

### The Parable of the Friend at Midnight
The parable of the friend knocking at midnight (Luke 11:5-8) draws on the conventions of Near Eastern hospitality, where providing for a traveler was a sacred duty even at inconvenient hours. The point is not the friend's friendship but his persistence (*anaideia* — "shamelessness").

### Beelzebub Controversy
The accusation that Jesus casts out demons by [[Beelzebub]] (Luke 11:14-23) reflects the developed demonology of [[Second Temple]] Judaism. The "strong man armed" parable uses the imagery of a householder defending his palace — Jesus is the stronger one who overpowers [[Satan]].

### The Sign of Jonah
The request for a sign (Luke 11:29-32) receives the same response as in Matthew 12:38-42 — the "sign of Jonah." But Luke interprets this as Jonah's preaching bringing [[Nineveh]] to repentance, not specifically Jonah's three days in the fish. The Queen of the South (the [[Queen of Sheba]], [[1 Kings 10_1-13.md]]) will also condemn this generation.

### Woes to the Pharisees
Luke's woes against the Pharisees (Luke 11:37-54) parallel Matthew 23 but are placed in the context of a dinner invitation. The Pharisees' concern with ritual washing (Luke 11:38) reflects the *Oral Law* traditions. The "woes" use prophetic judgment language (cf. [[Isaiah 5_8-23.md]]). The mention of [[Abel]] to [[Zechariah]] (Luke 11:51) spans the entire Hebrew canon ([[Genesis 4.md]] to [[2 Chronicles 24.md]]).
