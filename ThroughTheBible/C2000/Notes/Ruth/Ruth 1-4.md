## Key Themes

- **Kinsman-Redeemer ([[Joel.md]])**: [[Boaz]] acts as a kinsman-redeemer, foreshadowing Christ as the ultimate Redeemer ([[Ruth 3_9]]; [[Ruth 4_1-12]])
- **Faithfulness and Loyalty ([[Covenant Faithfulness.md]])**: [[Ruth]]'s devoted love to [[Naomi]] exemplifies covenant loyalty ([[Ruth 1_16-17.md]])
- **Divine Providence**: God's sovereign hand works behind the scenes, guiding Ruth to Boaz's field ([[Ruth 2_3]])
- **Gentile Inclusion**: [[Ruth]] the [[Moab]]itess is brought into the covenant community, prefiguring the inclusion of Gentiles in God's redemptive plan
- **From Bitterness to Joy**: [[Naomi]]'s journey from emptiness ([[Ruth 1_20-21.md]]) to fullness ([[Ruth 4_14-17.md]]) illustrates God's [[Restoration|restoration]]
- **The Lineage of David**: Ruth becomes the great-grandmother of King David, showing God's providential preparation for the [[monarchy|monarchy]] and ultimately for the Messiah ([[Ruth 4_17-22.md]])

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- **Setting**: "In the days when the judges ruled" (ca. 1100 BC) — a time of spiritual darkness, yet this story shines as a beacon of faithfulness and redemption.
- **Moabite background**: Moab descended from Lot ([[Genesis 19_30-38.md]]), and the Moabites were traditional enemies of Israel. Yet God sovereignly works through Ruth, a Moabitess, demonstrating His grace beyond the borders of Israel.
- **The Kinsman-Redeemer (Gaol/Goel)**: A close relative who had the right to redeem land and people. This institution foreshadows Christ as the ultimate Redeemer.
- **Harvest customs**: Ancient Israel practiced gleaning rights per [[Leviticus 19_9-10.md]] and [[Deuteronomy 24_19-22.md]], allowing the poor and foreigners to gather leftover grain — a provision that brought Ruth and Boaz together.
- **Bethlehem**: Later known as the City of David and prophesied as the birthplace of the Messiah ([[Micah]]).
