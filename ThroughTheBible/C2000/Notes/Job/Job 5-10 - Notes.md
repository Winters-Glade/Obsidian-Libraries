# Job 5-10 - Notes

## Summary

Chuck Smith explores the book of Job, the oldest of the books of poetry, examining the profound themes of suffering, divine sovereignty, and theodicy. Job's story of losing everything — possessions, family, health, and friends — and his struggle to understand God's purposes in the midst of unexplained suffering provides timeless lessons on faith, patience, and the sovereignty of God.

### Ancient Near Eastern Parallels

The Book of Job belongs to a wider [[Ancient Near Eastern Wisdom Literature|wisdom tradition]] that flourished across Mesopotamia and Egypt. The [[Ludlul bel nemeqi]] ("I Will Praise the Lord of Wisdom"), an Akkadian poem from the Kassite period (mid-2nd millennium BCE), features a righteous nobleman named Šubši-mašrâ-Šakkan who loses everything — status, health, and divine favor — before being restored by the god [[Mark.md]]. The Sumerian [[Dispute Between a Man and His Ba.md]] (c. 19th–16th centuries BCE) similarly wrestles with undeserved suffering. Both texts anticipate Job's central struggle with [[theodicy]].

### Hebrew Poetry & Structure

Job exemplifies the Hebrew poetic form of [[Parallelism]] within a unique structure: a prose [[Prologue]] (chapters 1–2) and [[Decalogue.md]] (42:7–17) frame an extended poetic [[Decalogue.md]] (chapters 3–41). This envelope structure — rare in the Hebrew Bible — anchors the raw emotional and theological poetry within a narrative framework. The poetic core employs [[Synonymous Parallelism|synonymous]], [[Antithetic Parallelism|antithetic]], and [[Synthetic Parallelism|synthetic parallelism]] to build its arguments.

### Historical Background

Scholars date the final composition of Job to the [[Persian Period]] (6th–4th centuries BCE), though the story's setting in the land of [[Uz]] (southern Edom / northern Arabia) and its patriarchal tone suggest ancient oral traditions. The author is unknown but writes in a highly literary Hebrew with [[Aramaic]] influences, lending authenticity to the distant Edomite setting. The Elihu speeches (chapters 32–37) are widely regarded as a later addition due to stylistic and structural differences.

### New Testament Cross-References

The New Testament draws on Job's example in several key passages:
- [[James]] — "You have heard of the [[Patience]] of Job" — presenting Job as the archetype of endurance under trial
- [[1 Corinthians 3_19.md]] — Paul quotes Job 5:13: "He catches the wise in their craftiness"
- [[1 Corinthians 1_19.md]] — echoes of Job's theme that divine wisdom confounds human wisdom
- [[Hebrews 11_32–40.md]] — Job is listed among the heroes of faith who endured suffering

### Messianic Connections

[[Job 7_16]] — "I know that my [[kinsman-redeemer]] lives, and that He shall stand at the latter day upon the earth" — has been read by Christian tradition since the early church ([[Herod the Great.md]], [[Thomas Aquinas]]) as a proto-Messianic confession, pointing to [[Christ]] as the ultimate vindicator of the righteous.
