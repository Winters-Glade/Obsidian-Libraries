## Key Themes

- **The Gibeonite Deception**: Israel fails to seek God's counsel and makes a treaty with the [[Gibeonites]], illustrating the danger of decisions made by sight rather than by faith ([[Joshua 9_1-27.md]])
- **The Long Day of Joshua**: God fights for Israel as the sun stands still at [[Gibeon]], demonstrating His power over creation ([[Joshua 10_1-15.md]])
- **Southern Campaign**: Joshua conquers the southern coalition of kings, including [[Hazor]] and [[Jerusalem]] ([[Joshua 10_16-43.md]])
- **Northern Campaign**: Joshua defeats the northern coalition led by Jabin of Hazor ([[Joshua 11_1-23.md]])
- **Dividing the Land**: The land is allocated among the tribes by lot, fulfilling God's promise to Abraham ([[Joshua 13_1]]-[[Joshua 19_51.md]])
- **Caleb's Inheritance**: Caleb, at 85, claims [[Hebron]] as his inheritance, demonstrating faithful perseverance ([[Joshua 14_6-15.md]])
- **Incomplete Conquest**: The failure to fully drive out the Canaanites sows seeds of future trouble ([[Joshua 15_63.md]]; [[Joshua 16_10.md]])

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

The book of Joshua is set during the Late Bronze Age (ca. 1400–1200 BC), a period of significant upheaval in the ancient Near East. The conquest narrative describes Israel's entry into Canaan under Joshua's leadership following the Exodus from Egypt.

Key archaeological findings illuminate this period:

- **The Destruction of Jericho (Tell es-Sultan)**: Excavations by Kathleen Kenyon (1950s) revealed collapsed walls and evidence of destruction by fire, though the dating remains debated. Some scholars place this at ca. 1550 BC (late Bronze Age I), while others argue for a later date consistent with the biblical timeline (ca. 1400 BC).
- **The Merneptah Stele (ca. 1208 BC)**: Discovered in 1896, this Egyptian inscription contains the earliest known extra-biblical reference to "Israel," describing them as a people group in Canaan. This confirms that Israel was present in the land by the late 13th century BC.
- **The Amarna Letters (14th century BC)**: A cache of clay tablets from Egyptian archives, these letters mention the Habiru/Apiru—wandering, stateless people sometimes associated with the Hebrews. The letters describe instability in Canaan and requests for Egyptian military aid.
- **The Battle of Gibeon**: Excavations at Beth Horon and surrounding sites have uncovered evidence of Late Bronze Age destruction layers consistent with the military campaign described in Joshua 10.

**Historical Interpretations of the Conquest:**

- **Military Invasion Model**: Traditional view that Israel conquered Canaan through a swift, unified military campaign as described in Joshua.
- **Gradual Infiltration Model**: Albrecht Alt and Martin Noth proposed that Israel emerged gradually through peaceful settlement and integration into Canaanite society.
- **Internal Revolt / Peasant Revolt Model**: George Mendenhall and Norman Gottwald suggested the conquest reflects an internal uprising of oppressed Canaanite classes against their city-state rulers, with the "Israelites" being displaced Canaanites.
- **Deuteronomic History Perspective**: Scholars widely view Joshua—together with Deuteronomy, Judges, 1–2 Samuel, and 1–2 Kings—as the "Deuteronomic History," a theological retelling compiled during the 7th–6th centuries BC (the Josianic reform and Babylonian exile periods). This framework emphasizes covenant faithfulness: obedience brings victory, disobedience brings defeat.
