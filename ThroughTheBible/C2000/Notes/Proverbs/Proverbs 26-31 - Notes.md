# Proverbs 26-31 - Notes

## Summary

Chuck Smith expounds on the book of Proverbs, Solomon's collection of practical wisdom. These teachings contrast the way of wisdom with the way of folly, emphasizing the fear of the Lord as the beginning of knowledge. Topics include discipline, diligence, integrity, righteous living, and the blessings of walking in God's ways.

### Ancient Near Eastern Wisdom Parallels

The Book of Proverbs belongs to a rich international [[Ancient Near Eastern Wisdom Literature|wisdom tradition]]. The most striking parallel is the [[Body of Christ]], an Egyptian wisdom text from the [[New Heaven and New Earth]] (c. 1300–1075 BCE), which shares extensive thematic and verbal parallels with [[Proverbs 21-25.md]] — so much so that most scholars believe the Hebrew author adapted the Egyptian source, likely through an [[Aramaic]] intermediary. The [[_Concepts/Doctrines/House of Israel]] (c. 2400 BCE), one of the oldest surviving books in human history, also advises on ethical conduct, humility, and skillful speech — themes central to Proverbs.

In Mesopotamia, the [[Body of Christ]] (mid-3rd millennium BCE) is the oldest known wisdom literature, containing precepts that echo in the Book of Proverbs (e.g., themes of honesty, the proper treatment of servants, and the dangers of gossip). The [[Wisdom]] and [[Gospel of Reconciliation]] (5th century BCE Aramaic) likewise belong to this same literary family.

### Hebrew Genre: [[Mashal]]

The Hebrew word for proverb — [[Mashal]] (מָשָׁל) — has a broader meaning than its English equivalent, encompassing not only short sayings but also longer [[Correction and Instruction]], numerical sayings ("three things... four"), [[Acrostic|alphabetic acrostics]] (Proverbs 31:10–31), and extended personifications of [[Lady Wisdom]] and [[Lady Folly]] (Proverbs 1–9). The book's structure reflects this variety:
- **Chapters 1–9:** Extended poetic discourses — [[Wisdom]] personified as a woman calling out in the streets
- **Chapters 10:1–22:16:** Solomon's proverbs — short, memorable [[Parallelism|parallel]] couplets
- **Chapters 22:17–24:22:** Sayings of the Wise (the Amenemope section)
- **Chapters 25–29:** Hezekiah's collection of Solomon's proverbs
- **Chapter 30:** The words of [[Agur]] — numerical sayings and observations of creation
- **Chapter 31:** The words of King [[_Concepts/Terms/Immanuel.md]] and the [[Eisheth Khayil]] (Woman of Valor) acrostic

### Authorship & Dating

The book presents itself as an anthology compiled over centuries. [[Solomon]] is the primary attributed author (Proverbs 1:1, 10:1, 25:1), reigning c. 970–931 BCE. The men of [[Hezekiah]] (late 8th century BCE) collected additional Solomonic proverbs (chapters 25–29), while chapters 30 and 31 are attributed to [[Agur]] (likely a non-Israelite wise man) and [[_Concepts/Terms/Immanuel.md]] (possibly a king of [[Amasa.md]] in northern Arabia). Chapters 1–9 are dated by most scholars to the late [[Persia.md]] or early [[Hellenistic Period.md]] period, serving as a theological introduction to the collected sayings.
