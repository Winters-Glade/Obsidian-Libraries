## Overview

Last week, we left [[David]] in very sad shape. His son [[Absalom]] had rebelled against him, had gone down to [[Hebron]], and had gathered Israel to his support, who he had gradually been sort of enticing away from his father. And when he felt that he had enough strength, he announced his kingdom and began moving with his troops towards [[Jerusalem]]. And [[David]], rather than having a direct confrontation with his son [[Absalom]], began to flee from [[Jerusalem]] with a great company of people with him, leaving the city of Jerusalem, going through the valley of the [[Kidron Valley|Kidron]], and up over the [[Mount of Olives]], covering his head with a handkerchief and weeping as he went. At this particular time, it seemed that it gave [[David]]'s enemies all a chance to come out and get their licks in against [[David]]. And [[Shimei]], who was one of the relatives of [[Saul]], came along, cursed [[David]], threw rocks at him and his company, and [[Abishai]], one of [[David]]'s generals, wanted to go up and take the guy's head off, but [[David]] said, no, maybe God's laid upon his heart to curse me, let him go. And we noticed that [[David]] was very resigned to all of these things, that were happening. He accepted them as really God's judgment against him for his sin. ## Key Themes

- **[[Ahithophel]]'s Treachery and [[Suicide]]**: Ahithophel's counsel is rejected; he sets his house in order and hangs himself ([[2 Samuel 17_1-23.md]])
- **[[Absalom]]'s Defeat and Death**: Absalom is killed by [[Joab]] despite [[David]]'s plea to "deal gently" with the young man ([[2 Samuel 18_1-33.md]])
- **[[David]]'s Grief for [[Absalom]]**: David mourns, "O my son Absalom, would to God I had died for thee" — a father's anguished love ([[2 Samuel 18_33.md]])
- **[[Joab]]'s Rebuke of [[David]]**: Joab confronts David's excessive grief, warning that he will lose the loyalty of his men ([[2 Samuel 19_1-8.md]])
- **[[David]]'s Return to [[Jerusalem]]**: David pardons [[Shimei]] and restores [[Mephibosheth]], demonstrating [[mercy|mercy]] and political wisdom ([[2 Samuel 19_9-43.md]])
- **[[Sheba]]'s Rebellion**: A Benjamite leads a revolt against David; the wise woman of [[Abel]] saves her city ([[2 Samuel 20_1-26.md]])
- **The [[Gibeonites|Gibeonite]] Vengeance**: Three years of famine end when Saul's sons are executed for breaking Israel's [[Covenant|covenant]] with the Gibeonites ([[_Bible/2 Samuel/2 Samuel 21_1-14.md]])
- **Wars with [[Philistines|Philistine]] Giants**: David's mighty men defeat the descendants of [[Goliath]], including a giant with six fingers and six toes ([[2 Samuel 21_15-22.md]])
- **[[David]]'s Song of Deliverance**: David's psalm of praise, nearly identical to [[Psalm 18]] ([[2 Samuel 22_1-51.md]])
- **[[David]]'s Last Words**: The Spirit of the Lord speaks through the "sweet psalmist of Israel" ([[2 Samuel 23_1-7.md]])
- **[[David]]'s Mighty Men**: The exploits of David's warriors — Adino, Eleazar, Shammah, and the Thirty ([[2 Samuel 23_8-39.md]])
- **[[David]]'s Census and the [[Judgment|Plague]]**: David numbers Israel; 70,000 die; the angel stays his hand at the threshing floor of [[Araunah]] ([[2 Samuel 24_1-25.md]])
- **Costly [[Sacrifice]]**: David's principle — "I will not offer to God that which costs me nothing" ([[2 Samuel 24_24.md]])
- **The Site of the [[Temple]]**: David purchases the threshing floor of [[Araunah]], which becomes the site of [[Solomon]]'s [[Temple|temple]] ([[2 Samuel 24_18-25.md]]; [[2 Chronicles 3_1.md]])

## Connections

- **[[Ahithophel]] and [[Judas Iscariot|Judas]]**: Ahithophel's betrayal of David and his suicide by hanging ([[2 Samuel 17_23.md]]) prefigures Judas Iscariot's betrayal of Jesus and his suicide ([[Matthew 27_5.md]]; [[Psalm 41_9]])
- **[[Psalm 18]] / [[2 Samuel 22.md]]**: This song of deliverance appears in both books, linking David's military victories with God's faithfulness and providing a model for royal thanksgiving psalms
- **The [[Temple]] Site**: David's purchase of Araunah's threshing floor ([[2 Samuel 24_18-25.md]]) is the acquisition of [[Mount Moriah]], the site where [[Abraham]] offered Isaac ([[_Bible/Genesis/Genesis 22.md]]), and where [[Solomon]] would build the [[Temple|temple]] ([[2 Chronicles 3_1.md]])
- **David's Mighty Men and [[C2000/Transcripts/Hebrews/Hebrews 11.md]]**: The list of David's warriors ([[2 Samuel 23_8-39.md]]) exemplifies the faith and courage commended in the New Testament "Hall of Faith"
- **[[Sheba]]'s Rebellion and Tribal Tensions**: The revolt highlights the ongoing tension between Judah and the northern tribes that would eventually lead to the divided monarchy
- **David's Census and [[Satan]]**: The Chronicler's account ([[1 Chronicles 21_1.md]]) explicitly names Satan as the inciter, while 2 Samuel attributes it to God's anger, reflecting complementary theological perspectives on divine sovereignty and human responsibility
- **[[David]]'s Flight as Type of [[Christ]]**: David's flight from [[Absalom]] across the [[Kidron Valley]] and up the [[Mount of Olives]] ([[2 Samuel 15_23-30.md]]) parallels Jesus' journey to the cross ([[John 18_1]]; [[Luke 22_39-46.md]])
- **Costly [[Sacrifice]] Principle**: David's refusal to offer to God that which costs him nothing ([[2 Samuel 24_24.md]]) is echoed in the widow's mite ([[Mark 12_41-44.md]]) and the prophetic call for genuine [[Repentance|repentance]] ([[Malachi 1_6-8.md]])
- **[[Rizpah]]'s Vigil**: Rizpah's protection of the bodies of Saul's sons ([[2 Samuel 21_10-14.md]]) reflects loyal love and finds a parallel in the burial of [[Jesus]] by [[Joseph of Arimathea]] ([[John 19_38-42.md]])
- **[[_MOC_04_Kingdom_Era|Kingdom Era]]**: These final chapters of 2 Samuel conclude David's reign, setting the stage for [[Solomon]] and the divided kingdom

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- **David's reign over Judah ([[Hebron]], 7 years) and then all Israel ([[Jerusalem]], 33 years), ca. 1010–970 BC** — After Saul's death, David was anointed king over Judah in Hebron. Civil war between Israel and Judah ended with David ruling a united kingdom from Jerusalem.
- **The capture of [[Jerusalem]] (Jebusite city) and making it the political and religious capital** — David conquered the Jebusite stronghold, bringing the Ark of the [[Covenant]] there and establishing it as the center of worship and government ([[2 Samuel 5.md]]–[[2 Samuel 6.md]]).
- **The Davidic [[Covenant]] ([[2 Samuel 7.md]]): God's promise of an eternal dynasty** — God promised David that his throne would be established forever. This is a pivotal theological moment, setting the stage for the [[Prophecy|messianic hope]] fulfilled in Jesus Christ.
- **Archaeological findings:**
  - *Tel Dan Stele* (9th century BC) — Inscribed with the Aramaic phrase "House of David," the first extra-biblical evidence of the Davidic dynasty.
  - *Moabite Stone / Mesha Stele* — Also references the "House of David," confirming a historical Davidic lineage.
  - *City of David excavations* — The stepped-stone structure and Gihon Spring fortifications attest to a substantial 10th-century BC Jerusalem.
- **David's empire** — At its height, Israel's territory stretched from the Euphrates River to the border of Egypt, encompassing Edom, Moab, Ammon, Philistia, and Syria as vassal states.
- **The Succession Narrative: the political intrigue around David's throne ([[2 Samuel 9.md]]–[[2 Samuel 20.md]], [[1 Kings 1.md]]–[[1 Kings 2.md]])** — A continuous literary source detailing the struggles of David's later reign, including [[Absalom]]'s rebellion, Adonijah's conspiracy, and [[Solomon]]'s eventual succession.
- **David's sin with [[Bathsheba]] and its consequences** — David's adultery and murder of [[Uriah]] brought severe judgment: the sword never departed from his house, leading to family tragedy and national turmoil.
