### Hebrew Poetry: The Art of [[Parallelism]]

The Psalms are the Bible's masterclass in Hebrew poetry. Unlike English poetry that rhymes sound, Hebrew poetry rhymes [[drought.md]] through three primary forms of [[Parallelism]]:
- **[[Synonymous Parallelism]]** — the second line repeats the first in different words ("The LORD is my light and my salvation; whom shall I fear?" — [[Psalm 27_1]])
- **[[Antithetic Parallelism]]** — the second line contrasts the first ("For the LORD knows the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish" — [[Psalm 1_6]])
- **[[Synthetic Parallelism]]** — the second line expands or completes the first ("My mouth is filled with your praise, all the day with your lauding" — [[Psalm 71_8]])

Some psalms employ [[Acrostic]] structures, where each verse or stanza begins with successive letters of the [[Hebrews]] — most famously [[_Bible/Psalm/Psalm 119.md]] (22 stanzas × 8 verses = 176 verses, one for each letter).

### Ancient Near Eastern Parallels

Israel's hymnbook did not emerge in isolation. [[Psalm 29]] shows marked linguistic and thematic parallels with [[Ugaritic]] poetry describing the storm-god [[Baal]], transposing Canaanite imagery into praise of [[Yahweh]]. [[Psalm 104.md]] echoes the [[Egyptian]] (14th century BCE) in its celebration of creation, while [[Psalm 74_13–15.md]] alludes to the [[Babylonian]] creation epic [[Enuma Elish]], recasting the chaos-dragon as a subordinate creature of YHWH.

### [[Messianic Psalms]]

Christian tradition identifies several psalms as directly messianic — pointing to Jesus Christ:
- **[[Psalm 2]]** — "You are my Son, today I have begotten You" — quoted in [[Acts 13_33]], [[Hebrews 1_5]]
- **[[Psalm 16]]** — "You will not abandon my soul to [[Sheol]]" — cited by [[Peter]] at [[Pentecost]] as a prophecy of [[Resurrection]] ([[Acts 2_25–31.md]])
- **[[Psalm 22]]** — "My God, my God, why have You forsaken me?" — [[Jesus]]' final words on the cross; the psalm's detail (pierced hands and feet, casting lots for garments) anticipates the crucifixion
- **[[_Bible/Psalm/Psalm 110.md]]** — "The LORD said to my Lord, 'Sit at My right hand'" — the most-quoted psalm in the [[_MOC_By_Book.md]], used to establish Christ's priestly and kingly authority ([[Matthew 22_44.md]], [[Hebrews 5_6]], [[Hebrews 7_17.md]])
- **[[Psalm 145]]** — A royal wedding song read as a picture of Christ the King and His [[The Bride]]

### Authorship & Dating

The Psalms were composed over roughly five centuries (10th–5th centuries BCE). Traditional attributions: [[David]] (73 psalms), [[Asaph]] (12), [[Sons of Korah]] (11), [[Solomon]] (2), [[Moses]] (1). The final Psalter was compiled in the [[Second Temple.md]], with the [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] (11QPs) showing that the ordering of Psalms 90–150 was still fluid in the mid-1st century CE.

The Psalter is organized into five books, mirroring the [[Torah]]:
- **Book 1:** Psalms 1–41
- **Book 2:** Psalms 42–72
- **Book 3:** Psalms 73–89
- **Book 4:** Psalms 90–106
- **Book 5:** Psalms 107–150

Each book ends with a [[Doxology]] (e.g., Psalm 41:13; 72:18–19; 89:52; 106:48; 150 itself).

### New Testament Usage

The Psalms are the most-quoted Old Testament book in the New Testament, with over 300 direct citations and allusions. [[Jesus]] quotes the Psalms extensively, especially during His passion ([[Psalm 22]] on the cross, [[Psalm 31_5]] as His final words in Luke). The [[Epistle to the Hebrews]] builds its entire argument for Christ's superiority on Psalm 110 (Melchizedek priesthood) and Psalm 95 (the [[Rest]] of God). [[Paul]] uses the Psalms to establish the universality of sin ([[Psalm 14]] in [[Romans 13]]).
