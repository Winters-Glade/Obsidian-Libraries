# Acts 9 – Summary & Themes

## Overview

Now let's turn to Acts chapter 9. When Alexander the Great conquered the world, he left pockets of Greek culture throughout the world. And so these pockets of Greek culture became very influential. And even though the Roman Empire conquered the Grecian Empire, yet the Grecian culture remained as a dominant characteristic throughout the world. So the world was under the Roman Empire, but it was dominated by Grecian culture. Now the Grecian culture was more towards the arts.

### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.

### The Conversion of Saul (Acts 9)
The [[Paul|Damascus Road]] experience — the most famous conversion in Christian history — transformed the foremost persecutor of the church into its greatest missionary. The [[Damascus]]-[[Jerusalem]] road, about 135 miles, would have taken about a week's journey. The heavenly light brighter than the noonday sun (9:3; 22:6; 26:13) — an epiphany (divine appearance) — and the voice calling [[Saul of Tarsus|Saul]] in [[Aramaic]]/[[Hebrew language|Hebrew]] (26:14) — "Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting me?" — identifies Jesus with His suffering followers. [[Ananias of Damascus|Ananias]]'s initial reluctance ("Lord, I have heard from many about this man") reflects Saul's reputation. Saul's blindness and recovery (9:8-18) — three days without sight — parallels a symbolic death and resurrection. The immediate shift from persecutor to preacher demonstrates the radical nature of the transformation (9:20-22).
