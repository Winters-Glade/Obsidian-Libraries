# Acts 16 – Summary & Themes

## Overview

Shall we turn now in our Bibles to the 16th chapter of the Acts of the Apostles? We remember at the end of our study last week, there arose a contention between Paul and Barnabas, who had been close companions on the first missionary adventure of the church. But because Barnabas was insisting on taking his nephew John Mark, who deserted Paul and Barnabas on the first trip, Barnabas was wanting to take him on the second trip, and Paul was objecting because of his defection on the first trip.

### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Sergius Paulus|Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.
- The [[Lysias inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): A 1st-century inscription naming a Roman military tribune, corroborating the administrative structure described in Acts 21-23.

### The Macedonian Call (Acts 16)
The "man of [[Macedonia]]" in Paul's vision (16:9) — a region in northern [[Greece]] — signals the gospel's expansion into [[Europe]]. Paul's route from [[Troas]] (port city in northwest [[Asia Minor]]) crossed the [[Aegean Sea]] to [[Neapolis]] (modern [[Kavala]], [[Greece]]) and then along the [[Via Egnatia]] — the major Roman road connecting the [[Adriatic Sea]] to [[Byzantium]] — to [[Philippi]]. The Via Egnatia, built c. 146 BC, was the Roman Empire's main artery across the [[Balkans]].

### Lydia and the Philippian Jailer (Acts 16)
[[Lydia of Thyatira|Lydia]] — a merchant of purple cloth from [[Thyatira]] (modern [[Akhisar]], Turkey) — was a [[God-fearer]] meeting for prayer at the river outside Philippi (16:13). Her conversion and hospitality ("if you have judged me to be faithful to the Lord, come into my house") established the first European house church. The exorcism of the [[python (spirit)|spirit of divination]] from a slave girl (16:16-18) disrupted the local economy of [[fortune-telling|fortune-telling]], leading to Paul and [[Silas]]'s arrest, beating, and imprisonment. The earthquake that opened the prison doors (16:26) — Paul and Silas were praying and singing hymns at midnight — and the jailer's subsequent conversion (16:27-34) contain the most famous conversion narrative in Acts outside of Paul's own. The jailer's question — "What must I do to be saved?" (16:30) — crystallizes the central message of Acts.
