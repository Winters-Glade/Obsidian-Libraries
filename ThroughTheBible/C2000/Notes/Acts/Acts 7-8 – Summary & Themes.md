### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.

### Stephen's Defense and Martyrdom (Acts 7-8)
[[Stephen (biblical)|Stephen]]'s speech before the Sanhedrin — the longest sermon in Acts — is a masterful re-reading of Israel's history from [[Abraham]] through [[Solomon]], arguing that Israel had persistently resisted God's messengers and that the temple was never intended to be permanent or to contain God (7:48-50, quoting [[Isaiah 66_1-2.md]]). The accusation that Stephen spoke against the temple and the law (6:13-14) mirrors the charges against Jesus. Stephen's vision of "the heavens opened and the Son of Man standing at the right hand of God" (7:56) — the only post-resurrection use of "[[Son of Man]]" outside the Gospels — echoes [[Daniel 7_13-14.md]]. His prayer "Lord, do not hold this sin against them" (7:60) directly parallels Jesus' words from the cross ([[Luke 23_34]]). [[Saul of Tarsus]] (the future Paul) is introduced as a witness and consenting party to the execution (7:58; 8:1).

### Philip in [[Samaria]] (Acts 8)
[[Philip the Evangelist]]'s mission to [[Samaria]] (8:5-25) fulfills Jesus' commission to be witnesses "in [[Jerusalem]] and in all [[Judea]] and [[Samaria]]" (1:8). [[Simon Magus]] (8:9-24) — a former sorcerer who offered money for apostolic power (giving rise to the term [[simony|simony]]) — represents the danger of confusing spiritual power with magic. The Ethiopian [[eunuch|eunuch]]'s baptism (8:26-40) — a high official of the [[Candace of Ethiopia|Candace]] (queen mother) of the [[Kingdom of Kush]] (modern Sudan) — represents the gospel crossing ethnic, geographic, and social boundaries. The eunuch was reading [[Isaiah 53_7-8.md]] — the [[Suffering Servant]] passage — which Philip used to preach Jesus. This encounter marks the first conversion of an African in the biblical record.
