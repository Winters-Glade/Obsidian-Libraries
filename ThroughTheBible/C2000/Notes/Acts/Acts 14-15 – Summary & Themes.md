# Acts 14-15 – Summary & Themes

## Overview

So we turn in our Bibles to Acts chapter 14. Paul and Barnabas, in carrying the Gospel, have come into the uncharted territories, as far as the Gospel is concerned, into Asia Minor. They came from Cyprus to the area that is now known as Turkey. They did not remain in the coastal plains of Pamphylia. It is thought that Paul probably got malaria fever. It was a very prevalent thing around the coastal plains of Pamphylia at that time.

### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Sergius Paulus|Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.
- The [[Lysias inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): A 1st-century inscription naming a Roman military tribune, corroborating the administrative structure described in Acts 21-23.

### Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe (Acts 14)
Paul and Barnabas' ministry in the [[Lycaonia]]n cities of [[Iconium]], [[Lystra]], and [[Derbe]] — located in modern-day central [[Turkey]] — reveals the volatility of 1st-century mission work. In Lystra, the healing of a man crippled from birth (14:8-10) causes the crowd to mistake Paul and Barnabas for [[Hermes]] and [[Zeus]] — a reaction rooted in local mythology ([[Ovid]]'s ''[[Metamorphoses]]'' 8.626-724 records a legend that Zeus and Hermes once visited the region incognito and were turned away by all except an elderly couple, [[Philemon and Baucis]]). Paul's speech at Lystra (14:15-17) adapts his message for a purely pagan audience with no knowledge of the OT. The stoning of Paul (14:19) — by Jews from Antioch and Iconium — left him for dead outside the city, but he recovered and continued preaching, demonstrating remarkable resilience.

### The [[Jerusalem]] Council (Acts 15)
The [[Council of Jerusalem]] (c. AD 48-49) addressed the most critical theological question of the early church: must Gentile converts keep the [[Mosaic Law]]? [[Peter]]'s speech argues from experience (God gave the Spirit to Gentiles without the law, 15:7-11); [[James (brother of Jesus).md]]'s judgment appeals to Scripture ([[Amos 9_11-12.md]], 15:13-18). The four prohibitions imposed — abstaining from food polluted by idols, from sexual immorality, from strangled meat, and from blood (15:20, 29) — derive from the [[Noahide laws]] ([[Genesis 9_4]]) and [[Leviticus 18.md]], rules that would allow Jewish and Gentile believers to share table fellowship. [[Paul]]'s later account of this meeting ([[Galatians 2.md]]) emphasizes his independence and the agreement that he would go to the Gentiles and Peter to the Jews.
