### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Sergius Paulus|Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.
- The [[Lysias inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): A 1st-century inscription naming a Roman military tribune, corroborating the administrative structure described in Acts 21-23.

### Paul on Malta and Arrival in Rome (Acts 28)
Paul's [[shipwreck|shipwreck]] on [[Malta]] ([[Melita]], 28:1) — the [[Mediterranean]] island about 60 miles south of [[Sicily]] — opens a window into the local population (described as "barbarous people" — not uncivilized but non-Greek speakers). Paul's survival of the [[viper|viper]] bite (28:3-6) — the locals interpret it as divine justice, then reverse their judgment to deification — echoes Jesus's promise that believers "will pick up serpents" ([[Mark 16_18]]). [[Publius (Acts 28)|Publius]] — the "chief man" (''protōs'') of the island — is confirmed by Maltese inscriptions using this exact title. Paul's healing of Publius's father and others (28:8-9) continues the pattern of apostolic signs. The three-month stay (28:11) — the typical winter layover before sailing resumed — ended with the journey via [[Syracuse, Sicily|Syracuse]] and [[Rhegium]] ([[Reggio Calabria]]) to [[Puteoli]] (modern [[Pozzuoli]], near [[Naples]]), where Paul found believers (28:13-14).

### Paul in Rome (Acts 28:16-31)
Paul's arrival in Rome — "the brothers... came as far as the [[Forum of Appius]] and [[Three Taverns]]" (28:15) — demonstrates that a Christian community already existed in the capital. Paul's meeting with the Jewish leaders (28:17-28) — who had "received no letters from [[Judea]]" about him — shows that the Jewish authorities in [[Jerusalem]] had not formally pursued their case against him at the imperial level. Paul's quotation of [[Isaiah 6_9-10.md]] (28:26-27) — the same passage Jesus used to explain parables — frames Jewish rejection as prophetic fulfillment. The two full years under house arrest (28:30-31) — "welcoming all who came to him, proclaiming the kingdom of God and teaching about the Lord [[Jesus Christ]] with all boldness and without hindrance" — form an abrupt but triumphant ending. Acts ends not with Paul's death (traditionally AD 64-67 under [[Nero]]) but with the word "unhindered" (*akōlytōs*), emphasizing that the gospel cannot be stopped.
