# Acts 26-27 – Summary & Themes

## Overview

Let's open our Bibles to the 26th chapter of Acts. Paul was rescued by Lysias, the captain of the Roman guard, from the mob that was attempting to beat him to death in [[Jerusalem]] on the Temple Mount. He was taken into protective custody by the Roman government, sent under special guard to Caesarea for his own protection, where he appeared before the governor, Felix, who held Paul a prisoner for two years, more or less as a political pawn.

### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Sergius Paulus|Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.
- The [[Lysias inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): A 1st-century inscription naming a Roman military tribune, corroborating the administrative structure described in Acts 21-23.

### Paul Before Agrippa (Acts 26)
Paul's defense before [[Herod Agrippa II]] — the great-grandson of [[Herod the Great]] — is the most polished of his three speeches in the Caesarea imprisonment. Agrippa, an expert in Jewish customs (26:3), was the ideal audience. Paul's personal testimony — his upbringing as a Pharisee (26:4-5), his persecution of Christians (26:9-11), his [[Damascus]] Road encounter (26:12-18), and his commission to the Gentiles (26:16-18) — all structured around the theme of the [[Resurrection]] as the fulfillment of "the promise our twelve tribes hope to attain" (26:7). Festus's outburst — "Paul, you are out of your mind; your great learning is driving you insane" (26:24) — reflects the cultural chasm between Roman rationalism and the message of a resurrected [[Messiah]]. Paul's famous response — "I am not mad, most excellent Festus, but I speak words of truth and reason" (26:25) — and his appeal to Agrippa: "Do you believe the prophets? I know that you do" (26:27). Agrippa's reply — "In a short time would you persuade me to be a Christian?" (26:28, ESV: "In a short time you think to make me a Christian!") — and Paul's response — "whether short or long, I would to God that not only you but also all who hear me this day might become such as I am—except for these chains" (26:29) — is a rhetorical masterpiece.

### The Voyage to Rome (Acts 27)
Luke's detailed account of Paul's voyage and shipwreck (ch. 27) is one of the most vivid ancient descriptions of Mediterranean seafaring. The narrative follows the standard sailing route from [[Caesarea Maritima]] along the [[Levantine]] coast — with stops at [[Sidon]] (27:3) and [[Myra]] in [[Lycia]] (27:5) — and the ship change to an [[Alexandria]]n grain vessel (27:6) heading for Rome. The mention of "the Fast" (the [[Day of Atonement]], [[Yom Kippur]], 27:9) dates the voyage to late autumn when Mediterranean sailing became dangerous. The storm — a Euroclydon (Northeaster) — drove the ship for 14 days (27:27) across the [[Adriatic Sea|Adriatic]] until running aground on [[Malta]] ([[Melita]], 28:1). Paul's prophecies (27:10, 22-26, 31, 34) and his leadership during the crisis — including the dramatic moment when he took bread, gave thanks, and ate (27:35) — echo the [[Eucharist|Eucharistic]] actions of Jesus ([[Luke 22_19]]). The [[sounding (navigation)|sounding]] of 20 [[fathom|fathom]]s (120 feet, 27:28) reflects Luke's use of nautical terminology.
