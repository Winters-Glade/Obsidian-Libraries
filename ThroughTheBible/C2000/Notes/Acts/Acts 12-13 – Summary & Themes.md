# Acts 12-13 – Summary & Themes

## Overview

Shall we turn now in our Bibles to Acts chapter 12. Now about that time, Herod the king stretched forth his hands to vex certain of the church. There are just a lot of Herods in the Bible. And it is a little difficult to keep them all straight. In fact, I don't expect you to keep them all straight. This particular Herod was Herod Agrippa the first. He was the grandson of [[Herod the Great]], who was the Herod at the time of the birth of Jesus. [[Herod the Great]] had ten wives.

### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Sergius Paulus|Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.
- The [[Lysias inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): A 1st-century inscription naming a Roman military tribune, corroborating the administrative structure described in Acts 21-23.

### Herod Agrippa's Persecution (Acts 12)
[[Herod Agrippa I]] — grandson of [[Herod the Great]] — ruled [[Judea]] AD 41–44 and is depicted by the historian [[Josephus]] as a popular king who observed Jewish traditions. His execution of [[James the Great|James the Apostle]] (son of [[Zebedee]]) and imprisonment of Peter (12:1-4) during the [[Feast of Unleavened Bread]] aimed to please the Jewish leadership. Peter's miraculous escape from prison (12:6-19) — walking past two guard posts and through an iron gate that "opened of its own accord" — is one of the most dramatic deliverances in Acts. The disciples' prayer meeting at [[Mary (mother of John Mark)|Mary]]'s house — where the servant [[Rhoda (biblical figure)|Rhoda]] leaves Peter standing at the gate in her joy (12:12-16) — provides a touch of human comedy. Herod's death (12:20-23) is independently corroborated by Josephus (''[[Antiquities of the Jews]]'' 19.8.2), who records he was struck down after accepting divine honors at [[Caesarea]], eaten by worms — a death reminiscent of [[Antiochus IV Epiphanes]] ([[_Concepts/Biblical_References/Acts 2]]).

### First Missionary Journey (Acts 13)
The [[Holy Spirit]]'s explicit commissioning of [[Barnabas]] and [[Paul]] at Antioch (13:2-3) — "Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul for the work to which I have called them" — marks the beginning of the organized Gentile mission. Their journey took them from [[Seleucia Pieria]] to [[Cyprus]] (the native island of Barnabas, 4:36), where they confronted the magician [[Elymas Bar-Jesus|Elymas]] before [[Sergius Paulus]], the Roman proconsul (13:6-12). Paul's sermon in [[Pisidian Antioch]] (13:16-41) — the first recorded in detail — follows the pattern of [[Diaspora]] synagogue preaching: rehearsing [[salvation history|salvation history]] from the Exodus through David to Jesus, emphasizing [[Justification by Faith|justification by faith]] (13:38-39).
