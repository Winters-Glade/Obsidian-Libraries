## Overview

Now, when they had passed through Amphipolis and Apollonia, they came to Thessalonica, where there was a synagogue of the Jews. Now, Luke passes that off in one verse. From Philippi to Amphipolis was 30 miles, another 30 miles on to Apollonia, and another 37 miles on to Thessalonica. So it no doubt took them several days to travel almost 100 miles to Thessalonica.

### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Sergius Paulus|Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.
- The [[Lysias inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): A 1st-century inscription naming a Roman military tribune, corroborating the administrative structure described in Acts 21-23.

### Thessalonica and Berea (Acts 17:1-15)
Paul's pattern — entering the synagogue, reasoning from the Scriptures (17:2-3) — followed his strategy of planting the gospel in existing Jewish communities. [[Thessalonica]] (modern [[Thessaloniki]]), capital of the province of Macedonia, was a free city governed by *[[politarchs|politarchs]]* (17:6-8) — a title Luke uses correctly, confirmed by over 30 inscriptions. The accusation "These men who have turned the world upside down" (17:6) unintentionally testifies to the gospel's transformative power. The [[Berea|Berean Jews]]' practice of "examining the Scriptures daily to see if these things were so" (17:11) made them the model of critical engagement with apostolic teaching.

### Paul at the Areopagus (Acts 17:16-34)
Paul's address on [[Mars' Hill]] (the [[Areopagus]]) in [[Athens]] — the intellectual capital of the ancient world — represents his most philosophically sophisticated sermon. His approach adapts to his audience: he quotes [[Epimenides of Crete|Epimenides]] ("In him we live and move and have our being," 17:28) and [[Aratus]]/[[Cleanthes]] ("For we are indeed his offspring," 17:28, from the [[Phaenomena]]). The altar "TO AN UNKNOWN GOD" (17:23) — altars of this type existed in Athens, used as insurance against offending a deity whose name the worshiper did not know — becomes Paul's entry point for proclaiming the God who is knowable through Christ. The mention of the [[Resurrection]] (17:31-32) causes division: some mock, some express interest, and some (including [[Dionysius the Areopagite]] and [[Rhoda (biblical figure).md]]) believe.
