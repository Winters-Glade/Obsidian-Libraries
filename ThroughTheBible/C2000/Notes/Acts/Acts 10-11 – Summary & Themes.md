# Acts 10-11 – Summary & Themes

## Overview

So we turn now in our Bibles to Acts chapter 10. In the first chapter of Acts, Jesus said to his disciples, you will receive power when the [[Holy Spirit]] comes upon you and you will be witnesses unto me both in [[Jerusalem]] and in [[Samaria]], or both in [[Jerusalem]] and [[Judea]] and in [[Samaria]] and unto the uttermost parts of the earth. Here, Jesus had commanded his disciples to go into all the world and preach the gospel to every creature. And he that believeth and is baptized shall be saved.

### Historical Reliability of Acts
The book of Acts, written by [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] (a companion of [[Paul]]), demonstrates remarkable accuracy in its details of 1st-century Roman administration, geography, and social customs. The British classicist [[Sir William Ramsay]] (1851–1939) initially dismissed Acts as a 2nd-century forgery but reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in [[Asia Minor]], concluding it was "a history of first-rate historical value." Luke's precise use of official titles — *politarchs* in [[Thessalonica]] (17:6, confirmed by 31 inscriptions), *proconsul* (anthypatos) for [[Sergius Paulus|Gallio]] in [[Achaia]] (18:12, confirmed by the [[Delphi inscription]]), and *asiatichs* (temple wardens) in [[Ephesus]] (19:31) — reveals intimate knowledge of local administrative structures.

### Archaeological Confirmations
- The [[Gallio Inscription]] (Delphi, c. AD 52): A fragment of a letter from Emperor [[Claudius]] mentioning [[Gallio (proconsul)|Proconsul Gallio]] — Acts 18:12-17 — providing a key anchor for Pauline chronology.
- The [[Erastus Inscription]] (Corinth, 1st century): A paving stone inscribed "Erastus, procurator and aedile, laid this pavement at his own expense" — likely the same [[Erastus of Corinth|Erastus]] mentioned in Acts 19:22 and Romans 16:23 (the city's director of public works).
- The [[Politarch Inscriptions]] (Thessalonica): Multiple inscriptions confirming the office of *politarch* (city officials), a term used by Luke in Acts 17:6-8.
- The [[Theodotos Inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): Confirms the existence of a synagogue with hostel facilities for pilgrims, consistent with Acts 6:9.
- The [[Lysias inscription]] ([[Jerusalem]]): A 1st-century inscription naming a Roman military tribune, corroborating the administrative structure described in Acts 21-23.

### Cornelius and the Gentile Pentecost (Acts 10-11)
The conversion of [[Cornelius the Centurion]] — a [[God-fearer]] (a Gentile who worshiped the God of Israel without full conversion to Judaism) stationed in [[Caesarea Maritima]] — represents an epochal moment: the deliberate inclusion of Gentiles without requiring them to become Jews. [[Caesarea Maritima]], built by [[Herod the Great]] (22–10 BC), was the capital of the Roman province and the seat of the Roman procurator. Its massive harbor ([[Sebastos]], named after Augustus) was an engineering marvel using [[hydraulic concrete|hydraulic concrete]] — a technology later associated with the [[Roman Pantheon]].

Peter's rooftop vision of the sheet with clean and unclean animals (10:9-16) — "What God has cleansed, do not call common" — overturned centuries of [[kosher|kosher]] dietary laws that had defined Jewish identity. The command appears three times, echoing Peter's threefold denial and eventual restoration ([[John 21]]). The [[Holy Spirit]] falling on Cornelius's household before baptism (10:44-48) — a "Gentile Pentecost" — mirrors Acts 2 and convinces the [[Jerusalem]] church (11:1-18). Peter's defense at [[Jerusalem]] - "who was I to stand in God's way?" (11:17) - established the principle that God's actions must determine church policy.

The term [[Christian]] was first used in [[Antioch]] (11:26), the third-largest city of the Roman Empire after Rome and [[Alexandria]].
