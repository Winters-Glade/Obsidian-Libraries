# Ezekiel 31-35 — Notes

## Overview

**Source:** C2000 Series — Pastor Chuck Smith  
**Book:** [[Ezekiel]]  
**Chapters:** 31-35  
**Transcript:** [[Jeremiah 31-35 Transcript.md]]

## Summary

Chapter 31 presents the **parable of the great cedar** — a warning to [[Pharaoh]] of [[Egypt]]. Ezekiel likens Pharaoh to [[Assyria]], which was like a magnificent cedar in [[Lebanon]], taller than all the trees of the field, watered by the deep. The cedars in the garden of God could not rival it. But because it was lifted up in pride, God delivered it to the mighty one of the nations, and it was cut down — the nations trembled at its fall. The message is clear: even the greatest empire is subject to God's judgment.

Chapter 32 is a **lamentation over Pharaoh** — a funeral dirge describing the descent of Egypt and its allies into the pit ([[Sheol]]), where other defeated nations — Assyria, [[Elam]], [[Meshech]], [[Tubal]], [[Edom]], [[Sidon]] — lie in shame.

Chapter 33 marks a major turning point. The **watchman** passage is restated (paralleling Ezekiel 3:17-21), and the news arrives: Jerusalem has fallen. Ezekiel had been a "sign" to the people — and now they come to him saying, "Come, sit and hear the word." The individual responsibility principle is reaffirmed in even clearer terms: when a righteous man turns from his righteousness and sins, his former righteousness will not save him; when a wicked man turns from his wickedness, his former sins will not condemn him.

Chapter 34 is one of Scripture's most beloved passages — the **Good Shepherd** prophecy. God pronounces woe against the shepherds of Israel who have fed themselves but not the flock. They have not strengthened the weak, healed the sick, bound the broken, brought back the straying, or sought the lost. God declares, "Behold, I, even I, will both search for My sheep and seek them out." He will feed them in good pasture and bind up the broken. Then comes the climactic promise: "**I will set up one Shepherd over them, and He shall feed them — My servant David**" — a messianic prophecy pointing to [[Jesus Christ]], the [[Good Shepherd]] (Ezekiel 34:23-31). God makes a [[Covenant of Peace]] with His flock.

Chapter 35 pronounces **judgment against [[Mount Seir]] ([[Edom]])** because they harbored perpetual enmity against Israel and sought to possess the land when Israel was desolate.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

This section of Ezekiel spans the period immediately before and after the fall of Jerusalem in **586 BC**. The watchman's recommissioning in chapter 33 and the report of Jerusalem's destruction mark the transition from Ezekiel's messages of judgment to his messages of consolation and hope. The reference to "My servant David" (Ezekiel 34:23-24) is not a promise of David's literal resurrection but a messianic prophecy pointing to a descendant of David — the ultimate Shepherd-King. Jesus directly claimed this identity in [[John 10]], declaring, "I am the Good Shepherd." The [[Covenant of Peace]] (Ezekiel 34:25) connects to the New Covenant and the peace that Christ brings ([[John 1]]).

The judgment against Edom (chapter 35) reflects Edom's opportunistic treachery during Judah's downfall. [[Obadiah]]'s entire prophecy is dedicated to the same theme. Edom's perpetual hostility began with Jacob and Esau and continued through the centuries, culminating in the Idumean Herod dynasty of New Testament times.
