# Ezekiel 11-15 — Notes

## Overview

**Source:** C2000 Series — Pastor Chuck Smith  
**Book:** [[Ezekiel]]  
**Chapters:** 11-15  
**Transcript:** [[Jeremiah 11-15 Transcript.md]]

## Summary

In chapter 11, the Spirit transports Ezekiel from Babylon back to Jerusalem, where he sees the **princes of the people** — some of the Jewish zealots and leaders who have rebelled against [[Nebuchadnezzar]]. False prophets in Jerusalem are encouraging the rebellion, telling the people they will defeat Babylon and return soon. Jeremiah, the true prophet in Jerusalem, is imprisoned for treason for urging surrender. Ezekiel in Babylon declares the same message: the exiles should settle down and build houses because they will be there for 70 years. Ezekiel calls out against the princes, declaring that God will bring judgment upon them. Pelatiah dies as Ezekiel prophesies, prompting Ezekiel to cry out, "Ah Lord God! Will you make a full end of the remnant of Israel?" Despite the judgment, God promises restoration: He will gather His people, give them one heart, and put a **new spirit** within them, removing the heart of stone and giving a heart of flesh.

Chapter 12 rebukes the **rebellious house** — people who have eyes to see but do not see, ears to hear but do not hear. Ezekiel performs a symbolic act: packing his belongings and digging through the wall, acting as a captive going into exile, as a sign to the people.

Chapter 13 pronounces **woe against the false prophets** who prophesy out of their own hearts and follow their own spirit. They are like foxes in the deserts who have not gone up into the gaps to stand in the breach for the house of Israel. They daub the wall with untempered mortar — a covering that cannot hold when God's storm comes.

In chapter 14, the **elders of Israel** come to Ezekiel, but they have set up idols in their hearts. God declares that even if [[Noah]], [[Daniel]], and [[Job 38-41]] were in the land, they could only deliver themselves by their righteousness. God sends four severe judgments: the sword, famine, wild beasts, and pestilence. Yet a remnant shall be left.

Chapter 15 presents the **parable of the vine** — Jerusalem is like the wood of a vine, which is useless for any work and fit only for burning. As the vine is cast into the fire, so Jerusalem will be given to the flames of judgment.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

These prophecies date to approximately **592-591 BC**, during the interval between the second deportation (597 BC) and the final destruction of Jerusalem (586 BC). The political situation was chaotic: Zedekiah, Nebuchadnezzar's appointed puppet-king in Jerusalem, was being pressured by pro-Egyptian factions to rebel against Babylon. False prophets exploited this nationalist fervor, promising divine deliverance. Meanwhile, Jeremiah in Jerusalem and Ezekiel in Babylon both proclaimed that rebellion would bring complete destruction. The reference to Daniel (Ezekiel 14:14, 20) is significant — it confirms that Daniel's reputation for righteousness was already established in Babylon, even though his book had not yet been written. The triad of Noah, Daniel, and Job represents three figures known for their righteousness through extreme trials.

The imagery of **idols in the heart** (Ezekiel 14:1-6) is a profound theological insight — idolatry is not merely an external act but an internal disposition. This anticipates Jesus' teaching that sin begins in the heart (Matthew 5:28). The promise of a **new heart and new spirit** (Ezekiel 11:19) is one of the great Old Testament anticipations of the New Covenant, later quoted by Paul in [[2 Corinthians 3_3.md]].
