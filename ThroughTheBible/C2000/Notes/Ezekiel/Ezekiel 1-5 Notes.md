## Summary

Ezekiel begins his prophecy in the 30th year (his 30th year of life) in the fourth month, on the fifth day — July 5th — as he was among the captives by the river Chebar in Babylon. Ezekiel was taken in the second captivity when Jehoiachin was carried away (Daniel had gone in the first captivity). He was both a [[Priest]] and a [[Prophet]], and the heavens were opened as he saw visions of God. The vision opens with a whirlwind from the north, a cloud with flashing fire, and four living creatures (cherubim) each with four faces — man, lion, ox, eagle — and four wings. Above them was a firmament like crystal, and above the firmament a throne of sapphire on which sat the appearance of a man with fire from the waist down and amber from the waist up — the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the Lord. Ezekiel fell on his face.

God commissions Ezekiel as a watchman to the house of Israel, telling him not to be afraid of their words or their faces. He eats the scroll — sweet as honey in his mouth — containing lamentations, mourning, and woe. He is assigned to sit among the captives at Tel-abib by the river Chebar, overwhelmed for seven days.

The watchman commission (Ezekiel 3:17-21) establishes the principle of individual responsibility: if the watchman warns the wicked and they do not turn, they die in their iniquity but the watchman has delivered his own soul; if the watchman does not warn, the wicked die in their iniquity but their blood is required at the watchman's hand.

Ezekiel then performs symbolic acts: lying on his left side for 390 days bearing the iniquity of Israel, then on his right side for 40 days bearing the iniquity of Judah. He shaves his head and beard, dividing the hair into thirds — one third burned, one third struck with the sword, one third scattered to the wind — symbolizing the judgment coming upon Jerusalem.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Ezekiel began his ministry in **593–592 BC** (the fifth year of Jehoiachin's exile), approximately seven years before Jerusalem's final destruction in 586 BC. The Babylonian deportations occurred in three waves: the first in 605 BC (including Daniel), the second in 597 BC (including Ezekiel and Jehoiachin), and the final in 586 BC when Nebuchadnezzar destroyed Jerusalem and the temple. Ezekiel ministered to the exiles in Babylon while Jeremiah prophesied in Jerusalem. False prophets in Babylon were telling the people that their captivity would be short, urging them not to settle down; Ezekiel and Jeremiah both insisted they would be in Babylon for 70 years.

The vision of God's throne in chapter 1 is foundational to biblical theology, establishing that even in exile, God's glory and sovereignty are not diminished. The four living creatures (cherubim) correspond to the cherubim on the mercy seat of the ark of the covenant, connecting Ezekiel's vision to the tabernacle/temple imagery. The "hand of the Lord" upon Ezekiel is a phrase used seven times in the book, marking key transitions in his prophetic experience.
