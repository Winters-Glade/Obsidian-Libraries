# Ezekiel 26-30 — Notes

## Overview

**Source:** C2000 Series — Pastor Chuck Smith  
**Book:** [[Ezekiel]]  
**Chapters:** 26-30  
**Transcript:** [[Jeremiah 28-30 Transcript.md]]

## Summary

Chapters 26-28 pronounce **judgment against [[Tyre]]** (ancient Phoenicia). In 586 BC (the 11th year of Zedekiah), when Jerusalem fell, Tyre rejoiced ("Aha, she is broken — now I shall be replenished"). God declares He is against Tyre and will bring **many nations** against her like the waves of the sea. The prophecy describes Tyre being scraped bare like the top of a rock, becoming a place for fishermen to spread their nets — a remarkably specific prediction that was literally fulfilled when [[Nebuchadnezzar]] besieged Tyre for 13 years, and later when [[Alexander the Great]] built a causeway from the mainland to the island city, scraping the debris of the old city into the sea to fulfill Ezekiel's words.

Chapter 27 is a **lamentation over Tyre**, portraying the city as a magnificent merchant ship — built with fir from [[Senir]], cedar from [[Lebanon]], oaks from [[Bashan]], embroidered linen from [[Egypt]], and manned by sailors from [[Sidon]] and [[Arvad]]. The ship sinks in the depths, and the trading nations of the world lament its fall.

Chapter 28 contains two oracles: one against the **prince of Tyre** (a human ruler claiming to be a god, sitting in the seat of God), and one against the **king of Tyre** — a passage that soars beyond any human ruler to describe the fall of [[Satan]] himself. "You were in [[Eden]], the garden of God; every precious stone was your covering; you were the anointed cherub who covers; you were perfect in your ways from the day you were created, till iniquity was found in you." This passage is foundational to the biblical understanding of Satan's origin and fall (echoed in [[Isaiah 14_12-15.md]]).

Chapters 29-30 pronounce **judgment against [[Egypt]]**. Egypt is portrayed as a great dragon (or crocodile) lurking in the [[Nile]], claiming "My river is my own, I have made it for myself." God declares He will bring Egypt desolate for 40 years and scatter her people among the nations, then gather them again as a base kingdom. Nebuchadnezzar will be given Egypt as payment for his hard service against Tyre.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

These prophecies date to **586-585 BC**, the very season of Jerusalem's destruction. Tyre was an ancient Phoenician port city with two parts: a mainland settlement and a heavily fortified island city half a mile offshore. Nebuchadnezzar besieged the mainland city for 13 years (585-572 BC) but the island portion remained unconquered at that time. Two and a half centuries later, Alexander the Great (332 BC) built a causeway using the debris of the mainland city, scraping it "like the top of a rock" to reach the island — fulfilling Ezekiel's prophecy in precise detail. Today the causeway remains and Tyre is indeed a place where fishermen spread their nets.

The oracle against the king of Tyre (Ezekiel 28:11-19) is one of two key Old Testament passages (alongside [[_Bible/Isaiah/Isaiah 14.md]]) that describe the origin and fall of Satan. The five "I will" statements of Lucifer in Isaiah parallel the description of the anointed cherub whose beauty led to pride and whose pride led to expulsion.

Egypt's judgment (chapters 29-30) includes the 40-year desolation — a period that likely corresponded to the Neo-Babylonian domination of Egypt (c. 568-528 BC), after which Egypt re-emerged as a minor power under Persian rule.
