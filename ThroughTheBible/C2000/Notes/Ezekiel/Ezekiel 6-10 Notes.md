# Ezekiel 6-10 — Notes

## Overview

**Source:** C2000 Series — Pastor Chuck Smith  
**Book:** [[Ezekiel]]  
**Chapters:** 6-10  
**Transcript:** [[Jeremiah 6-10 Transcript.md]]

## Summary

Ezekiel addresses a prophecy against the **mountains of Israel** (chapter 6), where the people had built high places and altars to [[Baal]], [[Molech]], and [[Mammon]] instead of worshipping [[Jehovah]]. God declares He will destroy these high places, break their images, and scatter their bones around their altars. The phrase "**ye shall know that I am the Lord**" appears repeatedly — this refrain occurs 62 times in Ezekiel, emphasizing that God's judgments serve to reveal His identity to His people and the nations. Yet even in judgment, God declares He will leave a **remnant** — some who escape the sword among the nations.

Chapter 7 proclaims "the end has come" — the day of the Lord is near. The buyer shall not rejoice nor the seller mourn, for the land is given over to judgment. The time of buying and redemption (the go-el / kinsman-redeemer principle) has passed; the inheritance is lost.

In chapter 8, the Spirit transports Ezekiel to Jerusalem where he sees the **abominations** committed in the temple: the image of jealousy at the north gate, seventy elders of Israel burning incense to idols in a secret chamber, women weeping for [[Tammuz]], and twenty-five men worshipping the sun with their backs to the temple of the Lord. The glory of God progressively departs — from the cherubim to the threshold, then to the court, then to the mountain east of the city.

Chapter 9 shows the **marking of the faithful** — a man with a writer's inkhorn marks those who sigh and cry over the abominations, while the executioners slay everyone else. Ezekiel cries out, "Ah, Lord God! Will You destroy all the remnant of Israel?" (the "I Was Left" moment — Ezekiel 9:8). God answers that the sins of the people are so great that judgment must come, but the marked ones are spared.

Chapter 10 describes the **cherubim** and the **wheels** — identified explicitly as the living creatures Ezekiel saw in chapter 1. The glory of the Lord departs from the temple, moving to the threshold, then to the cherubim, and finally to the mountain east of Jerusalem (the [[Mount of Olives]]).

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

Chapters 6-10 of Ezekiel span prophecies delivered around **592-591 BC**, while Jerusalem was still standing but under Babylonian siege pressure. The people of Israel had filled the land with high places — mountain-top shrines dedicated to Canaanite fertility deities. After centuries of prophetic warnings, judgment was imminent. The vision of temple abominations (chapter 8) reveals the depth of syncretism that had corrupted Judah's worship: Mesopotamian (Tammuz), Egyptian (animal imagery), and astral (sun worship) cults had all infiltrated the temple precincts. The "image of jealousy" (Ezekiel 8:3) likely refers to an [[Asherah]] pole or idol set up in the temple courts — a direct violation of the first and second commandments.

The departure of the [[Shekinah Glory]] from the temple (chapters 9-10) is one of the most tragic moments in Scripture. The glory had filled the tabernacle at Sinai, filled Solomon's temple at its dedication, and now — because of persistent unfaithfulness — it departs. The phrase "I Was Left" (Ezekiel 9:8, T2000: [[_transcripts/I Was Left, Ezekiel 9_8.md|I Was Left]]) captures Ezekiel's anguished intercession for the remnant.
