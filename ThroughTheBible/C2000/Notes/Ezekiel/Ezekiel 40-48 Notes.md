# Ezekiel 40-48 — Notes

## Overview

**Source:** C2000 Series — Pastor Chuck Smith  
**Book:** [[Ezekiel]]  
**Chapters:** 40-48  
**Transcript:** [[Ezekiel 40-48 Notes.md]]

## Summary

Chapters 40-48 contain Ezekiel's vision of the **future temple** — the most challenging and debated passage in the book. Ezekiel confesses that of all the Bible, he understands this section the least, yet it is given for a purpose. The vision comes in the 25th year of Ezekiel's captivity (c. **573 BC**), 14 years after the fall of Jerusalem.

A man whose appearance was like [[brass|brass]] measures the temple with a measuring reed of six cubits (a cubit plus a span). The temple complex is a large square, 500 cubits each way. The outer wall has three gates on each side (east, north, south) with 30 chambers along the wall. The **east gate** is particularly significant — it is the gate through which the glory of the Lord will enter and it is to remain shut because the Lord has entered through it.

The **temple proper** consists of the porch, the sanctuary (holy place), and the most holy place, with side chambers on three levels. The **wall of separation** (Ezekiel 42:20) separates the holy from the profane — this is the subject of the T2000 teaching "Wall of Separation" (Ezekiel 42:20).

In chapter 43, the **glory of the Lord returns** — filling the temple as it had filled Solomon's temple at its dedication. The voice speaks: "Son of man, this is the place of My throne and the place of the soles of My feet, where I will dwell in the midst of the children of Israel forever." The altar is described and consecrated.

Chapter 44 describes the **prince** and the **priests** — only the sons of [[Zadok]] (who remained faithful when Israel went astray) may approach the Lord in ministry. Specific regulations for the priests' garments, conduct, and inheritance are given.

Chapter 45 details the **holy district** for the sanctuary, the city, and the prince, along with the system of offerings and sacrifices.

Chapter 46 describes the **worship regulations** for the prince and the people on Sabbaths and new moons.

Chapter 47 contains the vision of the **river of life** — water issuing from under the threshold of the temple, flowing eastward. It begins ankle-deep, then knee-deep, then waist-deep, and finally waters to swim in. The river flows down to the [[Arabah]] and into the [[Dead Sea]], **healing its waters** so they teem with fish. On both banks grow trees whose leaves are for healing — a direct parallel to [[Revelation 22_12.md]].

Chapter 48 describes the **division of the land** among the twelve tribes, with the sanctuary in the midst, and the city's gates named after the tribes — and the final words of the book: "**THE LORD IS THERE**" (Yahweh Shammah).

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

The vision of chapters 40-48 was given in **573 BC**, 14 years after Jerusalem's fall and approximately 25 years into Ezekiel's ministry. By this time, Ezekiel was about 55 years old and had been among the exiles for a generation. A new generation had grown up in Babylon who had never seen the temple. This vision gave them hope — not just of return, but of a glorious future where God would once again dwell among His people.

Interpretations of this temple vision vary widely:

- **Literal view**: A future temple to be built during the [[Millennial Kingdom]] after Christ's return, where sacrificial worship will be restored in some form as a memorial. This was Chuck Smith's position.
- **Spiritual/allegorical view**: The temple represents the church or the New Jerusalem in heaven.
- **Already-fulfilled view**: Describes the temple rebuilt after the exile (though the dimensions clearly exceed the Second Temple).

The river of life (Ezekiel 47) directly parallels the river in [[Revelation 22.md]] — one of the strongest connections between the Old and New Testaments. The climatic final words — "**The Lord is there**" (Yahweh Shammah) — are a fitting conclusion to a book that began with the departure of God's glory and ends with His glorious return.
