# Ezekiel 21-25 — Notes

## Overview

**Source:** C2000 Series — Pastor Chuck Smith  
**Book:** [[Ezekiel]]  
**Chapters:** 21-25  
**Transcript:** [[Jeremiah 23-25 Transcript.md]]

## Summary

Chapter 21 opens the "**song of the sword**" — a dramatic poetic passage where the sword of the Lord is drawn against Jerusalem. The prophets of God often performed colorful acts to draw attention, and here Ezekiel is commanded to **sigh with the breaking of his loins**, bitterly sighing before the people until they ask why, giving him the opening to declare that tidings are coming — every heart will melt. The sword is sharpened and polished, given to the slayer. The king of [[Nebuchadnezzar|Babylon]] stands at the fork of the road, using divination — shaking arrows, consulting household gods, looking at a liver — to decide whether to attack [[Jerusalem]] or [[Rabbath]] of the Ammonites. The lot falls on Jerusalem. Ezekiel laments the ruin of the kingdom: "I will overturn, overturn, overturn" — the turban/crown is removed until He comes whose right it is.

Chapter 22 catalogues the **sins of Jerusalem** in graphic detail: the princes are like devouring lions, the priests have violated the law, the prophets have daubed with untempered mortar, and the people practice extortion. God sought for a man to **stand in the gap** before Him for the land, that He should not destroy it, but **found none** (Ezekiel 22:30-31). Therefore God pours out His indignation upon them.

Chapter 23 presents the **allegory of two sisters**: [[Oholah]] ([[Samaria]], the northern kingdom) and [[Oholibah]] ([[Jerusalem]], the southern kingdom). Both played the harlot — Oholah with [[Assyria]], Oholibah even more flagrantly with [[Babylon]]. The sisters represent the northern and southern kingdoms' pursuit of political alliances with pagan nations rather than trusting God. Their judgment is detailed in vivid language.

Chapter 24 records the **parable of the boiling pot** — a pot set on the fire with choice pieces of meat, representing Jerusalem under siege, and the scum of the pot representing the city's filthiness. Then Ezekiel's wife dies — "the desire of your eyes" — yet God commands Ezekiel not to mourn openly, as a sign to Israel that they too will lose the desire of their eyes (the temple) without being able to mourn.

Chapter 25 begins the cycle of **judgments against the surrounding nations**: [[Ammon]], [[Moab]], [[Edom]], and the [[Philistines]] — because they rejoiced in Israel's downfall and acted vengefully against God's people.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

These prophecies date to approximately **589-588 BC**, as the final Babylonian siege of Jerusalem was beginning. Chapter 21's vivid description of Nebuchadnezzar using divination (belomancy — shaking arrows; hepatoscopy — examining livers) reflects actual Babylonian military practices documented in ancient Near Eastern texts. The fateful decision to attack Jerusalem rather than Rabbath determined the course of ancient history.

The cry "**I sought for a man to stand in the gap**" (Ezekiel 22:30-31) is one of Scripture's most poignant verses — God wanted someone to intercede, but found no one. This stands in stark contrast to [[Abraham]], who interceded for Sodom ([[Genesis 18.md]]), and [[Moses]], who stood in the breach for Israel ([[Psalm 106_23.md]]). The ultimate "man in the gap" is [[Jesus Christ]], who intercedes for us ([[Romans 8_3]]; [[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 7_25.md]]).

The death of Ezekiel's wife (chapter 24) is one of the most personal and tragic moments in the prophetic books. God took the "desire of Ezekiel's eyes" as a sign that the temple — the desire of Israel's eyes — would be destroyed. Ezekiel's obedience in not mourning publicly demonstrates the cost of prophetic ministry.
