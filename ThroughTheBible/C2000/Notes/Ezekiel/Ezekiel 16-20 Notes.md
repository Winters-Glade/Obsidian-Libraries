# Ezekiel 16-20 — Notes

## Overview

**Source:** C2000 Series — Pastor Chuck Smith  
**Book:** [[Ezekiel]]  
**Chapters:** 16-20  
**Transcript:** [[Jeremiah 16-20 Transcript.md]]

## Summary

Chapter 16 contains one of the most powerful allegories in Scripture — the story of **Jerusalem as an unfaithful wife**. God commands Ezekiel to "cause Jerusalem to know her abominations." The city's origin is traced to the land of Canaan — her father an [[Amorite]], her mother a [[Hittite]]. At birth she was unwanted: her navel was not cut, she was not washed, salted, or swaddled, but cast out into the open field. God passed by, saw her, and said "live." When she grew to maturity, God spread His skirt over her, entered into covenant with her, and she became His. He adorned her with jewels, fine linen, and the finest food — and she became **perfectly beautiful** (Ezekiel 16:1-14). But Jerusalem trusted in her own beauty and played the harlot, lavishing God's gifts on every passing idol. She even sacrificed her own children to [[Molech]]. God declares He will judge her as an adulteress, yet the chapter ends with a promise of restoration and an everlasting covenant.

Chapter 17 presents the **parable of the two eagles**: a great eagle ([[Nebuchadnezzar]]) came to Lebanon (Jerusalem), took the top of the cedar (the king and nobles), and planted the seed of the land (Zedekiah) in Babylon. A second eagle ([[Pharaoh]] of Egypt) caused the vine to turn toward it, violating the covenant with Babylon. God declares that the vine will be uprooted for breaking the oath — a direct warning against Zedekiah's rebellion.

Chapter 18 delivers the great treatise on **individual responsibility**. God refutes the proverb "The fathers eat sour grapes, and the children's teeth are set on edge." Each person is judged for their own sin: the righteous father who sins not shall live; the wicked son of a righteous father shall die; and the wicked man who turns from his sins shall live. God takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that they turn from their ways and live.

Chapter 19 is a **lamentation** for the princes of Israel — a lioness (the nation) whose cubs (the kings) were taken to Egypt and Babylon.

Chapter 20 recounts the **history of Israel's rebellion** from Egypt through the wilderness to the land — despite God's Sabbaths, statutes, and judgments, the people continually rebelled, yet God withheld His wrath for His name's sake.

## 🏛️ Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

This section of Ezekiel spans prophecies from approximately **592-591 BC**. The allegory of the unfaithful wife (chapter 16) draws on ancient Near Eastern marriage customs — the practice of salting newborns as an antiseptic, the legal rights of a husband over an adulterous wife, and the covenant marriage metaphor that [[Hosea]], [[Jeremiah]], and [[Isaiah]] also employed. The reference to Jerusalem's Canaanite ancestry emphasizes that the city itself was originally a Jebusite stronghold (Joshua 15:63; 2 Samuel 5:6-9) — God's choice of Jerusalem was pure grace, not based on any inherent merit.

The parable of the two eagles (chapter 17) reflects the geopolitical struggle between Babylon and Egypt for control of Judah. Zedekiah had sworn allegiance to Nebuchadnezzar (a covenant made in God's name, 2 Chronicles 36:13), but sought military alliance with Egypt. Ezekiel insists that breaking an oath — even to a pagan king — is a sin against God. This historical event is corroborated by Egyptian records of Pharaoh Hopfrey (Apries), who briefly campaigned in Palestine in 589 BC.

Chapter 18's teaching on individual responsibility marks a significant development in biblical theology, moving beyond the corporate understanding of sin and punishment that underlay earlier judgments (like Achan's sin in Joshua 7). This principle is reaffirmed in the New Testament ([[Romans 2_6]]; [[2 Corinthians 5_10.md]]).
