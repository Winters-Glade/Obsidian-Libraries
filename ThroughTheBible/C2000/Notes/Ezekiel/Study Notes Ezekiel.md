## Historical Context
These prophecies were delivered during the [[Babylonian Exile]] (605-536 BC). Ezekiel spoke to the captives in Babylon, while Daniel served in the royal court of Babylon. Both addressed God's sovereignty over history and the future restoration of Israel.

## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Ezekiel, chapters 1-48.

**Ezekiel:** The book of Ezekiel contains some of the most vivid visions in all of Scripture. Ezekiel, both a priest and a prophet, was called to minister to the Jewish exiles in Babylon. The first half of the book (chapters 1-24) warns of Jerusalem's coming judgment as God's glory departs the temple. The middle section (chapters 25-32) pronounces judgment on surrounding nations. The final section (chapters 33-48) shifts to hope and restoration: the valley of dry bones, the defeat of Gog and Magog, and the vision of a restored temple with the river of life flowing from it.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

The Book of Ezekiel is set against the backdrop of the [[Babylonian Exile]] — the most traumatic event in Israel's pre-Christian history. Babylonian records, including the **Babylonian Chronicle** (BM 21946, now in the British Museum), confirm Nebuchadnezzar's siege of Jerusalem in 597 BC and the subsequent deportations. The **Nebuchadnezzar Cylinder** describes the king's building projects and military campaigns, corroborating the biblical account of Babylon's imperial reach.

The **Lachish Letters** (ostraca discovered at Tel Lachish from the 1930s onward) date to the final days of Judah before Nebuchadnezzar's destruction in 586 BC. These inscribed pottery fragments show Hebrew script contemporary with Ezekiel's time, providing a vivid window into the panic and desperation of the final years of the Judean monarchy.

The **Cyrus Cylinder** (discovered 1879, now in the British Museum) records Cyrus the Great's policy of repatriating exiled peoples — the very policy that ended the Babylonian captivity and made possible the return prophesied by Ezekiel and others. While Ezekiel himself did not live to see the return, his prophecies of restoration (Ezekiel 36-37) find their historical fulfillment in the Persian period.

The **Dead Sea Scrolls** contain multiple copies of Ezekiel (including a notable scroll from Cave 4 at Qumran, 4QEzek), confirming the careful transmission of the text. The Qumran community studied Ezekiel's prophecies intently, particularly his visions of the temple and the New Jerusalem, which they interpreted eschatologically.

## Eschatological Significance

Ezekiel's prophecies are foundational to biblical eschatology. Among Chuck Smith's teachings, several themes receive particular emphasis:

- **The Valley of Dry Bones** (Ezekiel 37): Seen as a prophecy of Israel's national restoration — both physical (the return to the land) and spiritual (national revival). In Chuck Smith's eschatology, this chapter is tied to the modern regathering of Israel.
- **Gog and Magog** (Ezekiel 38-39): Interpreted as a future invasion by a northern coalition (often identified with Russia and allied nations) that God will supernaturally destroy, leading the nations to know that He is the Lord. This forms a major component of the prophetic timeline taught by Chuck Smith.
- **Ezekiel's Temple** (Ezekiel 40-48): The vision of a future temple with sacrificial worship. Interpretations vary widely — literal Jewish millennial temple, symbolic representation of the church, or a vision of God's ultimate dwelling with His people. Chuck Smith taught a literal future temple during the millennial reign of Christ.
- **The River of Life** (Ezekiel 47): A direct parallel to [[Revelation 22.md]], connecting Ezekiel's closing vision to the final vision of the New Testament.

Key study notes: Ezekiel's "watchman" imagery (Ezekiel 33:1-9) is cited by Chuck Smith as the role of the prophet and teacher — to warn the people.

## New Testament Connections

- **Ezekiel 11:19** / [[_Bible/Ezekiel/Ezekiel 36_26-27.md]] — The promise of a new heart and new spirit. This is echoed by Jesus in [[John 3_5-8]] (being born of the Spirit) and by Paul in [[2 Corinthians 3_3.md]] (the Spirit writes on hearts of flesh).
- **Ezekiel 34** — The Shepherd imagery. Jesus declares Himself the [[Good Shepherd]] in [[John 10]], directly echoing Ezekiel's prophecy against false shepherds and God's promise to shepherd His flock Himself.
- **Ezekiel 37** — The dry bones resurrection is referenced by Paul in [[1 Corinthians 15.md]] in his discussion of the resurrection body.
- **Ezekiel 47** — The River of Life flows directly into [[Revelation 22_12.md]], where John sees the river of the water of life flowing from the throne of God and the Lamb.

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Ezekiel and [[Daniel]]** — Both prophesied during the Babylonian exile. Ezekiel ministered to the captives by the river Chebar; Daniel served in the royal court. Their messages are complementary: Ezekiel reveals God's glory departing and returning to the temple; Daniel reveals God's sovereignty over the kingdoms of men. Both speak of the end times (Ezekiel 38-39; Daniel 7-12).
- **Ezekiel and [[Isaiah]]** — Ezekiel 47's river of life echoes [[Isaiah 11.md]] where the Spirit of the Lord rests on the Branch, and [[Isaiah 35.md]] where waters break forth in the wilderness.
- **Ezekiel and [[Zechariah]]** — Zechariah picks up Ezekiel's temple and kingly themes. Both see a future cleansing fountain (Ezekiel 36:25; Zechariah 13:1).
- **Ezekiel and [[Revelation 19]]** — The parallels between Ezekiel and the Book of Revelation are extensive: four living creatures (Ezekiel 1; Revelation 4), the measuring of the temple (Ezekiel 40-42; Revelation 11), Gog and Magog (Ezekiel 38-39; Revelation 20), and the river of life (Ezekiel 47; Revelation 22).
