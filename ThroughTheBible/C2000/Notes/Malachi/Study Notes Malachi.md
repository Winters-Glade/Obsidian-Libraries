## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Malachi, chapters 1-4.

**Malachi:** The last book of the Old Testament. Malachi addresses spiritual apathy and corruption in post-exilic Judah. He confronts the people about their contempt for God's table, their divorce practices, and their robbing God in tithes and offerings. The book ends with the promise of Elijah's coming before the great and terrible Day of the Lord - bridging to the New Testament.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Malachi is the last of the post-exilic prophets, ministering in the mid-to-late 5th century BC (c. 450–430 BC), roughly a generation after Haggai and Zechariah:

- The **Elephantine Papyri** (c. 495–399 BC) provide extensive background on Jewish religious life in the Persian period. They mention the temple at Elephantine, references to the Jerusalem temple authorities, and names of high priests — helping confirm the historical context of Malachi's complaints about corrupt priesthood and mixed marriages.
- Malachi's criticisms of corrupt sacrifices and tithes reflect conditions also addressed by Nehemiah (Nehemiah 13), suggesting the post-exilic community struggled with spiritual decline over multiple generations. The **Nehemiah Memoir** in the Bible describes the same problems — priests defiling the temple, tithing neglected, Sabbath broken, and mixed marriages.
- Archaeological excavations in **Judah** during the Persian period show a small, impoverished province — the "returned remnant" was a fraction of the pre-exilic population. This explains the economic hardships and spiritual discouragement behind Malachi's message.
- The **Mesad Hashavyahu Ostracon** (late 7th century BC), though earlier than Malachi, shows that disputes about wages and social justice (cf. Malachi 3:5) were long-standing issues in Israelite society.

## Eschatological Significance

- **Malachi 3:1** — "The messenger of the covenant" who will suddenly come to His temple — seen as John the Baptist preparing the way for the Messiah (first coming) and possibly as Elijah's return (Malachi 4:5-6) before the second coming.
- **Malachi 4:1-3** — "The day is burning like an oven" — the judgment of the proud and evildoers on the Day of the Lord, contrasted with the "sun of righteousness" rising with healing for those who fear God. Chuck Smith taught this as the contrast between the tribulation/judgment and the millennial kingdom, when Christ the Sun of Righteousness brings healing to the nations.
- **Malachi 4:5-6** — The promise of Elijah — "I will send you Elijah the prophet before the great and terrible day of the Lord comes" — this bridges the Old and New Testaments. In Chuck Smith's teaching, this has a dual fulfillment: John the Baptist (who came "in the spirit and power of Elijah") at Christ's first coming, and potentially a literal Elijah before Christ's second coming (consistent with the dispensational interpretation of Revelation 11's two witnesses).

## New Testament Connections

- **Malachi 3:1** — "I will send my messenger, who will prepare the way before me" — quoted by Jesus in [[Matthew 11_10.md]] and [[Luke 7_27]] as referring to John the Baptist. Mark begins his Gospel with this quotation ([[Mark 1_2]]), establishing Malachi as a bridge to the NT.
- **Malachi 3:1** (continued) — "The Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to his temple" — referenced in [[Matthew 21_12.md]] (Jesus cleansing the temple) and [[Malachi 3_2-3.md]] as the refiner's fire, which is echoed by [[1 Corinthians 3_13.md]] about testing by fire.
- **Malachi 3:10** — "Bring the whole tithe into the storehouse" — this passage on tithing is the primary OT text used in discussions of stewardship and giving in the NT (cf. [[1 Corinthians 16_2.md]]; [[2 Corinthians 9_6-8.md]]).
- **Malachi 4:5** — Elijah's coming — fulfilled in John the Baptist ([[Matthew 11_14.md]]; [[17:10-13]]), yet interpreted by many as having a future reference to one of the two witnesses in [[Revelation 11.md]].

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Malachi and [[Haggai]]** — Both address post-exilic spiritual decline. Haggai focused on rebuilding the temple; Malachi, a generation later, focuses on the quality of worship within the rebuilt temple.
- **Malachi and [[Zechariah]]** — Zechariah's visions of the coming King and Priest find their complement in Malachi's call for faithful waiting. Zechariah's promise of a cleansing fountain (Zechariah 13:1) sets up Malachi's "sun of righteousness" with healing in its wings.
- **Malachi and [[Joel]]** — Both end with the [[Day of the Lord]] and the promise of the Spirit. Joel foresees the outpouring of the Spirit; Malachi promises Elijah's coming before the Day.
- **Malachi and [[John the Baptist]]** — As the last prophetic voice before 400 years of silence, Malachi directly sets the stage for John the Baptist, the final OT-style prophet before Christ.
