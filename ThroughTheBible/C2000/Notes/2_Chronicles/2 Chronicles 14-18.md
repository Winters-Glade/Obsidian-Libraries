## Overview

In [[2 Chronicles 14.md]], we find the death of Abiyah listed and the son [[Asa]] coming to the throne. And Asa's son reigned in his stead. The land was quiet for ten years. [[Asa]] did that which was good and right in the eyes of the Lord his God. He took away the altars of the strange God, the high places. He broke down the images and cut down the groves. And he commanded Judah to seek the Lord, God of their fathers, and to do the law and the commandment. Now, you remember under the reign of [[Rehoboam]], they began to, he turned away from God. And the people turned away from God also, began to worship these other gods. Began to plant these groves as places to worship God. Build the altars under these false gods and so forth. And so now, Abiyah allowed these things to exist. And there was a commingling. During his reign, God was still worshipped in [[Jerusalem]]. But yet, there were people who were allowed to worship these other gods in these other ways. Now, when [[Asa]] came to the throne, he established a spiritual reform. He got rid of all of the idols and the images. He got rid of the altars that had been erected to these other gods. He cut down the groves. This section covers the reigns of [[Asa]] (ch. 14-16) and [[Jehoshaphat]] (ch. 17-18). Asa's righteous reforms and his cry to God for help against the Cushite army (ch. 14), the prophetic encouragement from Azariah and Asa's further reforms (ch. 15), Asa's later foolish alliance with [[Ben-Hadad]] of Syria and his failure to trust God in his sickness (ch. 16). Then [[Jehoshaphat]]'s righteous reign, his strengthening of the kingdom and teaching of the Law (ch. 17), and his tragic alliance with the wicked [[Ahab]] of Israel leading to the battle at [[Ramoth-Gilead]] (ch. 18). The Chronicler presents the cycle of faithfulness rewarded and unfaithfulness judged, emphasizing the need to trust God rather than foreign alliances.

## Key Themes

- **Reform and Revival**: [[Asa]] and [[Jehoshaphat]]'s religious reforms — removing idolatry and restoring true [[Worship|worship]] of God ([[2 Chronicles 14_2-5.md]], [[2 Chronicles 17_3-6.md]])
- **Trusting God in Battle**: Asa's prayer for help against the Cushites ([[2 Chronicles 14_11.md]]) and Jehoshaphat's preparation for war — God fights for those who trust Him
- **The Danger of Unholy Alliances**: Both kings made unwise alliances — Asa with [[Ben-Hadad]] of [[Syria]] ([[2 Chronicles 16_7-9.md]]) and Jehoshaphat with [[Ahab]] of Israel ([[2 Chronicles 18.md]]) — showing the Chronicler's concern with trusting God rather than political/military alliances
- **Prophetic Preaching**: The role of prophets like [[Azariah]] (ch. 15), [[Hanani]] (ch. 16), and [[Micaiah]] (ch. 18) in calling kings to faithfulness
- **The Cycle of Sin and Repentance**: Asa's decline from faithful reformer to one who relies on human strength illustrates the Chronicler's theology of immediate consequences for sin

## Connections

- **1 Kings 15-22 (Parallel Account)**: The Chronicler adds unique material — the battle against the Cushites (14:9-15), Azariah's prophecy (ch. 15), and Jehoshaphat's teaching mission (17:7-9) — emphasizing the blessings of faithfulness and the role of prophetic preaching
- **Asa's Prayer (14:11)**: Asa's cry, "Lord, it is nothing with You to help, whether with many or with those who have no power," echoes throughout Scripture as a model of faith — connecting to [[1 Samuel 14_6.md]], [[Psalm 20_7]], and [[_Bible/Hebrews/Hebrews 11_32-34.md]]
- **Jehoshaphat and Ahab (ch. 18)**: The Chronicler uses this alliance to illustrate the danger of "friendship with the world" (cf. [[James 4_4]]), showing that even a righteous king can be led astray by ungodly associations
- **Teaching the Law (17:7-9)**: Jehoshaphat's commission of princes, Levites, and priests to teach the Law throughout Judah is unique to Chronicles and prefigures the post-[[Exile|exile]] emphasis on Torah instruction (cf. [[Nehemiah 8.md]])
- **The Cushite Invasion (14:9-15)**: The mention of Zerah the Cushite (possibly Ethiopian) connects to international context, showing God's sovereignty over the nations
- **Asa's Unfaithfulness (ch. 16)**: Asa's reliance on [[Ben-Hadad]] rather than God (16:7-9) is condemned by the prophet [[Hanani]], illustrating the Chronicler's theme that human alliances cannot substitute for trust in God

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

The book of **2 Chronicles** was written post-exile (ca. 450–400 BC) as the second part of the Chronicler's history (originally one work with 1 Chronicles). The Chronicler writes from a priestly-Levitical perspective, emphasizing the Davidic covenant, the Temple, and proper worship.

- **2 Chronicles 14–18** covers the reigns of **Asa** and **Jehoshaphat**, two kings who pursued religious **reform and reliance on God**. Asa removed the foreign altars and high places (ch. 14) and defeated the Cushite army through prayer (14:9–15). Jehoshaphat strengthened the kingdom, sent out teachers of the Law (17:7–9), and famously cried out to the Lord when surrounded by enemies (ch. 20). Yet both kings made unwise alliances: Asa with Ben-Hadad of Syria (ch. 16) and Jehoshaphat with Ahab of Israel (ch. 18), showing the Chronicler's concern with trusting God rather than foreign powers.
- The Chronicler's unique contributions include: the prominence of the **Levites** in worship and instruction, the pattern of **prophetic preaching** (Azariah in ch. 15, Hanani in ch. 16, Jehu in ch. 19, Jahaziel in ch. 20), and the theological cycle of **sin → judgment → repentance → restoration**.
- **Archaeology**: The **Siloam Tunnel inscription** (Hezekiah's Tunnel), the **Broad Wall** in Jerusalem (Hezekiah's fortification), and **LMLK jar handles** (royal seal impressions from Hezekiah's reign) all provide extrabiblical confirmation of the period covered in 2 Chronicles.
- Unlike the Deuteronomistic History (Joshua–Kings), Chronicles emphasizes **immediate divine retribution** for sin and highlights the **centrality of the Temple** as the place where God's presence dwells with His people.
