## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Habakkuk, chapters 1-3.

**Habakkuk:** Habakkuk's unique structure is a dialogue between the prophet and God. Habakkuk questions why God allows injustice and why He would use wicked Babylon to judge His people. God's answer - ''the just shall live by his faith'' - becomes a foundational verse for the New Testament teaching on justification by faith. The book concludes with Habakkuk's magnificent psalm of trust.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Habakkuk prophesied in the late 7th century BC (c. 609–598 BC), during the rise of the Neo-Babylonian Empire under Nabopolassar and Nebuchadnezzar:

- The **Babylonian Chronicles** document the rise of Babylon from a vassal state to the dominant power in the Near East. The Battle of Carchemish (605 BC), in which Nebuchadnezzar defeated the Egyptian army at what is now the Turkish-Syrian border, established Babylon as the supreme power — fulfilling Habakkuk's prophecy of the Chaldeans as "bitter and hasty" (Habakkuk 1:6).
- The **Nebuchadnezzar Cylinder** and the **East India House Inscription** describe Babylon's wealth and military might, confirming the accuracy of Habakkuk's depiction of Babylon as a proud and ruthless empire.
- The **Dead Sea Scrolls** include the **Habakkuk Pesher** (1QpHab), a unique commentary from the Qumran community that interprets Habakkuk's prophecy in light of the community's own struggles with the "Kittim" (likely the Romans). This scroll is one of the most important discoveries at Qumran, demonstrating how Jewish readers applied Habakkuk's oracles to their own time.

## New Testament Connections

- **Habakkuk 2:4** — "The just shall live by his faith" — this single verse is arguably the most theologically significant Old Testament quotation in the New Testament. It is cited in three critical passages:
  - [[Romans 1_17]] — Paul uses it to establish the doctrine of justification by faith as the foundation of the gospel.
  - [[Galatians 3_11.md]] — Paul uses it to argue that the law cannot justify, since the righteous live by faith, not by works of the law.
  - [[Hebrews 10_38.md]] — The writer uses it to contrast faith with shrinking back, placing it in a context of persevering faith.
- This makes Habakkuk 2:4 the scriptural bedrock for the Reformation doctrine of sola fide (faith alone) — a truth Chuck Smith regularly emphasized in his teaching.

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Habakkuk and [[Isaiah]]** — Both express oracles against Babylon (Isaiah 13-14; 21; 47) and both describe God's sovereignty over the nations who serve as instruments of His judgment.
- **Habakkuk and [[Jeremiah]]** — Contemporaries in the final years of Judah. Both warned of the Babylonian threat. Jeremiah counseled submission to Babylon; Habakkuk wrestled with why God would use a wicked nation to judge His people.
- **Habakkuk and [[Ezekiel]]** — Ezekiel 18 develops the principle of individual responsibility, which Habakkuk's "the just shall live by his faith" complements by emphasizing personal trust in God.
- **Habakkuk and [[Paul]]** — While not a prophet, Paul's use of Habakkuk 2:4 in Romans and Galatians makes Habakkuk the most theologically significant of the Minor Prophets in terms of New Testament doctrine.
