## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Amos, chapters 1-5.

**Amos:** Amos, a shepherd and fruit picker from Tekoa, was sent to the Northern Kingdom with a message of social justice. He denounces the wealthy elite who oppress the poor while maintaining religious observance. His famous call - ''Let justice roll down like waters'' - and the vision of the plumb line measure Israel against God's standard. Yet even in judgment, Amos sees a remnant restored.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Amos prophesied during the reign of Jeroboam II of Israel (c. 786–746 BC) and Uzziah of Judah — a time of great prosperity but spiritual decline:

- The **Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III** and later Assyrian annals document Assyria's growing power, which Amos warns is the instrument of God's judgment against Israel. Though Assyria was not yet at its peak in Amos's day, the threat was already discernible.
- The **Tel Dan Stele** (9th century BC) confirms the existence of the "House of David" and the divided kingdom context in which both Amos and his contemporary Hosea ministered.
- The **Samaria Ostraca** (8th century BC) record transactions of wine and oil shipments, evidencing the luxury economy that Amos condemns — the wealthy elite living in houses of ivory (Amos 3:15), drinking wine from bowls (Amos 6:6), and lounging on beds of ivory (Amos 6:4).
- The **Sargon II Prism** (c. 712 BC) documents the Assyrian conquest of Samaria that fulfilled Amos's warnings — roughly 30 years after the prophet's ministry.

## New Testament Connections

- **Amos 5:24** — "Let justice roll down like waters" — while not directly quoted in the NT, this verse underlies the Christian call for justice and righteousness. James's emphasis on caring for the poor ([[James 2_1-9]]) and John the Baptist's call for economic justice ([[Luke 3_10-14.md]]) echo Amos's social concern.
- **Amos 5:18-20** — The Day of the Lord as darkness — Paul uses similar imagery in [[1 Thessalonians 5_2-3.md]] when describing the suddenness of the Lord's return: "the day of the Lord comes like a thief in the night."
- **Amos 9:11-12** — "I will restore the fallen tent of David" — quoted by James at the Jerusalem Council ([[Acts 15_16-17.md]]) to argue that God's plan includes the Gentiles. This is one of the most significant OT citations in the NT, used to resolve the Gentile inclusion debate.

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Amos and [[Hosea]]** — Contemporaries ministering to the Northern Kingdom. Both use [[Covenant Lawsuit]] language. Amos focuses on social justice; Hosea emphasizes God's marital love for Israel.
- **Amos and [[Isaiah]]** — Both prophesied during the reigns of Uzziah (Amos 1:1; Isaiah 6:1). Both call for justice and righteousness as the proper response to God's covenant.
- **Amos and [[Micah]]** — Both denounce social injustice among the wealthy and call for ethical religion. Micah 6:8 ("do justice, love mercy, walk humbly") is a powerful summary of Amos's message.
- **Amos and [[Zephaniah]]** — Both develop the [[Day of the Lord]] theme, emphasizing its dark and terrible aspects.
