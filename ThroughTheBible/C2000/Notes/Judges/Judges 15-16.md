## Key Themes

- **[[Samson]]'s Nazirite Vow**: Samson was set apart from birth, yet consistently compromised his calling ([[Judges 13_5]]; [[Judges 16_17.md]])
- **The Riddle and the Betrayal**: Samson's Philistine wife betrays him by coaxing the answer to his riddle ([[Judges 14_12-18.md]])
- **Samson's Vengeance**: Samson burns Philistine fields and slaughters his enemies with supernatural strength ([[Judges 15_1-8.md]])
- **Jawbone of a Donkey**: Samson slays 1,000 Philistines with the jawbone of a donkey at [[Lehi (Ramath-lehi).md]] ([[Judges 15_9-20.md]])
- **Delilah's Betrayal**: Samson's love for Delilah leads to the revelation of his secret and his capture ([[Judges 16_4-21.md]])
- **The Loss of Strength**: Samson's Nazirite vow is violated; his hair is cut and the Lord departs from him ([[Judges 16_19-20.md]])
- **Samson's Final Act**: In death, Samson destroys more Philistines than in his life, pulling down the temple of [[Dagon]] ([[Judges 16_23-31.md]])
- **God's Sovereignty**: Despite Samson's personal failings, God uses him to deliver Israel from Philistine oppression

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

The events of Judges 15-16 — the Samson cycle — take place during the Iron Age I period (ca. 1200-1020 BC), late in the era of the judges. Samson's conflicts with the Philistines reflect a historical reality: the Philistines were among the "Sea Peoples" who migrated to the Canaanite coastal plain in the early 12th century BC, establishing a pentapolis (Gaza, Ashdod, Ashkelon, Gath, Ekron) that dominated the region. Their monopoly on iron-smithing technology (1 Samuel 13:19-22) gave them a military advantage over the Israelites.

Key archaeological discoveries illuminating this period include:

- **The Merneptah Stele** (ca. 1208 BC): Discovered by Flinders Petrie in 1896 at Thebes, this Egyptian victory stele contains the earliest known extra-biblical reference to "Israel," depicting them as a people group already settled in Canaan during the reign of Pharaoh Merneptah. This confirms that Israel was present in the land by the late 13th century BC, consistent with the biblical timeline of the Judges period.

- **Destruction Layers at Hazor, Megiddo, and Lachish**: Archaeological excavations have uncovered destruction layers at these major Canaanite cities dating to the late Bronze Age / early Iron Age transition, consistent with Israelite military campaigns described in Joshua and Judges.

- **The Bull Site** (Mt. Ebal): Discovered in the early 1980s, this open-air cultic site on Mt. Ebal dates to the Iron Age I period and is associated with early Israelite worship. An enclosure with a stone structure interpreted as an altar, along with burned animal bones, aligns with the description of Joshua building an altar on Mt. Ebal (Joshua 8:30-35). It reflects the decentralized religious practices that characterized the period of the Judges.

The book of Judges presents a recurring cyclical pattern that drives the narrative:

> **Sin** → **Oppression** → **Cry for Deliverance** → **Deliverer Raised Up** → **Peace** → **Sin Again**

This cycle appears repeatedly throughout the book. In Samson's case, the pattern is complicated by Samson's personal failings — his Nazirite vows are compromised, his marriages to Philistine women entangle him with the enemy, and his strength is ultimately lost through Delilah's betrayal. Yet God still uses Samson's final act to strike a blow against Philistine oppression.

The political vacuum between Joshua's death (Joshua 24:29-31) and the monarchy created conditions where charismatic, ad-hoc leaders — the judges — arose to meet specific crises. These were primarily tribal or regional leaders rather than national rulers, reflecting the decentralized nature of pre-monarchic Israel.
