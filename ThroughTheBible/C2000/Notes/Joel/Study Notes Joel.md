## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Joel, chapters 1-3.

**Joel:** Joel uses a devastating locust plague as a metaphor for the coming ''Day of the Lord.'' He calls for national repentance with fasting and prayer. The book is best known for the prophecy quoted at Pentecost: ''I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh.'' Joel's message moves from immediate judgment to the promise of restoration and the final judgment of the nations.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Joel's prophecy is notoriously difficult to date — estimates range from the 9th to the 4th century BC. The book mentions no specific king or known historical event, and scholars debate whether it is pre-exilic or post-exilic:

- The reference to the "Greeks" (Joel 3:6) trading slaves has led some to suggest a post-exilic date (after the Greek ascendancy in the 4th century), though Greeks were known in the east as early as the 8th century BC through Ionian trade.
- The mention of the temple functioning (Joel 1:9, 2:17) suggests a time when the temple was standing — narrowing it to either the pre-exilic First Temple or the post-exilic Second Temple period.
- Locust plagues were (and remain) a recurring natural disaster in the Middle East. The **Sennacherib Prism** and other Assyrian annals record locust infestations as divine judgments, showing that Joel's imagery would have been immediately understood in its ancient context.
- The **Dead Sea Scrolls** include copies of Joel among the Twelve Minor Prophets (4QXII<sup>a</sup>, MurXII), confirming its place in the collection of the Twelve.

## Eschatological Significance

Joel's "Day of the Lord" theology is foundational to biblical eschatology:

- **The Day of the Lord** — Joel describes it as a day of darkness, gloominess, and divine judgment, but also as the occasion of salvation for those who call on the name of the Lord (Joel 2:32). This dual aspect (judgment and salvation) runs through all later prophetic treatments of the theme.
- **The Outpouring of the Spirit** (Joel 2:28-32) — Prophesies a future time when God's Spirit would be poured out on all people, with prophetic dreams and visions accompanying the event. This is the centerpiece of Joel's eschatological hope.
- **The Valley of Jehoshaphat** / Valley of Decision (Joel 3) — An image of the final judgment of the nations, a theme that reappears in Zechariah 14 and Revelation 16 at Armageddon.

Chuck Smith taught Joel's prophecy as having two fulfillments: (1) a partial fulfillment at Pentecost (Acts 2) — "this is that which was spoken by the prophet Joel" — and (2) a future, greater fulfillment in the last days associated with the tribulation period.

## New Testament Connections

- **Joel 2:28-32** — "I will pour out my Spirit" — the most quoted OT passage at Pentecost, cited by [[Acts 2_17-21.md]] as the explanation for the disciples speaking in tongues. Peter explicitly says "this is what was spoken through the prophet Joel."
- **Joel 2:32** — "Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved" — quoted by Paul in [[Romans 10_13.md]] to argue that salvation in Christ is available to all who call upon Him, Jew and Gentile alike.
- **Joel 3:2** — The gathering of nations to judgment — echoed in [[Revelation 16_14-16.md]] (the gathering at Armageddon) and [[Matthew 25_31-46.md]] (the judgment of the nations by the Son of Man).

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Joel and [[Amos]]** — Both use the [[Day of the Lord]] as a central theme. Amos warns that the Day of the Lord is darkness not light (Amos 5:18-20), while Joel emphasizes both judgment and the outpouring of the Spirit.
- **Joel and [[Ezekiel]]** — Joel's imagery of judgment against the nations (Joel 3) parallels Ezekiel's oracles against the nations (Ezekiel 25-32), especially Tyre and Edom.
- **Joel and [[Zephaniah]]** — Both use the Day of the Lord as their organizing framework. Zephaniah expands on the cosmic dimensions of the Day.
- **Joel and [[Malachi]]** — Malachi promises Elijah's return "before the great and terrible day of the Lord" (Malachi 4:5), directly echoing Joel's language.
