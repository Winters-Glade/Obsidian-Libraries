## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Nahum, chapters 1-3.

**Nahum:** Nahum's prophecy is a poetic declaration of Nineveh's coming destruction. A century after Jonah's preaching brought repentance, Nineveh has returned to violence and oppression. Nahum describes God as slow to anger and great in power, and assures Judah that their oppressor will fall.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

Nahum prophesied against Nineveh sometime between the fall of Thebes (No-Amon, Nahum 3:8) in 663 BC and the destruction of Nineveh in 612 BC:

- **Nineveh's destruction** in 612 BC is extensively documented in non-biblical sources. The **Fall of Nineveh Chronicle** (BM 21901) describes a coalition of Medes, Babylonians, and others besieging and destroying the city. The **Nabopolassar Chronicle** confirms that the Assyrian capital fell to the combined forces, exactly as Nahum prophesied.
- The city was so thoroughly devastated that it became proverbial — when Alexander the Great marched past its ruins two centuries later, it lay uninhabited and unrecognizable. The Greek historian **Xenophon** (c. 400 BC) marched past Nineveh with the Ten Thousand and described it as an abandoned ruin, fulfilling Nahum's prophecy of desolation.
- Archaeological excavations of Nineveh (by Layard and others in the 19th century) revealed the massive scale of the city — walls 12 miles in circumference, the Palace of Sennacherib with its reliefs and inscriptions, and the vast Library of Ashurbanipal. The level of destruction found in the 612 BC layer confirms the thoroughness of the city's fall.
- The **Sennacherib Prism** describes Nineveh's water supply system, including canals and aqueducts that Sennacherib built — the very city infrastructure that Nahum describes being destroyed when the river gates are opened (Nahum 2:6).

## New Testament Connections

- **Nahum 1:7** — "The Lord is good, a stronghold in the day of trouble" — while not directly quoted, this verse resonates with [[Hebrews 6_18.md]] (God as our refuge and strength) and [[2 Thessalonians 1_6-7.md]] (God's justice in repaying affliction to those who trouble His people).
- **Nahum 1:3** — "The Lord is slow to anger and great in power" — this self-description of God (echoed from Exodus 34:6-7) is foundational for understanding God's character in both Testaments, and is cited by Peter in [[2 Peter 3_9]] (God's patience in delaying judgment).

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Nahum and [[Jonah]]** — The two books form a theological diptych on Nineveh. In Jonah (c. 760 BC), God shows mercy when Nineveh repents. In Nahum (c. 650–630 BC), God judges Nineveh for returning to its violent ways. Together they illustrate both aspects of God's character: "slow to anger" (Nahum 1:3) yet "will not leave the guilty unpunished" (Nahum 1:3).
- **Nahum and [[Isaiah]]** — Isaiah also prophesies against Assyria (Isaiah 10; 14:24-27), promising that God will break Assyria's power, which Nahum sees fulfilled.
- **Nahum and [[Zephaniah]]** — Both prophesy the downfall of Assyria and the surrounding nations, and both see this judgment as part of the [[Day of the Lord]].
