## Historical & Contextual Background

> [!QUESTION]- This enrichment needs QT verification

**The Elder:** Both letters are written by "the elder" (*ho presbyteros*)*.* Church tradition identifies this as John the Apostle, the aged leader of the churches in Asia Minor. Papias of Hierapolis (c. AD 130) distinguishes between John the Apostle and John the Presbyter, though Early Church writers typically identified both as the same figure.

**2 John — Warning Against False Teachers:** This brief letter (13 verses, the shortest New Testament book by verse count) addresses the "elect lady and her children" — likely a local church and its members. The warning against showing hospitality to false teachers (vv. 10–11) reflects the critical issue of itinerant teachers in early Christianity. The *Didache* (c. AD 100) gives similar instructions: test traveling teachers and do not support false prophets.

**3 John — Diotrephes & Church Authority:** This personal letter to Gaius deals with a leadership conflict: Diotrephes "loves to be first" and refuses to receive the elder's representatives, excommunicating those who do (vv. 9–10). This reveals early tensions between centralized apostolic authority emerging local church leadership. Demetrius is commended as a counterexample.

**Hospitality & Support:** The practice of supporting traveling missionaries with hospitality was essential to early Christian expansion (cf. Matthew 10:9–15; Romans 16:1–2; Hebrews 13:2). The Johannine letters show how critical hospitality disputes were to the health of the early church network.
