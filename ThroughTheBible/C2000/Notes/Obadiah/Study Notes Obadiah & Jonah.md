## Overview
This teaching from [[Pastor Chuck Smiths]] [[_HOME|C2000 Series]] covers Obadiah & Jonah, chapters 1-4.

**Obadiah:** The shortest book in the Old Testament. Obadiah pronounces judgment on Edom for their pride and their violence against their brother Jacob when Jerusalem was attacked. The book concludes with the promise that deliverance will come from Mount Zion and the kingdom will be the Lord's.

**Jonah:** Unlike other prophetic books, Jonah is primarily narrative. God sends Jonah to Nineveh, but he flees in the opposite direction. After being swallowed by a great fish, he reluctantly obeys. The Ninevites repent, and Jonah sulks at God's mercy. The book reveals God's heart for the nations and His compassion even toward Israel's enemies.

## Archaeological & Historical Background

**Obadiah:** The Book of Obadiah is the shortest in the Old Testament (21 verses) and notoriously difficult to date. It addresses the bitter conflict between Jacob (Israel) and Esau (Edom), descendants of the twin brothers. Edom was located in the mountainous region southeast of the Dead Sea (modern-day southern Jordan), known for its capital [[Petra]] (Sela) carved into the rock cliffs — referenced in Obadiah's taunt about dwelling "in the clefts of the rock" (Obadiah 1:3).

- Archaeological surveys at **Buseirah** (biblical Bozrah, the Edomite capital) and sites in the Negev have confirmed Edomite occupation from the 8th–6th centuries BC. Edom is also mentioned in Egyptian and Assyrian records.
- Obadiah's charge against Edom — that they stood aloof when Jerusalem fell and even looted the city — is consistent with what we know of Edomite opportunism. The **Babylonian Chronicles** describe the fall of Jerusalem in 597 and 586 BC, events in which Edom was implicated.
- The **Dead Sea Scrolls** include a copy of the Minor Prophets (4QXII) that contains Obadiah, confirming its ancient textual tradition.

**Jonah:** Jonah's historicity has been strengthened by archaeological discoveries:

- **Nineveh**, Assyria's capital, was excavated by Austen Henry Layard in the 1840s. The city was enormous for its day — roughly 3 miles across at its widest, with walls over 12 miles in circumference. The **Palace of Sennacherib** and the **Library of Ashurbanipal** (containing over 30,000 cuneiform tablets) confirmed Nineveh's grandeur, matching the biblical description of a "great city" (Jonah 1:2; 3:3).
- The **Nimrud Annals** describe Assyria's brutal military campaigns, providing context for why Jonah fled — the Assyrians were renowned for their cruelty, including impaling and flaying captives.
- The **Cyrus Cylinder** and other Persian-era documents mention the Assyrian practice of deporting conquered peoples, the very fate Jonah was likely sent to avert — he was to call Nineveh to repentance before judgment fell.
- Jonah's prayer from the fish (Jonah 2) uses language drawn from the Psalms, especially Psalms 42, 69, and 130, demonstrating Jonah's Jewish piety even in rebellion.

## New Testament Connections

- **Jonah 1:17** (the great fish) — Jesus explicitly references Jonah's experience as a sign in [[Matthew 12_39-41.md]] and [[Luke 11_29-32.md]]: "The men of Nineveh will stand at the judgment with this generation and condemn it, for they repented at the preaching of Jonah, and now something greater than Jonah is here." Jesus compares His three days in the tomb to Jonah's three days in the fish.
- **Jonah 3** — The repentance of Nineveh is held up as a model of true repentance that contrasts with the unbelief of Jesus' generation.
- **Obadiah 1:21** — "Deliverers will go up on Mount Zion to judge the mountains of Esau, and the kingdom will be the Lord's" — this eschatological vision of God's kingdom is echoed throughout the NT, including [[Revelation 11_15.md]] ("The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of His Christ").

## Connections to Other Prophets

- **Jonah and [[Nahum]]** — Nineveh features prominently in both. A century after Jonah's preaching brought repentance, Nahum pronounces Nineveh's destruction for returning to violence. Together they show God's justice and mercy in balance.
- **Obadiah and [[Ezekiel]]** — Ezekiel 25:12-14 and 35:1-15 also pronounce judgment on Edom (Mount Seir) for their violence against Israel. The theme of Edom's punishment is a thread running through the prophetic tradition.
- **Obadiah and [[Amos]]** — Amos 1:6, 9, 11 also includes oracles against Edom.
- **Obadiah and [[Joel]]** — Both speak of the [[Day of the Lord]] involving judgment on the nations and deliverance on Mount Zion.
- **Jonah and [[Joel]]** — Joel 2:13 ("rend your heart and not your garments") echoes God's character as "gracious and compassionate, slow to anger and abounding in love" — the very reason Jonah fled: he knew God would relent (Jonah 4:2).
