## Overview

Tonight, let's turn to 1 Kings, beginning with [[1 Kings 11.md]]. [[1 Kings 1_1-11.md]], of course, is just the continuation of the history of the kings of [[monarchy|Judah and Israel]]. [[1 Samuel 1.md]] and [[2 Samuel 1.md]] are taken up pretty much with the time from [[Samuel]] through the reign of [[David]] and the establishment of the [[Covenant|Davidic Covenant]] ([[2 Samuel 7_1-17.md]]). As we get into the book of the kings, we continue now the story of the history of the kings of Israel and Judah after [[David]] passes from the scene ([[1 Kings 2_10.md]]). [[1 Kings 1.md]] and [[2 Kings 1.md]] actually cover a period of approximately 400 years, from the united [[monarchy|monarchy]] under [[Solomon]] through the divided kingdom and the [[Exile|exile]] in [[Babylon]]. The opening chapters detail the succession struggle between [[Solomon]] and his brother Adonijah ([[1 Kings 1_5-53.md]]), [[David]]'s final charge to his son ([[1 Kings 2_1-9.md]]), and [[Solomon]]'s famous request for [[wisdom|wisdom]] from God ([[1 Kings 3_1-15.md]]), which established his early legacy as the wisest king of Israel.

## Key Themes

- The Davidic succession and the establishment of Solomon's kingdom ([[1 Kings 1_1-53.md]], [[1 Kings 2_1-46.md]])
- Solomon's wisdom as a gift from God sought above riches and power ([[1 Kings 3_1-15.md]])
- The golden age of the united monarchy at its peak under Solomon ([[1 Kings 4_1-34.md]])
- God's covenant faithfulness to the Davidic line ([[2 Samuel 7_12-16.md]])
- Transition from the warrior-king David to the builder-king Solomon

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- Solomon's reign (ca. 970–930 BC) marks the Golden Age of Israel, the united monarchy at its peak
- Solomon's wisdom, wealth, and international trade network included Egyptian horses, Phoenician cedar from Lebanon (via [[Hiram]] of Tyre), and Arabian gold
- The request for wisdom (1 Kings 3) rather than riches or military power established Solomon's early legacy
- Davidic succession in 1 Kings 1–2 demonstrates the transition from the warrior-king David to the builder-king Solomon
- Archaeological evidence for this period includes the Megiddo stables (once attributed to Solomon) and the Gezer gate, often linked to Solomonic construction (1 Kings 9:15–17)
- The seeds of the later divided kingdom appear already in [[1 Kings 11.md]] with Solomon's foreign wives and idolatry
