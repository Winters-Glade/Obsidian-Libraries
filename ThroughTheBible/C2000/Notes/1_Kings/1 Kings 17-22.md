## Overview

Let's turn to the 1st Kings, [[1 Kings 17_1.md]]. The 17th chapter of 1st Kings introduces us now to a very interesting character, [[Elijah]] the Tishbite, whatever Tishbite means. Now [[Elijah]] came from the area of Gilead, which you would call today Transjordan if you were in Israel. It was across Jordan in the area of Gad, and so he came from the area of Gilead. It is thought that it is possible that Tishbite means that he was not really an Israelite. That he was some other nationality, but that is only a conjecture, we don't know for sure. But he certainly had a very interesting career, and he comes to the apostate northern tribe at really sort of its lowest point, when [[Ahab]] is the king with his wicked wife [[Jezebel]] ([[1 Kings 16_29-33.md]]). And they have just about eliminated the worship of Jehovah. They have introduced Baal worship to Israel ([[1 Kings 16_31-32.md]]). They have broken down the altars of God. They have slain the prophets of God. And they have just about eliminated the worship of God from the northern kingdom of Israel. And so at this dark period of history, [[Elijah]] comes. [[Elijah]] comes on the scene with the message of God and the warning of God for the people ([[1 Kings 17_1.md]]). And so he's a very interesting character indeed. It is prophesied in Malachi that before Jesus comes, Elijah will return ([[Malachi 4_5-6.md]]). [[Elijah]] confronts [[Ahab]] and [[Jezebel]], performs mighty miracles — including the raising of the widow's son ([[1 Kings 17_17-24.md]]), the fire from heaven on [[Mount Carmel]] ([[1 Kings 18_1-40.md]]), and the defeat of the prophets of Baal — and then flees to [[Mount Horeb]] where God speaks to him in a still small voice ([[1 Kings 19_1-18.md]]). The chapters also record [[Ahab]]'s continued wickedness, including the murder of [[Naboth]] for his vineyard ([[1 Kings 21_1-29.md]]), and [[Ahab]]'s death in battle at [[Ramoth-Gilead]] ([[1 Kings 22_1-40.md]]).

## Key Themes

- The prophetic ministry of [[Elijah]] as God's instrument of [[judges|judgment]] and [[Repentance|call to repentance]] ([[1 Kings 17_1-24.md]], [[1 Kings 18_1-46.md]])
- The showdown on [[Mount Carmel]]: the Lord vs. Baal — demonstrating God's supremacy over false gods ([[1 Kings 18_20-40.md]])
- [[idolatry|Idolatry]] as the root of Israel's spiritual apostasy under [[Ahab]] and [[Jezebel]] ([[1 Kings 16_30-33.md]], [[1 Kings 18_18.md]])
- God's still small voice: [[Prophecy|prophetic]] communication beyond the spectacular ([[1 Kings 19_11-13.md]])
- The [[Covenant|covenant]] faithfulness of God even when His people have abandoned Him ([[1 Kings 19_14-18.md]])
- [[Ahab]]'s failure to heed [[Prophecy|prophetic warning]] and his violent death ([[1 Kings 20_1-43.md]], [[1 Kings 21_1-29.md]], [[1 Kings 22_1-40.md]])
- The injustice of [[Naboth]]'s vineyard and God's judgment on [[Ahab]] and [[Jezebel]] ([[1 Kings 21_17-24.md]])

## Historical Context

> [!qt-verified] Historical Context

- The ministry of Elijah (ca. 860s BC) took place during the reign of Ahab (ca. 874–853 BC), king of the northern kingdom of Israel
- The Omride dynasty (Omri, Ahab, Ahaziah, Jehoram) was a period of political strength but spiritual decline, heavily influenced by Phoenician culture through [[Jezebel]], daughter of Ethbaal, king of Sidon
- The confrontation on [[Mount Carmel]] (1 Kings 18) was a direct challenge to Baal worship, the Canaanite fertility god promoted by Jezebel
- Samaria, the capital of the northern kingdom built by Omri, has been extensively excavated, revealing large royal palaces and Phoenician-style ivory inlays (the "ivory house" of [[1 Kings 22_39.md]])
- Archaeological evidence from the period includes:
  - The **Tel Dan Stele** (ca. 840 BC) — though from a slightly later period, it confirms the political landscape of the northern kingdom
  - The **Moabite Stone** (Mesha Stele, ca. 840 BC) mentions Omri and the oppression of Moab
  - Samaria ostraca — administrative records from the reign of Jeroboam II that shed light on the economy and administration of the northern kingdom
- The role of prophets intensified during the divided monarchy: they were the primary channel of God's word to both kings and people, calling Israel back to covenant faithfulness
- Elijah's departure (2 Kings 2) and the prophecy of his return ([[Malachi 4_5–6.md]]) established a Messianic expectation fulfilled in [[John the Baptist]] ([[Matthew 11_14.md]], [[Matthew 17_10–13.md]])
