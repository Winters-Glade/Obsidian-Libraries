# Pergamum

## Description

Pergamum (also spelled Pergamos) was a prominent city in Mysia, in northwestern Asia Minor (modern Bergama, Turkey), about 15 miles inland from the Aegean coast. It was the capital of the Attalid kingdom before bequeathing its territory to Rome in 133 BC, and later the capital of the Roman province of Asia. Pergamum was a center of pagan worship, with a massive altar to Zeus (the "Throne of Satan," Revelation 2:13), temples to Athena, Dionysus, and Asclepius, and a major cult of emperor worship. It was also famous for its library (second only to Alexandria's), and its invention of parchment (from *Pergamena*). The church at Pergamum is one of the seven churches of Revelation. Jesus commended them for holding fast to His name even in the city "where Satan's throne is" and where Antipas was martyred. However, He rebuked them for tolerating those who held the teaching of Balaam and the Nicolaitans (Revelation 2:12-17). Pergamum represents the challenge of maintaining pure doctrine in a hostile, idolatrous environment.
