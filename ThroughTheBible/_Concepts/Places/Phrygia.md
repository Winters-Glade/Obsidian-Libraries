# Phrygia

## Description

Phrygia was a large inland region in west-central Asia Minor (modern Turkey), with no fixed boundaries but generally extending from the upper reaches of the Maeander River eastward to the Salt Lake region. It was known for its mountainous terrain, large estates, and distinctive culture. The Phrygians spoke an Indo-European language and were known for the worship of the mother goddess Cybele. In the New Testament, Phrygia is mentioned in Acts as one of the regions where Jewish pilgrims came from for Pentecost (Acts 2:10). Paul and Silas traveled through Phrygia on Paul's second missionary journey (Acts 16:6) and again on his third journey (Acts 18:23). The cities of Colossae, Laodicea, and Hierapolis were located in Phrygia. Paul's letter to the Colossians was addressed to a Phrygian church. The region was evangelized early and remained an important center of Christianity.
