# Corinth

## Description

Corinth was one of the most prominent cities of Roman Greece. Destroyed by the Roman consul Mummius in 146 BC, it was refounded as a Roman colony (Colonia Laus Iulia Corinthiensis) by Julius Caesar in 44 BC. By Paul's time, it had become the capital of the Roman province of Achaia (modern southern Greece) and a bustling commercial center with two major ports — Cenchreae to the east (on the Saronic Gulf) and Lechaion to the west (on the Gulf of Corinth) — controlling trade routes between Italy and Asia. The population was a mix of Roman colonists, Greeks, Jews, and people from across the empire.

The city was notorious even in the pagan world for its moral laxity. The Greek verb *korinthiazesthai* meant "to act like a Corinthian" — to practice fornication. The Temple of Aphrodite atop the Acrocorinth — a towering 1,886-foot citadel — dominated the skyline.

Chuck Smith emphasizes that Paul ministered in Corinth for 18 months during his second missionary journey (c. AD 50-52) — one of his longest stays in any city. The church faced many challenges: factionalism, immorality, lawsuits among believers, abuse of spiritual gifts, and confusion about the resurrection. Paul's two letters to the Corinthians address these issues with profound theological depth. Pastor Smith often highlights 1 Corinthians 13 (love), 1 Corinthians 15 (resurrection), and the theme of the cross as the power of God.
