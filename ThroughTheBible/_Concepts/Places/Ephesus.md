# Ephesus

## Description

Ephesus was the capital of the Roman province of Asia (western Asia Minor, modern Turkey) and one of the largest cities in the Roman Empire, with a population estimated at 200,000-250,000. Located at the mouth of the Cayster River, it served as a major port city with a thriving commercial economy. The city was dominated by the Temple of Artemis (Diana), one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, which made Ephesus a center of pagan worship and pilgrimage.

Chuck Smith teaches that Ephesus was a strategic center for Paul's ministry, serving as his base of operations in Asia Minor for about three years during his third missionary journey (c. AD 52-55). The church in Ephesus received one of Paul's most profound letters (Ephesians) and was one of the seven churches addressed in Revelation. Pastor Smith emphasizes that the Ephesian church's problem was not doctrinal error but the loss of its first love (Revelation 2:1-7). The city declined after the silting of its harbor and was gradually abandoned.

Ephesus is also associated with Timothy's pastoral ministry, Aquila and Priscilla's tentmaking business, and the riot of the silversmiths (Acts 19).
