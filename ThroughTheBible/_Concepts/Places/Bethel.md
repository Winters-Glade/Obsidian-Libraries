# Bethel

## Description

Bethel was a significant city in the hill country of Ephraim, approximately 12 miles north of Jerusalem on the central ridge route. Originally called Luz (Genesis 28:19), it was renamed Bethel, meaning "House of God," by Jacob after his famous dream of a ladder reaching to heaven with angels ascending and descending (Genesis 28:10-22). It was at Bethel that God confirmed His covenant with Jacob, promising the land to his descendants.

Chuck Smith highlights Bethel as a place of divine encounter and spiritual significance throughout biblical history. Bethel served as a religious center during the period of the judges (the Ark of the Covenant was kept there, Judges 20:18-28) and was one of the two golden calf worship centers established by Jeroboam I (1 Kings 12:25-33) to rival Jerusalem — making it a symbol of apostasy as well as devotion. The prophets Amos, Hosea, and Jeremiah pronounced judgment against Bethel's idolatry. Despite this, Bethel was also the home of prophets and was visited by Elisha after Elijah's translation (2 Kings 2:1-3).

Pastor Smith uses Bethel to illustrate both the heights of spiritual encounter and the depths of religious corruption — a place where God revealed Himself but where human tradition eventually replaced divine truth.
