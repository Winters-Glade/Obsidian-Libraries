# Phoenicia

## Description

Phoenicia was a coastal region along the eastern Mediterranean, corresponding to modern Lebanon and parts of Syria and northern Israel. Its most famous cities were Tyre and Sidon. The Phoenicians were renowned as the greatest sailors and shipbuilders of the ancient world, establishing colonies across the Mediterranean (including Carthage). They developed the alphabet that became the basis for Greek and Latin scripts. In the Old Testament, Hiram king of Tyre supplied cedar and craftsmen for Solomon's Temple (1 Kings 5). The Phoenicians worshiped Baal and Ashtoreth, whose cults led Israel into idolatry — especially through Jezebel, daughter of the Sidonian king Ethbaal (1 Kings 16:31). Elijah's conflict with the prophets of Baal was a direct confrontation with Phoenician religion. In the New Testament, Jesus ministered in the region of Tyre and Sidon (Matthew 15:21-28), where He healed the Syro-Phoenician woman's daughter. Paul visited Phoenician churches (Acts 21:2-4).
