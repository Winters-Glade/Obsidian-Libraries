# Thessalonica

## Description

Thessalonica (modern Thessaloniki, Greece) was the capital of the Roman province of Macedonia and one of the largest and most important cities in the region. Located on the Thermaic Gulf at the junction of the Via Egnatia — the main Roman road connecting the Adriatic Sea to Byzantium — it was a major port and commercial center. Founded around 315 BC by Cassander of Macedon and named after his wife Thessalonike, half-sister of Alexander the Great, the city was a free city under Roman rule, governed by its own magistrates called *politarchs* (a title confirmed by inscriptions, Acts 17:6).

Chuck Smith teaches that Paul established the church in Thessalonica during his second missionary journey (c. AD 49-50), preaching for three Sabbaths in the Jewish synagogue. The church was composed primarily of Gentile converts who turned from idols to serve the living God (1 Thessalonians 1:9). Paul's letters to the Thessalonians emphasize the second coming of Christ, the hope of the resurrection, and holy living in light of the Lord's return. Pastor Smith highlights that the Thessalonian believers were models of faith, love, and perseverance despite persecution.

The city continued to prosper through the Byzantine and Ottoman periods and remains a major Greek city today.
