# Athens

## Description

Athens was the intellectual and cultural center of the ancient Greek world, located in the region of Attica, about 5 miles from its port city of Piraeus. Famous for its philosophers (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle), its Acropolis crowned by the Parthenon, and its schools of Stoic and Epicurean philosophy, Athens represented the pinnacle of human wisdom and learning in the first century AD.

Chuck Smith highlights Paul's visit to Athens during his second missionary journey (c. AD 50-52) as recorded in Acts 17. Paul's sermon on Mars Hill (Areopagus) is a masterclass in contextualized evangelism — he engaged the Athenians on their own ground, quoting their own poets (Aratus, Epimenides), and proclaimed the "unknown god" they worshipped ignorantly. Pastor Smith often uses this passage to teach about the contrast between human wisdom and divine revelation, and how the gospel addresses both Jews (who seek signs) and Greeks (who seek wisdom). Athens represents the world's intellectual pride confronted by the simple message of the cross and the resurrection.

The city's response was mixed — some mocked, some wanted to hear more, and a few believed, including Dionysius the Areopagite and Damaris (Acts 17:32-34).
