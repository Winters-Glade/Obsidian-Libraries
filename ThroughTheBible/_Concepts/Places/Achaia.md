# Achaia

## Description

Achaia was the Roman province that covered most of southern and central Greece, with its capital at Corinth. Established after the Roman conquest of Greece in 146 BC, it was governed by a proconsul (senatorial province) from 27 BC onward. The province included Athens, Corinth, Cenchreae, and the Peloponnese peninsula. It was separated from the province of Macedonia by a line running roughly from the Adriatic Sea to the Aegean Sea.

Chuck Smith notes that Achaia is mentioned in the New Testament in connection with Paul's missionary work, particularly his ministry in Corinth and Athens. The churches in Achaia contributed to the collection for the Jerusalem saints, and Paul commends their generosity (2 Corinthians 9:2). The proconsul Gallio, who dismissed charges against Paul in Corinth (Acts 18:12-17), ruled Achaia in AD 51-52 — an event that provides a key chronological anchor for Paul's ministry.

The region was known for its Greek culture, philosophy, and pagan temples. Its spiritual need was great, and Paul invested significant time there.
