# Asia Minor

## Description

Asia Minor (modern-day Turkey) is the large peninsula that forms the westernmost extension of Asia, bordered by the Black Sea to the north, the Aegean Sea to the west, and the Mediterranean Sea to the south. In the Roman period, it was divided into several provinces including Asia, Galatia, Cappadocia, Bithynia, Pontus, Lycia, Pamphylia, and Cilicia. The region was densely populated with Greek and Roman cities connected by well-built Roman roads.

Asia Minor is the most important region for early Christian history after the land of Israel. Paul was born in Tarsus (Cilicia) and conducted his missionary journeys primarily through the cities of Asia Minor — including Ephesus, Antioch of Pisidia, Iconium, Lystra, Derbe, and the cities of Galatia. The seven churches of Revelation (Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, Laodicea) were all in Asia Minor. Peter's first epistle is addressed to believers scattered throughout Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia — all regions in Asia Minor.

Chuck Smith highlights Asia Minor as the fertile soil where the early church took root and flourished despite severe persecution. The British classicist Sir William Ramsay, initially a skeptic of Acts, reversed his position after archaeological fieldwork in Asia Minor, concluding Acts was "a history of first-rate historical value."
