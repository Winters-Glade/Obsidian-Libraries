# Cenchreae

## Description

Cenchreae (modern Kechries) was the eastern port of Corinth, located on the Saronic Gulf approximately 5 miles east of the city center. It served as Corinth's gateway to the Aegean Sea and points east including Asia Minor, Ephesus, and the eastern Mediterranean. The western port of Corinth was Lechaion, on the Gulf of Corinth, providing access to Italy and the Adriatic. This dual-port configuration made Corinth the commercial hub of Greece, controlling trade routes between east and west.

In the New Testament, Cenchreae is mentioned in connection with Paul's ministry. Paul sailed from Cenchreae to Ephesus at the end of his second missionary journey (Acts 18:18), having cut his hair there because of a vow. A woman named Phoebe is mentioned by Paul in Romans 16:1 as a "servant of the church at Cenchreae" — likely the deaconess or patron who delivered Paul's letter to the Romans. This indicates that a Christian community existed in this port town, likely founded by Paul during his 18-month stay in Corinth.

Chuck Smith notes that the ports of Corinth — Cenchreae and Lechaion — were integral to the city's commercial wealth and cosmopolitan character, which shaped both the opportunities and challenges Paul faced in ministering there.
