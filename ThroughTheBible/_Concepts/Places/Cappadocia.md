# Cappadocia

## Description

Cappadocia was a large inland region in eastern Asia Minor (modern central Turkey), known for its unique volcanic landscape, underground cities, and cave dwellings. It became a Roman province in AD 17. In the New Testament, it is mentioned as one of the regions from which Jewish pilgrims came to Jerusalem for Pentecost (Acts 2:9). Peter addressed his first epistle to the elect "sojourners of the Dispersion" in Cappadocia, among other provinces (1 Peter 1:1). The early church spread there early, and by the fourth century, Cappadocia was a center of Christian theology, producing the Cappadocian Fathers (Basil the Great, Gregory of Nyssa, Gregory of Nazianzus) who were instrumental in defining the doctrine of the Trinity. The region contains numerous rock-cut churches from the Byzantine era.
