# Nineveh

## Description

Nineveh was the magnificent capital of the Assyrian Empire, located on the eastern bank of the Tigris River opposite modern Mosul, Iraq. At its height under Sennacherib (704-681 BC), it was the largest city in the world, surrounded by massive walls (7.5 miles in circumference, high enough for three chariots to ride abreast) and adorned with splendid palaces, libraries, and temples. The city featured an elaborate aqueduct system and the famous "Library of Ashurbanipal" containing thousands of cuneiform tablets. In the Bible, Nineveh is best known as the destination of the prophet Jonah, who reluctantly preached repentance to its inhabitants. The entire city repented — from the king to the common people — and God relented of the disaster He had planned (Jonah 3:1-10). The prophet Nahum pronounced judgment on Nineveh, foretelling its complete destruction (Nahum 1-3). Nineveh fell to a coalition of Medes, Babylonians, and Scythians in 612 BC, ending the Assyrian Empire. The city's ruins lay buried until excavations in the 19th century.
