# Lechaion

## Description

Lechaion was the western port of Corinth, located on the Gulf of Corinth approximately 3 miles northwest of the city center. It served as Corinth's gateway to Italy, the Adriatic Sea, and the western Mediterranean. Together with Cenchreae (the eastern port on the Saronic Gulf), Lechaion made Corinth the commercial hub of Greece, connecting trade routes between east and west. The two ports were connected to Corinth by long walls (similar to the Long Walls of Athens) for protection.

In the New Testament context, Lechaion was vital to Corinth's prosperity and cosmopolitan character. The city's dual-port configuration brought travelers, merchants, sailors, and cultural influences from across the Roman Empire. This international character shaped the Corinthian church — a diverse congregation of Jews, Greeks, Romans, and others from various social strata.

Chuck Smith notes that the ports of Corinth contributed to both the city's wealth and its moral challenges. The constant flow of travelers and sailors meant that Corinth was exposed to every form of vice and pagan religion from across the empire.
