# Acrocorinth

## Description

Acrocorinth is the towering 1,886-foot (575-meter) monolithic rock citadel that dominates the landscape of ancient Corinth. Located about 3.5 miles south of the city center, this massive limestone peak served as the acropolis (high city) and fortress of Corinth from antiquity through the Middle Ages. Its summit was crowned by the Temple of Aphrodite, the Greek goddess of love, which in the classical period was said to employ a thousand temple prostitutes — though modern scholarship questions the extent of cultic prostitution in the Roman period.

Chuck Smith references Acrocorinth in the context of Paul's ministry in Corinth, noting that the temple of Aphrodite atop this mountain was visible from everywhere in the city and symbolized the moral corruption that pervaded Corinthian society. The verb *korinthiazesthai* ("to act like a Corinthian") reflected the city's reputation for sexual immorality, which the church at Corinth struggled to overcome. The Acrocorinth thus represents the spiritual battlefield of the Corinthian church — a city dominated by pagan idolatry and immorality where the gospel of grace confronted the prevailing culture.

The Acrocorinth was also the site of the Peirene Spring, which provided water to the lower city. Today, the site contains ruins of temples, fortifications from various periods, and offers panoramic views of the Corinthian and Saronic Gulfs.
