# Babylon

## Description

Babylon was the magnificent capital of the Babylonian Empire, located on the Euphrates River in what is now central Iraq. At its height under King Nebuchadnezzar II (605-562 BC), it was the largest city in the world, famous for its massive walls, the Ishtar Gate, the Hanging Gardens (one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World), and its towering ziggurat. The name "Babylon" comes from the Akkadian *Bab-ilim*, meaning "gate of the god" — but the Bible connects it to the Tower of Babel (Genesis 11:9), where God confused human language.

Chuck Smith presents Babylon as the archetype of human pride, rebellion against God, and worldly power. It was the instrument of God's judgment on Judah, destroying Jerusalem and the Temple in 586 BC and taking the people into exile. The prophets Isaiah and Jeremiah pronounced judgment on Babylon, and its fall is prophesied in vivid detail. In the New Testament, Babylon becomes a symbol of the world system opposed to God, especially in Revelation. Pastor Smith often highlights the contrast between Babylon (human effort, confusion) and Jerusalem (God's dwelling, peace).

Babylon was occupied by the Persian Empire after Cyrus conquered it in 539 BC, then fell into decline. The site today (modern Hillah, Iraq) consists of extensive ruins.
