# Pontus

## Description

Pontus was a region in northeastern Asia Minor (modern Turkey), along the southern coast of the Black Sea. It was originally a Hellenistic kingdom that became a Roman province in 64 BC after the defeat of King Mithridates VI. In the New Testament, Pontus is mentioned as one of the regions from which Jewish pilgrims came to Jerusalem for Pentecost (Acts 2:9). Aquila, a Jewish Christian whom Paul met in Corinth, was a native of Pontus (Acts 18:2-3). Peter addresses his first epistle to the "elect sojourners of the Dispersion" in Pontus, among other provinces (1 Peter 1:1). The earliest Christian presence in Pontus likely resulted from the Pentecost visitors returning home with the gospel. Pliny the Younger, the Roman governor of Bithynia-Pontus (c. AD 112), wrote to Emperor Trajan about Christians in the region, indicating a strong early church presence. Pontus was also the home of the church father Gregory Thaumaturgus and the heretic Marcion.
