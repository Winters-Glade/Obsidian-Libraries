# Parallelism

## Definition

Parallelism (also called *parallelismus membrorum* or "thought rhyme") is the foundational structural device of Hebrew poetry, first formally classified by Robert Lowth in the 18th century. Unlike Western poetry that relies on rhyme and meter, Hebrew parallelism achieves its effect through semantic balance — the second line of a couplet echoes, contrasts with, or develops the thought of the first line. This creates rhythm, emphasis, and depth, inviting the reader to meditate on the layered meaning of the text.

## Types of Parallelism

Biblical Hebrew poetry features three primary types of parallelism, along with more complex variations:

- **Synonymous Parallelism** — The second line restates the first using different words for emphasis (e.g., Psalm 24:1).
- **Antithetic Parallelism** — The second line contrasts with the first, common in wisdom literature (e.g., Proverbs 10:1).
- **Synthetic Parallelism** — The second line completes or expands upon the first (e.g., Psalm 1:3).
- Other forms include **chiastic parallelism**, **staircase parallelism**, and **emblematic parallelism**.

## Key Bible Verses

- [[Psalm 41]] — Synonymous: "The earth is the LORD's, and all its fullness, the world and those who dwell therein."
- [[Proverbs 10_12]] — Antithetic: "A wise son makes a glad father, but a foolish son is the grief of his mother."
- [[Psalm 132]] — Synthetic: "He shall be like a tree planted by the rivers of water..."
- [[Psalm 19]] — Multiple forms within one passage
- [[Isaiah 55_6-7]] — Prophetic parallelism

## Connection to Chuck Smith / C2000

Pastor Chuck Smith, teaching verse by verse through the Psalms and the Prophets, frequently drew attention to parallelism as a key to understanding the poetic and prophetic books. His expository style naturally highlighted how the parallel structure of Hebrew poetry reinforces theological truth — showing how the second line of a verse amplifies or clarifies the first. He often noted that recognizing parallelism helps the reader avoid misinterpretation by understanding the Bible in its original literary context.
