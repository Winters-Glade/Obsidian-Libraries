# Mashal

## Definition

*Mashal* (Hebrew: משל, plural *meshalim*) is the Hebrew word for "proverb," "parable," or "allegory" — a short, pithy saying that conveys a moral or spiritual truth through comparison or analogy. The Book of Proverbs is called *Mishlei* in Hebrew, meaning "Proverbs of Solomon." In rabbinic literature, the *mashal* developed into an elaborate narrative form with a stated moral (*nimshal*). Talmudic scholar Daniel Boyarin describes the *mashal* as a process of "exemplification," essential to understanding both biblical and rabbinic thought.

## Key Bible Verses

- [[Proverbs 2_1-6]] — The purpose of the proverbs: "to know wisdom and instruction."
- [[Proverbs 10_12]] — The beginning of the proverbial sayings of Solomon.
- [[2 Samuel]] — Nathan's parable (a *mashal*) to David about the poor man's lamb.
- [[Ezekiel]] — Ezekiel commanded to "put forth a riddle and speak a *mashal*" to Israel.
- [[He Could, He Should, He Didn't, Judges 16_15-22 ]] — Jotham's parable of the trees choosing a king.
- [[Psalm 80_3, 7, 19]] — "I will open my mouth in a *mashal*; I will utter dark sayings of old."

## Connection to Chuck Smith / C2000

Chuck Smith's teaching style itself reflected the *mashal* tradition — he frequently used simple illustrations and comparisons drawn from everyday life to illuminate deep spiritual truths. In his C2000 series on Proverbs, he treated each proverb as a concentrated nugget of divine wisdom meant to be pondered and applied. He emphasized that the *mashal* form invites the reader to slow down and reflect, making biblical wisdom accessible to every believer.
