# Acrostic

## Definition

An acrostic is a literary device in which successive lines, verses, or stanzas begin with the letters of the Hebrew alphabet in order. In the Bible, acrostic poems serve both artistic and mnemonic functions — they demonstrate the completeness of a subject (from Aleph to Tav, the first and last letters) and aid in memorization. Biblical acrostics are found primarily in the Psalms, Lamentations, and Proverbs.

## Key Bible Verses

- [[Psalm 19]] — The most elaborate acrostic: 22 stanzas of 8 verses each, one for each letter of the Hebrew alphabet, each stanza celebrating God's Word.
- [[_Concepts/Biblical_References/Psalm 37]] — A partial acrostic on the theme of trusting in the Lord rather than envying the wicked.
- [[Proverbs 31_10-31]] — The "virtuous wife" passage is an acrostic in the original Hebrew.
- [[Lamentations]] — Four acrostic poems lamenting the destruction of Jerusalem.
- [[Psalm 2]] — An incomplete acrostic expressing trust in God.

## Connection to Chuck Smith / C2000

Chuck Smith frequently pointed out the acrostic structure of Psalm 119 in his C2000 teachings, marveling at how the psalmist devoted eight verses to each Hebrew letter, all focused on the beauty and sufficiency of God's Word. He used this structure to emphasize the completeness of Scripture — just as the acrostic covers the entire alphabet, so God's Word covers every aspect of life.
