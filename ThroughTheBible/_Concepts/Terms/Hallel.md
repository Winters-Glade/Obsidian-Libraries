# Hallel

## Definition

The Hallel (Hebrew for "praise") refers to Psalms 113–118, a collection of praise psalms traditionally recited during major Jewish festivals including Passover, Pentecost, Tabernacles, and Hanukkah. These psalms proclaim God's mighty acts of deliverance and are closely associated with the Passover Seder, after which Jesus and His disciples sang a hymn (likely the Hallel) before going to the Mount of Olives.

## Key Bible Verses

- [[Psalm 115_3]] — Praise to God who raises the poor
- [[Psalm 14]] — Israel's deliverance from Egypt
- [[Psalm 115_3]] — Glory to God alone, not idols
- [[Psalm 16]] — Thanksgiving for deliverance from death
- [[Psalm 17]] — The shortest psalm, calling all nations to praise
- [[Psalm 118]] — The cornerstone psalm quoted in the New Testament
- [[Matthew]] — Jesus and the disciples singing a hymn (the Hallel) after the Last Supper

## Connection to Chuck Smith / C2000

Chuck Smith highlighted the Hallel psalms in his C2000 teaching on the Psalms, noting that Psalm 118 — particularly the verse about the stone the builders rejected becoming the chief cornerstone — was quoted by Jesus regarding His own rejection and exaltation. He also connected the Hallel to the Passion narrative, pointing out that Jesus sang these very psalms on the night of His betrayal.
