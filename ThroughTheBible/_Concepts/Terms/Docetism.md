# Docetism

## Definition

Docetism (from Greek *dokein*, "to seem" or "to appear") is an early Christian heresy that denied the true humanity of Jesus Christ, teaching that He only *seemed* to have a physical body. According to Docetic thought, because matter was considered inherently evil, the divine Christ could not have taken on real flesh. This false teaching emerged in the first and second centuries and is combated in several New Testament writings. Docetism was unequivocally rejected at the First Council of Nicaea (AD 325).

## Key Bible Verses

- [[1 John 4_1-3]] — "Every spirit that confesses that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh is of God."
- [[2 John 1_7]] — "Many deceivers have gone out into the world who do not confess Jesus Christ as coming in the flesh."
- [[Colossians 2_9]] — "For in Him dwells all the fullness of the Godhead bodily."
- [[John 1_14]] — "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us."
- [[Luke]] — Jesus after the resurrection: "Handle Me and see, for a spirit does not have flesh and bones as you see I have."

## Connection to Chuck Smith / C2000

In his C2000 teachings through the epistles of John and Paul, Chuck Smith addressed Docetism as one of the early distortions of the Christian faith that the apostles had to confront. He emphasized that the incarnation — God becoming truly human in Jesus Christ — is essential to the gospel. If Christ did not genuinely suffer and die, there would be no atonement for sin. This connection between Christ's real humanity and the efficacy of His sacrifice was a recurring theme in his teaching.
