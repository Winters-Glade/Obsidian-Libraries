# Mediation

## Definition

In biblical theology, mediation refers to the work of a go-between who intercedes to reconcile two parties who are at odds. The central mediation in Scripture is that of Jesus Christ, who as the "one Mediator between God and men" (1 Timothy 2:5) reconciles sinful humanity to a holy God through His sacrificial death. Christ's mediation is superior to all previous mediators (Moses, the priests, the prophets) because He is both fully God and fully man, and His once-for-all sacrifice secures eternal redemption. The concept also encompasses the intercessory work of Christ at the right hand of the Father.

## Key Bible Verses

- [[1 Timothy 5-6]] — "For there is one God and one Mediator between God and men, the Man Christ Jesus."
- [[Hebrews 8_6]] — Christ is the "Mediator of a better covenant, which was established on better promises."
- [[Hebrews 9_15]] — "He is the Mediator of the new covenant, by means of death, for the redemption of the transgressions under the first covenant."
- [[Hebrews 12_24]] — "Jesus the Mediator of the new covenant."
- [[Romans 8_3]] — Christ's ongoing intercession: "Who is he who condemns? It is Christ who died, and furthermore is also risen, who is even at the right hand of God, who also makes intercession for us."
- [[Exodus]] — The people asked Moses to be their mediator, foreshadowing the need for Christ.

## Connection to Chuck Smith / C2000

In his C2000 teachings on Hebrews and the Pastoral Epistles, Chuck Smith emphasized the uniqueness and sufficiency of Christ's mediation. He taught that all other mediators — priests, saints, or religious rituals — are unnecessary and ineffective because Christ's work is complete. This truth was central to his understanding of the gospel: that sinners are reconciled to God not by their own efforts but through the sole mediation of Jesus Christ. He often contrasted the Old Testament priesthood, which required repeated sacrifices, with the perfect, once-for-all mediation of Christ.
