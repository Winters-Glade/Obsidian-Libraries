# Deutero-Isaiah

## Definition

Deutero-Isaiah (or Second Isaiah) is the scholarly designation for the anonymous prophet(s) whom many critical scholars propose authored Isaiah 40–55, written during the Babylonian exile (c. 540 BCE). The theory posits that these chapters differ in historical setting, style, and theological emphasis from Isaiah 1–39, addressing the exiles in Babylon with messages of comfort, hope, and the promise of deliverance through Cyrus.

## Connection to Chuck Smith / C2000

Chuck Smith's C2000 notes reference Deutero-Isaiah in the context of the "I Am" statements of God in Isaiah 40–55, which John's Gospel echoes in Jesus' seven "I Am" declarations. While Smith generally held to the unity of Isaiah (attributing the entire book to the eighth-century prophet), the C2000 notes acknowledge the scholarly theory of Deutero-Isaiah as a framework for understanding the literary structure of the book.
